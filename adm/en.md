GMO corn and soy processing contributing to deforestation and river nitrate pollution.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. ADM's antitrust crimes in price-fixing directly harmed millions of consumers and producers worldwide, causing artificial food price increases. Toxic discharges from the company's facilities poisoned drinking water sources in Illinois and Iowa.

1. **Lysine price-fixing conspiracy (1992–1995)** — ADM organised an international cartel to fix prices on lysine — a feed amino acid that affects meat prices worldwide. As a result of the conspiracy, consumers overpaid by hundreds of millions of dollars. In 1996, ADM pleaded guilty and paid a record fine of $100 million. Three top executives received prison sentences.
   *Source: U.S. Department of Justice, Antitrust Division, 1996*

2. **Citric acid and high-fructose corn syrup price-fixing (1990s)** — In parallel with the lysine cartel, ADM participated in conspiracies on citric acid and HFCS markets. Total fines and civil suits exceeded $400 million. Food consumers worldwide became victims of artificially inflated prices.
   *Source: FBI "Informant" case files; Eichenwald K. "The Informant", 2000*

3. **Drinking water contamination in Illinois (2000s–2010s)** — ADM facilities in Decatur, Illinois discharged industrial effluents into the Sangamon River with nitrate and ammonia levels exceeding permitted standards. Local municipalities bore additional water treatment costs. Residents downstream recorded elevated toxin levels in the water.
   *Source: Illinois EPA enforcement records; Environmental Working Group, 2014*

## 2. Has the corporation's activity led to the destruction of natural ecosystems?

**Answer: YES**. ADM is one of the largest drivers of agro-industrial expansion in Brazil and the USA, directly linked to the destruction of the Cerrado and Amazon biomes, as well as the creation of the "dead zone" in the Gulf of Mexico.

1. **Cerrado deforestation through soy supply chains (2010s–2020s)** — A Rainforest Action Network investigation (2022) showed that ADM purchased soy from producers clearing land in the Cerrado biome — Brazil's second-largest tropical forest. The Cerrado loses approximately 1 million hectares annually. ADM was listed in the Forest 500 as a company with critically insufficient forest protection measures.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network, "Buyers of Brazilian Destruction", 2022*

2. **"Dead zone" in the Gulf of Mexico** — The annual hypoxic zone off the coast of Louisiana, covering 13,000–20,000 km², forms from nitrate runoff from corn fields. ADM, as the largest corn processor in the USA, stimulates overproduction requiring excess fertilisers. The zone destroys fish populations, depriving thousands of fishermen of their livelihoods.
   *Source: NOAA Gulf of Mexico Dead Zone reports; EWG, 2023*

3. **Construction of a grain terminal in Pará, Brazil** — ADM invested in grain export infrastructure in the Brazilian state of Pará — an Amazon region with intensive land clearing for soy. The creation of export logistics accelerated economic pressure to deforest buffer zones.
   *Source: Trase.earth supply chain data, 2021*

## 3. Has the corporation used or permitted forced or child labour?

**Answer: YES**. In the cocoa bean supply chains of ADM's ADM Cocoa division, systemic child labour rights violations have been documented on farms in West Africa. Cocoa pickers in Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana work under conditions recognised as forced and child labour.

1. **Child labour in cocoa supply chains (Côte d'Ivoire, 2000s–2020s)** — Investigations by the International Labour Organization (ILO) and Slave Free Chocolate revealed that children under 14 work on cocoa farms supplying ADM Cocoa. Work included the use of chemicals and machetes. ADM became one of the defendants in a US class action lawsuit (2021).
   *Source: ILO, "Child Labour in the Cocoa Sector", 2020; U.S. District Court, 2021*

2. **Child slave lawsuit against ADM, Cargill, and Barry Callebaut (2021)** — Eight Malian women filed a US lawsuit claiming they were sold into slavery as children on cocoa farms supplying major traders. ADM was listed among the defendants as a buyer of cocoa from these supply chains.
   *Source: Nestlé USA, Inc. v. Doe, U.S. Supreme Court, 2021*

3. **Non-fulfilment of commitments under the Harkin-Engel Protocol (2001–2021)** — ADM signed the voluntary protocol to eliminate "the worst forms of child labour" in the cocoa sector by 2005. Twenty years later, independent audits continued to document child labour in the company's supply chains at a systemic scale.
   *Source: U.S. Department of Labor, "2020 Cocoa Report"*

## 4. Has the corporation violated the rights of indigenous peoples or local communities?

**Answer: YES**. The expansion of soy production in Brazil, which ADM stimulates through its purchases, directly threatens indigenous territories in the Cerrado and Amazon biomes. Local communities in the USA also suffered from toxic impacts of the company's facilities.

1. **Encroachment on indigenous territories in the Cerrado (Brazil)** — ADM suppliers in Mato Grosso and Bahia cleared land bordering officially recognised indigenous territories. The Cerrado Campaign (2021) documented cases of threats and violence against community leaders opposing the expansion of soy plantations.
   *Source: Cerrado Campaign / Mighty Earth, 2021*

2. **Pollution and community health in Decatur, Illinois** — ADM facilities in industrial Decatur are the largest source of VOC emissions and industrial dust. Districts with predominantly African-American populations near the plants recorded elevated rates of asthma and respiratory diseases.
   *Source: EPA TRI database; Illinois EPA, 2018*

3. **Land grabbing from smallholder farmers in Brazil (through suppliers)** — Trase.earth analysis (2022) showed that some ADM suppliers acquired land through mechanisms contested by local peasant communities as "grilagem" — the Brazilian practice of illegal land seizure with forged documents.
   *Source: Trase.earth, "Land Conflicts in Cerrado", 2022*

## 5. Has the corporation concealed information, falsified data, or misled the public?

**Answer: YES**. ADM used an aggressive disinformation strategy during the price-fixing investigation, and its corporate rhetoric on "sustainable development" systematically diverges from the company's real performance.

1. **Destruction of evidence during the antitrust investigation (1995)** — ADM top executives, upon learning of the FBI wiretap, began destroying documents related to the lysine cartel. Internal whistleblower Mark Whitacre disclosed this activity to authorities. Concealment of evidence became a separate count in the indictment.
   *Source: FBI investigation records; Eichenwald K. "The Informant", 2000*

2. **Greenwashing through "sustainable" declarations (2015–2023)** — ADM publishes annual ESG reports claiming "responsible" soy supply chains. Meanwhile, independent audits by Trase.earth and RAN note that the company does not disclose its full list of second-tier suppliers, making verification impossible.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network, "Forest 500 Assessment", 2022*

3. **Lobbying for biofuel subsidies without disclosing conflicts of interest** — ADM is the largest corn ethanol producer in the USA and intensively lobbied for the federal Renewable Fuel Standard (RFS), receiving billions in subsidies. At the same time, the company did not disclose that expanding corn cultivation for biofuel disproportionately increases river nitrate pollution.
   *Source: Environmental Working Group, "Ethanol's Dirty Secret", 2021*

## 6. Has the corporation engaged in tax evasion or used illegal financial schemes?

**Answer: YES**. ADM applies a complex of offshore structures and transfer pricing to minimise tax obligations, as confirmed by journalistic investigations and EU tax authority inquiries.

1. **Offshore structures in the Netherlands and Switzerland** — A Tax Justice Network investigation (2021) showed that ADM routes a significant portion of commodity trading profits through subsidiaries in the Netherlands and Switzerland, taking advantage of preferential tax regimes. By estimates, hundreds of millions of dollars are removed from the US tax base annually.
   *Source: Tax Justice Network, "Big Ag, Big Tax Avoidance", 2021*

2. **Transfer pricing in grain trading** — Chain Reaction Research analysis (2020) identified signs of ADM using transfer pricing mechanisms in transactions between the parent company and subsidiary trading structures, reducing taxable profit in countries of operation.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research, 2020*

3. **Subsidiary structures in tax havens** — According to the OpenCorporates database, ADM has registered structures in Luxembourg, the Cayman Islands, and other zero- or low-tax jurisdictions used to accumulate trading profits.
   *Source: OpenCorporates; ICIJ Offshore Leaks Database*

## 7. Has the corporation exerted illegal or unethical influence on policy and regulators?

**Answer: YES**. ADM has one of the most documented histories of lobbying pressure and political corruption among American agro-industrial corporations. The company was called a "political machine" due to decades of donations and influence in Washington.

1. **Financing political campaigns and "revolving door" (1980s–1990s)** — Former ADM CEO Dwayne Andreas became famous for large-scale political donations to both parties. In the 1980s–90s, ADM was the largest corporate donor to congressional agricultural committees, lobbying for corn ethanol subsidies and price support.
   *Source: Center for Responsive Politics; Bovard J. "Archer Daniels Midland: A Case Study in Corporate Welfare", 1995*

2. **Lobbying for the Renewable Fuel Standard at the expense of the environment** — ADM spent more than $50 million on lobbying between 2008 and 2023. The main goal was to maintain and expand the federal corn ethanol mandate, despite scientific consensus that corn ethanol increases rather than reduces pressure on ecosystems.
   *Source: OpenSecrets.org lobbying database; Congressional Research Service, 2022*

3. **Blocking environmental nitrate standards** — Through industry associations (Corn Refiners Association, National Corn Growers), ADM systematically opposed the introduction of federal standards on nitrate runoff, which would have required farmers to reduce fertiliser use — thereby reducing demand for corn.
   *Source: Environmental Defense Fund; Politico "Big Ag's war on clean water", 2019*

## 8. Is there evidence that the violations are systemic — repeated, recurring rather than isolated incidents?

**Answer: YES**. ADM's violations span several decades, several continents, and several categories of offences — from cartel conspiracies to environmental pollution and labour rights violations.

1. **Three parallel cartel conspiracies simultaneously (1990s)** — ADM's simultaneous participation in price-fixing on lysine, citric acid, and HFCS markets was not accidental but a corporate culture. FBI internal recordings documented that top management regarded cartels as a standard business tool.
   *Source: DOJ Antitrust Division case history; FBI surveillance recordings, 1992–1995*

2. **Repeated environmental law violations in Illinois** — The Illinois EPA recorded violations by ADM facilities in Decatur repeatedly during the 2000s and 2010s: effluent standard exceedances, odour, air pollution. Fines were paid, but production practices did not fundamentally change.
   *Source: Illinois EPA, enforcement actions database*

3. **Twenty years of unfulfilled promises in cocoa supply chains** — Since signing the Harkin-Engel Protocol in 2001, ADM consistently missed agreed deadlines for eliminating child labour — 2005, 2008, 2010, 2020. Each time the company declared "progress," while independent researchers documented the persistence of the problem.
   *Source: U.S. Department of Labor Cocoa reports, 2001–2020*

## Summary

1. **Antitrust crimes**: ADM organised international cartel conspiracies on lysine, citric acid, and HFCS markets, paying fines exceeding $400 million and sending managers to prison — one of the largest antitrust cases in US history.
2. **Environmental damage**: The company's activities are a structural cause of the annual "dead zone" in the Gulf of Mexico covering up to 20,000 km², destroying marine ecosystems.
3. **Deforestation**: Soy purchases in Brazil are linked to the destruction of the Cerrado biome; the company is listed in the Forest 500 as an entity with insufficient forest protection measures.
4. **Child labour**: The ADM Cocoa division used supply chains with documented cases of child and forced labour in West Africa for twenty years.
5. **Tax schemes**: Offshore structures in the Netherlands, Switzerland, and Luxembourg are used to remove profits from the tax base.
6. **Political corruption**: Decades of aggressive lobbying secured ADM multi-billion-dollar subsidies for corn ethanol, directly damaging ecosystems.
7. **Systemic character**: Violations are not isolated incidents — the company demonstrates a persistent pattern: offence, fine, promise of reform, repetition.
8. **Information opacity**: Greenwashing through ESG reports while simultaneously refusing to disclose second- and third-tier supply chains makes independent verification of the company's claims impossible.
