Alcoa and the Red Mud Disaster

In October 2010, a tailings dam at the MAL Magyar Aluminium alumina plant (an Alcoa supplier) near Ajka, Hungary, burst, releasing approximately one million cubic metres of highly corrosive waste with a pH of around 13 over several villages. Ten people were killed and around 150 suffered chemical burns. The flow of sludge reached the Danube, triggering an international environmental emergency.

Red mud — a by-product of alumina production — contains arsenic, chromium and heavy metals. Remediation took years; the village of Devecser was completely evacuated.

Alcoa itself operates red mud tailings storage facilities covering hundreds of hectares in Western Australia. Leaks from these sites are periodically recorded in groundwater, threatening coastal wetlands.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Aluminium production at Alcoa's facilities has been accompanied by deaths, occupational diseases, and exposure of workers and local communities to toxic substances.

1. **Red mud disaster in Hungary (October 2010)** — The failure of a tailings dam at the MAL Magyar Aluminium plant in Ajka released approximately one million cubic metres of highly corrosive waste (pH ≈ 13) into residential areas. Ten people were killed and around 150 suffered chemical burns to skin and respiratory tracts; the village of Devecser was fully evacuated.
   *Source: BBC News, "Hungary toxic sludge", October 2010; European Environment Agency Emergency Report 2010*

2. **PFAS contamination near the Tennessee plant (2000s–2010s)** — High concentrations of per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) were found in drinking water of communities near Alcoa's aluminium plant in Rockdale, Tennessee. PFAS are linked to kidney cancer, immune disorders and endocrine disruption; residents filed a class action lawsuit against the company.
   *Source: Earthjustice, "PFAS Contamination near Alcoa Tennessee", 2019; EPA PFAS National Action Plan*

3. **Occupational diseases among aluminium smelter workers (ongoing)** — Workers in Alcoa's aluminium smelting facilities are exposed to polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) from coal anodes. Long-term studies have documented elevated rates of bladder and lung cancer among employees at the company's smelters in the USA and Australia.
   *Source: NIOSH (USA), "Occupational Cancer in Aluminum Smelters", 2015; Cancer Council Australia*

## 2. Has the corporation caused major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Bauxite mining and alumina production have generated large-scale contamination of soil, water and air across multiple countries.

1. **Red mud contamination of the Danube (2010)** — Following the dam failure at Ajka, a wave of alkaline sludge (pH 13) swept through the Marcal River and reached the Danube. In the first days, mass mortality of fish and aquatic invertebrates was recorded along tens of kilometres of the Marcal. The Danube suffered a significant, if brief, pollution episode.
   *Source: European Environment Agency, "Ajka alumina sludge accident", 2010; UNEP report 2011*

2. **Groundwater contamination from tailings storage in Western Australia (2000s–present)** — Alcoa's red mud tailings ponds at Pinjarra and Wagerup (Western Australia) cover hundreds of hectares and have periodically recorded seepage of alkaline leachate into groundwater, threatening the Peel-Harvey wetland ecosystems and coastal lagoons.
   *Source: Environment Protection Authority Western Australia, 2017; Australian Broadcasting Corporation, "Red mud leaks", 2018*

3. **Ecosystem destruction from bauxite mining in Brazil (2010s)** — Bauxite mining at Alcoa's Moisés Coelho mine in Pará state, Brazil, was accompanied by tropical forest clearance and the silting of local watercourses. Local communities reported contamination of water used for drinking and agriculture.
   *Source: Amazon Watch, "Bauxite Mining and Amazon Deforestation", 2016; IBAMA (Brazil)*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Alcoa actively positions itself as a leader in "green aluminium" while downplaying the toxic waste and high energy consumption inherent in its production processes.

1. **Promoting aluminium as "sustainable" while concealing red mud problems (2015–2024)** — Alcoa actively participates in campaigns presenting aluminium as a "lightweight and recyclable" material for the green economy, without highlighting that each tonne of aluminium generates approximately 1–1.5 tonnes of red mud — a toxic waste for which disposal remains unresolved.
   *Source: World Aluminium, "Life Cycle Assessment", 2019; Earthjustice "Alcoa's Red Mud Legacy"*

2. **"Green aluminium" certification while maintaining high-carbon smelting (2020–2023)** — Alcoa sells aluminium under the SUSTANA brand, claiming a reduced carbon footprint through the use of hydroelectric power. However, a significant share of its smelting continues to rely on coal or gas, and the environmental problems of tailings facilities remain unaddressed.
   *Source: Alcoa SUSTANA product page; Bloomberg Green, "Greenwashing in Aluminum", 2022*

3. **Claiming climate neutrality via offsets without reducing actual emissions (2021–2024)** — Alcoa has declared ambitions for "climate neutrality" by 2050, relying on carbon credits. However, Climate Action 100+ analysis found that the company's near-term targets are not aligned with the Paris Agreement — actual emissions from its own operations have barely declined.
   *Source: Climate Action 100+ Assessment, 2023; Carbon Disclosure Project (CDP) 2022*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Alcoa actively publishes ESG reports and uses certifications to attract "sustainable" investors.

1. **ESG reporting to GRI and SASB standards, emphasising "achievements" while downplaying risks (2019–2023)** — Alcoa publishes comprehensive annual sustainability reports covering water use, emissions and biodiversity metrics. NGO critics note that these reports systematically highlight improvements while omitting the chronic problems associated with red mud.
   *Source: Alcoa Sustainability Report 2023; Earthjustice commentary*

2. **Green bond programme to finance low-carbon technologies (2021)** — Alcoa issued green corporate bonds, declaring they would fund the ELYSIS electrolysis technology (developed jointly with Rio Tinto and Apple). The technology has not yet moved beyond pilot scale.
   *Source: Alcoa press release "Green Bond", 2021; ELYSIS project page*

3. **Participation in the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) and climate commitments (2022)** — Alcoa announced it had applied for verification of its climate targets through SBTi. However, the company formally appears on the SBTi list with "commitments" that analysts assess as insufficient in terms of the pace of emissions reduction.
   *Source: SBTi Corporate Database, 2023; Financial Times "Alcoa ESG claims scrutinized", 2022*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. Despite its environmental violations, Alcoa has received recognition in ESG ratings.

1. **Inclusion in the FTSE4Good index (2019–2022)** — Alcoa was periodically included in the FTSE4Good index, which selects companies based on ESG metrics. The company used this fact in investor communications as confirmation of "responsible business conduct".
   *Source: FTSE Russell ESG ratings database; Alcoa Investor Relations presentations*

2. **Sustainalytics "Low Risk" ESG award (2021)** — The analytics agency Sustainalytics assigned Alcoa a "medium" ESG risk rating, which the company used in materials for institutional investors. External NGO observers considered this rating understated given the unresolved risks of red mud.
   *Source: Sustainalytics ESG Risk Rating, 2021; Morningstar*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Alcoa labels some of its products as "sustainable" and "low-carbon" without disclosing the full range of environmental consequences of production.

1. **SUSTANA brand for "low-carbon" aluminium (2020–2024)** — Alcoa assigns some of its aluminium to the SUSTANA brand, claiming a reduced carbon footprint. However, the criteria for the designation are non-transparent, and the full lifecycle — including bauxite mining and toxic waste — is not reflected in the labelling.
   *Source: Alcoa SUSTANA brochure; Responsible Steel standard comparison*

2. **"Recycled" aluminium labelling without disclosing the energy intensity of primary production (2018–2023)** — Alcoa actively promotes the high recyclability of aluminium, placing corresponding marks on marketing materials. Yet primary production — the largest segment of the company's business — carries no label indicating its environmental impact.
   *Source: European Aluminium "Recycling factsheet"; Alcoa marketing materials*

3. **Use of eco-certificates for alumina plants without disclosing tailings problems (2021–2023)** — Alcoa obtained several industry certifications for its alumina plants in Australia. Meanwhile, the red mud tailings storage facilities co-located at those sites remain sources of environmental risk that the certificates do not communicate to buyers.
   *Source: Aluminium Stewardship Initiative (ASI), 2022; EPA Western Australia*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Alcoa conducts environmental events whose scope is incommensurate with the scale of industrial harm.

1. **Bauxite mine rehabilitation programme in Australia (2010s–present)** — Alcoa carries out forest restoration on exhausted bauxite quarries in the Darling Range (Western Australia). The programme is prominently featured in corporate reports; however, the pace of rehabilitation significantly lags behind the rate of new mining.
   *Source: Alcoa Sustainability Report 2022; WWF Australia commentary*

2. **Corporate tree-planting initiative in Brazil (2019)** — On Earth Day, employees of Alcoa's Brazilian subsidiary planted several thousand trees near the mine. The event received extensive coverage in corporate media, while the area of land being rehabilitated remained incomparable to the scale of extraction.
   *Source: Alcoa Brazil press release, April 2019*

3. **Sponsorship of local environmental programmes in Tennessee (2018–2020)** — Alcoa funded small water-quality monitoring programmes on rivers near its Tennessee plants. These initiatives did not prevent complaints from residents about PFAS contamination, which would have required substantially larger investments to remediate.
   *Source: Alcoa Community Relations Report 2019; Earthjustice*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Alcoa actively participates in industry lobbying, promoting narratives of "sustainable aluminium" while opposing stricter requirements for toxic waste management.

1. **Lobbying through the Aluminium Association against strict red mud storage standards (2010s)** — Following the 2010 Hungarian disaster, the Aluminium Association (of which Alcoa is a member) actively resisted the introduction of mandatory US federal standards for the storage of "non-mining" industrial solid waste — the category that encompasses red mud — insisting on "voluntary regulation".
   *Source: EPA "Hazardous Waste Listing for Bauxite Residue", Federal Register; Earthjustice regulatory filings*

2. **Promoting the narrative of aluminium as a "green metal" in government programmes (2019–2023)** — Alcoa and industry associations actively lobbied for aluminium to be classified as a "critical material for the green transition" in US and EU programmes. This positioning allows the company to access subsidies and incentives originally intended for genuinely clean industries.
   *Source: US Department of Energy "Critical Materials Strategy"; Corporate Europe Observatory*

3. **Funding educational programmes on the "aluminium economy" without mentioning toxic waste (2015–2022)** — Through corporate education foundations and industry organisations, Alcoa supported curricula presenting aluminium as "environmentally safe" and "fully recyclable" without mentioning red mud.
   *Source: Alcoa Foundation education grants; Global Aluminium website educational materials*
