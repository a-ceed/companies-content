Google and the Thirst of Data Centres

Google's data centres are cooled by evaporative systems that consume billions of gallons of fresh water each year. According to the company's own figures, its data centres consumed approximately 15.8 billion litres of water in 2021 — a year when major reservoirs across the western United States fell to historic lows.

In the Netherlands, Google's data centre in IJmuiden uses around 12 million cubic metres of drinking water annually — a quarter of Amsterdam's total yearly water consumption. In 2022, Dutch regulators froze the expansion of the data centre citing threats to the water supply.

Despite announcing "100% renewable energy", independent audits found that the company largely covers this figure through certificates (RECs) that do not represent actual consumption of green electricity. Google is one of the largest corporate purchasers of nuclear and gas-generated power.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Google's operations have harmed the health and well-being of people through brutal working conditions for content moderators, anti-competitive practices that destroyed small businesses, and involvement in surveillance and drone projects for military purposes.

1. **Psychological harm to content moderators (2019–2022)** — Thousands of content moderators working for Google/YouTube through contractors (Accenture, Cognizant) were required to review extreme material daily: executions, violence, child pornography. Mass lawsuits documented cases of severe PTSD, depression and suicidal ideation. In 2020, Cognizant paid $40 million in a settlement with moderators.
   *Source: The Verge, "The Trauma Floor", 2019; Cognizant Moderators Association lawsuit, 2020*

2. **Project Maven — AI development for military drones (2017–2018)** — Google secretly participated in a US Department of Defense project to develop AI systems for object recognition in military drone video. More than 4,000 employees signed a protest petition and around 12 resigned. Critics noted that the technology was directly applied in lethal weapons systems.
   *Source: New York Times, "Google Is Helping the Pentagon Build AI for Drones", 2018*

3. **Anti-competitive practices destroying businesses (2010–2023)** — A series of EU regulatory decisions (fines totalling €8.25 billion from 2017 to 2019) found that Google had abused its dominant position in search, advertising and mobile operating systems. Small publishers and competing services lost revenue and shut down, leaving thousands jobless.
   *Source: European Commission, rulings 2017, 2018, 2019*

## 2. Has the corporation caused major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Google's data centres consume water in arid regions, triggering water stress, while its tax arrangements deprive governments of funds for environmental programmes.

1. **Water crisis in the Netherlands (2022)** — Google's data centre in IJmuiden consumed 12 million cubic metres of drinking water from the municipal network in a single year. The Dutch water company PWN warned that planned expansion would threaten the water supply for residential areas of North Holland Province. Authorities imposed a moratorium on new data centre permits.
   *Source: NOS Nieuws, 2022; PWN official statement, 2022*

2. **Water consumption in drought-affected Chile (2021–2023)** — Google built a data centre in the Valparaíso region of Chile during a declared water emergency in 2021–2023. Chilean environmental organisations called for an investigation after the company was drawing water from state reservoirs at a time of acute shortage — while rural communities faced restrictions.
   *Source: El Mostrador, 2022; Chile Sustentable report, 2023*

3. **Carbon footprint from rising electricity demand (2019–2023)** — Internal Google reporting showed energy consumption growing by 20% annually as AI services were rolled out. Despite purchases of renewable energy certificates, the real demand for electricity caused local grids to burn more gas and coal. In Iowa, Google's data centres run on coal power.
   *Source: Google Environmental Report 2023; Bloomberg Green, 2023*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Google systematically misleads consumers and regulators by presenting the purchase of "green certificates" as a genuine transition to renewable energy.

1. **Manipulation of renewable energy certificates (RECs) (2017–2023)** — Google has repeatedly claimed "100% renewable energy" in press releases and annual reports. Independent researchers at the Rocky Mountain Institute established that the company actually buys Energy Attribute Certificates (EACs) — paper instruments that do not connect physical electricity consumption to specific green sources. During peak consumption hours at night, its servers are powered by coal and gas plants.
   *Source: Rocky Mountain Institute, "The Promise and Limits of Corporate Renewable Energy", 2021*

2. **Advertising "carbon-neutral" travel through Google Flights (2021)** — Google launched a "green flights" feature in Google Flights, labelling flights with lower CO₂ emissions. A Transport & Environment study found that the calculation methodology understated the real climate impact of aviation by a factor of 2–4, failing to account for contrails and non-linear atmospheric effects at altitude.
   *Source: Transport & Environment report, 2022*

3. **Claims of "water-positive" data centres by 2030 (2021)** — Google announced a goal to become "water-positive" without disclosing the methodology. Ecologists at the Pacific Institute noted that the promised "water restoration measures" primarily relate to other catchments and do not compensate for actual harm in the locations where data centres are sited.
   *Source: Pacific Institute, "Corporate Water Accounting", 2022*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Google actively uses ESG rhetoric to improve its reputation and attract institutional investors, while failing to meet the standards it declares.

1. **Google Sustainability Reports and ESG ratings (2019–2023)** — Alphabet publishes comprehensive annual sustainability reports including a climate commitments roadmap. On the basis of these reports, the rating agencies MSCI and Sustainalytics assign Google high ESG ratings, attracting investment from "sustainable" funds. Meanwhile, actual Scope 3 emissions (supply chain) are not fully disclosed.
   *Source: Alphabet Sustainability Report 2022; MSCI ESG Ratings Alphabet*

2. **Membership of the RE100 coalition (since 2016)** — Google became one of the founding members of the corporate climate coalition RE100, declaring a transition to 100% renewable energy. This membership is actively used in ESG communications, although the RE100 criteria permit the use of EAC certificates without requiring hourly matching of consumption and generation.
   *Source: RE100 Annual Report 2022; Carbon Brief analysis, 2021*

3. **Partnership with green bond funds (2020–2022)** — Google issued green bonds totalling $5.75 billion — the largest corporate issuance at the time. Proceeds were directed towards purchasing RECs and building efficiency. Critics at the Climate Bonds Initiative noted that a significant share of "green" projects funded operating costs rather than new green infrastructure.
   *Source: Google Green Bond Impact Report, 2021; Climate Bonds Initiative assessment, 2021*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. Google regularly tops global ESG rankings, which are largely formed on the basis of corporate self-reporting.

1. **Corporate Knights Global 100 Most Sustainable Corporations (2020, 2021)** — Alphabet was repeatedly included in the list of the 100 most sustainable corporations in the world by Corporate Knights. The inclusion was based on data from Alphabet's own reporting, without independent verification of its water or carbon footprint.
   *Source: Corporate Knights Global 100, 2021*

2. **Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI) (2019–2022)** — Alphabet was included in the DJSI World and DJSI North America indices, signalling "ESG leadership" to institutional investors. S&P Global's methodology for DJSI is criticised for excessive reliance on company self-questionnaires.
   *Source: S&P Global DJSI, reports 2019–2022*

3. **Nomination as "Best Employer in Sustainability" (2022)** — Google received recognition in Newsweek's "America's Most Responsible Companies" ranking in the environmental responsibility category, despite publicly documented problems with data centre water consumption and contractor working conditions.
   *Source: Newsweek Most Responsible Companies 2022*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Google placed environmental labels and claims on devices and services whose production and operation entail significant environmental harm.

1. **"Carbon neutral" labelling on Pixel devices (2020–2022)** — Google labelled Pixel smartphones as "carbon neutral" through the purchase of carbon offset credits. Research by the Rainforest Action Network and Verra showed that a significant share of Google's offset projects (forestry REDD+) does not deliver the declared CO₂ absorption.
   *Source: Guardian "Carbon offsets used by major airlines based on flawed system", 2023; analogous methodology used by Google*

2. **Environmental claims for Google Nest (2021–2023)** — Google Nest devices (thermostats, cameras, speakers) were promoted with emphasis on "energy efficiency" and reducing household carbon footprints. Yet the manufacture of these devices in China and Vietnam relies on supply chains with unverified labour and environmental standards.
   *Source: Greenpeace "Green Electronics Guide", 2022*

3. **Advertising Google Cloud as "the cleanest cloud" (2020–2023)** — Google Cloud actively marketed services to corporate clients under slogans about "the cleanest cloud" and zero emissions. Independent analysis by Wired and Bloomberg showed that actual emissions from Google Cloud server operations per unit of computation are no lower than those of competitors AWS and Azure.
   *Source: Wired, "The Dirty Secret of Your 'Clean' Google Cloud", 2022*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Google systematically holds volunteer environmental events whose scale is disproportionate to the real environmental harm caused by the company's operations.

1. **"Googler-to-Googler" tree-planting programme (2019–2022)** — Google organised corporate volunteer tree-planting days through Employee Resource Groups. Several thousand trees were planted at campuses worldwide. Yet a single Google data centre consumes enough water in a year to sustain tens of millions of trees.
   *Source: Google ESG Report 2022, "Community" section*

2. **"Google for Earth Day" campaign (annually since 2017)** — Every year on 22 April, Google publishes a themed doodle, launches online campaigns and collects "green pledges" from users. The events are purely digital and marketing in nature and have no direct impact on reducing environmental harm from Google's operations.
   *Source: Google Blog, Earth Day posts 2017–2023*

3. **Beach clean-ups by Google employees (2021, Singapore and Ireland)** — Google organised coastal clean-ups for employees in Singapore and Ireland, widely covered in corporate media. The volume of litter collected amounted to a few hundred kilograms — while a single query to Bard/Gemini consumes ten times more energy than a standard Google search query.
   *Source: Google Singapore office press releases, 2021*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Google actively promotes the narrative that digital technology and AI are the key tool for combating the climate crisis, while concealing their own environmental harm.

1. **"AI for Earth" campaign (2019–2022)** — Google, through the Google.org platform, funded projects positioning AI as "the solution to the climate crisis". Meanwhile, internal company data showed that training a single AI model emits as much CO₂ as five cars over their entire operational lifetime. Promoting "AI for the climate" masked the real growth of the carbon footprint from AI operations.
   *Source: MIT Technology Review, "Training a single AI model can emit as much carbon as five cars in their lifetimes", 2019*

2. **Lobbying against nuclear regulation on environmental grounds (2022–2023)** — Google actively supported authorisation for small modular reactors (SMRs) and promoted nuclear energy as "green", investing in Kairos Power. Critics from Beyond Nuclear and environmental organisations argued this lobbying primarily served corporate interests in securing cheap energy for data centres.
   *Source: E&E News, "Google bets on nuclear to power AI data centers", 2023*

3. **Funding climate sceptics through trade associations (2015–2020)** — An investigation by The Intercept and InfluenceMap found that Google was a member of and funded ALEC (American Legislative Exchange Council) — a trade association that actively blocked the adoption of climate legislation at state level. Google only withdrew from ALEC following a public scandal in 2014, but continued to fund related lobbying structures.
   *Source: The Intercept, "Google, Facebook funded climate denial lobbying", 2019; InfluenceMap report, 2020*
