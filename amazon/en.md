Amazon: Packaging Monster and Climate Promises

In 2021, Amazon used approximately 600 million pounds of plastic packaging — 18% more than the previous year, despite declared sustainability commitments. An Oceana investigation found that in 2020 the company produced the equivalent of 22 million plastic bottles in the form of bubble wrap and polyethylene bags in the United States alone.

Amazon's logistics expansion is accompanied by the construction of thousands of warehouses and sorting centres, often located near low-income and minority communities. Diesel trucks and Prime Air aircraft generate significant NO₂ emissions, degrading air quality in surrounding neighbourhoods.

In 2022, the US Securities and Exchange Commission launched an investigation into Amazon's claims of climate neutrality. The company declared "zero" emissions in certain operations, but independent analysts noted that the calculation excluded third-party (Scope 3) emissions, which account for more than 75% of the corporation's total carbon footprint.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Working conditions in Amazon warehouses have led to deaths and serious injuries; logistics emissions worsen the health of residents in surrounding areas.

1. **Deaths and injuries in warehouses (2017–2023)** — According to investigations by The Atlantic (2019) and the US Senate Health Committee, the rate of workplace injuries in Amazon warehouses is nearly double the industry average. In 2022, the Federal Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA) identified systematic violations — including prohibitions on taking breaks — responsible for musculoskeletal and cardiovascular damage to thousands of workers.
   *Source: The Atlantic, "I Was a Warehouse Wage Slave", 2019; OSHA Inspection Reports, 2022*

2. **Diesel trucks and childhood asthma (2021)** — A University of Southern California study showed that children living near Amazon logistics centres in Riverside and San Bernardino counties (California) have elevated rates of asthma and respiratory diseases due to NO₂ emissions from diesel trucks. More than 50 Amazon facilities are located in these counties.
   *Source: USC Dornsife, "Community Impact of Warehouse Logistics", 2021*

3. **Deaths of subcontractor drivers (2018–2022)** — ProPublica journalists documented at least 60 deaths of drivers working for Amazon Delivery Service Partner subcontractors between 2015 and 2021. Pressure to maintain delivery speeds — enforced via "Rabbit", a GPS tracker recording every stop — creates conditions in which drivers break traffic laws.
   *Source: ProPublica, "The Deadly Race to Deliver", 2022*

## 2. Has the corporation caused major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Amazon generates billions of units of plastic waste and carries out large-scale destruction of unsold goods.

1. **Plastic crisis in the oceans (2020–2021)** — According to an Oceana report (2021), Amazon produced at least 465 million pounds of plastic packaging in the USA alone in 2020. Some of this waste entered waterways: researchers identified Amazon-branded packaging on Atlantic coastlines and in Florida river estuaries.
   *Source: Oceana, "Exposed: Amazon's Plastic Pollution Problem", 2021*

2. **Destruction of millions of unsold goods (2021)** — ITV News journalists investigated an Amazon warehouse in Dunfermline, Scotland: hidden camera footage documented the destruction of more than 130,000 items per week — new laptops, phones, books and toys. Amazon described this as a "last resort", but environmental groups noted the systemic nature of the problem.
   *Source: ITV News, "Amazon destroying millions of unsold goods", June 2021*

3. **Prime Air fleet and carbon emissions (2019–2023)** — Amazon's air fleet numbers more than 100 cargo aircraft. According to Stand.earth calculations, a single Amazon flight emits 50 times more CO₂ per parcel than ground delivery. In 2022, total emissions from Amazon's air logistics exceeded 1.5 million tonnes of CO₂ equivalent.
   *Source: Stand.earth, "Amazon's Airline Problem", 2022*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Amazon has repeatedly made environmental responsibility claims that diverge from actual data on emissions and resource consumption.

1. **SEC investigation into climate claims (2022)** — The US Securities and Exchange Commission initiated an investigation into Amazon's claims of having achieved "climate neutrality" in certain operations. Independent analysts at Carbon Disclosure Project established that the company had excluded Scope 3 emissions from its calculations — accounting for over 75% of the real carbon footprint.
   *Source: Bloomberg, "SEC Investigates Amazon Climate Claims", 2022*

2. **"Climate Pledge" and rising emissions (2019–2021)** — In 2019, Amazon ceremonially signed the "Climate Pledge" and announced targets for net-zero emissions by 2040. However, between 2019 and 2021 its carbon footprint grew from 51.17 to 71.54 million tonnes of CO₂ equivalent — a 40% increase that directly contradicts those commitments.
   *Source: Amazon Sustainability Report 2021; Carbon Brief analysis*

3. **"Biodegradable" packaging that does not degrade (2021–2022)** — Amazon advertised new bags as "biodegradable", but tests by the Plastic Soup Foundation showed these bags only degrade in specialist industrial composters unavailable to most consumers. Under ordinary conditions they persist in the environment for centuries.
   *Source: Plastic Soup Foundation, "Amazon's Fake Eco-Packaging", 2022*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Amazon actively uses ESG narratives in investor communications and publishes extensive sustainability reports.

1. **ESG report and "1 billion trees" (2021)** — In its annual sustainability report, Amazon claimed participation in a programme to plant 1 billion trees. Ecologists from Forest Watch noted that a significant share of the plantings are monoculture plantations that destroy local biodiversity and do not replace primary forests.
   *Source: Forest Watch / Amazon Sustainability Report 2021*

2. **$2 billion Climate Pledge Fund (2020)** — Amazon announced the creation of a $2 billion Climate Pledge Fund to invest in "green" technologies. Critics at As You Sow noted that the fund represents less than 0.5% of the company's annual revenue and imposes no obligations to reduce the company's own emissions.
   *Source: As You Sow, Shareholder Resolution Analysis, 2021*

3. **"Sustainable Operations" for investors (2022–2023)** — In investor presentations, Amazon regularly uses ESG metrics — "green" warehouses, Rivian electric vans, solar panels. Yet of 100,000 planned electric vans, fewer than 3,000 were actually in use by 2022, while the diesel fleet numbered tens of thousands of vehicles.
   *Source: Reuters, "Amazon Electric Van Rollout", 2023*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. Amazon has received ESG recognition despite criticism from environmental organisations.

1. **FTSE4Good index (2020–2022)** — Amazon was repeatedly included in the FTSE4Good index, which targets companies with high ESG standards. The inclusion drew criticism from Amnesty International and Oxfam, who cited systematic labour rights violations and tax avoidance.
   *Source: FTSE Russell, FTSE4Good Index Series Reports, 2021–2022*

2. **CDP "Climate Leadership" (2021)** — Amazon received a "B" rating from the Carbon Disclosure Project in the "climate leadership" category, despite growing absolute emissions. Climate Action 100+ noted that CDP methodology allows high ratings when targets are declared, regardless of whether actual emissions are being reduced.
   *Source: CDP Climate Scores 2021; Climate Action 100+ analysis*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Amazon uses its own eco-labels that lack independent certification.

1. **"Climate Pledge Friendly" badge (2020–2023)** — Amazon introduced its own "Climate Pledge Friendly" label for products it certifies itself using non-transparent criteria. A Wall Street Journal investigation (2022) found the badge was awarded to products in multi-layer plastic packaging and goods with high production carbon footprints — provided they carried any of more than 30 certifications accepted by Amazon, some of which are issued by the company itself.
   *Source: Wall Street Journal, "Amazon's Green Label Program", 2022*

2. **Proprietary "Compact by Design" certificate (2019)** — Amazon awards products a "Compact by Design" label signifying reduced packaging. Some labelled products were sold in packaging significantly larger than the item itself, and the criteria for the label were not published.
   *Source: Greenpeace UK, "Amazon's Eco-labels Under Scrutiny", 2021*

3. **Labelling of "biodegradable" bags (2021)** — Bags bearing Amazon's "I'm biodegradable" claim were found by the UK's Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) in 2022 to be misleading: without industrial composting, they do not degrade under natural conditions.
   *Source: UK Advertising Standards Authority, Ruling on Amazon, 2022*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Amazon regularly organises public initiatives that are insignificant relative to the actual harm caused by its operations.

1. **"Clean-up days" at warehouses on Prime Day (2022–2023)** — Ahead of Prime Day sales, Amazon organised "environmental" activities for warehouse employees: waste sorting and flower planting on site. At the same time, the volume of Prime Day 2022 orders exceeded 300 million items, generating a corresponding increase in plastic packaging and carbon emissions.
   *Source: Amazon PR releases, 2022–2023; Oceana analysis*

2. **"Trees for employees" (2021)** — As part of the "Employees for Sustainability" programme, Amazon planted 100,000 trees on corporate grounds — against an annual carbon footprint of over 71 million tonnes of CO₂. According to ecologists' calculations, compensating Amazon's annual emissions would require more than 70 billion trees.
   *Source: Amazon Sustainability Report 2021; carbon offsetting calculators*

3. **Eco-display at COP26 (2021)** — At the COP26 climate summit in Glasgow, Amazon organised an exhibition stand demonstrating "sustainable" logistics solutions. During the same period, the company was lobbying the US Congress for lower emissions requirements for freight air transport.
   *Source: The Guardian, "Amazon at COP26", November 2021*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Amazon funded lobbying against climate regulation and promoted the narrative of "responsible consumption" as a substitute for systemic change.

1. **Funding the Climate Pledge Alliance while lobbying against regulation (2019–2022)** — Amazon leads the Climate Pledge Alliance, yet its Washington lobbyists actively worked against legislative initiatives on carbon pricing (carbon tax) and mandatory Scope 3 emissions reporting. In 2021–2022, Amazon spent over $18 million on lobbying.
   *Source: OpenSecrets.org, Amazon Lobbying Disclosures, 2022*

2. **Promoting the idea of "conscious consumption" (2020–2023)** — In environmental campaigns, Amazon focuses on consumer choice ("choose products with a green label") rather than reducing production or limiting packaging. This rhetoric mirrors BP's classic "personal carbon footprint" narrative, which shifts responsibility onto consumers.
   *Source: Influence Map, "Big Tech and Climate Policy", 2022*

3. **Corporate eco-education without action (2022–2023)** — Amazon launched an educational portal "Amazon Sustainability Hub" with tips for shoppers on sustainable consumption. In parallel, the company was engaged in litigation against several US states attempting to introduce mandatory requirements for the use of recycled materials in packaging.
   *Source: E&E News, "Amazon and Packaging Legislation", 2023*
