Anglo American: Mine Drainage and South Africa's Coal Legacy

Anglo American — one of the world's largest diversified mining conglomerates — owns coal mines in Mpumalanga (South Africa), where closed coal shafts have created a massive problem of acid mine drainage.

The groundwater of Mpumalanga is saturated with sulphates and heavy metals from pyrite oxidation in mine workings. According to the Council for Geoscience of South Africa, by the 2030s acidic waters may begin seeping to the surface and into rivers of the Thukela basin.

In Colombia, the Cerrejón coal mine — the largest open-cast coal mine in the world — has caused large-scale environmental damage: over 40 years of operation, approximately 15,000 people from indigenous Wayúu communities were displaced. The Ranchería River, altered by mining operations, has been depleted of fish. Constantly raised coal dust settles in the lungs of people and livestock.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Anglo American has historically been one of the largest mining employers in South Africa, and its mines have recorded thousands of deaths and occupational diseases over decades of operation.

1. **Deaths of miners in South African mines (1970s–2010s)** — Anglo American operated the largest coal, gold and platinum mines in South Africa. According to South Africa's Department of Mineral Resources, tens of miners died annually at the company's mines and subsidiaries during the 2000s alone. Working conditions in gold mines of the Witwatersrand — tunnels reaching depths of 4 km — created extreme risks of collapses and rockbursts.
   *Source: Department of Mineral Resources South Africa, annual injury statistics; Mining Weekly, "Anglo American fatalities", 2010*

2. **Silicosis and tuberculosis epidemic among miners (1990s–2010s)** — Thousands of former Anglo American mine workers contracted silicosis — an incurable occupational lung disease — through inhalation of silica dust. In 2016, Anglo American and several other South African mining companies paid approximately 400 million rand (around $30 million) in a class action settlement brought by miners and their families.
   *Source: South African Human Rights Commission, 2015; Reuters, "Anglo American silicosis settlement", 2016*

3. **Coal dust and respiratory disease in communities near Cerrejón in Colombia (2000s–2010s)** — Indigenous Wayúu communities living near the Cerrejón open-cast mine recorded elevated rates of respiratory illness among children and adults. Amnesty International documented cases where coal dust settled on homes and food crops.
   *Source: Amnesty International, "The Cerrejón Mine and the Wayúu People", 2012; Human Rights Watch Colombia*

## 2. Has the corporation caused major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Anglo American's activities have left large-scale environmental consequences in South Africa, Colombia and Chile.

1. **Acid mine drainage in Gauteng, South Africa (2002–present)** — Closed coal and gold mines of the Anglo American group in the Western Witwatersrand basin (Gauteng) generate a massive flow of acid mine drainage (AMD). In 2002, acidic waters began emerging at the surface in Krugersdorp. According to the Council for Geoscience of South Africa, the situation is characterised as a "declassified environmental disaster" threatening the Vaal and Thukela rivers.
   *Source: Council for Geoscience South Africa, "AMD Western Basin Report", 2010; GroundWork, 2016*

2. **Diversion of the Ranchería River and destruction of biodiversity in Colombia (1983–present)** — In the course of expanding the Cerrejón open-cast mine, the course of the Ranchería River — the only permanent water source for thousands of people and livestock — was redirected and partially destroyed. Biologists recorded a sharp decline in fish stocks. Plans for further mine expansion in 2011 triggered an international protest campaign.
   *Source: BankWatch, "Cerrejón expansion", 2012; CEDHA Colombia*

3. **Watercourse contamination from copper mining in Chile (2010s–2020s)** — Anglo American's Los Bronces copper mine in Chile is located in an arid Andean region where water resources are extremely scarce. Mining operations consume significant volumes of water from the Maipo River basin, which supplies Santiago. In 2019, the Chilean government launched an investigation into the mine's impact on the capital region's water supply.
   *Source: Chilean Water Authority (DGA), 2019; El Mercurio, "Los Bronces water conflict", 2020*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Anglo American positions itself as a "responsible" miner of the future, using innovation and sustainability rhetoric while continuing harmful activities.

1. **"FutureSmart Mining" concept without remediation of past harm (2018–2023)** — Anglo American actively promotes its FutureSmart Mining™ concept, presenting the mining of the future as "environmentally neutral". Meanwhile, the company has not set binding deadlines for remediation of acid mine drainage harm in South Africa.
   *Source: Anglo American Annual Report 2022; GroundWork "FutureSmart or Future Harm?", 2021*

2. **Claims of "responsible" copper mining while water conflicts in Chile continue (2020–2024)** — Anglo American promotes copper mining as "critically necessary for the green transition", while the water conflict around Los Bronces remains unresolved and mine expansion threatens Santiago's water supply.
   *Source: Earthjustice; Bloomberg, "Copper mining and water wars in Chile", 2023*

3. **Exiting coal mining as a "green" step — in practice a sale of assets without remediation (2021–2023)** — Anglo American announced the sale of its coal assets in South Africa and Colombia. Critics note that selling "dirty" assets does not eliminate the environmental damage — it is simply transferred to new owners, while acid mine drainage continues to contaminate watercourses regardless of who holds the title.
   *Source: Reuters "Anglo American coal divestment", 2021; GroundWork commentary*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Anglo American publishes detailed ESG reports and actively uses "responsible mining" rhetoric to attract institutional investors.

1. **Reporting against UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) and integrated reporting (2015–2023)** — Anglo American was one of the first mining companies to adopt integrated reporting under the IIRC standard. The company systematically links its activities to the UN SDGs, including SDG 6 (clean water) — despite the water conflict in Chile.
   *Source: Anglo American Integrated Annual Report 2023; IIRC*

2. **Inclusion in MSCI ESG and FTSE4Good indices (2018–2023)** — Anglo American is present in leading ESG indices and actively mentions this in investor communications. The ratings are largely formed on the basis of data provided by the company itself.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings; FTSE4Good Index, 2023*

3. **Zimele programme — corporate community development as an ESG argument (2010s–2020s)** — The Zimele small business support programme in South Africa is widely used in the company's ESG communications. However, the scale of investment in the programme is incomparable with the financial harm caused to communities near the mines.
   *Source: Anglo American Zimele Report; ActionAid South Africa*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. Anglo American has received industry ESG awards despite ongoing environmental and social violations.

1. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI) World (2017–2020)** — Anglo American was included in the DJSI World, which the company used as confirmation of "sustainable" business conduct in the mining industry.
   *Source: S&P Global DJSI, 2020; Anglo American Investor Relations*

2. **Responsible Business Alliance award for "supply chain transparency" (2019)** — The company received industry recognition for De Beers' diamond traceability system (Tracr), positioned as "responsible mining".
   *Source: De Beers Tracr press release; World Diamond Council*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Anglo American uses environmental certifications and labelling for some products while concealing the full picture of their impact.

1. **Tracr system for "ethical" De Beers diamonds (2018–2024)** — De Beers (controlled by Anglo American) introduced the Tracr blockchain system for tracing diamond provenance and labelling them as "conflict-free" and "responsible". Critics note that the system does not reflect the environmental damage from diamond quarries.
   *Source: De Beers Tracr platform; Global Witness "Diamond greenwashing", 2021*

2. **Marketing copper as "environmentally necessary" for the green energy transition (2020–2024)** — Anglo American promotes its copper under a narrative of "inevitability" of extraction for solar panels and electric vehicles, effectively using this as a marketing "green" label. The environmental consequences of mining in Chile are not reflected in the labelling.
   *Source: Anglo American "Copper for the Green Economy" campaign materials*

3. **Responsible Jewellery Council certificates for platinum products (2015–2022)** — Platinum from Anglo American Platinum's South African mines is certified under the Responsible Jewellery Council standard. However, the standard has been criticised for paying insufficient attention to the environmental damage from platinum mining.
   *Source: Responsible Jewellery Council standards; Mining Watch Canada critique*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Anglo American runs environmental programmes whose scale is incomparable with the consequences of its mining activities.

1. **Water conservation programme in Chile (2019–2022)** — Anglo American announced a water recirculation project at Los Bronces aimed at reducing water abstraction from the Maipo River. However, the volume of water recycled represents only a fraction of total consumption, and the conflict with water users in the valley remains unresolved.
   *Source: Anglo American sustainability update 2022*

2. **Tree planting under a "green belt" programme near South African mines (2018–2021)** — The company funded the planting of tens of thousands of trees along the perimeter of its Mpumalanga mines. The area of greening is incomparable with the area of degraded land around the coal workings.
   *Source: Anglo American Social Investment Report 2020*

3. **Corporate Environmental Day with volunteers (2019–2023)** — Anglo American organises annual volunteer eco-days for employees, including river bank clean-ups and planting. The events receive wide coverage in corporate communications while remaining symbolic against the backdrop of the company's operational scale.
   *Source: Anglo American Communities & Sustainability Report 2022*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Anglo American actively participates in industry lobbying, promoting narratives of a "necessary" mining industry for the "green transition".

1. **Lobbying through ICMM (International Council on Mining and Metals) against strict reclamation requirements (2010s–2020s)** — ICMM, of which Anglo American is a member, has systematically lobbied against the introduction of mandatory federal funding for mine closure and against strengthening reclamation standards in South Africa and Colombia.
   *Source: ICMM lobbying disclosures; ActionAid "Mining Lobby Power", 2019*

2. **Promoting the concept of "critical metals for the green transition" to avoid environmental restrictions (2018–2023)** — Anglo American participates in coalitions lobbying for accelerated licensing of mining projects on the grounds of the "urgent necessity" of copper and platinum for electrification. In several countries, this has enabled permits to be obtained while bypassing full environmental assessments.
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory "Critical Minerals Lobbying", 2022*

3. **Funding educational programmes on the "future of mining" (FutureSmart Mining) at South African universities (2018–2022)** — Anglo American funded university programmes and scholarships linked to the FutureSmart Mining concept, cultivating a positive image of the mining industry among the next generation of specialists without critical examination of the environmental consequences of existing operations.
   *Source: Anglo American Foundation university grants; Wits University partnership*
