APP Sinar Mas: A Forest Catastrophe of Global Scale

Asia Pulp & Paper — one of the world's largest paper companies — was identified by Greenpeace throughout the 2000s as the primary destroyer of Indonesia's tropical forests. Clearance rates reached 1,000 hectares per day; through the destruction of peatlands, Indonesia entered the top three global CO₂ emitters in the world.

In 2013, under pressure from major retail clients (Walmart, Mattel, Unilever), APP adopted a "No Deforestation Policy", pledging to cease clearance of natural forests. Initial results were positive, but subsequent investigations (2019–2022) revealed logging in the buffer zones of protected areas.

In 2019, forest fires in Indonesia — many of which were deliberately lit to clear plantation land — burned more than 8 million hectares. According to BNPB, several fire outbreaks were located on concession land linked to Sinar Mas.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Forest fires associated with Sinar Mas / APP concessions created deadly air pollution across Sumatra and Kalimantan, while the destruction of peatlands deprived local and indigenous communities of their livelihoods.

1. **Smog from peatland fires in 2015 — a regional-scale environmental disaster** — Forest fires on the peatlands of Sumatra and Kalimantan in 2015, some of which occurred on Sinar Mas concession land, caused smog that engulfed Indonesia, Singapore and Malaysia. According to Harvard and Columbia University researchers, the 2015 smoke event led to the premature deaths of approximately 100,000 people across Southeast Asia from respiratory diseases.
   *Source: Marlier et al., "Climatic and fire influences on haze in Equatorial Asia", Environ. Res. Lett., 2015; World Bank "The Cost of Fire", 2016*

2. **Violations of rights and livelihoods of indigenous peoples of Sumatra (2000s–2010s)** — The expansion of APP / Sinar Mas plantations destroyed forests on which the livelihoods of the Orang Rimba and other communities of Riau Province depended. Greenpeace and RAN documented cases of forced land seizure without consent or compensation.
   *Source: Greenpeace "Pulping the Planet", 2010; Rainforest Action Network, "APP and Indigenous Rights", 2012*

3. **Air and water pollution near pulp and paper mills (2000s–present)** — Effluents from APP pulp mills in Riau Province contain organochlorine compounds and dioxins. Local communities using rivers for drinking water reported poisonings and skin diseases. Indonesia's BAPEDAL agency recorded violations of discharge standards.
   *Source: WALHI (Friends of the Earth Indonesia), 2009; Human Rights Watch "Indonesia: Pulp and Paper", 2013*

## 2. Has the corporation caused major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. APP / Sinar Mas is one of the largest historical destroyers of tropical forests and peatlands in the world.

1. **Destruction of Sumatran tropical forests — loss of up to 2 million hectares (1990s–2013)** — According to Greenpeace and WWF, at the peak of its clearing operations APP was destroying up to 1,000 hectares of tropical forest per day. In total, the company bears responsibility for the destruction of several million hectares of forests on Sumatra, including habitats of the Sumatran tiger, orangutan and Sumatran elephant.
   *Source: Greenpeace, "APP Deforestation", 2012; Mongabay forest cover analysis*

2. **Draining and burning of peatlands — massive CO₂ emissions (1990s–2019)** — Sumatra and Kalimantan's peatlands had accumulated carbon over millennia. The drainage of peatlands for APP plantations led to their slow combustion and colossal CO₂ emissions: burning peat is a source of carbon that cannot be absorbed even through reforestation over centuries.
   *Source: Miettinen et al., "Peatland Degradation in Southeast Asia", Global Change Biology, 2011; CIFOR*

3. **2019 forest fires on Sinar Mas concessions** — In 2019, the Indonesian government opened cases and imposed administrative sanctions on several Sinar Mas group companies in connection with fires on their concession land. BNPB (National Disaster Management Agency) recorded fire outbreaks directly within group concessions.
   *Source: BNPB Indonesia, fire registry 2019; Mongabay "Sinar Mas fire concessions", October 2019*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. APP adopted its "No Deforestation Policy" in 2013 under pressure from clients and NGOs, but subsequent investigations documented continued violations.

1. **"No Deforestation Policy" of 2013 as a PR tool while violations continued (2013–2022)** — Following a boycott by Mattel, Unilever, Walmart and others, APP adopted a zero-deforestation declaration. However, investigations by the Rainforest Action Network and Eyes on the Forest in 2019–2021 revealed continued deforestation in buffer zones of protected areas and among third-party suppliers.
   *Source: RAN, "APP Zero Deforestation Assessment", 2021; Eyes on the Forest, 2020*

2. **Attempts to obtain FSC certification while violating its principles (2005–2012)** — APP repeatedly attempted to certify its plantations under the FSC (Forest Stewardship Council) standard despite documented clearance of natural forests. In 2007, FSC expelled the company from its system, characterising it as a "corruption" of certification principles.
   *Source: FSC "Disassociation from APP", 2007; WWF Forest Network*

3. **Positioning pulp mills as "sustainable" operations (2015–2022)** — Following adoption of the zero-deforestation policy, APP began actively labelling its products as "sustainably sourced" and publishing "green" reports. Independent auditors (The Forest Trust, later Earthqualizer) documented a gap between declarations and the actual situation in supply chains from third-party suppliers.
   *Source: The Forest Trust "APP monitoring reports"; Earthqualizer assessment 2022*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. APP / Sinar Mas actively uses ESG rhetoric to rebuild its reputation after years of NGO criticism.

1. **ESG reporting emphasising "monitoring" and "progress" (2016–2023)** — APP publishes annual sustainability progress reports, systematically emphasising positive indicators (no new clearance on own land) while avoiding disclosure of information about third-party suppliers.
   *Source: APP Sustainability Report 2022; RAN assessment commentary*

2. **Public commitments on peatland protection and participation in UN initiatives (2015–2022)** — APP signed a peatlands declaration under the High Carbon Stock Approach (HCSA) platform and engages with the UN-REDD+ programme. Critics point to formal rather than real compliance with these commitments.
   *Source: High Carbon Stock Approach Steering Group; UN-REDD Programme*

3. **Marketing of "sustainable packaging" for FMCG brands (2018–2023)** — APP supplies paper packaging to major consumer brands, positioning it as "environmentally responsible" and "from sustainable sources". This claim is used by clients in their own ESG communications, despite unresolved problems with suppliers.
   *Source: APP "Sustainable Packaging" client communications; Chain Reaction Research*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. Despite reputational damage, APP received some industry recognition for its declared "progress".

1. **CDP recognition in the "forest management progress" category (2018)** — In 2018, CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project) included APP in the category of companies "demonstrating progress" in managing forest risks. This occurred after several years of positive reporting on the zero-deforestation policy, before new violations were identified.
   *Source: CDP Forests score 2018; APP Investor Relations*

2. **Regional award for "responsible forestry" from Indonesia's Ministry of Forestry (2017)** — Indonesia's Ministry of Forestry (KLHK) presented awards to several APP enterprises within the national sustainable forestry programme, which the company used in its PR materials.
   *Source: KLHK Indonesia Awards 2017; APP press release*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. APP labels paper products as "sustainable" despite unresolved environmental problems in its supply chains.

1. **"PEFC/CERFLOR" labelling on products while continuing to source from problematic suppliers (2015–2022)** — Some APP products received PEFC certification through Brazil's CERFLOR system. Critics note that the certification covers only the company's own plantations, while a significant share of raw materials comes from third-party suppliers with questionable practices.
   *Source: PEFC International; Rainforest Action Network critique of PEFC*

2. **"FSC mix" labelling on paper packaging without full supply chain disclosure (2016–2022)** — APP used "FSC Mix" labelling on some products, indicating the presence of a proportion of FSC-certified raw material. NGOs criticised this practice, noting that the remaining non-certified proportion may originate from destroyed ecosystems.
   *Source: FSC standards on "Mix" claims; Earthqualizer 2022*

3. **Positioning as "Carbon Neutral Paper" for corporate clients (2020–2023)** — APP offered clients an option of "carbon neutral" paper through the purchase of carbon credits. Independent research showed that the carbon sequestration projects used for these credits included peatland initiatives whose verifiability was questionable.
   *Source: APP "Carbon Neutral" product page; Rainforest Action Network 2022*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. APP runs environmental programmes whose scale is incomparable with the damage it has caused.

1. **Restorasi Ekosistem Riau (RER) ecosystem restoration programme on 150,000 hectares (2013–present)** — APP has set aside 150,000 hectares of degraded peatlands for an ecological restoration programme. This represents only a small fraction of the millions of hectares destroyed over previous decades. Independent observers note that peatland restoration takes centuries, not years.
   *Source: APP RER Annual Progress Report 2022; Wetlands International*

2. **Corporate "Earth Day" tree-planting programmes (2016–2022)** — APP organises annual tree-planting events in Indonesia involving employees and local schoolchildren. The events receive wide coverage in corporate media, but their scale is incomparable with the historic scale of forest loss.
   *Source: APP Corporate Communications, "Earth Day 2022"*

3. **Fire safety training programme for suppliers (2016–2020)** — Following the 2015 fire scandal, APP launched a fire prevention training programme for small raw material suppliers. The programme reached a few hundred participants from a supplier base of thousands, many of whom continued burning practices.
   *Source: APP "Fire-Free Village Programme"; Eyes on the Forest 2020*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. APP / Sinar Mas has influenced discourse around sustainable forestry, promoting narratives that benefit the pulp and paper industry.

1. **Lobbying for weaker national certifications to replace FSC standards in Indonesia (2010s)** — APP and other Indonesian pulp and paper industry operators lobbied for the national SVLK system as an alternative to the more demanding FSC, promoting it as an "equivalent" environmental standard. NGOs pointed out fundamental differences in stringency.
   *Source: WWF "Indonesia's SVLK vs FSC"; Chain Reaction Research*

2. **Promoting the narrative of "plantation forest" as equivalent to natural forest (2013–2022)** — In corporate communications and industry publications, APP systematically presented industrial acacia plantations as "forest", creating a false impression of equivalence with biologically diverse tropical forests.
   *Source: APP "Sustainability" website; WWF critique of "plantation = forest" framing*

3. **Funding industry research programmes on the "role of industrial plantations in climate preservation" (2015–2020)** — APP sponsored a number of publications and seminars emphasising the "climate benefit" of fast-growing acacia plantations for carbon sequestration. Independent climate scientists noted that plantations absorb incomparably less carbon than the peat tropical forests they replace.
   *Source: CIFOR critique of APP-sponsored studies; Global Forest Watch*
