Mining toxic minerals for electronics creates toxic waste in developing countries.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Working conditions at Foxconn supplier factories, child labour in DRC cobalt mines, and a wave of suicides were a direct consequence of Apple's supply chain.

1. **Wave of suicides at Foxconn factories (2010)** — In 2010, 14 workers took their own lives at Foxconn factories in Shenzhen (the main iPhone assembler); more than 20 others attempted suicide. Reporters from the NYT and investigators found that the causes were 12-hour shifts, a ban on talking during work, overcrowded dormitories, and psychological pressure.
   *Source: New York Times, "In China, Human Costs Are Built Into an iPad", January 2012*

2. **Child labour in DRC cobalt mines (2016)** — Amnesty International documented the use of child labour (children from age 7) in cobalt mines in Katanga province, from which Apple's suppliers sourced cobalt for iPhone and MacBook batteries. Mining in hand-dug shafts without protective equipment caused respiratory diseases and injuries.
   *Source: Amnesty International, "This Is What We Die For", January 2016*

3. **Occupational diseases among supplier workers in China (2011)** — Greenpeace China recorded illnesses among workers at Apple supplier factories in Guangdong caused by exposure to n-hexane — a solvent used for polishing screens. N-hexane damages the peripheral nervous system; more than 100 workers at the Wintek factory suffered irreversible neurological damage.
   *Source: Greenpeace China, "Where Does E-Waste Go", 2011; Bloomberg, "Apple's Supplier Responsibility", 2011*

## 2. Has the corporation been responsible for major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. The extraction of mineral raw materials for Apple devices and the planned obsolescence of products cause large-scale environmental destruction in several countries.

1. **River contamination from cobalt mining in the DRC (2016–2022)** — Mines in Katanga province supplying cobalt to Apple discharge acid mine drainage into the Lualaba River and its tributaries. A 2019 study by USGS and the "DRC Project" recorded concentrations of cobalt, copper, and sulphuric acid in drinking water sources of villages near the mines exceeding permissible levels.
   *Source: USGS, "Environmental Impacts of Artisanal Cobalt Mining", 2019*

2. **Toxic waste from supplier factories in China (2011)** — Greenpeace China collected water and sediment samples near Apple supplier factories in Guangdong and Jiangsu provinces: concentrations of heavy metals — nickel, cadmium, chromium — exceeded WHO norms by tens of times. The contamination affected water bodies serving as drinking water sources for more than 2 million residents.
   *Source: Greenpeace China, "Poisoning the Pearl", 2011*

3. **iPhone e-waste at Accra landfills (Ghana) (2014–2021)** — The Basel Action Network (BAN) tracked old iPhones submitted to Apple's "official" recycling programmes in the USA using GPS trackers — they ended up at the Agbogbloshie dump in Accra (Ghana). Open burning of circuit boards and cables releases dioxins and furans; blood tests of local recyclers showed lead levels 5–10 times above norms.
   *Source: Basel Action Network, "Holes in the Circular Economy", 2016*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Apple actively positions itself as an environmental leader, yet specific actions systematically diverge from its declarations.

1. **"Carbon neutrality" alongside growing sales (2020–2023)** — Apple announced "carbon neutrality" across all corporate operations (Scope 1+2) from 2020, yet Scope 3 emissions (supply chain + device use) still account for over 97% of the carbon footprint and continue to grow. Emission restraint is achieved primarily through the purchase of offset credits.
   *Source: Apple Environmental Progress Report 2022; Carbon Trust analysis*

2. **Opposition to the right to repair (2018–2023)** — Apple actively lobbied against "Right to Repair" legislation in the USA, EU, and Australia, obstructing the ability of owners to repair their devices themselves. According to iFixit calculations, the forced shortening of the iPhone lifecycle creates millions of tonnes of additional e-waste annually, contradicting the company's environmental claims.
   *Source: iFixit, "Apple and Right to Repair", 2020; EFF, "Apple's War on Repair", 2021*

3. **Removing the charger from the box as an "environmental" measure (2020)** — Apple removed the charger from the iPhone 12 package, citing "environmental" motives. Critics and regulators (Brazil, Italy) pointed out that this step forces consumers to buy new chargers, while the packaging savings are negligible compared to the compulsion to make additional purchases. A Brazilian court ordered Apple to include the charger.
   *Source: Reuters, "Brazil Court Orders Apple to Include Charger", 2022; Which?, "Is Apple's No-Charger Move Really Eco?", 2020*

## 4. Does the company participate in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Apple regularly publishes extensive environmental reports and actively uses ESG narratives in investor relations.

1. **"Apple 2030" programme (2020)** — Apple announced a plan to achieve full carbon neutrality across its entire value chain by 2030. ESG analysts at Goldman Sachs and MSCI assigned Apple high ESG ratings, while CorpWatch noted that the plan includes no quantitative interim targets for reducing absolute Scope 3 emissions.
   *Source: Apple Environmental Progress Report 2020; CorpWatch, "Apple 2030: Green or Greenwash?", 2020*

2. **"100% recycled aluminium" in MacBook (2019)** — Apple made a marketing centrepiece of the recycled aluminium used in the MacBook Air (2018) chassis. Yet the total carbon footprint of MacBook Air production fell by only 11% compared to the previous model, while the advertising created the impression of a radical breakthrough.
   *Source: Apple Environmental Report, MacBook Air 2018; Greenpeace, "Guide to Greener Electronics", 2019*

3. **"Green Bonds" (2016–2022)** — Apple issued $4.7 billion in green bonds — the largest issuance among technology companies. The funds were directed towards renewable energy for Apple's own offices and data centres, rather than towards changing production processes at suppliers, who are responsible for the dominant share of emissions.
   *Source: Apple Green Bond Impact Report 2022*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG competition nominations?

**Answer: YES**. Apple regularly receives high ESG ratings despite human rights issues in its supply chain.

1. **MSCI ESG Rating "AAA" (2020–2023)** — MSCI assigned Apple the highest "AAA" ESG rating for several consecutive years, placing it among exemplary companies. Know The Chain criticised MSCI's methodology for underestimating the risks of forced labour in supply chains.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings, Apple Inc., 2022; Know The Chain Benchmark Report, 2021*

2. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI) (2018–2022)** — Apple was included in the DJSI World index as a "sustainable" technology sector company, despite systematic violations of labour conditions at supplier factories documented by the Fair Labor Association.
   *Source: S&P Global, DJSI 2022; Fair Labor Association Audit Reports, 2021*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Apple uses its own environmental declarations on devices and in marketing that do not reflect the real product lifecycle.

1. **"Made from recycled materials" on AirPods (2021)** — Apple stated that the AirPods casing is partly made from "recycled materials" — a magnet from recycled rare-earth metals. AirPods remain practically impossible to disassemble and repair: iFixit gave them a repairability rating of 0 out of 10, effectively guaranteeing their disposal within 2–3 years.
   *Source: iFixit, AirPods Pro Teardown, 2021; Apple AirPods Environmental Report, 2021*

2. **"Energy efficiency" claims for M1 Macs (2020–2021)** — Apple actively advertised the reduced energy consumption of M1-chip Macs as an environmental achievement. At the same time, the company did not disclose data on the full carbon footprint of producing the new chip and did not provide the ability to expand RAM or replace storage — making the devices impractical to repair.
   *Source: Carbon Brief, "Apple M1 Chip and the Environment", 2021*

3. **Eco-labelling on the iPhone 14 as "emissions-neutral" (2023)** — Apple presented the Apple Watch Ultra 2 and a number of accessories as "carbon neutral" products, relying on offset credits. Carbon Market Watch noted that the credits used by Apple belong to REDD+ schemes (forest protection), whose effectiveness is actively disputed by the scientific community.
   *Source: Carbon Market Watch, "Apple's Carbon Neutral Claims", 2023*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Apple holds public environmental initiatives that are incomparable in scale to the waste it produces.

1. **"Apple Trade In" — device return programme (2018–2023)** — Apple promotes the old-device exchange programme as a contribution to reducing e-waste. According to BAN data, a significant share of devices accepted through Trade In are resold in the secondary market in South-East Asia or sent to incomplete-cycle recycling centres; the actual share of devices processed by the Daisy robot amounts to less than 1% of all iPhones sold.
   *Source: Basel Action Network, E-Waste Reports, 2019; Apple Environmental Progress Report 2022*

2. **Earth Day tree-planting initiative (2021–2023)** — Apple ties corporate initiatives to Earth Day annually: tree planting, App Store developer competitions on eco themes, green app icons. Meanwhile, annual sales of more than 240 million iPhones generate a carbon footprint that would require billions of trees to offset.
   *Source: Apple Newsroom, Earth Day initiatives, 2021–2023*

3. **"Apple + Conservation International" competition (2016–2021)** — Apple, together with Conservation International, funded forest conservation in Kenya, Colombia, and China, offsetting emissions through carbon credits. Independent audits showed that the area of protected forest amounted to less than 0.1% of the scale of destruction associated with mineral extraction in Apple's supply chain.
   *Source: Conservation International / Apple Partnership Reports; Carbon Plan analysis, 2021*

## 8. Has the company lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories or greenwashing outreach?

**Answer: YES**. Apple actively lobbied against legislation that would genuinely reduce environmental harm, and promoted narratives about the "responsible consumer".

1. **Lobbying against right-to-repair laws (2018–2023)** — Apple spent millions of dollars opposing Right to Repair bills in the USA (California, New York, Minnesota) and the EU. The company argued that self-repair was "dangerous" and harmful to the environment due to the risk of improper disposal. The New York law was signed in 2023, but with significant exceptions lobbied by Apple.
   *Source: EFF, "Apple's Lobbying Against Right to Repair", 2021; NY State Legislature Records, 2023*

2. **The narrative that "recycling will solve e-waste" (2019–2022)** — In marketing materials Apple presents the Daisy robot and the Trade In programme as the solution to the e-waste problem, diverting attention from the need to extend device lifespans. Researchers at MIT Media Lab noted that shifting to a "repair and durability" model would reduce the carbon footprint of the iPhone 40–50% more effectively than recycling.
   *Source: MIT Media Lab, "Repairability vs. Recyclability", 2022*

3. **Funding "green" educational programmes while opposing systemic regulation (2020–2023)** — Apple funds educational projects "Everyone Can Code" and environmental courses through the Apple Developer Academy — while lobbying against mandatory environmental standards for electronics manufacturers in the EU (ecodesign regulations).
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory, "Big Tech and EU Ecodesign Lobbying", 2022*
