ArcelorMittal: Steel, Polluted Air, and Protests

ArcelorMittal — the world's largest steel producer — owns plants on four continents. Many are located near densely populated industrial cities.

In Temirtau (Kazakhstan), the ArcelorMittal steel complex is the country's largest single source of industrial emissions. Environmental organisations and city residents record systematic exceedances of limits for dust, sulphur oxides and nitrogen oxides. Average life expectancy in Temirtau is 5–7 years below the national average for Kazakhstan.

At Burns Harbor (Indiana, USA), the ArcelorMittal plant discharged chromium-contaminated wastewater into Lake Michigan in 2019 — a drinking water source for 7 million people. Chromium concentrations at Chicago's water intake exceeded permissible limits.

In Jharkhand (India), plans to expand iron ore extraction threaten the forests of Adivasi tribal communities and water sources for millions of state residents.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. ArcelorMittal production facilities in several countries are sources of chronic air and water pollution leading to higher disease rates and excess mortality among residents of surrounding areas.

1. **Temirtau, Kazakhstan (1990s–present)** — The steel complex systematically exceeds limits for PM2.5, SO₂ and NO₂. Epidemiological data from Kazakhstan's Ministry of Health record elevated mortality from respiratory diseases and cancer in Temirtau. Average life expectancy in the city is 5–7 years below the national average.
   *Source: Human Rights Watch — "Toxic Air: The Price of Fossil Fuels" (2020); Kazakhstan Bureau for Human Rights*

2. **Chromium discharge into Lake Michigan, Burns Harbor, USA (2019)** — In April 2019, the ArcelorMittal plant discharged process effluents exceeding hexavalent chromium limits into the Buffalo River, which flows into Lake Michigan. Samples from Chicago's water intake serving around 7 million people detected traces of chromium. Indiana state authorities issued a notice of environmental law violation.
   *Source: Chicago Tribune — "ArcelorMittal Chrome Discharge" (April 2019); IEPA Violation Notice*

3. **Air pollution near the Ghent plant, Belgium (2010s)** — Residents of the Voorhaven and North Ghent districts reported metallic dust settling on allotments and elevated benzo[a]pyrene concentrations. Belgium's Departement Omgeving recorded exceedances of PAH standards near the complex. Local health organisations reported higher rates of asthma and bronchitis among children in surrounding neighbourhoods.
   *Source: Departement Omgeving Vlaanderen — Air Quality Reports (2018–2020)*

4. **Explosions and accidents at the Temirtau plant (2006, 2008, 2020)** — The Kazakhstan complex has recorded numerous industrial accidents claiming workers' lives. In 2006, a methane explosion at the Lenin mine (part of the ArcelorMittal complex) killed more than 40 miners. Mining unions accused the company of cutting corners on safety.
   *Source: Reuters — "Kazakhstan Mine Blast Kills 41" (2006); BBC News*

## 2. Has the corporation caused major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. ArcelorMittal plants in Kazakhstan, the USA, Belgium and Brazil have been identified as sources of large-scale air, water and soil contamination documented by regulators and environmental organisations.

1. **Chronic air pollution in Temirtau (ongoing)** — According to Kazakhstan state monitoring, concentrations of dust, sulphur dioxide and nitrogen oxides near the complex systematically exceed limits. Green Salvation (Kazakhstan) documented soil and water contamination by heavy metals — cadmium, lead and zinc — at distances of up to 30 km from the plant.
   *Source: Green Salvation — Environmental Monitoring of Temirtau (2015–2022)*

2. **Wastewater discharges in Indiana, USA (2017–2019)** — The EPA recorded multiple violations of pollutant discharge limits at the Burns Harbor plant, including exceedances for ammonium, zinc and suspended solids. The 2019 large single discharge attracted national attention.
   *Source: EPA Enforcement Records — Burns Harbor (2017–2019)*

3. **Contamination of the Durme River, Belgium (2018)** — A process failure at the Ghent plant caused industrial effluents with elevated heavy metal content to enter the Durme River. Fishing associations recorded mass fish kills in the lower reaches. ArcelorMittal paid compensation at the regulator's direction.
   *Source: VMM (Vlaamse Milieumaatschappij) — Incident Report 2018*

4. **Coke battery emissions in Brazil, Espírito Santo state** — At the Companhia Siderúrgica de Tubarão complex (CST, part-owned by ArcelorMittal), repeated exceedances of coke gas and suspended particle emissions were recorded. Brazil's Ibama issued fines for standard violations. Vitória residents reported persistent odours and blackening of building walls.
   *Source: Ibama — Embargos e Multas Ambientais (CST, 2012–2016)*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. ArcelorMittal actively promotes a narrative of "green steel" and carbon neutrality, while the overwhelming share of production continues to use conventional blast furnace methods with a high carbon footprint.

1. **"XCarb" campaign — "green steel" with a negligible share of real production (2020–present)** — ArcelorMittal launched the XCarb sub-brand to designate "low-carbon" steel and actively promotes it in marketing materials. Independent analysts SteelWatch and Global Efficiency Intelligence noted that the volume of genuinely "green" steel produced is a negligible fraction of total output of 70+ million tonnes, while the campaign vastly overstates the scale of decarbonisation.
   *Source: SteelWatch — "ArcelorMittal Climate Assessment" (2022); Global Efficiency Intelligence*

2. **Claim of "carbon neutrality" in Europe by 2050 without a Scope 3 reduction plan** — The company publicly declares plans for neutrality in Europe and globally by 2050, yet Climate Action Tracker found that interim targets are not consistent with a 1.5°C trajectory and do not cover supply chain emissions.
   *Source: Climate Action Tracker — ArcelorMittal Assessment (2021)*

3. **Participation in EU "green" tenders while lobbying against strict ETS rules** — In 2021–2022, ArcelorMittal simultaneously participated in EU "green" financing programmes and, through Eurofer, lobbied against tightening emissions requirements under the EU ETS, seeking free allowances for the steel industry.
   *Source: InfluenceMap — "Steel Industry EU ETS Lobbying" (2022)*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. ArcelorMittal publishes annual sustainability reports and actively promotes ESG narratives in investor communications, while actual environmental indicators in the regions where it operates continue to record violations.

1. **Annual sustainability report with CO₂ targets (2019–present)** — The company publishes comprehensive ESG reports annually, declaring "progress" on reducing the carbon intensity of production. Reclaim Finance noted that the declared reduction is largely explained by production cuts during the pandemic rather than structural changes.
   *Source: Reclaim Finance — Steel Sector ESG Analysis (2021)*

2. **Participation in the ResponsibleSteel programme and plant certification** — ArcelorMittal is pursuing certification of its facilities under the ResponsibleSteel standard. SteelWatch found that certified plants were still recorded as violating environmental standards during the period their certification was valid.
   *Source: SteelWatch — "Responsible Steel? A Critical Review" (2023)*

3. **Partnership with the European Investment Bank for "green" projects** — ArcelorMittal received EIB funding for DRI hydrogen production research, actively covering this in an ESG context. Environmental analysts noted that in parallel the company was continuing to expand conventional coal blast furnace capacity in Kazakhstan and India.
   *Source: EIB Project Announcements (2021); SteelWatch*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. ArcelorMittal has received ESG recognition from several rating agencies, despite documented violations of environmental standards on multiple continents.

1. **Inclusion in the FTSE4Good index (2010s–2020s)** — ArcelorMittal was periodically included in the FTSE4Good index. Activist investors at Reclaim Finance noted that the index methodology did not take into account actual pollution monitoring data from Temirtau and Ghent.
   *Source: FTSE Russell — FTSE4Good Index Series; Reclaim Finance critique*

2. **CDP "B" climate rating (2021)** — Carbon Disclosure Project gave ArcelorMittal a "B" rating for climate transparency — higher than most steel companies. SteelWatch analysts noted that the rating assesses disclosure completeness, not actual emissions reductions.
   *Source: CDP — ArcelorMittal Climate Score 2021*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. ArcelorMittal applied "green" labelling to steel products whose carbon footprint significantly exceeds the advertised figures.

1. **XCarb® steel with a claim of "carbon neutrality"** — ArcelorMittal labels certain batches of steel as "carbon neutral" or "low carbon" on the basis of purchasing renewable energy certificates and emission offsets. SteelWatch published an analysis showing that the real carbon footprint of such steel differs only marginally from standard product, and the labelling misleads buyers.
   *Source: SteelWatch — "The XCarb Problem" (2022)*

2. **Promoting "ecological steel" for the automotive industry (Europe, 2019–2023)** — The company actively sells steel to automakers (Volkswagen, BMW) under the "Steligence" and "S-in motion" brands, describing products as "sustainable" due to improved strength. Independent LCA studies showed that the lifecycle environmental impact of such steel is comparable to standard product.
   *Source: European Environment Agency — Steel LCA Review (2021)*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. ArcelorMittal runs a number of environmental programmes whose scale is incomparable with the damage from the company's production activities.

1. **Forest restoration programme in Brazil ("1 million trees")** — ArcelorMittal Brasil announced a programme to plant 1 million trees by 2025. Given annual CO₂ emissions from the CST complex running into hundreds of thousands of tonnes, the absorption capacity of the planned plantings amounts to less than 1% of the complex's annual emissions.
   *Source: ArcelorMittal Brasil — Sustainability Report 2022*

2. **"Green corridor" around the Ghent plant, Belgium** — The company planted ornamental greenery along the plant perimeter and announced the creation of a "biocorridor" for pollinators. Naturalists at Natuurpunt (Belgium) noted that the area of plantings represents fractions of a percent of the territory disturbed by production.
   *Source: ArcelorMittal Belgium — Environmental Program 2021; Natuurpunt commentary*

3. **Sponsoring environmental olympiads for schoolchildren in Kazakhstan** — ArcelorMittal Temirtau funds annual environmental olympiads for schoolchildren in Karaganda Oblast, noting this in its sustainability reports. Meanwhile Temirtau remains one of the most polluted cities in the country.
   *Source: ArcelorMittal Kazakhstan — CSR Report 2021*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. ArcelorMittal, through industry associations, actively lobbied against the tightening of climate standards, while simultaneously promoting the narrative of "green steel" as an already-solved problem.

1. **Lobbying through Eurofer against EU ETS (2018–2022)** — ArcelorMittal is a key member of Eurofer — the European steel manufacturers' association — which systematically sought free CO₂ emission allowances and opposed the introduction of carbon border adjustment (CBAM). InfluenceMap gave Eurofer a rating of "E" — one of the lowest among European industry lobbies.
   *Source: InfluenceMap — "Eurofer Lobbying Assessment" (2022)*

2. **Promoting "hydrogen steel" as a distraction from real reductions (2020–2023)** — The company actively publishes materials about future hydrogen DRI technologies, creating the impression of inevitable decarbonisation while deferring real emissions reductions from blast furnace production. Climate Catalyst characterised this rhetoric as a "technology distraction".
   *Source: Climate Catalyst — "Steel Sector Distraction Tactics" (2022)*

3. **Corporate "eco-education" programmes in communities near plants** — ArcelorMittal funds eco-education programmes in schools in Temirtau. Local activists from Green Salvation noted that the programmes cultivate a favourable attitude towards the industrial enterprise among children without informing them of the real risks of industrial pollution.
   *Source: Green Salvation — Critical Analysis of ArcelorMittal CSR Programmes (2019)*
