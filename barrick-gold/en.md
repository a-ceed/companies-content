---
title: Barrick-Gold
brands: Nevada Gold Mines, Porgera JV
crime: Gold mining contaminates rivers with cyanide, arsenic and mercury in developing countries.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Barrick Gold bears responsibility for the deaths of local residents at the hands of mine security, sexual violence against women in Papua New Guinea, and systematic human rights violations in Tanzania.

1. **Killings of local residents by security at North Mara mine, Tanzania (2000–2014)** — Security personnel at North Mara mine (majority-owned by Barrick Gold) repeatedly used firearms against local residents attempting to illegally mine gold on the tailings. According to investigations by MiningWatch Canada and Human Rights Watch, more than 30 people were killed between 2000 and 2014. Barrick paid compensation to some families without admitting liability.
   *Source: Human Rights Watch, "Toxic Toil" (2015); MiningWatch Canada*

2. **Sexual violence by security at Porgera mine, Papua New Guinea (2005–2011)** — A 2011 Human Rights Watch report documented numerous cases of sexual violence against local women by guards at Porgera JV mine (a Barrick Gold operation). In 2011–2013, Barrick Gold acknowledged the violations and established the "Remediation Framework" compensation programme, paying out to more than 100 survivors.
   *Source: Human Rights Watch, "Gold's Costly Dividend" (2011)*

3. **Cyanide and heavy metal poisoning of Strickland valley residents, Papua New Guinea (1990s–2000s)** — Porgera mine discharged up to 16,000 tonnes of tailings containing cyanide, arsenic and mercury into the Strickland River annually. Local fishermen and farmers of the Huli people lost their livelihoods; cases of skin and gastrointestinal diseases were linked to water contamination.
   *Source: Earthworks; OK Tedi Fly River Watch (2007)*

---

## 2. Has the corporation been responsible for major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Barrick Gold's operations in several countries were accompanied by cyanide spills, destruction of glaciers, and mass contamination of water resources.

1. **Tailings discharge into the Strickland River, Papua New Guinea (1990–2020)** — Porgera mine used riverine tailings disposal — a method banned in most developed countries. Tens of thousands of tonnes of waste containing cyanide, arsenic and mercury entered the river annually. By 2010, the riverbed was clogged with metal-bearing sediment for hundreds of kilometres.
   *Source: OK Tedi Environmental Study; Earthworks "Fool's Gold" (2012)*

2. **Destruction of Atacama glaciers at Pascua-Lama project, Chile/Argentina (2006–2013)** — Mining operations at Pascua-Lama caused documented damage to the Toro I, Toro II and Esperanza glaciers in the Andes. These glaciers are the freshwater source for the arid valleys of the Atacama — one of the most waterless regions in the world. In 2013 a Chilean court froze the project and ordered Barrick to remedy the violations; the fine amounted to $16 million.
   *Source: Chilean Environmental Tribunal (2013); Copiapó River Watershed studies*

3. **Cyanide spill at Cortez mine, Nevada, USA (2008)** — At Cortez Hills mine (Barrick Gold) a cyanide solution leak contaminated soil and threatened groundwater. The EPA required the company to carry out large-scale remediation works. The incident became part of systemic criticism of Barrick's heap-leach practices at its Nevada mines.
   *Source: US EPA; Earthworks Nevada Mining Watch*

---

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Barrick Gold actively used the narrative of "responsible mining" and "sustainable gold" while continuing operations with high environmental and human rights costs.

1. **Declarations of "responsible mining" while discharging tailings into rivers in Papua (2005–2015)** — Barrick Gold signed the UN Global Compact and included declarations of "responsible mining" in its annual reports, while simultaneously continuing riverine tailings disposal at Porgera — a method prohibited in OECD countries. The company justified this as having no "technically feasible alternatives".
   *Source: UN Global Compact; Earthworks (2011)*

2. **Narrative of "zero glacier impact gold" in the face of documented evidence to the contrary (2007–2013)** — In PR materials on Pascua-Lama, Barrick claimed "zero contact" between mining operations and glaciers, citing its own monitoring. Independent satellite imagery and field research by the University of Chile recorded clear signs of glacier degradation in the project zone.
   *Source: Universidad de Chile glacier research (2009); CONAF Chile*

3. **Promoting Barrick as an "environmentally certified" gold producer (2018–2022)** — Barrick Gold actively promoted certification under the Responsible Gold Mining Principles (RGMP) standard, marketing itself as a leader in "sustainable" gold mining. Critics noted that RGMP was developed by the industry itself and did not provide for independent auditing of key operations.
   *Source: World Gold Council RGMP framework critique; MiningWatch Canada (2020)*

---

## 4. Does the company participate in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Barrick Gold uses ESG rhetoric as an investor relations tool, while its actual performance significantly lags behind its stated commitments.

1. **ESG reporting to GRI and TCFD standards (2019–2023)** — Barrick regularly publishes extensive ESG reports citing compliance with GRI, TCFD and UN Sustainable Development Goals. After the North Mara disaster and Porgera scandal, the reports grew considerably in length, yet observers pointed to the absence of independent verification of key metrics.
   *Source: Barrick ESG Report 2022; ShareAction analysis*

2. **"Transformation" narrative under CEO Mark Bristow (2019–2022)** — Following the leadership change, Barrick actively promoted the image of a "transformed company" with a new approach to community engagement. Investigative journalists documented continuing violations at mines in the DRC and Papua New Guinea during the same period.
   *Source: Bloomberg Businessweek (2021); Business & Human Rights Resource Centre*

3. **Membership in initiatives such as EITI and ICMM as a marketing argument** — Barrick used membership in the Extractive Industries Transparency Initiative (EITI) and ICMM in investor communications as evidence of responsible governance. Critics pointed out that membership in these organisations does not require remediation of specific human rights violations.
   *Source: ICMM membership list; Global Witness critique (2019)*

---

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG competition nominations?

**Answer: YES**. Barrick Gold received awards and positive assessments from ESG agencies that were at odds with its actual performance.

1. **Inclusion in FTSE4Good and MSCI ESG indices** — Barrick Gold was periodically included in the FTSE4Good indices and received above-industry-average ratings from MSCI ESG, allowing ESG-oriented funds to hold its shares in their portfolios.
   *Source: FTSE4Good Index Series; MSCI ESG Ratings*

2. **"Best Community Engagement Practice" award from the Mining Association of Canada (2015)** — Barrick Gold received an industry award for "exemplary community engagement" — in the same year that legal proceedings continued over killings at North Mara and the payment of compensation to victims of violence at Porgera.
   *Source: Mining Association of Canada; MiningWatch Canada critique*

3. **High CDP scores for climate risk disclosure (2020–2021)** — CDP awarded Barrick relatively high scores for climate risk disclosure, which the company used in investor materials despite ongoing environmental violations.
   *Source: CDP Climate Disclosure Scores 2020*

---

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Barrick Gold used "responsible gold" certification in its marketing despite ongoing violations at several mines.

1. **Responsible Jewellery Council (RJC) certification (2012–2020)** — Barrick Gold obtained RJC certification, enabling it to position its gold as "responsibly mined" for jewellery companies. RJC audits did not fully cover the key problematic mines; the standard was criticised for self-auditing and weak verification mechanisms.
   *Source: RJC certification records; Human Rights Watch critique of RJC (2012)*

2. **Promoting gold with a "traceable supply chain" for the jewellery market (2019–2022)** — Barrick participated in initiatives to label gold with a "traceable" chain, including partnerships with major jewellery retailers. Environmentalists noted that traceability of metal origin is not equivalent to the absence of environmental damage.
   *Source: Barrick supply chain communications 2022*

3. **Using RGMP certification as a "quality mark" (2018–2023)** — Barrick positioned its assets that met the Responsible Gold Mining Principles as "certified", using this in B2B and investor marketing materials. The standard was developed by the gold mining industry itself through the World Gold Council without the participation of environmental NGOs.
   *Source: World Gold Council RGMP (2018)*

---

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Barrick Gold implements local environmental programmes that are incomparable in scale with the environmental damage caused by its operations.

1. **Strickland River fishery restoration programme (2012–2018)** — Barrick Gold funded small fish-stocking programmes in the Strickland River and ichthyofauna monitoring near Porgera mine. The volume of stocking material was incomparable with the annual tailings discharges that continued in parallel.
   *Source: Porgera Joint Venture environmental reports*

2. **Revegetation in Nevada (2015–2020)** — Barrick Gold implemented sagebrush habitat restoration programmes near its Nevada mines as part of US Fish & Wildlife Service mitigation requirements. The area of compensatory plantings amounted to a fraction of a percent of the area of disturbed land.
   *Source: Barrick Nevada environmental compliance reports*

3. **Environmental education in Tanzania and DRC (2016–2021)** — As part of its CSR programmes, Barrick funded environmental lessons and tree plantings at schools near its mines in Tanzania. Critics noted that the programmes created a positive image for the company in communities that were simultaneously suffering from pollution.
   *Source: Barrick CSR Tanzania Reports 2018–2021*

---

## 8. Has the company lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories or greenwashing outreach?

**Answer: YES**. Barrick Gold actively participated in shaping a regulatory environment favourable to the gold mining industry and promoted concepts of "responsible mining" that weakened real environmental requirements.

1. **Lobbying against cyanide bans in Europe and the USA (2000s–2010s)** — Through the World Gold Council and industry associations, Barrick Gold opposed legislative initiatives to restrict the use of cyanide in gold mining, promoting the narrative of "safe cyanide management" with "best practices". Several European countries and the state of Montana (USA) banned cyanide gold mining despite industry lobbying.
   *Source: Earthworks "Cyanide Facts"; Montana cyanide ban history*

2. **Participation in shaping the RGMP standard in the industry's interests (2017–2018)** — Barrick played a key role in developing the Responsible Gold Mining Principles standard through the World Gold Council; it provided no independent auditing and was developed without the involvement of environmental NGOs. The standard was used as an argument against stricter government environmental requirements.
   *Source: World Gold Council RGMP background documents; MiningWatch Canada*

3. **Opposition to tightening riverine tailings disposal rules in Papua New Guinea (2000–2015)** — Barrick Gold lobbied to preserve the permit for riverine tailings disposal at Porgera mine, which would have been prohibited in most countries. The company presented it as a "culturally acceptable" solution for Papua New Guinea, simultaneously promoting the narrative of no technical alternative.
   *Source: Porgera Environmental Advisory Komiti (PEAK) reports; Earthworks*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Harm to human health**: Barrick Gold bears responsibility for the deaths of dozens of residents at the hands of mine security in Tanzania, documented sexual violence at Porgera mine, and chronic poisoning of river communities in Papua New Guinea.
2. **Environmental disasters**: Destruction of the Strickland River ecosystem through years of riverine tailings disposal and degradation of Atacama glaciers are direct consequences of the company's operations.
3. **Greenwashing**: Declarations of "zero glacier contact" in the face of their documented destruction; positioning riverine disposal as a "responsible" solution.
4. **ESG marketing**: After the 2019 leadership change, the "transformational" narrative was used for reputation management while violations continued.
5. **ESG awards**: Inclusion in FTSE4Good and MSCI ESG occurred in parallel with unresolved human rights claims.
6. **Eco-labelling**: RJC and RGMP certification was used to position gold as "responsible" without remedying actual violations.
7. **Symbolic actions**: Restocking the Strickland while tailings continued to be discharged — a model of symbolic environmentalism.
8. **Lobbying**: Active opposition to cyanide bans and shaping of industry-favourable "responsibility standards".
