BHP and the Samarco Disaster

BHP (jointly with Vale) co-owns Samarco — the company responsible for the failure of the Fundão dam on 5 November 2015. The event killed 19 people and caused the largest toxic tailings contamination of a river in Brazilian history.

Samarco ceased operations until 2020, when it resumed mining with significant restrictions. Full compensation payments were delayed: legal proceedings between Vale, BHP and Brazilian authorities continued for years.

In Chile, the Escondida mine — the world's largest copper mine — operates in the Atacama Desert, consuming billions of litres of water per year from already depleted underground aquifers. Environmental organisations and the indigenous Atacameño people warn of the risk of irreversible drying of unique ecosystems.

In Western Australia, iron ore mining in the Pilbara region has altered the flow regime of the Fortescue and De Grey rivers, damaging freshwater wetland ecosystems.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. BHP, as co-owner of Samarco, bears direct responsibility for the deaths of 19 people in the Fundão dam failure of 2015. Beyond the fatalities, hundreds of thousands of people were left without drinking water. At the Olympic Dam mine in Australia, occupational injuries and miner deaths were recorded. In Chile, labour disputes at Escondida were accompanied by multi-week strikes over working conditions.

1. **Fundão dam failure, Samarco (2015)** — 19 people were killed and 700,000 lost access to drinking water. Toxic tailings travelled 663 km along the Doce River and reached the Atlantic Ocean. BHP, as 50% co-owner of Samarco, was involved in years of legal proceedings.
   *Source: BBC News, "Brazil dam disaster: What happened at Samarco", 2016*

2. **Deaths at Olympic Dam mine (2000s–2010s)** — Several miners died in industrial accidents at one of Australia's largest underground mines. Unions repeatedly raised occupational safety concerns.
   *Source: Safe Work Australia, mining industry reports, 2010–2018*

3. **Water shortage for Atacameño indigenous communities, Chile (2010s)** — Mass water consumption by the Escondida mine led to the drying of traditional water sources of Atacameño communities in the Atacama Desert. Several villages lost access to drinking water from sources used for centuries.
   *Source: Amnesty International, "Water Rights and Mining in Atacama", 2019*

## 2. Has the corporation caused major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. BHP, through Samarco, orchestrated one of Latin America's largest environmental catastrophes. In addition, mining in the Atacama Desert and Australia's Pilbara region has caused long-term damage to water ecosystems.

1. **Contamination of the Doce River, Brazil (2015)** — 62 million cubic metres of toxic iron ore tailings destroyed the river ecosystem along 663 km. Mass fish kills were recorded, spawning grounds were silted up, and riparian vegetation was destroyed. The Doce River was effectively dead for several years.
   *Source: IBAMA (Brazilian Environment Agency), report 2016*

2. **Depletion of Atacama aquifers, Chile (1990s–present)** — The Escondida mine pumps more than 500 litres per second from underground aquifers. According to scientists at the University of Antofagasta, groundwater levels in some areas have fallen by 30–50 m, threatening the unique high-altitude desert ecosystem.
   *Source: Universidad de Antofagasta, Loa River basin hydrology study, 2020*

3. **Disruption of Pilbara river flows, Australia (2000s–present)** — Iron ore mining in the Pilbara region has altered the hydrological regime of the Fortescue and De Grey rivers. Wetlands listed under the Ramsar Convention have declined in area. Declining populations of endemic water birds have been recorded.
   *Source: WWF Australia, "Pilbara Rivers Report", 2018*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. BHP actively uses climate rhetoric and net-zero commitments while continuing to expand coal and oil and gas assets. The company positions itself as a leader in "sustainable mining" without fundamentally changing destructive practices.

1. **Declaration of "climate neutrality" while retaining coal assets (2019–2021)** — BHP announced plans to achieve zero Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 2050, while continuing to mine coking and thermal coal in Queensland. Critics noted that the "targeted" emissions cover only operational output, not emissions from customers burning the coal (Scope 3).
   *Source: Global Witness, "BHP's Climate Commitments Under Scrutiny", 2020*

2. **"Sustainable" branding of Escondida while water depletion continues (2018–2022)** — BHP presented Escondida as a "model of responsible mining", publishing energy efficiency reports. Meanwhile, water consumption from underground aquifers was not declining but increasing as the mine expanded.
   *Source: MiningWatch Canada, "BHP Escondida Water Controversy", 2021*

3. **Claims of "Doce River restoration" (2016–2020)** — Following the disaster, BHP funded a PR campaign about the "revival of the Doce River", while independent scientists continued to record heavy metal contamination and the absence of full biological ecosystem recovery.
   *Source: The Conversation, "Doce River, Five Years On", 2020*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. BHP is one of the most active participants in the ESG narrative in the mining industry. The company publishes extensive ESG reports, participates in climate initiatives and positions itself as a responsible producer of "materials for the future".

1. **Membership of Climate Action 100+ (2020–2023)** — BHP joined Climate Action 100+, a coalition of the world's largest institutional investors. The company declared alignment with the Paris Agreement, although independent analysts at InfluenceMap gave BHP a C- rating for misalignment between actual operations and climate commitments.
   *Source: InfluenceMap, "BHP Climate Policy Engagement Score", 2022*

2. **Publication of Sustainability Report (annually since 2004)** — BHP publishes multi-page ESG reports annually with target indicators for water, emissions and safety. After the Samarco disaster the "Safety" section was significantly expanded, but NGOs pointed to the absence of independent audits of key indicators.
   *Source: BHP Sustainability Report 2022; Earthjustice critique, 2022*

3. **"BHP Foundation" programme and social investment (2012–present)** — Through the BHP Foundation, the company funds educational and environmental programmes in Africa and Latin America, giving them extensive media coverage. Critics note that the scale of social investment is incomparable with the environmental damage caused by the company's operations.
   *Source: London Mining Network, "BHP Foundation: Social License or PR?", 2019*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. BHP has repeatedly received high scores in corporate ESG ratings, despite serious environmental incidents in its history.

1. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI) World (2018–2022)** — BHP was included in the DJSI World for several consecutive years as a "sustainability leader" in the mining industry. The inclusion occurred during years when legal proceedings over the Samarco disaster were still ongoing.
   *Source: S&P Global, DJSI results 2018–2022*

2. **MSCI ESG rating — "AA" (2021)** — MSCI assigned BHP an "AA" rating, corresponding to the "Leaders" category. NGOs criticised this rating as failing to account for the full scale of harm from Samarco and water management practices in the Atacama.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings, BHP Group, 2021*

3. **Dow Jones Sustainability Index "Industry Mover" award (2017)** — BHP was highlighted as the company showing the greatest ESG metrics progress in the mining sector, shortly after the Fundão catastrophe — which triggered a wave of criticism from environmental organisations.
   *Source: RobecoSAM (S&P), DJSI Annual Review 2017*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. BHP promotes copper, nickel and other metals as "critical for the green transition", positioning its products as environmentally significant despite the destructive consequences of extraction.

1. **Marketing Escondida copper as a "green metal" (2020–2023)** — BHP actively promotes copper as the key material for electric vehicles and renewable energy, without disclosing in advertising materials the water consumption in the Atacama or the fate of Atacameño communities.
   *Source: BHP marketing materials "Copper for the energy transition", 2021*

2. **Positioning Olympic Dam nickel as a "battery metal" (2021–2022)** — Against the backdrop of the EV boom, BHP presented nickel from Olympic Dam as "responsibly sourced" material for batteries. Independent auditors noted the absence of a full traceability chain.
   *Source: Reuters, "BHP's Battery Metals Strategy", 2022*

3. **Certification of Jansen potash as "sustainable fertiliser" (2023)** — BHP launched the Jansen Potash project in Canada, positioning it as producing "sustainable fertilisers for food security". Meanwhile, potash mining involves significant water consumption and a risk of contaminating Saskatchewan aquifers.
   *Source: Environmental Defence Canada, "Jansen Potash Environmental Concerns", 2023*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. BHP systematically organises and sponsors environmental activities that are incomparable in scale with the damage caused by its mining operations.

1. **Tree planting on the Doce River (2017–2020)** — Following the Samarco disaster, BHP and Vale funded a riparian vegetation restoration programme on the Doce River: approximately 1 million trees were planted. Yet 62 million cubic metres of tailings had been discharged, destroying the ecosystem along 663 km — the scales are incomparable.
   *Source: Samarco, "Rio Doce Restoration Report", 2019*

2. **Clean-up day at Escondida mine (annually)** — BHP holds annual clean-up events near the mine and sponsors environmental education in Antofagasta schools. The volume of water consumed by the mine in a single day exceeds the volume saved by all school projects over the course of a year.
   *Source: BHP, Sustainability Report 2021, "Community Programs" section*

3. **Sponsorship of "Nature and Technology" exhibition (2019)** — BHP partnered with an environmental biodiversity exhibition in Melbourne. The company's stand spoke of "caring for nature", while in the same year expansion of the Mt Arthur open-cast coal mine in New South Wales continued.
   *Source: Australian Mining, "BHP Sponsors Biodiversity Expo", 2019*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. BHP actively participates in shaping the climate agenda through industry associations and funding research centres, promoting the narrative of a "technological solution" to the climate crisis without reducing extraction.

1. **Membership of the Minerals Council of Australia — opposing climate regulation (2010s)** — BHP funded the Minerals Council of Australia, which actively lobbied against the introduction of a carbon tax in Australia and promoted the concept of "clean coal" as a climate solution.
   *Source: InfluenceMap, "Australian Mining Lobbying on Climate Policy", 2019*

2. **Funding the "carbon capture and storage" (CCS) narrative (2015–2022)** — BHP invested significantly in CCS technology research and promoted it as an alternative to abandoning coal. Critics note that CCS remains a commercially unviable technology, and its promotion serves to justify continued coal mining.
   *Source: Oil Change International, "Carbon Capture and the Mining Lobby", 2021*

3. **Withdrawal from the World Coal Council (2018) as a PR exercise** — BHP left the World Coal Council, widely announcing this step as a "climate decision". However, the company continued coal mining through its own assets and retained membership of other industry lobbying organisations opposing climate regulation.
   *Source: The Guardian, "BHP Quits World Coal Association", 2018*
