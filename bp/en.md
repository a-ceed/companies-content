BP Deepwater Horizon: The Largest Oil Spill in US History

On 20 April 2010, an explosion on the Deepwater Horizon oil platform killed 11 workers and became the largest man-made environmental disaster in US history. For 87 days, approximately 60,000 barrels of oil per day flowed into the Gulf of Mexico — nearly 5 million barrels in total.

The oil slick covered more than 68,000 square miles of water, affecting the coastlines of Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama and Florida. More than one million seabirds, 8,000 sea turtles and thousands of dolphins perished. The fishing industry of the Gulf of Mexico suffered damages in the tens of billions of dollars.

BP ultimately paid around $65 billion in compensation, fines and clean-up costs — a record sum in corporate history. However, scientists continue to record long-term consequences for tuna, dolphin and coral populations.

Earlier, in 2006, a pipeline rupture at Prudhoe Bay, Alaska, spilled 6,300 barrels of oil onto the tundra — the result of corrosion that BP knew about but ignored warnings of.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. BP's operations are linked to several of the largest industrial catastrophes, killing dozens of people and causing long-term health damage to thousands more.

1. **Texas City refinery explosion (2005)** — An explosion at BP's Texas City refinery killed 15 workers and injured 180. The US Chemical Safety Board (CSB) investigation found that BP had systematically ignored warnings about equipment failure in order to reduce operating costs. It was the largest fatal occupational incident in the USA in a decade.
   *Source: U.S. Chemical Safety Board — "Texas City Refinery Explosion" (2007)*

2. **Deaths of 11 workers on Deepwater Horizon (2010)** — The platform explosion killed 11 employees. The Presidential Commission found that BP had violated several critical safety procedures, including cutting cement tests to save time and money. BP's criminal prosecution ended with the company pleading guilty to "gross negligence".
   *Source: National Commission on the BP Deepwater Horizon Oil Spill — Final Report (2011)*

3. **Long-term illnesses among clean-up workers (2010–present)** — A Tulane University study (2016) found that workers who participated in the clean-up using the Corexit dispersant showed elevated rates of respiratory diseases, skin conditions and neurological disorders. More than 100,000 people participated in the clean-up.
   *Source: Tulane University — "Deepwater Horizon Health Outcomes Study" (2016); JAMA Internal Medicine*

4. **Poisoning of fishermen and coastal residents (2010)** — Louisiana fishermen hired by BP to collect oil reported acute poisoning: nausea, vomiting, headaches, skin rashes. Several were hospitalised. The Louisiana Department of Health documented more than 400 complaints of acute symptoms.
   *Source: Louisiana Department of Health — Oil Spill Health Surveillance Report (2010)*

## 2. Has the corporation caused major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. BP bears responsibility for several of the largest environmental catastrophes in oil industry history.

1. **Deepwater Horizon (2010)** — 4.9 million barrels of oil in the Gulf of Mexico over 87 days. 1,770 km of coastline was contaminated. More than 1 million birds, 8,000 sea turtles and thousands of dolphins perished. Seabed sediments were contaminated over an area of more than 9,000 km². Coral colonies at a depth of 2,000 m were destroyed within several kilometres of the wellhead.
   *Source: NOAA — "Deepwater Horizon Natural Resource Damage Assessment" (2016)*

2. **Prudhoe Bay spill, Alaska (2006)** — A burst in BP's corroded Transit pipeline released 6,357 barrels (267,000 gallons) of oil onto the tundra. This was the largest spill in North Slope of Alaska history. Internal BP documents revealed in prosecution showed the company had received warnings of corrosion years before the incident.
   *Source: U.S. Department of Justice — BP Prudhoe Bay Consent Decree (2007)*

3. **North Sea methane leaks (2019–2022)** — Satellite observations by the Kayrros group found that BP oil and gas facilities in the North Sea are among the largest sources of unplanned methane emissions in the European sector. Methane is a greenhouse gas 80 times more potent than CO₂ over a 20-year horizon.
   *Source: Kayrros — North Sea Methane Emissions Report (2021)*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. BP is one of the most striking examples of corporate greenwashing: the company introduced the concept of the "personal carbon footprint" and was the first among oil majors to rebrand itself as a "green" company.

1. **"Beyond Petroleum" rebrand (2000–2010)** — In 2000, BP spent $200 million on a rebrand with the new slogan "Beyond Petroleum" and a sunflower logo, positioning itself as "an oil company going beyond oil". Less than 5% of investment actually went to renewable energy. After the Deepwater Horizon disaster the company quietly dropped this brand.
   *Source: PR Week — "BP's Beyond Petroleum Rebrand Autopsy" (2010)*

2. **"Personal carbon footprint" calculator (2004)** — BP launched an online carbon footprint calculator for consumers and a major campaign inviting citizens to "take responsibility for the climate". MIT Media Lab researchers established that BP popularised the concept of the personal carbon footprint, shifting systemic responsibility for the oil industry onto individual consumers.
   *Source: MIT Technology Review — "The Carbon Footprint Sham" (2021)*

3. **Advertising about "transition to green energy" while cutting renewable investment targets (2022–2023)** — In 2022, BP announced reduced targets for cutting oil and gas production by 2030, but continued to run advertising emphasising investments in wind and solar power. The UK's Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) banned several BP advertising campaigns in 2023 as misleading.
   *Source: ASA — "BP Advertising Rulings" (2023)*

4. **Claims of "carbon-neutral" aviation fuels (2021–2022)** — BP's aviation division sold SAF (Sustainable Aviation Fuel) with claims of full carbon neutrality using poor-quality offsets. A Channel 4 News investigation found that some of the offset projects were not functioning properly.
   *Source: Channel 4 News — "BP Sustainable Aviation Fuel Investigation" (2022)*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. BP actively uses the ESG framework in investor communications, despite systematic divergence between declared and actual sustainability performance.

1. **Net Zero Ambition 2050 — investor presentation (2020)** — BP's new CEO Bernard Looney announced a strategy of "net zero across all operations" by 2050. Climate analysts at Carbon Tracker noted the strategy contained no binding interim targets for 2025 and 2030, and the term "net zero" was applied inconsistently.
   *Source: Carbon Tracker — "BP Net Zero Strategy Review" (2020)*

2. **ESG bonuses for senior management (2020–2023)** — BP introduced ESG criteria into executive compensation. However, a ShareAction analysis (2022) showed that climate metrics account for an insignificant share of the final bonus formula, while traditional financial metrics remain the primary compensation drivers.
   *Source: ShareAction — "Executive Pay and Climate" (2022)*

3. **Launchpad programme for "green startups" (2019–present)** — BP Ventures invests in clean energy startups and actively covers these investments in ESG reports. Analysts calculated that BP Ventures' total investment volume is incomparably small relative to capital expenditure on oil exploration and production.
   *Source: BP Annual Report 2022; Bloomberg NEF — Low Carbon Investment Tracker*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. BP received ESG recognition from several commercial rating agencies, which the company used in investor communications.

1. **MSCI ESG rating "A" (2019–2020)** — Ahead of its Beyond Petroleum 2.0 strategy announcement, MSCI assigned BP a relatively high ESG rating of "A", which the company cited in institutional investor presentations. Following a methodology review, the rating was downgraded.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings — BP Profile; BP Investor Presentation Q1 2020*

2. **Carbon Disclosure Project "A-List" inclusion (2016)** — CDP included BP in its list of companies most transparent in climate data disclosure, which BP actively used in PR. Critics noted that CDP was assessing reporting transparency, not actual environmental results.
   *Source: CDP — Climate A-List 2016; ClientEarth Analysis*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. BP applied green labelling and eco-rhetoric to products with documented negative climate and environmental impacts.

1. **BP Ultimate fuel labelled as "clean" (2000s–present)** — BP Ultimate is positioned as a "clean" high-quality fuel that cleans the engine and reduces harmful emissions. Regulators in several countries required BP to clarify the wording: the actual reduction in CO₂ emissions from using this fuel compared to standard product is climatically negligible.
   *Source: ASA — Rulings on BP Ultimate Advertising (2010–2019)*

2. **Labelling oil sands crude as "Canadian oil" (2010s)** — BP sold oil from Canadian oil sands without mentioning the carbon intensity of production, which is 17–23% higher than conventional oil. The European Court in 2013 considered a case on whether oil sands crude should be labelled "high carbon" under the EU Fuel Quality Directive.
   *Source: EU Court of Justice — Fuel Quality Directive Case (2013)*

3. **"Green" rebranding of BP filling stations with sunflower logo (2000–2010)** — Switching to the sunflower logo and green-yellow colour scheme gave consumers the impression of an environmentally oriented company, even though none of the products sold at BP filling stations became any less environmentally harmful. The Guardian called this rebrand "one of the greatest acts of greenwashing in corporate history".
   *Source: The Guardian — "Beyond Petroleum: The greenwash that fooled nobody" (2010)*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. BP runs environmental PR programmes whose scale and impact are disproportionate to the damage caused by its core business.

1. **Tree-planting programme in Indonesia (2020–2022)** — BP announced the planting of 100,000 trees as part of tropical forest restoration in Indonesia. Climate Accountability Institute calculations show that the total carbon absorption by these trees over 20 years amounts to less than 0.001% of the company's annual Scope 3 emissions.
   *Source: BP Press Release (2021); Climate Accountability Institute Calculation*

2. **"Target Neutral" internal carbon reduction competition for employees (2010s)** — BP launched an internal programme rewarding employees for reducing their personal carbon footprint: saving electricity at home, choosing public transport. Critics noted the irony: a company responsible for some of the world's largest industrial emissions was encouraging workers to turn off the lights.
   *Source: BP Internal Sustainability Programme Documentation; Guardian Energy (2019)*

3. **Sponsorship of environmental festivals and Green Awards (2010s)** — BP sponsored several UK environmental cultural festivals and educational events. From 2011 to 2016, the group "BP or not BP?" regularly staged protest actions at BP-sponsored cultural venues, including the British Museum and the National Portrait Gallery.
   *Source: BP or not BP? — Campaign Reports (2011–2016)*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. BP actively funded lobbying structures opposing climate regulation, while simultaneously running "awareness" campaigns that shifted responsibility onto consumers.

1. **Funding the American Petroleum Institute (API) (1990s–present)** — BP is one of the largest members of the API — an industry lobbying organisation that has systematically opposed the adoption of federal climate legislation in the USA, including opposing the introduction of a carbon tax in 2009–2010. A US Congressional investigation (2021–2022) documented the API's role in blocking climate laws.
   *Source: House Select Committee on the Climate Crisis — Staff Report (2022)*

2. **"Personal Carbon Calculator" campaign and consumer responsibility narrative (2004)** — By launching an online carbon footprint calculator in 2004, BP triggered a major media shift: responsibility for the climate crisis began to be discussed in terms of citizens' personal choices — such as holiday travel or meat consumption — rather than corporate decisions. This narrative was adopted by media and educational programmes in many countries.
   *Source: MIT Technology Review — "The Carbon Footprint Sham" (2021); Mashable — "BP invented personal carbon footprint" (2021)*

3. **Lobbying to delay tightening of EU emission standards through FuelsEurope (2015–2020)** — Through the FuelsEurope association, BP participated in lobbying against tightening regulations on fuel quality and CO₂ standards for road transport in the European Union. InfluenceMap assessed BP's climate lobbying activity as among the most intensive of any European oil company.
   *Source: InfluenceMap — "BP: Lobbying on Climate Policy" (2021)*

4. **"Energy for Life" educational programme (2000s–2010s)** — BP funded educational materials for UK and US schoolchildren that presented oil and gas as indispensable elements of modern life, covering climate change within a framework of "balance of opinions". Environmental educators criticised the programme for creating an unjustifiably positive image of fossil fuels in children's minds.
   *Source: Teach the Future — Report on Fossil Fuel Industry School Materials (2021)*
