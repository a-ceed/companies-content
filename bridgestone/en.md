Bridgestone/Firestone: Rubber at the Cost of Forests and Rights

Bridgestone (owner of Firestone) operates the world's largest rubber plantation — Firestone Liberia — covering approximately 50,000 hectares. The plantation was established as far back as 1926, when a significant area of Liberian forest was cleared to grow Hevea brasiliensis.

In 2005–2015, pollution of the Farmington River by rubber-processing chemicals, bank erosion and silting of fish spawning grounds were documented in the plantation zone. Local communities and the NGO Accountability Counsel filed complaints with the International Finance Corporation (IFC).

Global demand for natural rubber for car tyres is driving plantation expansion in Southeast Asia. In Indonesia and Thailand, forest clearance for rubber continues; Bridgestone has obtained FSC certification for some plantations, but critics consider this insufficient.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Pollution of the Farmington River by Firestone plantation waste and violations of workers' rights have been documented by international organisations.

1. **Farmington River pollution (Liberia)** — The use of chemicals in processing rubber latex (acids, coagulants) and discharge of process effluents led to the contamination of the Farmington River — the primary water source for villages near the plantation. Communities and Accountability Counsel filed complaints with the IFC citing health harm and poisoning of fishing waters.
   *Source: Accountability Counsel — Firestone Liberia complaint*

2. **Forced labour and living conditions of workers (historical)** — Human Rights Watch (2006) documented that workers on the Firestone plantation (more than 8,000 people) lived in slum conditions, lacked access to clean water, medical care and education for their children. In some cases, children were pressured to work.
   *Source: Human Rights Watch — "Deliberate Chaos" (2006)*

3. **Support for the Taylor regime and civil war** — Journalistic investigations documented that Firestone continued operations in Liberia during the civil war, paying taxes and concession fees to Charles Taylor's regime, which financed war crimes. This is a documented example of indirect contribution to mass harm.
   *Source: Global Witness — "Rubber Barons" (2013)*

## 2. Has the corporation caused major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Deforestation for the Firestone plantation and contamination of the Farmington River are documented environmental catastrophes.

1. **Historical scale of Liberian forest clearance (1926–1990s)** — The Firestone plantation, established in 1926, covers approximately 50,000 hectares — the world's largest rubber plantation. Its creation required the clearance of significant areas of Liberian tropical forest, one of the most biologically rich forest ecosystems in West Africa.
   *Source: Global Witness; Rainforest Action Network*

2. **Farmington River acid contamination** — Discharge of acids from latex processing led to bank erosion, silting and deterioration of water quality in the Farmington River. Local fishermen reported significant reductions in catches near the plant.
   *Source: Accountability Counsel — IFC complaint*

3. **Expansion of rubber plantations in Asia** — Bridgestone is one of the largest consumers of natural rubber from Indonesia and Thailand, where plantation expansion is linked to tropical forest destruction. Chain Reaction Research identified deforestation in Bridgestone's supply chain.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research — Natural Rubber Deforestation*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. FSC certification for some plantations creates an impression of responsible production while problems persist.

1. **FSC certification despite detected deforestation** — Bridgestone certified some rubber plantations under the FSC standard, positioning them as "sustainable". Independent audits found new clearances among third-party rubber suppliers, from whom Bridgestone did not withdraw.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research analysis*

2. **"Responsible rubber" commitments without verification** — Bridgestone publicly adopted commitments under the Global Platform for Sustainable Natural Rubber (GPSNR), but independent verification of third-tier supply chains was not carried out.
   *Source: GPSNR reports; Rainforest Action Network*

---

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Bridgestone actively promotes the ESG narrative through corporate reports and advertising campaigns.

1. **"Our Way to Serve" — ESG mission** — Bridgestone officially renamed its corporate strategy "Our Way to Serve", incorporating sustainability as a core element of corporate identity.
   *Source: Bridgestone Corporate Website*

2. **Annual ESG Reports** — The company publishes detailed sustainability reports to GRI standards, declaring targets for reducing CO₂ emissions in tyre production and transitioning to "responsible" rubber.
   *Source: Bridgestone ESG Data Book 2023*

---

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. Bridgestone has received ESG recognition in Japan and international ratings.

1. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI)** — Bridgestone is included in the DJSI World as one of the sustainability leaders in the automotive components sector.
   *Source: S&P Global DJSI*

2. **CDP A-List Forest** — Bridgestone has received high CDP scores in the Forest category for transparency in natural rubber supply chains.
   *Source: CDP Forest Scores*

---

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Tyres made from "certified" rubber are labelled as environmentally responsible.

1. **"Ecopia" — "eco-friendly" tyres** — Bridgestone sells the Ecopia tyre range, labelling them as "environmentally efficient" with emphasis on low rolling resistance and fuel economy. Meanwhile, the rubber production for these tyres is linked to documented deforestation.
   *Source: Bridgestone Ecopia marketing; RAN analysis*

2. **"Responsible Natural Rubber" labelling** — Some tyres are labelled as made from "responsible" rubber without disclosing the methodology for tracing second- and third-tier supply chains.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research*

---

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Bridgestone's ecosystem restoration programmes are negligible compared to the environmental footprint of tyre production.

1. **"RubberWay" programme** — Bridgestone launched the RubberWay app to monitor sustainability among rubber suppliers. The initiative covers only part of the supply chain and does not eliminate deforestation among third-tier suppliers.
   *Source: Bridgestone RubberWay*

2. **Tree planting in Liberia** — The company funds small reforestation programmes near the Firestone plantation, which are incomparable with the volume of forests historically destroyed.
   *Source: Bridgestone Sustainability Reports*

---

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Bridgestone lobbies for the production of "sustainable" rubber through industry associations.

1. **Participation in GPSNR as an alternative to regulation** — Bridgestone is a founding member of the Global Platform for Sustainable Natural Rubber (GPSNR) — a voluntary industry initiative. Critics note that GPSNR was created as a substitute for mandatory regulation and has no enforcement mechanisms.
   *Source: RAN analysis of GPSNR*

2. **"Tyres reduce vehicle CO₂" narrative** — Bridgestone actively promotes the claim that "eco-friendly" low rolling resistance tyres reduce vehicle CO₂ emissions, pushing the discussion about the environmental footprint of tyre production itself to the margins.
   *Source: Bridgestone Ecopia campaigns*
