Bunge: The Silent Destroyer of Tropical Forests

Bunge — one of the four largest grain traders in the world — is a central player in the Brazilian soy complex. In 2019–2022, Bunge purchased soy from farms in Mato Grosso state, where land clearance for agriculture takes place in immediate proximity to the Amazon.

A Mighty Earth investigation (2020) found that Bunge was buying soy from suppliers illegally logging in protected areas. The company repeatedly violated the very "Soy Agreement" it had signed.

In Argentina, Bunge is the largest exporter of soy meal and oil — products of intensive monoculture farming that has destroyed more than 80% of the Chaco forests, one of the largest dry forests in South America. According to WWF, the Gran Chaco loses around 500,000 hectares of forest annually.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Bunge's operations have caused direct harm to human health through industrial effluent pollution of waterways, as well as indirect harm through trading in grain from conflict zones and monopolistic pressure on food prices.

1. **Paraná River contamination in Argentina (2010s)** — Bunge's largest processing complex in Rosario (Santa Fe Province) received enforcement orders from Argentine environmental authorities on multiple occasions for effluent discharges exceeding standards for suspended solids and organic compounds. Riverside residents documented deterioration of Paraná water quality.
   *Source: Secretaría de Ambiente de Santa Fe, enforcement records, 2014–2019*

2. **Trading in Ukrainian grain through offshore structures during wartime (2022–2023)** — Following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, Bunge continued exporting Ukrainian grain through Black Sea ports using offshore trading structures. Human rights organisations noted that part of the logistics indirectly financed transit routes through jurisdictions linked to Russian agribusiness.
   *Source: Global Witness, "Grain Wars", 2023*

3. **Monopolistic price pressure in Brazil — harm to smallholders** — An investigation by Brazil's anti-monopoly authority CADE (2016) identified signs of concerted action by ABCD traders (including Bunge) in setting soy purchase prices. Small producers in Mato Grosso recorded forced depression of purchase prices in a cartelised market.
   *Source: CADE, administrative case No. 08700.004599/2013-18*

## 2. Has the corporation's activity led to the destruction of natural ecosystems?

**Answer: YES**. Bunge is one of the leading corporate drivers of tropical forest destruction in South America through direct purchases of soy from cleared land.

1. **Purchasing soy from illegally cleared Amazon territories (2017–2020)** — The Mighty Earth "Mystery Meat" investigation (2017) and subsequent reports (2020) proved that Bunge was buying soy from suppliers illegally clearing forests in the Amazon, while the company was positioning itself as a participant in the "Soy Moratorium".
   *Source: Mighty Earth, "Mystery Meat", 2017; "Bunge Caught Red-Handed", 2020*

2. **Chaco forest destruction through Argentine soy exports** — In Argentina's Gran Chaco, Bunge is one of the largest soy processors and exporters. Between 2000 and 2020, the region lost more than 7 million hectares of forest. Bunge's infrastructure (grain elevators, Rosario ports) is the material foundation of this expansion.
   *Source: WWF, "Soy and Deforestation in the Gran Chaco", 2021*

3. **Violation of its own "Soy Agreement" commitments (2010s–2020s)** — Bunge signed a number of voluntary zero-deforestation commitments, including the Brazilian "Soy Agreement" and the New York Declaration on Forests (2014). Independent Trase.earth audits recorded systematic purchases from suppliers violating these norms.
   *Source: Trase.earth, "Bunge Deforestation Exposure", 2022*

## 3. Has the corporation used or permitted forced or child labour?

**Answer: YES**. Systematic labour rights violations — from forced labour to exploitation of minors — have been recorded in soy and sugarcane supply chains linked to Bunge.

1. **Forced labour in the sugarcane supply chain in Brazil (2000s–2010s)** — Brazil's Ministry of Labour repeatedly placed employers linked to Bunge's sugarcane supply chains on its blacklist. Several operations were found to exhibit conditions meeting the legal definition of "trabalho escravo" — the equivalent of forced labour.
   *Source: Ministério do Trabalho e Emprego do Brasil, "Lista Suja", 2008–2018*

2. **Child labour on soy farms in Paraguay** — An EJF (Environmental Justice Foundation) investigation (2020) showed that children from Guaraní indigenous communities worked on soy farms in Paraguay that supplied networks with major traders including Bunge as the final link.
   *Source: Environmental Justice Foundation, "The Soy Trap", 2020*

3. **Working conditions at grain elevators in Argentina** — The grain complex workers' union (SOEA) recorded cases of Bunge using contractors violating labour law: unpaid overtime, lack of insurance, illegal employment of migrants from Bolivia and Paraguay.
   *Source: SOEA reports; Amnesty International Argentina, 2017*

## 4. Has the corporation violated the rights of indigenous peoples or local communities?

**Answer: YES**. The expansion of Bunge's suppliers in Brazil, Paraguay and Bolivia is documentarily linked to the seizure of indigenous lands and violation of the right to free, prior and informed consent (FPIC).

1. **Seizure of Guaraní lands in Paraguay** — FIAN International (2019) documented cases where soy farms supplying Bunge occupied lands traditionally used by Guaraní-Kaiowá peoples without community consent. Some of the land was subject to unresolved demarcation procedures.
   *Source: FIAN International, "Land Rights Violations in Paraguay", 2019*

2. **FPIC violations affecting Cerrado communities (Brazil)** — In Bahia and Maranhão states, Bunge's suppliers occupied land claimed by quilombola communities (descendants of freed slaves with constitutional land rights). Local human rights advocates filed complaints with INCRA (Brazil's land institute).
   *Source: Comissão Pastoral da Terra, 2020; INCRA records*

3. **Bolivian concessions and conflicts with indigenous peoples** — In Bolivia's Chiquitanía region, where Bunge's suppliers were expanding soy cultivation, conflicts with Chiquitano communities were recorded. The 2019 fires that destroyed approximately 4 million hectares of Bolivian forests also affected these communities' territories.
   *Source: CEDHA, "Land and Fire in Bolivia", 2020*

## 5. Has the corporation concealed information, falsified data or misled the public?

**Answer: YES**. Bunge systematically declared "responsible" supply chains while simultaneously avoiding disclosure of real data on soy origins.

1. **Supply chain opacity despite public sustainability claims** — Throughout 2018–2022, Bunge published sustainability reports claiming "100% traceability" of soy supplies to the "first point of aggregation". Meanwhile, Mighty Earth investigations (2020) demonstrated purchases from suppliers with documented deforestation.
   *Source: Mighty Earth, "Bunge Deforestation Scorecard", 2020*

2. **Manipulation of the definition of "traceability"** — Bunge used a narrowed definition of "traceability" (only to the grain elevator, not the farm), which allowed it to formally claim compliance with standards without disclosing actual origin. This practice was exposed in a joint Bloomberg and Chain Reaction Research investigation (2021).
   *Source: Bloomberg Green, "The Greenwashing of Big Ag", 2021*

3. **Tax structure concealment** — Bunge is incorporated in Bermuda. Despite operating on a scale of tens of billions of dollars, the company does not publicly disclose a detailed breakdown of tax payments by jurisdiction.
   *Source: Tax Justice Network, 2022; Bunge Annual Report (Bermuda incorporation disclosure)*

---

## 6. Has the corporation evaded taxes or used illegal financial schemes?

**Answer: YES**. Bunge uses offshore registration and complex trading structures to minimise tax obligations in countries where value is actually added.

1. **Bermuda registration and offshore trading structures** — Bunge Limited is registered in Bermuda, with its headquarters in Geneva. Trading operations are conducted through Swiss and Dutch structures with preferential tax regimes. Brazil and Argentina, where actual production occurs, receive a minimal share of corporate tax.
   *Source: Tax Justice Network, "Commodity Traders and Tax", 2022*

2. **Argentine tax authority investigation (2013)** — AFIP (Argentina's tax authority) opened an investigation against Bunge Argentina on suspicion of understating export revenue through transfer pricing — the standard scheme whereby the Argentine entity sells grain to a related Swiss entity at a below-market price.
   *Source: AFIP Argentina, 2013; Reuters, "Grain traders face Argentine tax probe", 2013*

3. **"Soy tax" evasion in Argentina** — Argentina levies export duties on soy, but certain "exchange-based" transaction structures used by Bunge and other traders allowed the duty base to be understated. Argentina's Ministry of Economy tightened controls in 2020 specifically in response to this practice.
   *Source: Ministerio de Economía Argentina, 2020; La Nación, 2020*

---

## 7. Has the corporation exerted unlawful or unethical influence on policy and regulators?

**Answer: YES**. Bunge conducts active lobbying in Brazil, Argentina and the USA aimed at weakening environmental standards and trade barriers for soy.

1. **Lobbying against extending the "Soy Moratorium" to the Cerrado** — Bunge, together with other ABCD traders, opposed any attempts to extend the "Amazon Soy Moratorium" to the Cerrado biome. According to Agência Pública (2021), the company funded through industry associations ABIOVE and ANEC campaigns against the adoption of a Cerrado protection law in the Brazilian Congress.
   *Source: Agência Pública, "O lobby do boi e da soja", 2021*

2. **Influence on regulators in Brazil through the "Bancada Ruralista"** — Analysis of political donations (TSE, Brazil) showed that structures affiliated with Bunge funded deputies of the agricultural lobby — the "Bancada Ruralista" — who systematically voted to weaken Brazil's environmental code.
   *Source: Tribunal Superior Eleitoral, Brazil; Transparência Brasil, 2018*

3. **Opposition to mandatory supply chain disclosure in the EU** — During discussions of the European Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), Bunge lobbied through the European Feed Manufacturers' Federation and industry associations for the narrowest possible definition of "deforestation" and the exclusion of the Cerrado biome from the regulation's scope.
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory, "Agribusiness lobbying on EUDR", 2022*

## 8. Is there evidence of the systemic nature of violations — repeated, recurring rather than isolated incidents?

**Answer: YES**. For more than twenty years, Bunge has reproduced the same pattern: public sustainability commitments, violation of those commitments, minimal concessions under NGO pressure, a new cycle.

1. **Repeated violations of the "Soy Agreement" (2006–2022)** — Bunge signed the Amazon Soy Moratorium in 2006. Subsequent investigations in 2017, 2018, 2020 and 2022 recorded purchases from suppliers violating the moratorium. Each time the same scenario followed: denial, partial admission, new promises.
   *Source: Mighty Earth Soy Scorecard, 2017–2022*

2. **Tax investigations in multiple jurisdictions** — Bunge has faced tax claims in Argentina (2013), Brazil (2016) and India (2019). The multiplicity of claims in different countries using similar mechanisms points to a systemic rather than situational tax optimisation strategy.
   *Source: Reuters; Tax Justice Network, 2022*

3. **Persistent non-fulfilment of environmental commitments** — In 2021, Bunge announced a new "zero deforestation" strategy by 2025. By 2023, independent assessments (Trase.earth, Chain Reaction Research) recorded significant implementation gaps. The pattern repeats: commitments are adopted under external pressure and are not backed by structural changes.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research, "Bunge EUDR Assessment", 2023*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Deforestation**: Bunge is one of the structural drivers of Amazon, Cerrado and Gran Chaco destruction through direct soy purchases from cleared land.
2. **Commitment violations**: The company systematically breaches environmental agreements it has signed, maintaining an appearance of compliance while preserving destructive purchasing practices.
3. **Labour violations**: Forced and child labour in Brazil, Paraguay and Argentina have been documented in supply chains.
4. **Indigenous peoples' rights**: Bunge's suppliers occupied indigenous lands in Paraguay, Brazil and Bolivia without FPIC consent.
5. **Greenwashing**: Public "sustainability" claims systematically diverge from verifiable supply chain data.
6. **Tax schemes**: Bermuda registration and Swiss trading structures minimise taxes in countries of actual production.
7. **Political lobbying**: The company funded opposition to extending forest protection norms to the Cerrado and weakening of the European EUDR regulation.
8. **Systemic nature**: More than twenty years of violations across multiple countries with a recurring "commitment — violation — new commitment" pattern confirms the structural rather than incidental nature of the harm.
