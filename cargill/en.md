Largest corporate driver of tropical deforestation through soy and palm oil supply chains.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Cargill facilities have been documented polluting drinking water rivers in the USA; working conditions at the company's meat processing plants have been repeatedly documented as life-threatening to workers.

1. **Pollution of the Minnesota River from a poultry plant (2000s–2010s)** — Cargill Turkey Products in Minnesota repeatedly received EPA notices for exceeding phosphorus, nitrogen, and pathogen standards in its discharge. Municipal water intakes downstream recorded deteriorating water quality affecting thousands of residents.
   *Source: Minnesota Pollution Control Agency, enforcement records; EPA, 2012*

2. **E. coli outbreak at a Cargill meat processing plant (2011)** — Cargill Beef's plant in Michigan released a batch of ground beef contaminated with E. coli O157:H7, leading to a major product recall (36 million pounds). One person died and several suffered serious complications. A USDA investigation revealed violations of sanitary standards.
   *Source: USDA FSIS recall notice, 2011; CDC investigation*

3. **Injuries and deaths of workers at slaughterhouses (systematic)** — Cargill's meatpacking plants rank among the facilities with the highest rates of occupational injury in the USA. During the COVID-19 pandemic, several of the company's plants became mass-infection clusters: over 900 cases at a single plant in High River (Canada, 2020) — the largest outbreak in the country's history at the time.
   *Source: Alberta Health Services, 2020; Human Rights Watch, "Meatpacking Pandemic", 2020*

## 2. Has the corporation been responsible for major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Cargill is the most documented corporate agent of tropical deforestation: Greenpeace, Global Witness, and Trase.earth have compiled extensive evidence across several continents.

1. **Construction of the Santarém terminal without environmental assessment (2003)** — Cargill built a grain terminal in Brazilian Santarém (Pará state, the heart of the Amazon) without the mandatory environmental impact statement (IBAMA). A court ordered the company to halt terminal operations; ultimately, the construction was retroactively legalised under pressure from federal authorities.
   *Source: IBAMA court records, 2003–2006; Greenpeace, "Eating Up the Amazon", 2006*

2. **Cerrado: illegal sourcing after the "Soy Moratorium"** — The Cerrado is not covered by the 2006 Soy Moratorium, and Cargill systematically exploits this gap. According to Trase.earth (2022), Cargill is the largest buyer of soy from the Cerrado, approximately 30% of which is linked to land clearing in violation of Brazil's Forest Code.
   *Source: Trase.earth, "Cargill Deforestation Exposure", 2022*

3. **Indonesian peatlands: palm oil supply chains with burning** — Global Witness (2019) and RAN (2020) documented that companies supplying palm oil to Cargill in Indonesia were clearing peat forests by burning in Kalimantan. Peat fires release ten times more CO₂ into the atmosphere than fires in ordinary forests.
   *Source: Global Witness, "Cargill's Dirty Palm Oil", 2019; RAN, 2020*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Cargill used its private-company status as a shield against public scrutiny, publishing a minimum of verifiable information about its supply chains.

1. **Refusal to disclose the list of soy suppliers** — Cargill is one of the few major agribusinesses that refuses to publish a complete list of its soy suppliers in Brazil. This makes independent verification of "sustainability" claims fundamentally impossible. The disclosure demand has been made by Greenpeace, Trase.earth, and shareholder investors.
   *Source: Greenpeace "Cargill's Secret Suppliers", 2020; ShareAction, 2022*

2. **Greenwashing through the "Food Systems Transformation Agreement"** — In 2021 Cargill signed a high-profile "transformation" agreement and announced specific climate targets. Independent analysis by Chain Reaction Research showed: the baseline methodology used by Cargill understates the actual carbon footprint of the supply chains.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research, "Cargill Climate Pledge Assessment", 2022*

3. **Under-reporting of pollution from US plants** — In a number of cases (Minnesota, Indiana) Cargill facilities submitted understated reports to the EPA on industrial discharge volumes, which was revealed when compared with independent monitoring data.
   *Source: Environmental Integrity Project; EPA enforcement actions, 2010–2018*

## 4. Does the company participate in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Being the largest private company in the USA, Cargill avoids public disclosure and uses offshore structures to minimise taxes.

1. **Offshore trading structures in Switzerland** — A significant portion of Cargill's trading operations are conducted through Swiss subsidiaries with preferential tax treatment. According to Tax Justice Network (2021), this allows hundreds of millions of dollars to be shifted out of the tax base in the USA and producing countries each year.
   *Source: Tax Justice Network, "Big Ag, Big Tax Avoidance", 2021*

2. **Tax authority claims in Brazil** — Receita Federal (Brazil's tax authority) has repeatedly issued transfer-pricing claims against Cargill Brasil. In 2019 the disputed additional assessments amounted to several hundred million reais.
   *Source: Reuters Brasil; Valor Econômico, 2019*

3. **Tax concessions in US states through threats to relocate production** — Cargill systematically secures tax concessions from US states and municipalities by threatening to move plants. In Kansas and Nebraska the company obtained tax exemptions worth hundreds of millions of dollars while simultaneously carrying out staff reductions.
   *Source: Good Jobs First, "Subsidy Tracker"; Kansas City Star investigations*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG competition nominations?

**Answer: YES**. Cargill is one of the largest agricultural-sector lobbyists in Washington and actively works with the governments of Brazil, Indonesia, and the EU.

1. **Lobbying against mandatory GMO labelling in the USA** — Cargill spent millions of dollars on campaigns against the introduction of mandatory GMO labelling at the state level (California, 2012; Washington, 2013). The company funded industry associations that distributed misinformation about the risks of labelling.
   *Source: Center for Food Safety; OpenSecrets.org, 2012–2014*

2. **Blocking tighter EU environmental standards through industry associations** — Cargill, through the European Oilseed Processors Association (FEDIOL), lobbied to soften requirements of the Sustainable Food Systems Regulation (Farm to Fork) and to narrow the scope of the EUDR (ban on deforestation products).
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory, 2021–2022*

3. **Opposing labour regulation in meat processing** — During the COVID-19 pandemic, Cargill lobbied the White House and Congress against closing meatpacking plants — even amid mass infection outbreaks among workers. The result was Trump's 2020 executive order that effectively barred states from closing the plants.
   *Source: NYT, "How the U.S. Government Helped Keep Meat Plants Open During COVID-19", 2020*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. There is evidence of a systemic pattern of violations spanning three decades — Cargill reproduces a systemic model of violations across different countries, industries, and domains, from the environment to human rights.

1. **Twenty years of violations in the Amazon** — From the construction of the illegal terminal in Santarém (2003) to current purchasing in the Cerrado (2022–2024), Cargill has consistently been linked to deforestation. Governments change, agreements change — the pattern remains.
   *Source: Greenpeace 2006; Trase.earth 2022; RAN 2024*

2. **Recurring environmental violations in the USA** — The EPA TRI database records violations at Cargill facilities in different states systematically throughout the 2000s and 2010s. Fines do not change production practices — they are built into the business model as predictable costs.
   *Source: EPA Enforcement and Compliance database*

3. **Long-standing failure to meet child labour commitments in cocoa** — Like ADM, Cargill signed the Harkin–Engel Protocol in 2001 and consistently failed to meet all agreed deadlines. Twenty years of "progress" on paper while systemic violations persisted — a documented fact.
   *Source: U.S. Department of Labor Cocoa reports, 2001–2020*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Cargill's infrastructure in the Amazon became a catalyst for land conflicts that struck indigenous peoples and traditional communities.

1. **The Santarém terminal and communities of the Tapajós River** — Construction of Cargill's grain terminal in Santarém prompted mass protest from fishing communities and indigenous peoples of the Tapajós River. The terminal altered the shoreline and disrupted traditional access to water resources on which the communities depended for their livelihoods.
   *Source: Greenpeace, "Eating Up the Amazon", 2006; IDSM (Instituto de Desenvolvimento Sustentável Mamirauá)*

2. **Land conflicts with the Guaraní people in Mato Grosso** — Cargill's suppliers in Mato Grosso occupied lands disputed as territory of the Guaraní people. FUNAI (Brazil's indigenous affairs agency) recorded threats and violence against community leaders who opposed the expansion of soy farms.
   *Source: FUNAI reports; Comissão Pastoral da Terra, 2019*

3. **Conflict with quilombola communities in Bahia** — A Trase.earth investigation (2022) linked Cargill's purchases in Bahia state to farms located on lands claimed by quilombola communities as their ancestral territories. A number of conflicts were accompanied by arson and threats.
   *Source: Trase.earth; Comissão Pastoral da Terra, 2022*

## 8. Has the company lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories or greenwashing outreach?

**Answer: YES**. Over more than thirty years Cargill has reproduced a systemic model of violations across different countries, industries, and domains.

1. **Deforestation**: Cargill has been recognised by Greenpeace as "the world's most destructive company" — direct investment in Amazon infrastructure has driven deforestation since the early 2000s.
2. **Environmental damage**: River pollution in the USA, toxic discharges from facilities, peat fires in Indonesia through palm oil supply chains.
3. **Health threat**: The E. coli outbreak (2011), occupational injuries at slaughterhouses, pandemic COVID-19 outbreaks at facilities.
4. **Child and forced labour**: Documented links to child slavery in the cocoa sector and forced labour in Brazil.
5. **Indigenous peoples' rights**: Cargill's infrastructure became a catalyst for land conflicts with indigenous peoples in the Amazon.
6. **Tax schemes**: Swiss and offshore structures systematically minimise tax obligations in countries of actual production.
7. **Lobbying**: Opposition to GMO labelling, weakening of environmental standards, and during the pandemic — prioritising profit over workers' health.
8. **Systemic nature**: More than twenty years of a reproduced pattern of violations across all key areas of the company's activity.
