Chemours: Next-Generation PFAS and the Cape Fear River

Chemours was spun off from DuPont in 2015 and inherited fluoropolymer production at the Fayetteville Works plant (North Carolina). The plant discharged a "replacement" PFAS — GenX — into the Cape Fear River, the drinking water source for more than 200,000 residents of Wilmington. GenX was created as a "safe" substitute for PFOA, but proved equally bioaccumulative.

In 2017, independent UNC researchers discovered GenX in Wilmington's drinking water. The scandal forced the EPA and state authorities to impose strict restrictions on Chemours' emissions. In 2022, Chemours agreed to pay $12 million and cease atmospheric PFAS discharges.

In the Netherlands, the Chemours plant in Dordrecht discharged PFAS into the Waal River — a tributary of the Rhine. In 2021, Dutch authorities found PFAS levels exceeding standards in eggs from local domestic poultry and in riverside soils up to 10 km from the plant.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. For decades, Chemours discharged PFAS chemicals ("forever chemicals") into rivers and the atmosphere, exposing hundreds of thousands of people to substances linked to cancer, immune disorders and hormonal disruption.

1. **GenX drinking water contamination (2006–2017, North Carolina)** — The Fayetteville Works plant systematically discharged GenX into the Cape Fear River. In 2017, UNC-Wilmington researchers detected the chemical in the drinking water of more than 200,000 Wilmington residents. Internal DuPont/Chemours documents revealed that the company had known about GenX toxicity since the 1980s but concealed this from regulators and the public.
   *Source: Cape Fear Public Utility Authority; UNC Wilmington, 2017; The Guardian, 2017*

2. **Atmospheric PFAS discharges over residential areas (2000s–2022)** — The Fayetteville Works plant released PFAS into the atmosphere, which settled in soil, water and animal organisms within tens of kilometres. A 2021 study found elevated PFAS concentrations in the blood of residents of Bladen and Cumberland counties — near the plant — associated with suppressed vaccine immune responses in children.
   *Source: NC DEQ, 2021; NCDHHS Blood Testing Program, 2021*

3. **Contamination in the Netherlands: PFAS in soil and food (2012–2021)** — The Chemours plant in Dordrecht discharged PFAS into the air and water for decades. In 2021, RIVM (the Netherlands National Institute for Public Health and the Environment) recorded PFAS levels exceeding standards in eggs from domestic poultry, meat and soil within a 10 km radius. Local residents were warned about the risks of consuming produce from their own gardens.
   *Source: RIVM, 2021; NOS Nieuws, 2021*

## 2. Has the corporation concealed information about environmental damage?

**Answer: YES**. Chemours and its predecessor DuPont systematically suppressed data on PFAS toxicity, knowing about the danger long before it was acknowledged by regulators.

1. **Concealment of GenX toxicity data since the 1980s** — Internal DuPont documents revealed in litigation showed that the company had research demonstrating the harmfulness of GenX precursors as early as the 1980s. When Chemours was spun off in 2015, this knowledge was inherited by the new corporation, which continued to publicly deny the chemical's danger.
   *Source: Sharon Lerner, The Intercept, 2015–2019*

2. **Underreporting of atmospheric emissions** — An NC DEQ investigation in 2019 established that Chemours had underreported volumes of atmospheric PFAS emissions in regulatory filings. Actual emissions proved significantly higher than declared.
   *Source: NC DEQ Consent Order, 2019*

3. **Obstruction of Dutch regulators** — In the Netherlands, Chemours spent years challenging the introduction of stricter PFAS emission standards, submitting data to regulatory authorities that understated the true scale of contamination. An independent audit in 2021 refuted the corporate calculations.
   *Source: Rijkswaterstaat; DCMR Milieudienst Rijnmond, 2021*

---

## 3. Has the corporation evaded accountability or obstructed justice?

**Answer: YES**. Chemours used legal, corporate and lobbying instruments to minimise liability for environmental damage.

1. **Chemours' spin-off from DuPont as an evasion mechanism (2015)** — A number of analysts and plaintiffs argued that the Chemours spin-off was structured to isolate DuPont from future PFAS-related environmental liabilities. Chemours inherited the toxic assets but initially had fewer financial reserves to cover claims.
   *Source: Bloomberg Law, 2019; class action lawsuits in US federal courts*

2. **Protracted legal battles against North Carolina** — Chemours challenged NC DEQ's emissions clean-up orders by initiating administrative appeals that delayed enforcement of environmental requirements for years.
   *Source: NC DEQ, Consent Order documentation, 2019–2022*

3. **Contesting compensation claims from affected residents** — In the Wilmington residents' class action, Chemours initially denied any causal link between GenX and health disorders, delaying payments. A settlement was only reached in 2023.
   *Source: WECT News, 2023; North Carolina Superior Court records*

## 4. Has the corporation violated the rights of indigenous peoples or vulnerable communities?

**Answer: YES**. Chemours' contamination disproportionately affected low-income and communities of colour, as well as rural residents dependent on well water.

1. **Disproportionate impact on rural and low-income communities in North Carolina** — Residents of Bladen, Pender and New Hanover counties — with high proportions of African American and low-income populations — were most dependent on well water and home-grown produce. They received the highest PFAS exposure doses.
   *Source: NC Environmental Justice Network; NCDHHS, 2021*

2. **Contamination in the Netherlands: impact on suburban families** — In the Dordrecht area, families keeping chickens and gardens several kilometres from the plant were required by authorities to destroy their food stocks. Renters and low-income households suffered disproportionate economic harm.
   *Source: RIVM, 2021; Gemeente Dordrecht*

3. **Absence of timely warnings for Spanish-speaking population** — In North Carolina counties, a significant proportion of farmworkers are Spanish-speaking migrants who did not receive timely PFAS warnings in Spanish. Rights organisations documented delays in translating official notifications.
   *Source: NC Environmental Justice Network, 2018*

## 5. Has the corporation's activity caused long-term damage to ecosystems?

**Answer: YES**. PFAS compounds discharged by Chemours have accumulated in rivers, wetlands and living organisms — from fish to birds — across two countries.

1. **PFAS bioaccumulation in Cape Fear River fish** — NC DEQ and the EPA documented high PFAS concentrations (including GenX) in the tissues of Cape Fear River fish. State authorities repeatedly issued warnings against consuming fish from the river for certain population groups.
   *Source: NC DEQ Fish Tissue Study, 2019; EPA*

2. **Contamination of wetlands and the estuary** — PFAS from the Fayetteville Works plant entered the wetlands of the lower Cape Fear basin, where these chemicals have been found in the tissues of frogs, waterfowl and invertebrates. The half-life of PFAS in the environment is measured in millennia.
   *Source: Duke University Nicholas School of the Environment, 2020*

3. **Contamination of Waal River sediments (Netherlands)** — Studies in 2021–2022 found elevated PFAS concentrations in Waal River sediments downstream from Dordrecht. The Dutch water authority initiated monitoring of PFAS accumulation in the river's fish populations.
   *Source: Rijkswaterstaat; RIVM, 2022*

---

## 6. Has the corporation engaged in financial manipulation or evasion of fines?

**Answer: YES**. Chemours' corporate structure, dividend policy and debt management drew accusations of prioritising shareholder payouts over building reserves for environmental liabilities.

1. **Dividend payments despite insufficient clean-up reserves** — In 2017–2018, Chemours continued paying shareholders hundreds of millions of dollars in dividends while simultaneously contesting clean-up orders in court. Plaintiffs and analysts argued that the company was deliberately failing to build adequate reserves.
   *Source: Bloomberg, 2019; SEC filings Chemours*

2. **DuPont lawsuit over "unfair" distribution of liabilities (2019)** — Chemours filed a lawsuit against DuPont, claiming that during the spin-off the parent company had deliberately transferred a disproportionate share of environmental liabilities to it. This lawsuit indirectly confirmed that the transaction was originally conceived as a means of isolating liability.
   *Source: Delaware Court of Chancery, 2019*

3. **EPA fine and clean-up agreement without admission of guilt (2022)** — In 2022, the EPA required Chemours to pay $12 million and implement remediation measures — without criminal prosecution and without an official admission of guilt. Environmentalists criticised the fine as disproportionately small relative to the scale of damage.
   *Source: EPA Press Release, February 2022*

---

## 7. Has the corporation lobbied against environmental standards?

**Answer: YES**. Chemours actively opposed the introduction of regulatory restrictions on PFAS at both federal and state level.

1. **Lobbying against EPA drinking water PFAS standards (2018–2022)** — Industry groups in which Chemours participated systematically pushed the EPA to set maximum allowable PFAS concentrations in drinking water as high as possible (and therefore less protective). Publication of final EPA standards was delayed by several years.
   *Source: PEER (Public Employees for Environmental Responsibility); EPA docket records*

2. **Opposition to classifying PFAS as hazardous waste** — Chemours opposed listing PFAS under the Superfund (CERCLA) hazardous substances list, which would have significantly complicated the clean-up of contaminated sites at corporate expense.
   *Source: Congressional testimony records; EWG, 2021*

3. **Lobbying in the Netherlands against tighter emission standards** — In the Netherlands, Chemours representatives met with officials from South Holland Province, seeking to delay the introduction of stricter PFAS atmospheric emission standards. Internal correspondence was disclosed following media requests in 2022.
   *Source: NRC Handelsblad, 2022*

---

## 8. Are violations repeated or ongoing?

**Answer: YES**. Despite fines and enforcement orders, Chemours continued to violate environmental standards, and PFAS contamination is of long-term character given the chemical persistence of these substances.

1. **Repeated emission limit exceedances after agreement (2020–2022)** — After signing a Consent Order with NC DEQ in 2019, Chemours repeatedly exceeded the agreed emission thresholds. The regulator issued additional orders and warnings, recording violations in official reports.
   *Source: NC DEQ compliance monitoring reports, 2020–2022*

2. **Continued PFAS discharges in the Netherlands above permitted levels** — In 2022, the Dutch regulator DCMR recorded that Chemours had exceeded the PFAS water discharge limits set in its environmental permit. The facility received an official warning.
   *Source: DCMR Milieudienst Rijnmond, 2022*

3. **Long-term contamination as a "continuing violation"** — Since PFAS compounds persist in the environment for thousands of years, the contamination created by Chemours is de facto permanent. Judges in several cases have characterised this as a "continuing violation", allowing claims to be filed beyond the standard statute of limitations.
   *Source: US District Court, Eastern District of North Carolina; NC Court of Appeals*
