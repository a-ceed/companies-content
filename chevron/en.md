Chevron/Texaco: The Amazon Chernobyl

The largest environmental lawsuit in history is linked to the operations of Texaco (acquired by Chevron in 2001) in the Ecuadorian Amazon. From 1964 to 1992, the company extracted oil in Sucumbíos Province, leaving behind approximately 1,000 unclosed oil pits and 18 billion gallons of toxic waste. Local indigenous peoples — Quechua, Shuar, Siona — recorded a sharp rise in cancer rates, birth defects and miscarriages.

In 2011, an Ecuadorian court ordered Chevron to pay $9.5 billion in compensation — one of the largest environmental judgements in history. Chevron refused to comply with the ruling and launched a global legal campaign against the plaintiffs.

In California, the company's Richmond refinery caused a major fire in 2012; toxic vapour releases forced 15,000 residents to seek medical attention. Chronic emission exceedances at this facility have been documented by regulators over 20 years.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Chevron/Texaco's operations in Ecuador, the USA and Nigeria directly harmed the health and lives of tens of thousands of people through contamination of water, air and soil with toxic petroleum products.

1. **Ecuador, Amazon (1964–1992)** — Texaco (subsequently Chevron) left approximately 1,000 open oil pits and discharged 18 billion gallons of toxic drilling waste in Sucumbíos Province. Medical studies recorded a dramatic rise in cancer rates, birth defects and miscarriages among the Quechua, Shuar and Siona indigenous peoples. Independent experts estimated the number of additional cancer deaths at several thousand.
   *Source: Amazon Watch, ChevronToxico.com; Cabrera report, 2008; Ecuador Supreme Court ruling, 2013*

2. **Richmond, California — refinery fire (2012)** — On 6 August 2012, a major fire occurred at Chevron's Richmond refinery; toxic vapour releases forced approximately 15,000 residents to seek emergency medical attention. Hospitals were overwhelmed; those affected included children with asthma attacks. The EPA established that the company had ignored pipeline corrosion warnings for several years before the accident.
   *Source: U.S. Chemical Safety Board, report 2015; Bay Area Air Quality Management District*

3. **Nigeria, Niger Delta (1990s–2000s)** — Joint operations by Chevron and Nigerian security forces resulted in the deaths of civilians protesting against pollution. In 1998, two activists were killed during a dispersal of protesters at the Parabe platform. Oil spills at Chevron Nigeria Ltd. facilities chronically contaminated fishing grounds and drinking water sources for communities.
   *Source: Amnesty International, "Petroleum, Pollution and Poverty in the Niger Delta", 2009; Human Rights Watch*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Chevron/Texaco bears direct responsibility for several of the largest environmental catastrophes of the 20th–21st centuries, affecting the unique ecosystems of the Amazon, California coast and Niger Delta.

1. **Amazon, Ecuador (1964–1992)** — Texaco's operations in Sucumbíos Province contaminated more than 450,000 hectares of tropical forest. Concentrations of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons in soil and water exceeded standards by hundreds of times. The traditional livelihoods of indigenous peoples were destroyed, and some communities were pushed to the brink of extinction.
   *Source: Independent expert Richard Cabrera's report, 2008; Aguinda v. ChevronTexaco case materials*

2. **Spill off the UK coast (2010)** — During shelf operations near the British coast, an oil leak occurred that required emergency services; contamination reached coastal bird colonies. The incident revealed systemic violations of maintenance procedures.
   *Source: UK Health and Safety Executive; BBC News, 2010*

3. **Angola offshore spill (2011)** — Multiple unplanned oil releases occurred at deepwater block 0 (Angola); according to Angolan regulator Sonangol data, leaks continued for several weeks. Contamination affected coastal ecosystems of Namibe and fishing grounds.
   *Source: Reuters, April 2011; AngolaPressNews; Sonangol reports*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. For decades, Chevron invested millions of dollars in advertising campaigns positioning the company as environmentally responsible, while simultaneously lobbying to weaken environmental standards and concealing the scale of damage.

1. **"We Agree" advertising campaign (2010)** — Chevron launched a major campaign with the slogan "Oil companies should support clean energy", accompanied by image videos about "green" projects. Activists from Ecuador and Richmond released a parody "anti-campaign" on the same platforms. Chevron's actual expenditure on renewable energy was less than 1% of capital expenditure.
   *Source: The Guardian, 2010; Yes Men / Rainforest Action Network response campaign*

2. **"Zero emission" declarations in Kazakhstan (2010s)** — Chevron, operator of Kazakhstan's largest Tengiz field, published flaring reduction progress reports without disclosing actual volumes of gas flaring and years of non-compliance with Kazakh standards. Local residents complained of acute air pollution.
   *Source: Kazakhstan environmental reports; KazTAG investigative journalism, 2015–2019*

3. **Legal campaign against Ecuadorian plaintiffs as a form of greenwashing (2011–2023)** — While Chevron ran sustainability advertising, the company conducted a years-long legal war against Ecuador communities' lawyers, achieving criminal prosecution of attorney Steven Donziger in the USA. This combination of PR and aggressive legal pressure is a classic environmental greenwashing strategy.
   *Source: The Intercept, 2019; Columbia Law School Human Rights Clinic; Bloomberg Law*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Chevron actively publishes ESG reports and uses ESG rhetoric in investor communications without backing up declarations with commensurate action.

1. **Chevron ESG reporting (2020–2024)** — Annual corporate sustainability reports declare targets for reducing carbon intensity, but independent Carbon Tracker analysis showed that the company's capital plans to 2030 are incompatible with the 1.5°C warming limit. Chevron's targets do not cover Scope 3 emissions (product combustion by consumers) — the largest category.
   *Source: Carbon Tracker Initiative, "Flying Blind", 2021; Chevron Corporate Sustainability Report 2023*

2. **Membership of the Oil and Gas Climate Initiative (OGCI)** — Chevron is a member of OGCI — an industry consortium declaring climate targets. Critics note that OGCI commitments are voluntary and unverifiable, and the emissions accounting methodology used by participants is questionable.
   *Source: Global Witness, "Big Oil Reality Check", 2021; InfluenceMap*

3. **Shareholder activism and ESG response rhetoric (2021)** — At the 2021 annual general meeting, activist fund Engine No. 1 demanded strengthened climate commitments. Chevron responded with intensified ESG communications and minor target adjustments, without changing its investment programme in favour of low-carbon solutions.
   *Source: Reuters, May 2021; Financial Times; Engine No. 1 proxy materials*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. Chevron received industry ESG awards that critics view as a legitimisation instrument rather than evidence of real progress.

1. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index — North America (2019–2022)** — Chevron was included in DJSI North America for several years, which was actively used in corporate communications. ESG Ratings Watch analysts noted that the DJSI methodology for the oil and gas sector does not account for Scope 3 emission intensity and historical environmental damage.
   *Source: S&P Global DJSI reports; ESG Ratings Watch, 2022*

2. **MSCI ESG rating — "BBB" (2022)** — Chevron received a mid-range MSCI ESG rating, which the company cited in investor presentations as confirmation of responsible governance. Critics noted that the rating did not reflect the unresolved environmental damage in Ecuador.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings; Chevron Investor Relations materials, 2022*

3. **Platts Global Energy Award — "ESG Leader" in oil and gas category (2021)** — Industry publication S&P Global Platts awarded Chevron in the responsible governance category for the oil and gas sector. Earthjustice and Amazon Watch published a joint statement criticising the award.
   *Source: S&P Global Platts Energy Awards 2021; Earthjustice and Amazon Watch press releases*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Chevron labelled motor oils and fuels with environmental symbols and claims lacking independent certification.

1. **Havoline motor oil with "Deposit Shield technology" — eco-labelling (2010s)** — Havoline oil packaging (a Chevron brand) featured "engine environmental protection" logos and claims about emission reduction. Independent tests by petroleum research institutes did not confirm any significant environmental impact difference compared to comparable products.
   *Source: Havoline product labeling; API certification database; Consumer Reports, 2017*

2. **Techron fuel with "cleaning" emission claims (2000s–2010s)** — Chevron advertised the Techron additive as a means of reducing harmful exhaust emissions, using green graphics in promotional materials. California regulators (CARB) did not classify Techron in a separate environmental category.
   *Source: CARB fuel additive database; Chevron Techron marketing materials; FTC guidelines on environmental marketing*

3. **"Environmentally preferred" lubricants for marine applications (2015–2020)** — Chevron promoted its Taro Special HT range as "environmentally safe" for marine engines. Environmental organisations pointed out that the product contains components potentially toxic to marine organisms in the event of spills.
   *Source: Chevron Marine Lubricants product sheets; IMO MARPOL compliance database*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Chevron regularly sponsored local environmental initiatives incomparable in scale with the real damage caused by its operations.

1. **Tree-planting programme in Richmond, California (2013–2015)** — Following the 2012 fire, Chevron provided funding for planting several thousand trees in districts near the refinery as part of an "environmental restoration programme". Environmental activists noted that the programme budget amounted to less than 0.1% of the damage caused by the fire, and that actual air quality in Richmond did not improve.
   *Source: City of Richmond Environmental Programs; Chevron Richmond community reports, 2013*

2. **"Earth Day" eco-activities at offices (annually)** — Chevron annually organises internal Earth Day events at its offices worldwide: waste sorting, employee educational seminars, children's ecology drawing competitions. These initiatives are widely covered on corporate social media.
   *Source: Chevron Earth Day corporate communications; LinkedIn Chevron official page*

3. **Sponsorship of "Ocean Week" in Angola (2018)** — Chevron Angola funded a "Clean Ocean Week" in Luanda: a beach clean-up with approximately 200 volunteers and educational lectures for schoolchildren. The event was covered in a corporate press release as a contribution to marine ecosystem protection.
   *Source: Chevron Angola press release, June 2018; SonAngol news*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. For decades, Chevron funded organisations questioning climate science and lobbied against carbon taxes and tightening of environmental standards.

1. **Funding climate sceptics through the American Legislative Exchange Council (ALEC)** — Chevron was a member of ALEC — an organisation lobbying against recognition of anthropogenic climate change at state legislature level. InfluenceMap documented years of Chevron contributions to ALEC and affiliated think tanks (Heartland Institute, Cato Institute).
   *Source: InfluenceMap, "Big Oil's Real Agenda on Climate Change", 2019; ALEC donor disclosures*

2. **Lobbying against Initiative I-732 in Washington State (2016)** — Chevron contributed large sums to the campaign against a carbon tax referendum in Washington State, while simultaneously publicly declaring support for "market mechanisms" to combat climate change.
   *Source: Washington State Public Disclosure Commission; The Seattle Times, 2016*

3. **"Eco-educational" grants with a petroleum bias (2000s–2010s)** — Chevron funded educational programmes in schools in oil-producing regions, including materials that downplayed the environmental consequences of extraction. In Nigeria and Angola, corporate "eco-educational" programmes did not include information about the real health risks of oil pollution.
   *Source: Corporate Accountability International; Oil Change International, "The Sky's Limit", 2016*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Mass harm to human lives?** — **YES** — Texaco/Chevron contaminated 450,000 ha of Amazon forest (Ecuador, 1964–1992), triggering a wave of cancer among indigenous peoples; Richmond refinery fire (2012) led to 15,000 medical consultations; Nigeria operations involved deaths of activists and poisoning of community water sources.

2. **Major environmental disasters?** — **YES** — The Amazon contamination is recognised as the largest environmental crime of the oil industry in Latin American history; spills in Angola and the Richmond refinery accident damaged coastal and urban ecosystems.

3. **Greenwashing?** — **YES** — "We Agree" campaign (2010); "zero emissions" declarations in Kazakhstan; simultaneous persecution of environmental lawyers in Ecuador.

4. **ESG marketing?** — **YES** — Chevron publishes ESG reports with declarative targets not covering Scope 3 emissions, citing DJSI and MSCI ratings in investor presentations.

5. **Wins in ESG competitions?** — **YES** — Included in DJSI North America (2019–2022); MSCI "BBB" rating; Platts Energy Award ESG nomination — despite unresolved environmental damage in Ecuador.

6. **Eco-labels on harmful products?** — **YES** — Havoline oils and Techron fuel labelled with "environmental" symbols without independent certification; marine lubricants promoted as "environmentally safe".

7. **Negligible eco-events?** — **YES** — Tree planting in Richmond after 2012 fire; corporate "Earth Days"; Angola beach clean-up sponsorship — incommensurate with the scale of damage caused.

8. **Lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?** — **YES** — Chevron funded ALEC and climate sceptics; lobbied against carbon tax in Washington State; distributed "eco-educational" materials downplaying the harm from oil extraction.
