CNPC: Oil and the Geopolitics of Ecology

CNPC (China National Petroleum Corporation) — China's largest state-owned oil company — operates actively in regions with minimal environmental standards.

In Sudan and South Sudan, CNPC's oil extraction was accompanied by chronic spills on the Nile lowlands — causing damage to the continent's most important wetlands. Spills threaten water sources for millions of nomadic pastoralists.

In Myanmar, construction of CNPC's pipeline through Rakhine State led to forced village relocation and disruption of coastal forest ecosystems. The International Federation for Human Rights documented numerous violations.

In Kazakhstan, CNPC participates in developing the Kashagan field in the Caspian Sea — the largest oil discovery in the past 30 years. In 2013, toxic hydrogen sulphide emissions at Kashagan forced Kazakhstan to temporarily halt production.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. CNPC/PetroChina operated in conflict zones and systematically contaminated water sources on which the survival of millions of people depended.

1. **Financing the Sudanese regime during the Darfur genocide (2003–2010)** — CNPC was the largest foreign investor in Sudan's oil industry. Tax revenues from oil extraction provided up to 70% of the military budget of Omar al-Bashir's government, which used weapons against the civilian population of Darfur. According to UN data, between 200,000 and 400,000 people died in the conflict.
   *Source: Human Rights Watch, "China's Involvement in Sudan: Arms and Oil", 2003; UN Commission of Inquiry on Darfur, 2005*

2. **Oil spill in Dalian (2010)** — An explosion and spill occurred on a pipeline connected to PetroChina's terminal in Dalian (Liaoning Province), discharging approximately 1,500 tonnes of crude oil into the bay. The contamination affected 430 sq km of water, destroyed coastal fishing, and food sources for local residents were placed at risk.
   *Source: China Daily, 2010; Greenpeace China, 2010*

3. **Myanmar pipeline: village displacement (2009–2013)** — Construction of CNPC's gas pipeline through Rakhine State led to the forced relocation of villages without adequate compensation. The International Federation for Human Rights (FIDH) documented land seizures, destruction of crops and violence against protesters.
   *Source: FIDH, "Total Denial Continues", 2012; EarthRights International, 2013*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. CNPC's operations are linked to large-scale oil spills and chronic contamination of rivers, wetlands and marine waters.

1. **Chronic oil spills in South Sudan (2000s–2010s)** — Hundreds of spills were documented at the oil fields of blocks 1, 2 and 4 managed by CNPC, contaminating the wetlands of the Nile River in areas inhabited by the Nuer people. According to Human Rights Watch (2013), degradation of the wetlands deprived thousands of families of water sources and pastureland.
   *Source: Human Rights Watch, "Unity State Oil Fields", 2013; Enough Project, 2012*

2. **Pipeline accident in Xinjiang (2011)** — A CNPC pipeline rupture in the Tarim Basin led to an oil release into desert and agricultural areas. Local authorities initially concealed the scale of the spill; independent assessments indicated hundreds of tonnes of oil discharged.
   *Source: China Environment News, 2011; Caixin Media, 2011*

3. **Tuohe River contamination (Inner Mongolia, 2006)** — A PetroChina pipeline rupture at the Hulunbuir field caused oil to enter the Tuohe River — a tributary of the Argun. The oil slick spread over 100 km; fishing and water abstraction were banned. Riparian areas traditionally used by Mongolian herders were affected.
   *Source: China Daily, 2006; State Environmental Protection Administration of China, 2006*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. PetroChina and CNPC publicly declared "green" commitments not backed by real changes in environmental practice.

1. **CNPC's "green" strategy while operating in conflict zones (2010s)** — CNPC published annual sustainability reports declaring "zero incidents" and "compliance with international standards" while documented spills and human rights violations continued in South Sudan.
   *Source: Global Witness, "China and Congo", 2011; CNPC Sustainability Report, 2012*

2. **PetroChina advertising campaigns about "clean energy" (2015–2020)** — PetroChina launched major advertising campaigns in international media highlighting investments in natural gas as a "transition fuel", while simultaneously increasing oil extraction in ecologically vulnerable areas and investing in Xinjiang coal deposits.
   *Source: PetroChina Annual Report, 2018; Carbon Tracker, 2019*

3. **"Green bonds" against a backdrop of rising emissions (2021)** — CNPC announced the issuance of 5 billion yuan in "green bonds" for "low-carbon projects", but independent analysts noted that most projects do not qualify as green under EU Taxonomy criteria.
   *Source: Bloomberg NEF, 2021; Climate Bonds Initiative, 2021*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. PetroChina actively uses ESG rhetoric to attract international investment, without disclosing the full picture of environmental and human rights violations.

1. **Inclusion of PetroChina in sustainability indices (2010s)** — PetroChina periodically received high assessments in certain Asian ESG ratings, despite documented violations in Sudan, Myanmar and Kazakhstan. Critics noted incomplete disclosure of emissions and human rights data.
   *Source: Sustainalytics ESG Risk Ratings; ShareAction, 2018*

2. **Reporting on UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)** — CNPC regularly publishes reports with a section on contributions to UN SDGs, but NGOs and academic researchers noted that the reporting systematically omits specific incidents and focuses on general declarations.
   *Source: UN Global Compact Watchdog; Corporate Accountability, 2020*

3. **ESG presentations for Western investors (2019–2022)** — At investor conferences, PetroChina presented "ESG slides" highlighting tree-planting programmes and "emission intensity reductions", avoiding data on absolute emission growth and operations in problematic jurisdictions.
   *Source: PetroChina Investor Day presentations, 2020–2021; MSCI ESG Research*

---

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. Despite violations, CNPC/PetroChina received certain ESG recognition at the domestic Chinese and regional level.

1. **"Best corporate social responsibility" awards in China (2010s)** — CNPC repeatedly appeared in "best CSR corporations" lists of Chinese business publications — during periods when its operations in Sudan and Myanmar were subject to severe criticism from international human rights organisations.
   *Source: China Business Journal CSR Awards; Caixin Media*

2. **Inclusion in MSCI ESG Ratings (2020)** — PetroChina received a "B" rating in the MSCI system, allowing a number of ESG-mandated funds to retain the company's shares. Critics noted that the rating did not account for operations in Sudan and violations in Myanmar.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings database, 2020; ShareAction*

---

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. CNPC/PetroChina promoted certain fuels and products under "environmental" brands despite an unchanged high environmental impact.

1. **Natural gas branded as "clean" fuel (2010s–2020s)** — The Kunlun Gas filling station network under CNPC's umbrella actively used green colour schemes and "clean energy" slogans to promote compressed and liquefied natural gas. Independent lifecycle assessments, including methane leaks, dispute this claim.
   *Source: CNPC Kunlun Gas marketing materials; IEA Methane Tracker, 2021*

2. **Labelling oil from sensitive ecosystems as "environmentally clean" (2010s)** — Oil from Myanmar and Central Asian fields entered commercial circulation without any indication of the environmental cost of extraction; CNPC positioned these assets as "technologically advanced and responsible".
   *Source: EarthRights International, 2013; CNPC Annual Report, 2015*

3. **"Green" PetroChina filling stations in China** — PetroChina launched a "green" filling station programme with energy-saving equipment and advertising of fuel bio-additives, creating an image of environmental responsibility while maintaining carbon-intensive core activities.
   *Source: PetroChina press releases, 2016–2018*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. CNPC/PetroChina systematically organised symbolic environmental events incomparable with the scale of damage caused.

1. **Tree-planting programme in the Gobi Desert (2010s)** — CNPC annually conducted greening actions near fields, planting thousands of trees. Against the backdrop of annual emissions of hundreds of millions of tonnes of CO₂ and oil spills across Africa and Asia, these actions served exclusively a PR function.
   *Source: CNPC Sustainability Reports, 2014–2018*

2. **"World Environment Day" at production facilities** — CNPC annually organised World Environment Day celebrations with facility clean-ups and school excursions. Meanwhile, operations in Sudan and Myanmar causing large-scale environmental violations continued.
   *Source: CNPC press releases, June 2013–2019*

3. **"Ecology" educational programmes for employees (2015–2020)** — CNPC introduced mandatory online environmental responsibility courses for staff. At the same time, the company created no independent reporting mechanisms capable of identifying violations in the field.
   *Source: CNPC HR reports; CDP Questionnaire responses, 2018*

---

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. CNPC/PetroChina actively promoted the narrative of natural gas as a "climate-neutral" solution and opposed strict international standards for extractive companies.

1. **Lobbying for the "natural gas as a bridge to green energy" concept in Asia** — CNPC funded think tanks and conferences promoting the idea that large-scale natural gas extraction is a necessary "transition" phase towards renewables, effectively postponing decarbonisation by decades.
   *Source: InfluenceMap, "China NOC Climate Lobbying", 2021*

2. **Opposition to mandatory methane emission reporting** — In negotiations on international oil and gas reporting standards (IOGP, UNEP), CNPC opposed mandatory monitoring and disclosure of methane emissions — the most potent short-term greenhouse gas.
   *Source: UNEP Oil & Gas Methane Partnership; Climate Action Tracker, 2020*

3. **Promotion of the "ecological modernisation" narrative to justify extraction in sensitive regions** — CNPC published materials on the "technological superiority" of its extraction methods, justifying operations in ecologically vulnerable regions of Central Asia and Africa, while independent assessments recorded standards below international norms.
   *Source: Global Witness, 2011; CEE Bankwatch, 2019*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Mass harm to human lives?** — **YES** — CNPC's oil revenues financed the Sudanese regime's military budget during the Darfur genocide; Dalian spill (2010) and South Sudan contaminated water sources for hundreds of thousands; Myanmar pipeline construction involved forced village displacement.

2. **Major environmental disasters?** — **YES** — Chronic spills at South Sudan oil fields destroyed Nile wetlands; Dalian accident (2010) affected 430 sq km of bay; Tuohe pipeline rupture (2006) damaged 100 km of river basin.

3. **Greenwashing?** — **YES** — CNPC published "green" reports and issued green bonds while spills continued in Africa; advertised natural gas as "clean fuel" despite rising absolute emissions.

4. **ESG marketing?** — **YES** — PetroChina was included in ESG indices despite violations in conflict zones; ESG investor presentations omitted operations in Sudan and Myanmar.

5. **Wins in ESG competitions?** — **YES** — CNPC received Chinese CSR awards and MSCI "B" rating during periods of documented violations.

6. **Eco-labels on harmful products?** — **YES** — Kunlun Gas network used green branding to promote natural gas; oil from sensitive ecosystems sold as "responsibly extracted".

7. **Negligible eco-events?** — **YES** — Gobi tree planting, "World Environment Days" and corporate eco-tours incommensurate with hundreds of millions of tonnes of CO₂ emissions and ecosystem destruction across four countries.

8. **Lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?** — **YES** — CNPC financed the narrative of gas as a "green bridge"; opposed mandatory methane reporting; promoted "ecological modernisation" concept to justify extraction in vulnerable regions.
