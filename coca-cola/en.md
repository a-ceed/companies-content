According to an analysis conducted by Greenpeace, Coca-Cola produces over 100 billion plastic bottles every year — or 3,400 per second.

«Coca-Cola does not plan to phase out single-use plastic bottles in the near future, as drinks in such packaging are in high demand among consumers," said the company’s vice president, Bea Perez.

By producing a product that has been repeatedly recognized as harmful to health, whose popularity in children’s minds has been cultivated by marketers in the best traditions of consumer society, Coca-Cola is a global leader in plastic pollution.

These are the results of 239 brand audits conducted by Break Free From Plastic and Greenpeace volunteers in 42 countries across six continents.

Coca-Cola Corporation, unable to solve the technical problem related to the safe design of its product’s packaging, with a complete disregard for this design principle and an awareness of the global environmental catastrophe, continues its eco-terrorist business to this day.

«Business won’t be business if we stop focusing on consumer demands," said Bea Perez.

The corporation has already inflicted irreparable damage on nature, witnessed by everyone from inhabitants of oceanic islands to mountain conquerors. There are virtually no places accessible to people where Coca-Cola bottles cannot be found.

Meanwhile, plastic production could double in the next 20 years and quadruple by 2050.

According to research by the Ellen MacArthur Foundation, between 5 to 13 million tons of plastic enter the world’s oceans annually, and by 2050 the ocean will contain more plastic by weight than fish.

Scientists estimate that 90% of seabirds' bodies contain plastic waste.

Scientists from Ghent University in Belgium recently estimated that people who consume seafood ingest up to 11,000 tiny pieces of plastic each year.

Plastic is carcinogenic and, when accumulated, leads to death. Not only fish, but also huge whales die because their stomachs are stuffed with packaging from Coca-Cola, Pepsi, Nestle, Mars, and others.

Coca-Cola has been repeatedly condemned by the scientific community. However, with vast experience in countering various medical and environmental organizations, backed by brilliant marketing talent and substantial capital, the company fights for its existence.

The company aggressively uses greenwashing techniques to convince the public that it is a nature-friendly organization and a leader in eco-innovation.

For several years in a row, Coca-Cola has been recognized as a leader in sustainable development* and almost always ranks among the top three global and European beverage producers.

*Mentioning the concept of sustainable development, it is worth noting that this organization, over its 50-year formation, has failed all its environmental goals and, due to a lack of understanding or unwillingness to see the root causes of the global systemic crisis, will fail them in the future, choosing as its sustainable leader-the planet’s sustainable eco-terrorist.

## 1. Has the corporation’s activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Coca-Cola has for decades promoted high-sugar products despite documented evidence linking them to diabetes and obesity, while systematically depleting water resources in regions where the population depended on those sources for survival.

1. **The diabetes crisis in Mexico (2000s — present)** — Mexico became one of the world’s largest consumers of Coca-Cola (on average more than 700 bottles per person per year). By 2013 the country ranked first in the world for adult obesity. A study published in Public Health Nutrition in 2016 showed a direct correlation between Coca-Cola’s aggressive marketing and the rise of type 2 diabetes. More than 80,000 people die from diabetes in Mexico every year.
   *Source: The Lancet — "Sugar-sweetened beverages and obesity in Mexico"*

2. **Depletion of aquifers in Kerala, India (2003–2004)** — The Coca-Cola plant in Plachimada (Kerala) pumped approximately 1.5 million litres of groundwater per day. This led to the drying up of wells within a radius of several kilometres and left about 2,000 families without drinking water. In 2004 local authorities revoked the plant’s licence. Residents reported illnesses caused by contaminated water from remaining wells.
   *Source: The Guardian — "India’s villagers fight Coca-Cola over water"*

3. **Water contamination in Rajasthan, India (2008)** — The Central Pollution Control Board of India (CPCB) established that the Coca-Cola plant in Kaladera was discharging toxic waste into nearby canals. The local population used these water bodies for drinking and irrigation. Elevated concentrations of cadmium, chromium and lead were detected, causing oncological diseases and damage to the nervous system.
   *Source: India Resource Center — "Coca-Cola and Water in India"*

## 2. Has the corporation been responsible for major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Coca-Cola has been ranked as the world’s largest producer of plastic waste in independent brand audits for several consecutive years, and its plants have repeatedly been caught contaminating water bodies and soil.

1. **Leadership in plastic pollution (2018–2024)** — According to the Break Free From Plastic movement, Coca-Cola topped the list of corporations by volume of branded plastic litter found during clean-ups around the world for six consecutive years (2018–2023). In 2022 Coca-Cola branded plastic was found in 51 of 55 surveyed countries. The company produces more than 3 million tonnes of plastic packaging annually.
   *Source: Break Free From Plastic — "Brand Audit Report 2022"*

2. **Pollution of the Atoyac River in Mexico (1990s — 2010s)** — The Coca-Cola FEMSA plant in Puebla state discharged production effluents into the Atoyac River. According to Mexico’s environmental prosecutor (PROFEPA), the concentration of organic pollutants in the river at certain points exceeded permissible limits by 8–12 times. The river, which once served as an irrigation source for hundreds of farms, was effectively rendered biologically dead for tens of kilometres.
   *Source: PROFEPA — report on the environmental condition of the Atoyac River, 2014*

3. **Destruction of the water balance in Kenya (2016–2020)** — The Coca-Cola plant in the Mawashi area (Nairobi) used millions of litres of water daily from the local water supply amid a chronic water shortage in the region. An investigation by Corporate Accountability showed that the plant’s water consumption during dry periods was equivalent to the water supply of entire city districts.
   *Source: Corporate Accountability — "Coca-Cola’s water footprint in Africa"*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Coca-Cola has systematically declared a transition to sustainable materials and plastic collection, while failing to meet stated targets and continuing to expand production of single-use packaging.

1. **Failure of the "World Without Waste" goal (2018–2025)** — In 2018 Coca-Cola announced the "World Without Waste" programme: to collect and recycle 100% of bottles produced by 2025. By 2023 the company was recycling only about 15% of its own packaging. At the same time, total plastic production grew from 2.9 to 3.2 million tonnes per year. The Earth Island Institute filed a lawsuit, characterising the statements as misleading marketing.
   *Source: Earth Island Institute v. Coca-Cola — complaint, 2021*

2. **Manipulation of the concept of "recycled plastic" (2022)** — Coca-Cola claimed that its bottles contain "up to 50% recycled material," but an independent check by Greenpeace showed that the actual share of recycled rPET in the majority of bottles in Asian and African markets did not exceed 3–7%. The company used averaged figures from select European markets in global advertising.
   *Source: Greenpeace — "Plastic Promises: Coca-Cola’s Recycling Deception"*

3. **Sponsorship of COP26 and single-use bottles (2021)** — Coca-Cola became an official sponsor of the UN climate summit COP26 in Glasgow, positioning itself as a company committed to the climate agenda. At the same time, activists recorded the distribution of thousands of single-use plastic Coca-Cola bottles at the event venue. More than 200 environmental organisations signed an open letter protesting against this sponsorship.
   *Source: Break Free From Plastic — "Open letter on COP26 sponsorship", 2021*

## 4. Does the company participate in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Coca-Cola actively uses the ESG narrative in communications with investors and consumers, publishing sustainability reports that contradict real data on the company’s activities.

1. **Annual ESG reports with unachievable targets (2019–2024)** — Coca-Cola publishes annual sustainability reports including targets for reducing its carbon footprint, transitioning to recycled raw materials and water conservation. Independent auditors from As You Sow have repeatedly pointed to discrepancies between declared targets and actual performance. In 2023 shareholders filed a resolution demanding an independent audit of the company’s climate commitments.
   *Source: As You Sow — "Coca-Cola Sustainability Report Analysis", 2023*

2. **Partnership with WWF as an image tool (2007–present)** — Since 2007 Coca-Cola has positioned its partnership with WWF on the protection of polar bears and water resources as proof of environmental responsibility. Critics note that the partnership’s funding is incomparably small compared to the damage caused by the company’s plastic pollution each year.
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory — "WWF and Coca-Cola: partnership or PR cover?"*

3. **Declarations of "zero emissions" amid a growing carbon footprint (2021–2023)** — In corporate materials Coca-Cola claimed "progress on the path to zero emissions by 2050." However, according to CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project), the company’s total carbon footprint grew in 2022 compared to 2020. Production growth in developing countries with energy-intensive technologies offset the claimed improvements.
   *Source: CDP — "Coca-Cola Climate Disclosure Report", 2022*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG competition nominations?

**Answer: YES**. Coca-Cola has repeatedly received recognition in ESG ratings, which has drawn sharp criticism from environmental organisations as an example of institutional greenwashing.

1. **Dow Jones Sustainability Index (2010–2020s)** — Coca-Cola was included for many years in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI World and DJSI North America), presenting this as confirmation of its ESG responsibility. Inclusion in the index was used in investor marketing materials. Critics from Corporate Accountability noted that the DJSI methodology does not account for real volumes of plastic pollution.
   *Source: Corporate Accountability — "Ratings and Greenwashing", 2020*

2. **Ethisphere "World’s Most Ethical Companies" awards (multiple times)** — Coca-Cola has appeared on the "World’s Most Ethical Companies" lists by the Ethisphere Institute, which was actively covered in corporate press releases. Simultaneously with receiving these awards the company was named in lawsuits over the marketing of sugary drinks to children and over plastic pollution.
   *Source: Ethisphere Institute — award lists; Earth Island Institute — litigation materials*

3. **Recognition in water management (CDP Water, 2019–2022)** — CDP assigned Coca-Cola high scores (A- and A level) for its water use policy. This recognition appeared particularly absurd against the backdrop of ongoing court proceedings in India and Kenya related to the depletion of groundwater by the company’s plants.
   *Source: CDP Water Disclosure — scores 2019–2022*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Coca-Cola has actively labelled products with recycling marks and ecological symbols, misleading consumers about the actual fate of the packaging.

1. **The "Tidyman" symbol and the illusion of responsibility (globally, 1990s — present)** — Coca-Cola bottles worldwide are labelled with the "Tidyman" symbol and the recycling sign. Yet in most countries in Africa, Asia and Latin America where the products are sold, the infrastructure for recycling PET plastic is virtually non-existent. The labelling creates a false sense of eco-friendliness in consumers.
   *Source: Tearfund — "Bottled Up: Coca-Cola, PepsiCo, Nestlé and the plastic pollution crisis", 2019*

2. **"100% recycled" labelling in limited regions with global advertising (2021–2023)** — Coca-Cola launched a line of 100% rPET bottles in the Netherlands and several European countries, but used images of these bottles in global advertising campaigns, creating the impression that all products are made from recycled materials. The share of rPET in the company’s global production in 2022 was approximately 15%.
   *Source: Plastics Europe — "Annual Report 2022"*

3. **The "PlantBottle" programme and misleading claims (2009–2020)** — Coca-Cola aggressively promoted the "plant-based" PlantBottle, made "from plants," while failing to mention that the material was ordinary polyethylene terephthalate (PET), only partially synthesised from biomass. The bottle was not biodegradable and was recycled in the same way as ordinary petrochemical plastic. The US Federal Trade Commission examined the company’s marketing claims for misleading advertising.
   *Source: FTC — Green Guides review materials; Eco-Business — "PlantBottle analysis"*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Coca-Cola regularly conducts and sponsors beach clean-up and tree-planting events whose scale is incomparable with the volumes of pollution produced by the company.

1. **Global "World Without Waste" campaign — beach clean-ups (2018–2023)** — Coca-Cola organised public beach clean-ups in dozens of countries, positioning them as a contribution to fighting plastic pollution. According to calculations by the Story of Stuff organisation, the amount of plastic collected in all such events in a year amounts to less than 0.01% of the plastic released by the company in the same period.
   *Source: Story of Stuff Project — "Corporate cleanup events analysis", 2020*

2. **Tree planting in India (2022–2023)** — Coca-Cola India announced the planting of 100,000 trees as part of an ecosystem restoration initiative. For comparison: the annual carbon footprint of just the Indian division of the company is equivalent to emissions that would require planting tens of millions of trees to offset.
   *Source: Coca-Cola India Sustainability Report 2023; climate calculations by Climate Action Network*

3. **Sponsoring beach "clean-ups" in the Philippines (2019)** — In the Philippines, which is among the top five countries in terms of ocean plastic pollution, Coca-Cola financed several public clean-ups. Yet according to the Philippine Statistics Authority, Coca-Cola packaging was the most frequently found litter on Philippine beaches. The event was widely covered in local media, while the volume of litter collected amounted to just a few tonnes.
   *Source: Break Free From Plastic — "Philippines Brand Audit 2019"*

## 8. Has the company lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories or greenwashing outreach?

**Answer: YES**. Coca-Cola financed scientific research promoting a narrative of "individual responsibility" instead of systemic solutions to the plastic pollution problem, and lobbied against the introduction of deposit return systems for packaging.

1. **Financing the concept of "personal responsibility" for litter (1953 — present)** — Coca-Cola was one of the founding organisations of Keep America Beautiful in 1953. This NGO successfully shifted public discourse from producer responsibility to consumer responsibility, introducing the concept of the "litterbug." Historical documents published by University of California researchers show that the organisation was created specifically to prevent legislation on mandatory deposit systems for packaging.
   *Source: University of California — "The history of Keep America Beautiful", Heather Rogers, 2005*

2. **Lobbying against bottle deposit return systems (2010s — 2020s)** — Coca-Cola, together with other beverage producers, actively lobbied against the expansion of bottle deposit programmes in the USA and Australia. In California the company financed a coalition against SB 54 — a bill on extended producer responsibility for plastic. According to OpenSecrets, Coca-Cola spent millions of dollars lobbying in the US Congress against plastic legislation.
   *Source: OpenSecrets — Coca-Cola lobbying expenditures; CalMatters — "Plastic bottle fight in California"*

3. **Financing pseudo-scientific research on the role of sugar in obesity (2015)** — JAMA Internal Medicine published an investigation showing that Coca-Cola had since the 1960s funded scientific research that shifted attention away from sugary beverages toward lack of physical activity as the main cause of obesity. The company spent millions of dollars on the organisation "Global Energy Balance Network," which promoted the idea that fighting obesity requires more exercise rather than dietary change.
   *Source: JAMA Internal Medicine — "Coca-Cola’s Funding of Obesity Research", 2015*
