Dow Chemical: From Bhopal to PFAS

Dow Chemical absorbed Union Carbide — the company responsible for the Bhopal disaster (India, 1984), where a methyl isocyanate leak killed between 15,000 and 25,000 people and damaged the health of more than 500,000. The factory site has still not been remediated, and groundwater remains contaminated.

In Midland, Michigan, Dow discharged dioxins into the Tittabawassee River for more than 60 years. Studies found dioxin concentrations in the blood of riverside residents several times above normal. In 2012, the EPA required Dow to carry out a costly clean-up of a 50-mile stretch of the river.

In West Virginia, the DuPont plant (which later merged with Dow) discharged PFOA into the Ohio River. This became the basis of a high-profile lawsuit that inspired the film Dark Waters (2019). Dow/DuPont settled thousands of individual claims, paying hundreds of millions of dollars.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. The activities of Dow and DuPont have documentably led to the deaths and serious health damage of tens of thousands of people worldwide.

1. **Bhopal disaster (1984)** — The Union Carbide plant (later acquired by Dow) released more than 40 tonnes of methyl isocyanate. Estimates suggest between 15,000 and 25,000 people died, and more than 500,000 suffered long-term health damage: blindness, lung damage and cancer.
   *Source: Bhopal Medical Appeal, Amnesty International*

2. **PFOA in Parkersburg, West Virginia ("Dark Waters", 1951–2001)** — The DuPont Washington Works plant discharged PFOA into the Ohio River and pumped it into unprotected landfills. An independent scientific panel established eight diseases reliably linked to PFOA: kidney cancer, testicular cancer, ulcerative colitis, thyroid disease, and others.
   *Source: Dark Waters (film 2019), C8 Science Panel*

3. **Dioxins in Midland, Michigan (1940s–2000s)** — Dow discharged dioxins into the Tittabawassee River for more than 60 years. Residents of riverside areas had dioxin concentrations in their blood 3–5 times above normal. Dioxins are linked to cancer, immune disorders and endocrine disruption.
   *Source: EPA Tittabawassee River Site, MDCH studies*

## 2. Has the corporation systematically violated environmental law?

**Answer: YES**. Dow and DuPont received dozens of enforcement orders and fines from the EPA and other regulators.

1. **Dioxin Consent Decree in Michigan (2012)** — The EPA required Dow Chemical to sign an agreement for mandatory clean-up of a 50-mile stretch of the Tittabawassee River at a cost exceeding $100 million. This was preceded by years of dioxin standard violations.
   *Source: EPA Consent Decree, 2012*

2. **Clean Water Act violations in West Virginia** — DuPont violated its Ohio River discharge permits for decades, failing to declare PFOA as a discharged substance since it was not officially included in the list of regulated substances.
   *Source: C8 litigation documents, EPA*

3. **Agent Orange discharge fines (Vietnam era)** — Dow Chemical manufactured the defoliant Agent Orange for the US military, which contained the dioxin TCDD. Despite awareness of its toxicity, the company did not disclose information to regulators.
   *Source: Veterans Administration, US court records*

---

## 3. Has the corporation concealed information about environmental damage?

**Answer: YES**. Internal DuPont documents revealed in litigation showed years of concealment of PFOA toxicity data.

1. **Internal DuPont memoranda on PFOA (1961–2001)** — Documents presented in the Dark Waters case showed that DuPont had been recording toxic effects of PFOA in animals since 1961, and since the 1980s had data on its detection in the blood of employees and residents. Instead of notifying the EPA, the company classified the results.
   *Source: Dark Waters litigation, New York Times*

2. **Dow's concealment of dioxin data** — Dow's internal 1965 studies showed that chlorophenols (dioxin precursors) are extremely toxic. Company management discussed the need to "control" public data on dioxins, according to litigation materials.
   *Source: EPA docket, declassified Dow memos*

3. **Bhopal: concealment of emission composition** — Immediately after the disaster, Union Carbide provided Indian authorities with incomplete information about the composition of the gas cloud, which delayed the provision of medical assistance to those affected.
   *Source: Bhopal Medical Appeal, Indian court records*

---

## 4. Has the corporation exerted political pressure to weaken environmental standards?

**Answer: YES**. Dow and DuPont are among the most active chemical industry lobbyists in the USA and EU.

1. **Lobbying to delay PFOA regulation (1990s–2000s)** — DuPont for a decade obstructed the inclusion of PFOA in the EPA's list of regulated pollutants, funding "independent" studies and pushing for a voluntary rather than mandatory agreement.
   *Source: EPA PFOA Stewardship Program history, EWG*

2. **Agent Orange: opposition to acknowledging harm** — Dow Chemical funded research for decades that disputed the link between Agent Orange and Vietnam veteran illnesses, delaying compensation.
   *Source: US Senate Veterans Affairs Committee*

3. **Lobbying against pesticide regulation** — After the DowDuPont merger, the company through CropLife America actively opposed tightening restrictions on chlorpyrifos and other pesticides for which the EPA had established neurotoxicity risks in children.
   *Source: NRDC, EPA chlorpyrifos record*

## 5. Has the corporation's activity caused long-term damage to ecosystems?

**Answer: YES**. Contamination by dioxins, PFAS and Agent Orange has caused irreversible damage to a number of ecosystems.

1. **Tittabawassee River: dioxin contamination of sediments** — Despite the clean-up begun in 2012, dioxins in river and floodplain sediments will persist for decades. A ban on consuming fish from the river was in effect for more than 20 years.
   *Source: EPA Tittabawassee Site assessment*

2. **PFOA in the West Virginia aquifer** — PFOA that accumulated in the Ohio River Valley is found in the drinking water of communities on both sides of the river. The half-life of PFOA in the environment is measured in hundreds of years.
   *Source: C8 Science Panel, EWG*

3. **Vietnamese ecosystems and Agent Orange** — The dioxin TCDD in Agent Orange contaminated soils and water bodies in application zones. Dioxin "hot spots" (especially near airbases) persist in Vietnam to this day.
   *Source: US-Vietnam Dialogue Group, UNDP*

---

## 6. Has the corporation evaded accountability and compensation for those harmed?

**Answer: YES**. Evasion of accountability — primarily in the Bhopal case — is one of the most documented examples of corporate impunity.

1. **Bhopal: refusal of full compensation** — Union Carbide (then Dow) paid only $470 million in compensation in 1989 — a sum recognised by victims and human rights advocates as catastrophically inadequate. Civil and criminal lawsuits in India have been effectively blocked. Dow to this day denies responsibility for Union Carbide's actions.
   *Source: Bhopal Medical Appeal, Amnesty International*

2. **PFOA: 30 years of avoiding payments** — DuPont settled claims from West Virginia residents only in 2017, paying $671 million, after internal documents about data concealment were presented in court. Before that, the company denied harm for 30 years.
   *Source: Dark Waters litigation, Reuters*

3. **Split into Corteva and Chemours to limit liability** — Before the Dow merger, DuPont spun off into Chemours (2015) its most "toxic" assets, including PFAS production, which rights advocates characterised as an attempt to protect DowDuPont from future claims.
   *Source: Bloomberg, EWG analysis*

---

## 7. Has the corporation continued environmentally harmful activities despite known risks?

**Answer: YES**. Dow (now Dow Inc.) continues manufacturing several hazardous chemicals and is expanding petrochemical capacity.

1. **Expansion of petrochemical capacity in Texas** — Dow is investing in expansion of its petrochemical complex on the Gulf Coast, including areas already experiencing significant environmental pressure.
   *Source: Dow Annual Report 2022*

2. **Continued production of chlorpyrifos** — Despite the EPA ban in the USA (2021), Dow through Corteva continued exporting chlorpyrifos to countries with less stringent standards, where this neurotoxic pesticide is used on food crops.
   *Source: NRDC, HRW Report 2020*

3. **Plastics production without transition to circular economy** — Dow is the world's largest polyethylene producer — the basis of ocean plastic pollution. The company declares recycling targets, but volumes of virgin plastic production continue to grow.
   *Source: Dow Sustainability Report, Break Free From Plastic*

---

## 8. Is the corporation complicit in human rights violations or environmental racism?

**Answer: YES**. Bhopal remains a symbol of corporate disregard for human lives in poor countries.

1. **Bhopal as environmental racism** — Safety standards at the Union Carbide plant in Bhopal were significantly lower than at comparable company facilities in the USA. The victims — poor residents of India — received incomparably less compensation than would have been awarded to those affected in a similar disaster in a developed country.
   *Source: Amnesty International, Bhopal Medical Appeal*

2. **PFOA contamination in working-class communities** — The Parkersburg residents affected by PFOA were predominantly working class. DuPont for decades was the region's largest employer, creating economic dependence that made fighting the contamination difficult.
   *Source: Dark Waters, New Yorker*

3. **Agent Orange use in Vietnam** — The manufacture of Agent Orange for use in war is one of the most large-scale cases of the use of an industrial toxin as a weapon against a civilian population in the 20th century. Vietnamese victims received no compensation from American chemical companies.
   *Source: Vietnam Agent Orange Relief & Responsibility Campaign*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Mass harm to human lives?** — **YES** — Bhopal (15,000–25,000 killed); PFOA in West Virginia; dioxins in Michigan.

2. **Systematic environmental law violations?** — **YES** — Dioxin Consent Decree (2012); Clean Water Act violations; Agent Orange.

3. **Concealment of environmental damage?** — **YES** — 40 years of PFOA data concealment; classified dioxin memoranda; disinformation after Bhopal.

4. **Political pressure to weaken environmental standards?** — **YES** — Delayed PFOA regulation; lobbying against chlorpyrifos standards; funding Agent Orange research.

5. **Long-term ecosystem damage?** — **YES** — Dioxins in the Tittabawassee River; PFOA in the aquifer; dioxin hot spots in Vietnam.

6. **Evasion of accountability and compensation?** — **YES** — Bhopal: $470 million against real damage in the tens of billions; PFOA: 30 years of denial; Chemours spin-off to shield against claims.

7. **Continued environmentally harmful activities despite known risks?** — **YES** — Petrochemical expansion in Texas; chlorpyrifos export; increased plastics production.

8. **Complicity in human rights violations or environmental racism?** — **YES** — Double safety standards in Bhopal; working-class West Virginia communities; Vietnamese Agent Orange victims without compensation.
