Duke Energy: Coal Ash and Poisoned Rivers

In February 2014, a pipe burst at Duke Energy's power station in Eden, North Carolina, releasing approximately 80,000 tonnes of coal ash and 27 million gallons of contaminated water into the Dan River. This was the largest coal ash spill in US history.

Coal ash contains arsenic, mercury, chromium, cadmium and other toxic metals. According to the EPA, Duke Energy managed hundreds of ash ponds without adequate lining, allowing chronic groundwater contamination.

In 2015, Duke Energy pleaded guilty to criminal violations of environmental law and paid $102 million in fines — the largest criminal penalty in Clean Water Act history at that time. By 2029, the company is required to close and remediate all coal ash ponds in North Carolina.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Duke Energy for decades contaminated drinking water sources with coal ash containing arsenic, mercury and other toxic metals, endangering the health of hundreds of thousands of Carolina residents.

1. **Groundwater contamination at 14 coal stations (1980s–2010s)** — The EPA and North and South Carolina authorities documented groundwater contamination with toxic metals — arsenic, chromium, cadmium, boron — at 14 Duke Energy coal power stations. Residents of nearby areas using well water were subject to chronic exposure to these substances.
   *Source: EPA Coal Ash Contamination Report, 2018; NC DEQ Groundwater Monitoring, 2017*

2. **Dan River spill (2014)** — A pipe burst beneath an ash pond at the Cliffside plant in Eden, North Carolina, releasing approximately 39,000 tonnes of ash and 27 million gallons of contaminated water into the Dan River. The Dan River is a drinking water source for Pittsylvania County (Virginia); authorities were forced to shut down water intakes.
   *Source: EPA, February 2014; USGS Water Quality Report, 2014; Washington Post, 2014*

3. **Elevated arsenic in wells near Allen Station (North Carolina, 2019)** — After protracted legal proceedings, it was established that coal ash from Allen Station's ponds had migrated into groundwater used by local residents. Duke Energy denied the contamination until a court ruling was issued.
   *Source: NC DEQ v. Duke Energy, 2019; Charlotte Observer, 2019*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Coal ash spills and chronic water body contamination made Duke Energy one of the most environmentally dangerous energy companies in the USA.

1. **Dan River spill — largest in US history (2014)** — 39,000 tonnes of coal ash containing heavy metals covered the riverbed over tens of kilometres. Ecological rehabilitation took several years; some contaminated sediments remained in the river years after the accident.
   *Source: Duke University Nicholas School; EPA Emergency Response, 2014*

2. **Contamination of 14 state rivers and reservoirs** — According to the Southern Environmental Law Center, Duke Energy's coal ash ponds in North Carolina were the source of chronic contamination of at least 14 water bodies. Among them is Lake Hyco (a water source for Charlotte), where elevated selenium levels were recorded.
   *Source: Southern Environmental Law Center, 2019; NC DEQ*

3. **Coal ash industry systemic risk** — While the Kingston ash spill in Tennessee (2008) was operated by TVA, the accident clearly demonstrated industry-wide risks. Duke Energy, under pressure from similar lawsuits, ultimately pleaded guilty in 2015, reflecting similar ash storage conditions across the sector.
   *Source: EPA Report on Coal Ash Impoundment Risks, 2015*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Duke Energy publicly declared a "green transition" while simultaneously delaying coal station closures and contesting demands for ash pond remediation.

1. **"Clean energy" advertising while maintaining coal dependence (2010s)** — Duke Energy launched expensive advertising campaigns about "the future of clean energy" while remaining the largest source of CO₂ emissions in US electricity generation; the transition to renewables was systematically postponed under shareholder pressure.
   *Source: Sierra Club "Beyond Coal" campaign reports; Greenpeace USA, 2016*

2. **"Green tariffs" as a tool for delaying real transition** — Duke Energy introduced "green tariff" programmes for corporate clients allowing them to "purchase" renewable energy. Critics noted these schemes did not incentivise construction of new renewable capacity but merely redistributed existing green generation.
   *Source: Rocky Mountain Institute, 2018; NRDC, 2019*

3. **Positioning natural gas as a "climate solution" (2015–2022)** — After the coal ash scandals, Duke Energy actively promoted natural gas as a "cleaner" alternative, investing in gas infrastructure under slogans of "emission reduction", despite scientific data on methane emissions from gas extraction.
   *Source: Duke Energy Integrated Resource Plans, 2017–2020; Earthjustice, 2020*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Duke Energy publicly exploits the ESG agenda, presenting itself as a sustainability leader in the energy sector while ranking at the bottom of real environmental ratings.

1. **Inclusion in Dow Jones Sustainability Index (2010s)** — Duke Energy repeatedly appeared in DJSI North America, positioning this as confirmation of environmental responsibility. Environmental organisations noted that inclusion in the index contradicted the company's actual practices.
   *Source: DJSI Reports; Sierra Club, 2015*

2. **ESG reports emphasising "progress" while absolute emissions grew** — Duke Energy's annual sustainability reports demonstrated declining "emission intensity" per unit of energy, while concealing the fact that absolute CO₂ emissions remained among the highest in the sector.
   *Source: Duke Energy Sustainability Reports, 2018–2021; Carbon Tracker*

3. **Public carbon neutrality commitments without binding mechanisms (2019)** — Duke Energy announced a "net zero by 2050" target, but the plan contained no binding interim commitments, funding or enforcement mechanisms. Independent analysts characterised the declaration as greenwashing.
   *Source: Climate Action Tracker; Rocky Mountain Institute, 2020*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. Despite criminal liability and large-scale contamination, Duke Energy received certain ESG recognition.

1. **EEI Emergency Response Award (on multiple occasions)** — Duke Energy received Edison Electric Institute industry awards for emergency response — used in corporate communications as evidence of "responsibility", despite parallel environmental violations.
   *Source: EEI Award Database; Duke Energy press releases*

2. **CDP climate rating "A-" (select years)** — Duke Energy received high scores in CDP (formerly Carbon Disclosure Project) surveys on certain transparency indicators, enabling major institutional investors to include shares in ESG portfolios.
   *Source: CDP Scores Database; Institutional Shareholder Services*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Duke Energy promoted its energy products and programmes with "green" labelling concealing their real environmental impact.

1. **"Green Source Advantage" programme — selling "green" energy to corporate clients** — Duke Energy actively sold "renewable energy" packages to corporate consumers at premium rates, while its actual generation mix remained predominantly coal and gas.
   *Source: Duke Energy commercial rates filings; NRDC, 2018*

2. **"Clean coal" in company communications (2000s–2010s)** — Duke Energy used the term "clean coal" in advertising materials and official statements, despite the absence of carbon capture technology at most of its stations.
   *Source: Duke Energy annual reports, 2008–2012; Environmental Defense Fund*

3. **Labelling of gas tariffs as "more ecological"** — After closing several coal stations, Duke Energy promoted natural gas as "environmentally clean" fuel without disclosing methane emission data across the supply chain.
   *Source: Duke Energy customer communications, 2018–2021*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Duke Energy regularly conducted symbolic environmental initiatives incomparable with the scale of damage caused.

1. **Tree planting in the Carolinas** — Duke Energy organised annual greening events with local schools, planting several thousand trees annually. Against the backdrop of 100+ million tonnes of CO₂ per year and contamination of 14 water bodies with ash, these events were symbolic in significance.
   *Source: Duke Energy community reports, 2015–2019*

2. **"Environmental education" programmes at plant visitor centres** — Duke Energy maintained visitor centres with environmental exhibits at several power stations, offering educational programmes for schoolchildren about "the importance of energy and nature".
   *Source: Duke Energy Foundation Annual Report, 2017*

3. **Funding local parks and "green spaces"** — Duke Energy donated funds for the development of local parks in several communities, positioning this as a contribution to "ecological wellbeing" — in the same counties where groundwater contamination continued.
   *Source: Duke Energy Foundation grants database, 2016–2020*

---

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Duke Energy actively lobbied against strict climate and environmental standards, promoting narratives that delayed real energy transition.

1. **Lobbying against the EPA federal coal ash rule (2010–2015)** — Duke Energy and industry associations actively opposed the introduction of a federal standard for safe coal ash disposal. The final EPA rules of 2015 were adopted only after major accidents (Kingston, Dan River) and years of legal pressure.
   *Source: EPA regulatory docket; Earthjustice, 2014*

2. **Funding climate-sceptic organisations** — Duke Energy was among the donors to ALEC (American Legislative Exchange Council) — an organisation distributing model legislation against renewable energy standards and climate regulation in state legislatures.
   *Source: InfluenceMap; CMD (Center for Media and Democracy), 2013*

3. **Opposition to renewable energy standards in North Carolina** — Duke Energy lobbied to weaken and repeal North Carolina's Renewable Energy Portfolio Standard (REPS), seeking to reduce mandatory renewable energy quotas. Lobbying was conducted through ALEC and industry associations.
   *Source: NC Policy Watch, 2013; Southern Alliance for Clean Energy*
