ExxonMobil and Environmental Catastrophes

ExxonMobil — the world's largest private oil company — is implicated in some of the most high-profile environmental scandals of the 20th–21st centuries. In 1989, the tanker Exxon Valdez ran aground off the Alaskan coast, spilling approximately 41,000 tonnes of crude oil and destroying more than 2,000 km of coastline. More than 250,000 seabirds, 2,800 sea otters, thousands of seals and killer whales perished. Ecosystem recovery took decades and is still not complete.

In Nigeria, subsidiaries of the company are linked to chronic spills in the Niger Delta: more than 7,000 incidents have been recorded in the region since the 1960s, turning fertile lands into toxic wastelands. Local residents have lost access to fishing grounds and drinking water.

ExxonMobil's internal documents, published in 2015, attracted particular attention: they proved that the company had known since the 1970s about the role of fossil fuels in climate change, but funded disinformation campaigns totalling more than $30 million. In 2022, US prosecutors opened an investigation into misleading investors about climate risks.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. ExxonMobil's operations across several countries have led to documented deaths, chronic population poisoning and mass illness.

1. **Exxon Valdez disaster (1989)** — The tanker ran aground off the Alaskan coast, spilling 41,000 tonnes of oil. The traditional fishing grounds of the Alutiiq and Athabascan peoples were destroyed; studies showed long-term increases in mental disorders, depression and alcohol abuse among affected communities. Thousands of fishermen were financially ruined; the way of life of entire communities was irreversibly destroyed.
   *Source: NOAA — "Exxon Valdez Oil Spill: 1989–2014"*

2. **Niger Delta contamination (1990s–present)** — ExxonMobil subsidiary Mobil Producing Nigeria recorded hundreds of spills; epidemiological studies found elevated rates of skin cancer, kidney and respiratory diseases among local populations. Drinking water in villages contains petroleum hydrocarbons exceeding WHO standards by dozens of times.
   *Source: Amnesty International — "Negligence in the Niger Delta" (2018)*

3. **Torture and killings in Indonesia (2001)** — Human rights organisation EarthRights International filed a lawsuit in the USA against ExxonMobil, accusing the company of employing Indonesian military personnel who used torture, killings and rape to guard the Arun oil and gas project in Aceh Province. The court accepted the lawsuit, acknowledging the possible application of the ATCA statute.
   *Source: EarthRights International — "ExxonMobil in Aceh" (2001–2016)*

4. **Torrance, California refinery explosion (2015)** — An explosion at ExxonMobil's plant released a cloud of toxic particles, forcing residents to shelter indoors. Four workers were injured. A Cal/OSHA investigation found serious safety violations; the company paid a fine of $566,000.
   *Source: Cal/OSHA — Investigation Report, Torrance Refinery (2015)*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. ExxonMobil is linked to several environmental catastrophes of planetary scale, each documented by regulators, courts and independent organisations.

1. **Exxon Valdez (1989)** — The spill destroyed more than 2,000 km of coastline. 250,000 seabirds, 2,800 sea otters, 300 seals, 250 bald eagles and 22 killer whales perished. Traces of oil are found in sedimentary deposits 35 years later.
   *Source: U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service — "Exxon Valdez Oil Spill Restoration Program"*

2. **Chronic spills in the Niger Delta (1969–present)** — In 2012, a rupture of the MPN pipeline near the town of Essen flooded 7,000 hectares of rice paddies and fishing grounds. Regulator NOSDRA found that the pipeline had long required replacement. ExxonMobil contested liability, blaming "vandals".
   *Source: Nigeria's NOSDRA — Spill Reports 2012; National Geographic, 2013*

3. **Yellowstone River spill, Montana (2011)** — The ExxonMobil Silvertip pipeline crossing under the Yellowstone River burst during a flood; 1,500 barrels of oil contaminated the river and riparian areas. Oil penetrated farmers' water intakes. Montana's emergency management authority declared a state of emergency.
   *Source: EPA Emergency Response — Yellowstone River Pipeline Spill (2011)*

4. **Gas flaring in the Permian Basin (2010s–present)** — NASA satellite data shows that ExxonMobil subsidiaries annually flare billions of cubic feet of associated gas in Texas and New Mexico. In 2022, the EPA reported that ExxonMobil is among the largest sources of methane emissions in the USA.
   *Source: NASA/JPL EMIT — Methane Mapping Report (2022); EPA GHGRP*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. ExxonMobil is one of the most documentarily confirmed cases of corporate greenwashing in history: the company's internal documents showed a radical divergence between its public position and actual actions.

1. **"Exxon Knew" — internal climate research (1977–1986)** — Journalists from InsideClimate News and Columbia University published internal ExxonMobil documents showing that the company funded cutting-edge climate research and in 1982 had accurate CO₂ growth models — then classified the results and began funding climate scepticism. An internal 1978 memorandum explicitly warned of catastrophic warming consequences.
   *Source: InsideClimate News — "Exxon: The Road Not Taken" (2015)*

2. **Funding climate deniers (1998–2014)** — ExxonMobil channelled more than $30 million to organisations spreading climate disinformation: the Heartland Institute, Global Climate Coalition, American Legislative Exchange Council. This was established by the New York Attorney General during the 2015–2019 investigation.
   *Source: Attorney General of New York — ExxonMobil Investigation (2019)*

3. **"Low-carbon future" advertising while increasing production (2019–2023)** — ExxonMobil placed advertising materials in the Wall Street Journal and Financial Times positioning the company as a leader in "clean energy" and carbon capture, while simultaneously increasing capital investment in oil and gas. In 2023, ClientEarth filed a complaint about misleading advertising with the SEC.
   *Source: ClientEarth — Complaint to SEC re ExxonMobil Advertising (2023)*

4. **Exaggerating the role of algae biofuel (2009–2013)** — ExxonMobil invested $600 million in algae biofuel research and actively advertised this direction. Independent experts noted that the scale of real investment was incomparable with the marketing reach of the campaign; by 2013, funding was sharply cut.
   *Source: Bloomberg — "Exxon Algae Bet Was Never What It Seemed" (2013)*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. ExxonMobil actively uses ESG rhetoric, while its real environmental, social and governance indicators remain at the bottom of sector rankings.

1. **Sustainability report and "Net Zero to 2050" (2022)** — ExxonMobil published plans to achieve net-zero Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 2050, actively promoting them in the media. However, the plan does not include Scope 3 emissions (from oil combustion by consumers) — approximately 85% of the total climate footprint of the company's products. Climate analysts at Reclaim Finance characterised the plan as inadequate.
   *Source: Reclaim Finance — "ExxonMobil Climate Analysis" (2022)*

2. **Membership in "responsible business" initiatives while simultaneously lobbying** — ExxonMobil participates in UN climate partnerships and publicly supports the Paris Agreement, while simultaneously funding opposition to federal climate regulations through the American Petroleum Institute (API). This contradiction was documented by InfluenceMap in 2022.
   *Source: InfluenceMap — "Big Oil Reality Check" (2022)*

3. **Promoting CCS as an alternative to reducing production** — The company invests in carbon capture and storage (CCS) technologies and actively positions them in ESG communications as a systemic solution to the climate crisis. Critics, including the IEA, note that at existing deployment rates, CCS cannot compensate for the scale of oil industry emissions.
   *Source: IEA — "The Role of CCUS in Low-Carbon Power Systems" (2020)*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. Despite a reputation as one of the largest polluters, ExxonMobil periodically received ESG ratings and awards from commercial agencies.

1. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI) (2019)** — ExxonMobil was included in DJSI North America, which the company used in press releases as confirmation of its "sustainability". The inclusion drew criticism from activist investors who pointed to the methodology's inconsistency with the company's real climate risks.
   *Source: S&P Global / DJSI — Annual Review 2019; Reuters*

2. **MSCI ESG rating "BBB" (2021)** — MSCI assigned ExxonMobil a BBB ESG rating, above the sector average. Rights advocates pointed out that MSCI's methodology does not account for historical oil spills and the proven funding of climate denial, and the company publicly cited this rating.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings — ExxonMobil Profile (2021)*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. ExxonMobil applied green labelling and eco-rhetoric to products whose production is associated with documented environmental damage.

1. **Labelling Mobil 1 motor oils as "environmentally efficient" (2010s)** — Mobil 1 was positioned as an "environmentally optimal" lubricant with improved fuel economy characteristics, implying a reduced carbon footprint. Independent lifecycle assessments showed that fuel savings when using the product are negligible compared to the impact of its production and disposal.
   *Source: European Lubricants Industry Report (2017); Consumer Reports*

2. **Promoting Esso fuel "with emission-reducing additives" (2000s–2010s)** — Esso Supreme fuel was positioned as "clean", reducing emissions and engine contamination. UK advertising standards regulators (ASA) found in 2008 that certain ExxonMobil advertising claims about "clean" fuel were misleading.
   *Source: ASA (UK Advertising Standards Authority) — Ruling re Esso fuel advertising (2008)*

3. **Marketing natural gas as "clean energy" (2015–2023)** — ExxonMobil systematically positioned natural gas as a "clean" transitional fuel in advertising materials and industry publications. EPA data and independent researchers show that methane leaks during extraction and transport make the climate footprint of gas comparable to coal over a 20-year horizon.
   *Source: EPA — Methane Challenge Program; Science (journal) — "Fossil fuel methane emissions" (2018)*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. ExxonMobil implements environmental programmes whose scale is incomparable with the damage from its core business.

1. **Tiger protection programme "Tigers Alive" (2009–present)** — ExxonMobil sponsors the conservation of Amur and Malayan tigers jointly with WWF, having allocated several million dollars over 15 years. Meanwhile, the company's annual revenue exceeds $300 billion, and operations in Malaysia and other tiger-range regions continue to destroy predator habitat through deforestation and oil extraction.
   *Source: ExxonMobil Corporate Citizenship Report (2022); WWF Tigers Alive*

2. **Sea turtle conservation programme in Papua New Guinea (2010s)** — As part of the PNG LNG project, ExxonMobil announced a leatherback sea turtle nesting monitoring programme costing several hundred thousand dollars — alongside a $19 billion extraction project that altered the hydrology of coastal areas.
   *Source: ExxonMobil PNG LNG Environmental Report (2015)*

3. **Earth Day coastline clean-ups (2019–2023)** — ExxonMobil organises annual volunteer coastline clean-ups in the USA as part of corporate volunteering, publishing reports about "tonnes of collected waste". Environmentalists noted the obvious disproportion between the scale of these events and the volume of contamination from the company's production facilities.
   *Source: ExxonMobil Corporate Volunteerism Reports (2019–2023)*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. ExxonMobil is one of the most documented corporate actors in the history of spreading climate disinformation and lobbying against climate policy.

1. **Funding the Global Climate Coalition (1989–2002)** — ExxonMobil was a key member and sponsor of the Global Climate Coalition — an industry lobbying group that deliberately blocked international climate negotiations, including the Kyoto Protocol. Internal coalition documents that became public showed that its members knew about the credibility of climate science but publicly denied it.
   *Source: The Guardian — "Revealed: how the fossil fuel industry blocked climate action for 50 years" (2022)*

2. **Creating a network of pseudo-scientific "think tanks" (1998–2006)** — According to the Union of Concerned Scientists, ExxonMobil funded the creation or activities of more than 40 organisations specialising in spreading climate scepticism: the Competitive Enterprise Institute, George C. Marshall Institute, Tech Central Station and others.
   *Source: Union of Concerned Scientists — "Smoke, Mirrors & Hot Air" (2007)*

3. **Educational programmes for schools with a petroleum bias (2000s–2010s)** — ExxonMobil funded educational materials for American schools through programmes like "Taking Energy Into Our Own Hands", promoting ideas about energy security and a "balanced approach" to fossil fuels without mentioning climate risks. NRDC critics characterised this as oil dependence propaganda disguised as education.
   *Source: NRDC — "Big Oil's Classroom Takeover" (2021)*

4. **Lobbying against the Paris Agreement (2016–2017)** — Despite publicly declaring support for the Paris Agreement, ExxonMobil supported the US withdrawal from the agreement in 2017 through the API and direct contacts with the Trump administration. This was documented in the House Select Committee on the Climate Crisis investigation (2022).
   *Source: House Select Committee on the Climate Crisis — "Fossil Fuel Industry Playbook" (2022)*
