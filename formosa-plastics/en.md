Formosa Plastics: "Plastic Snowfall" in Texas

Formosa Plastics — one of the world's largest producers of polyvinyl chloride (PVC) and petrochemicals — was subjected to one of the largest fines in American environmental history.

In 2019, environmental activist Diane Wilson and the San Antonio Bay Estuarine Waterkeeper won a court case proving that Formosa's plant in Point Comfort, Texas had been illegally discharging billions of plastic pellets, chips and powders into the bay for 7 years. Formosa paid a record $50 million — the largest settlement with a private company under the Clean Water Act at the time.

In Vietnam, the Formosa plant in Ha Tinh in 2016 caused one of the largest industrial discharges in Southeast Asian history: discharges of toxic effluent killed fish along 200 km of coastline across four provinces. Formosa paid $500 million in compensation to the Vietnamese government.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Formosa Plastics' production facilities systematically poisoned water bodies and coastal ecosystems, depriving fishing communities of their livelihoods and exposing populations to carcinogens.

1. **Ha Tinh disaster, Vietnam (2016)** — The Formosa metallurgical plant in Ha Tinh discharged a mixture of phenol, cyanide and formaldehyde into the sea via an underwater pipeline. Fish died along 200 km of coastline across four provinces. Approximately 200,000 fishermen and their family members lost their source of income; hundreds suffered poisoning.
   *Source: Vietnamese Ministry of Natural Resources and Environment, 2016; Human Rights Watch, 2016*

2. **Plastic pellet discharge into Point Comfort Bay, Texas (2012–2019)** — The Formosa plant in Texas illegally discharged billions of plastic pellets and petrochemical particles into the bay for 7 years. Local fishermen and shellfish harvesters from low-income populations suffered direct economic damage; plastic accumulated in the tissues of marine animals used for food.
   *Source: San Antonio Bay Estuarine Waterkeeper v. Formosa Plastics, US District Court, 2019*

3. **River contamination in Taiwan (chronic)** — Systematic discharges of vinyl chloride and petrochemical substances into rivers were documented at Formosa's Taiwanese plants. Studies showed elevated rates of liver cancer among populations near production facilities.
   *Source: Taiwan Environmental Protection Administration; Taiwanese academic epidemiological studies, 2000s*

---

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Environmental catastrophes linked to Formosa have destroyed marine ecosystems across vast territories in several countries.

1. **200 km of dead coastline in Vietnam (2016)** — The Ha Tinh plant's toxic discharge destroyed coral reefs, seagrass meadows and fish populations along a 200 km stretch of central Vietnam's coastline. Vietnamese scientists estimate ecosystem restoration will take decades.
   *Source: Vietnam Academy of Sciences; WWF Vietnam, 2016*

2. **Plastic contamination of Matagorda Bay estuary (Texas, 2012–2019)** — Billions of plastic pellets (nurdles) from the Point Comfort plant littered the beaches and seabed of Matagorda Bay. Nurdles accumulate in the bodies of fish, oysters and seabirds; the contamination is persistent and continues for decades.
   *Source: San Antonio Bay Estuarine Waterkeeper court filings, 2019; NOAA*

3. **Petrochemical plant accident in Ilcheong, Taiwan (2010s)** — Several emergency discharges at Formosa Chemicals' Taiwanese plants led to vinyl chloride and other chlorinated organic compounds entering rivers, creating long-term toxic pressure on aquatic ecosystems.
   *Source: Taiwan Environmental Protection Administration; Environmental Justice Atlas*

---

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. After the Vietnam disaster, Formosa Plastics used public commitments and compensation payments as a reputation management tool without changing systemic practices.

1. **"Sustainable development" in reports while chronic violations continued** — After 2016, Formosa Plastics Group published reports on responsible production, declaring commitment to environmental standards. During this period, Taiwanese plants continued to receive regulatory enforcement orders for discharge standard violations.
   *Source: Taiwan EPA enforcement records, 2017–2020*

2. **Rebranding and PR campaign after the Vietnam disaster** — Formosa Ha Tinh Steel conducted a series of PR events in Vietnam, including tree planting and donations to fishing communities, creating an image of a "responsible corporate citizen" without real structural changes to technological processes.
   *Source: Vietnamese media monitoring; Human Rights Watch, 2017*

3. **Declaration of commitment to "circular economy" while increasing PVC production** — Formosa Plastics positions itself as a circular economy participant, advertising recycling programmes, while being one of the world's largest producers of virgin PVC — the primary source of plastic pollution.
   *Source: Formosa Plastics corporate communications, 2019–2022; Break Free From Plastic*

---

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Formosa Plastics Group uses ESG rhetoric to access international capital markets without disclosing the full scope of environmental violations.

1. **Inclusion in Taiwanese ESG indices** — Despite the Vietnam disaster and Texas violations, the Formosa Group maintained a presence in certain Taiwanese corporate sustainability ratings. Critics pointed to the ineffectiveness of Taiwanese ESG frameworks regarding requirements for disclosing international violations.
   *Source: Taiwan Stock Exchange ESG Data; Corporate Accountability Taiwan, 2018*

2. **ESG reporting without data on Vietnam and Texas** — Formosa Plastics Group's annual sustainability reports systematically excluded detailed data on overseas disasters and fines, limiting themselves to general "compliance with standards" statements.
   *Source: Formosa Plastics Group CSR Reports, 2017–2020*

3. **Attracting financing on the basis of ESG declarations** — Formosa Plastics subsidiaries attracted bank financing by presenting themselves as compliant with the Equator Principles Financial Institutions (EPFI) responsible lending principles, despite documented violations.
   *Source: Equator Principles Financial Institutions monitoring; BankTrack, 2017*

---

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. Formosa Plastics received industry and regional recognition used to improve its reputation.

1. **Taiwanese "Best CSR" prize in the petrochemical sector (2010s)** — Individual Formosa Group enterprises repeatedly received Taiwanese industry awards for corporate social responsibility in the petrochemical sector — at a time when international courts were considering lawsuits against the company.
   *Source: Taiwan CSR Award records; Formosa Group PR materials*

2. **Inclusion in Responsible Care programmes** — Several Formosa divisions participated in the Responsible Care programme of the American Chemistry Council, which the company used in marketing materials as confirmation of safety standards.
   *Source: American Chemistry Council Responsible Care Program*

---

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Formosa Plastics used "environmental" labelling and terminology to promote products whose production is associated with large-scale contamination.

1. **Labelling PVC products as "safe" and "sustainable"** — Formosa Plastics promoted PVC (polyvinyl chloride) as a "durable" and "economical" material without disclosing dioxin and chlorinated organic compound emissions during production and disposal.
   *Source: Formosa Plastics product catalogues; Greenpeace Chlorine campaign reports*

2. **"Environmentally clean" branding of chemical products** — The Nan Ya Plastics division used green symbols on the labelling of certain industrial plastics without disclosing full information about the environmental footprint of the production cycle.
   *Source: Nan Ya Plastics product marketing, 2015–2019*

3. **Promoting "recyclable" PVC without recycling infrastructure** — Formosa advertised the "secondary recycling" of its PVC materials without establishing real collection infrastructure and programmes. According to Break Free From Plastic, PVC is one of the least recyclable plastics.
   *Source: Break Free From Plastic, Brand Audit Reports, 2019–2021*

---

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Formosa Plastics conducted symbolic environmental events, particularly after the Vietnam disaster, to restore its reputation.

1. **Tree planting and beach clean-ups in Vietnam (2016–2017)** — After the Ha Tinh disaster, Formosa Ha Tinh Steel organised greening actions and beach clean-ups, inviting local residents and journalists. Environmentalists noted that these events did not address the underwater coral reefs and benthic ecosystems affected by the poisoning.
   *Source: Vietnamese state media; HRW Vietnam report, 2017*

2. **"Ecology Day" at Taiwanese plants** — Formosa annually conducted corporate "ecology days" with facility clean-ups and nature conservation lectures for employees — against the backdrop of continuing discharge standard violations at production facilities.
   *Source: Formosa Plastics CSR reports, 2015–2019*

3. **Donations to Vietnamese fishing communities as "ecological rehabilitation"** — In addition to the official $500 million government compensation, Formosa organised the distribution of fishing equipment and food to affected families, positioning this as "community ecological restoration".
   *Source: Formosa Ha Tinh Steel press releases, 2016–2017*

---

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Formosa and its affiliated structures actively opposed tightening environmental standards and spread narratives justifying the plastics industry.

1. **Opposition to plastic restrictions through industry associations** — Formosa participated in the activities of the Plastics Industry Association, which ran campaigns against bans on single-use plastics and microplastic regulation, funding "eco-educational" programmes about "proper recycling".
   *Source: Plastics Industry Association lobbying disclosures; Greenpeace USA, 2020*

2. **Promoting arguments about the "economic necessity" of PVC** — In communications with Taiwanese regulators, Formosa Plastics argued that PVC is an indispensable material for "green construction" and "sustainable infrastructure", seeking exemptions from restrictions on chlorine-containing plastics.
   *Source: Taiwan EPA public consultation records, 2014–2019*

3. **Funding "independent research" on PVC safety** — As part of the American chemical industry, Formosa participated in funding academic programmes promoting positive assessments of PVC, reducing the likelihood of unfavourable regulatory decisions.
   *Source: Union of Concerned Scientists, "Manufacturers of Doubt", 2018*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Mass harm to human lives?** — **YES** — Ha Tinh disaster deprived 200,000 Vietnamese of their livelihood; pellet discharges in Texas undermined health of coastal communities; Taiwanese plants linked to elevated cancer rates.

2. **Major environmental disasters?** — **YES** — 200 km of dead Vietnamese coastline; destruction of coral reefs and fish populations; billions of plastic pellets in Matagorda Bay estuary; toxic discharges into Taiwanese rivers.

3. **Greenwashing?** — **YES** — PR campaigns after Vietnam disaster; "circular economy" declarations while increasing virgin PVC production; ESG reports without mention of overseas violations.

4. **ESG marketing?** — **YES** — Presence in Taiwanese ESG indices despite violations; attracting financing under EPFI declarations; SDG reporting without specifics.

5. **Wins in ESG competitions?** — **YES** — Taiwanese CSR awards in the petrochemical sector; Responsible Care programme inclusion during parallel violations.

6. **Eco-labels on harmful products?** — **YES** — PVC labelled as "sustainable" material; Nan Ya Plastics green branding; advertising "recyclable" PVC without real recycling infrastructure.

7. **Negligible eco-events?** — **YES** — Post-Ha Tinh greening actions; "ecology days" at Taiwanese plants; distribution of fishing equipment to fishermen — measures that do not address underwater ecosystems.

8. **Lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?** — **YES** — Participation in Plastics Industry Association campaigns against plastic restrictions; promoting PVC as a "green" material in regulatory processes; funding "independent" research on chloroplastics safety.
