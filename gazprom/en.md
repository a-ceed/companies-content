Gazprom: Siberia's Methane Bomb

Gazprom — the world's largest gas extraction company — manages the world's longest pipeline network (approximately 175,000 km). Methane leaks from ageing infrastructure represent a serious climate threat: methane is 80–87 times more potent as a greenhouse gas than CO₂ over a 20-year horizon.

According to satellite observations (GHGSat, ESA Sentinel-5P), anomalously high methane concentrations are recorded over Gazprom's pipelines and compressor stations in Western Siberia. Independent estimates suggest leaks in Russia's gas system amount to 1–2% of total production — billions of cubic metres per year.

In Yamal, gas extraction is conducted on the territories of the indigenous Nenets people. Gazprom infrastructure construction disrupted traditional reindeer grazing routes, and condensate spills at Bovanenkovo field facilities were recorded by Rosprirodnadzor.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Explosions at Gazprom's pipelines and facilities repeatedly resulted in deaths, while gas conflicts left populations of entire countries without heating in winter.

1. **Pipeline explosion in Nizhny Novgorod Oblast (2004)** — An explosion on a Gazprom underground pipeline killed 4 people and destroyed several residential buildings. A Rostekhnadzor investigation found corrosion wear on the pipe.
   *Source: Rostekhnadzor — "Report on accidents at gas supply facilities 2004"*

2. **Gas conflict with Ukraine — "Gas War" (2009)** — Gazprom halted gas supplies via Ukraine for 13 days in January 2009. In Bulgaria, Slovakia, Bosnia and several other European countries, hospitals and residential buildings were left without heating at temperatures down to −20°C. In Bulgaria alone, at least 14 people died from hypothermia in the first days of the crisis.
   *Source: BBC News — "Gas crisis: Europe's cold homes" (2009)*

3. **Compressor station explosion in Yamalo-Nenets Autonomous Okrug (2014)** — A pipeline rupture and fire at a Gazprom compressor station killed 3 employees, with 5 more sustaining burns. The incident occurred at the Urengoy field.
   *Source: RIA Novosti — "Accident at Gazprom facility at Urengoy" (2014)*

4. **Domestic gas explosions due to faulty infrastructure (2018–2021)** — Gazprom delivers gas through worn distribution networks. A series of domestic gas explosions in Russian cities (Magnitogorsk, 2018; Shakhty, 2019; Orekhovo-Zuyevo, 2021) collectively claimed more than 40 lives. Rostekhnadzor recorded emergency conditions of networks.
   *Source: Rostekhnadzor — "Analysis of accidents at gas distribution networks 2018–2021"*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Gazprom bears responsibility for large-scale methane emissions, condensate spills in the Arctic and ecosystem destruction during infrastructure construction.

1. **Gas flaring in Western Siberia (2000s–present)** — Gazprom annually flared between 13 and 20 billion cubic metres of associated gas across Russia. According to World Bank data (Global Gas Flaring Tracker, 2021), Russia ranks second only to Iraq in flaring volumes. This is equivalent to emissions of millions of tonnes of CO₂.
   *Source: World Bank — "Global Gas Flaring Tracker Report 2021"*

2. **Condensate spills at Bovanenkovo field (2012–2019)** — Rosprirodnadzor recorded numerous gas condensate spills on the Yamal Peninsula. Contamination affected tundra ecosystems and spawning rivers that are habitats for whitefish species. Environmentalists estimated the area of contaminated sites exceeded 2,000 hectares.
   *Source: WWF Russia — "Environmental risks of Yamal development" (2019)*

3. **Nord Stream 2 construction across the Baltic Sea (2018–2021)** — Pipeline construction damaged Baltic Sea benthic ecosystems: areas of relict seabed were destroyed, and Baltic cod and mussel habitats were disrupted. Swedish and Finnish environmental agencies recorded alarming sediment suspension in the laying zones.
   *Source: Swedish Environmental Protection Agency — "Nord Stream 2 Environmental Impact" (2021)*

4. **Nord Stream explosions and massive methane release (2022)** — Following the explosions of the Nord Stream and Nord Stream 2 pipelines in September 2022, between 75,000 and 500,000 tonnes of methane (by various estimates) were released into the atmosphere — one of the largest single greenhouse gas leaks in history. The gas belonged to Gazprom.
   *Source: IEA — "Nord Stream Methane Leak" (2022); Science — "Quantifying the Nord Stream methane release" (2023)*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Gazprom systematically positions natural gas as "green" fuel while concealing the scale of methane leaks and the real climate consequences of its activities.

1. **"Gas — the fuel of the future" campaign (2015–2020)** — Gazprom actively promoted natural gas as an "environmentally clean" alternative to coal and oil in advertising campaigns across Europe. The company did not disclose data on methane leaks which, by climate scientists' calculations, can completely negate the climate advantages of gas over coal within a 20-year horizon.
   *Source: Global Witness — "Fossil fuel industry greenwashing" (2020)*

2. **Declaring LNG "carbon neutrality" (2021)** — Gazprom announced its intention to supply "carbon-neutral" LNG to European markets using emission offset schemes. Critics — including Carbon Market Watch — noted that the quality of the proposed offsets was extremely low, and methane emissions during extraction and transport were not taken into account.
   *Source: Carbon Market Watch — "Carbon neutrality claims in LNG" (2021)*

3. **Concealing methane emission data (2019–2022)** — Gazprom repeatedly contested satellite observation data (GHGSat, Sentinel-5P) on significant methane leaks above its infrastructure. The IEA, in its "Methane Tracker 2022" report, indicated that official Russian reporting understates actual methane emissions in the oil and gas sector by 2–3 times.
   *Source: IEA — "Methane Tracker 2022"*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Gazprom publishes ESG reports and creates an appearance of sustainable development without changing a business model based on maximising fossil fuel extraction.

1. **Publication of ESG reports and "Sustainable Development Strategy" (2020–2022)** — Gazprom issued annual sustainability reports declaring emission reduction and social responsibility targets. Independent Climate Action 100+ analysis found that the company's long-term targets are incompatible with limiting warming to 1.5°C.
   *Source: Climate Action 100+ — "Benchmark Assessment: Gazprom" (2022)*

2. **Funding ecological grants "Gazprom for Children" and "New Impetus" (2010–2021)** — Through corporate foundations, Gazprom funded environmental educational programmes for thousands of dollars, positioning these as a contribution to sustainable development — against company revenues of tens of billions of dollars, making the share negligible.
   *Source: Gazprom — "Sustainability Report 2020"*

3. **Membership in IPEX initiatives and Oil and Gas Climate Initiative (2017–2021)** — Gazprom declared participation in OGCI industry climate initiatives, publishing targets for reducing methane emission intensity. At the same time, the company did not allow independent verification of data and did not disclose its measurement methodology.
   *Source: OGCI — "Annual Progress Report 2021"; Carbon Tracker — "Gazprom assessment" (2022)*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES (with caveats)**. Gazprom received corporate ESG awards primarily from Russian rating agencies, which are not recognised by the international investment community.

1. **RAEX-Europe ESG rating (2021)** — Gazprom received relatively high scores on certain indicators in the Russian ESG rating of agency RAEX-Europe. Critics noted that the rating methodology does not adequately account for methane emissions and climate targets.
   *Source: RAEX-Europe — "ESG Country Risk Atlas: Russia" (2021)*

2. **"Best sustainable development practice" nomination at Russian GRI Week (2019)** — Gazprom was nominated for a corporate award for GRI-standard ESG reporting. The company positioned this as recognition by the international community, although the nomination itself was local in character.
   *Source: GRI Russia — "Sustainability Reporting Week 2019"*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Gazprom used environmental labelling and positioning for products whose production causes significant damage to the climate and ecosystems.

1. **LNG labelling as "clean fuel" at Gazprom Neft filling stations (2018–2022)** — Gazprom Neft deployed a network of gas motor filling stations, actively using the slogan "Clean Fuel" and green symbols. Independent experts at Transport & Environment pointed out that at methane leak rates of 3.5% of sales volume, the climate footprint of natural gas as transport fuel is no better than diesel.
   *Source: Transport & Environment — "Gas trucks: a dead end for climate" (2020)*

2. **"Blue flame" and Gazprom branding as an ecological supplier (2016–2021)** — In European marketing materials, Gazprom consistently used images of clean blue flame, natural landscapes and a message about being an "environmentally responsible" supplier. The brand book was developed with a major agency, and campaigns covered Germany, Austria and the Netherlands.
   *Source: Global Witness — "How the gas industry funds climate denial" (2022)*

3. **"Gas — fuel of the green city" programme for municipalities (2019–2021)** — Gazprom promoted the conversion of urban transport to gas, presenting partner cities with special "green transition" certificates. The certificates had no independent lifecycle emission verification.
   *Source: Gazprom — "Gas motor fuel: annual report" (2021)*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Gazprom conducted environmental events incomparable in scale with the damage caused, using them exclusively for PR purposes.

1. **"Tree Planting Day" at Gazprom facilities (2015–2021)** — Annually, Gazprom employees planted trees near corporate facilities as part of the "Green Planet" event. Corporate data indicated 50,000–80,000 trees planted annually. Meanwhile, the company's methane emissions alone are equivalent to the emissions of tens of millions of cars per year.
   *Source: Gazprom — "Sustainability Report 2020"*

2. **Lake Baikal shoreline clean-up expedition (2018)** — Gazprom sponsored a one-day clean-up of a section of Lake Baikal's shore with 200 volunteers, widely covered by corporate media. The volume of waste collected was approximately 2 tonnes.
   *Source: Gazprom-Media — "Gazprom for Baikal" (2018)*

3. **"Environmental education schools" in Yamal (2016–2020)** — Gazprom funded a series of educational events for schoolchildren in Yamal under the banner of caring for Arctic nature, while simultaneously expanding gas extraction on the peninsula. The programme budget was several million roubles per year against company profits in the hundreds of billions of roubles.
   *Source: Gazprom — "Social Report 2019"*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Gazprom financed and promoted the narrative of natural gas as a "bridge to green energy", slowing the real energy transition.

1. **Funding pro-Russian think tanks and the "gas as bridge fuel" campaign (2014–2021)** — Gazprom funded European think tanks (including the German Economic Institute and several Austrian organisations) that published reports on the environmental advantages of natural gas over renewables. According to InfluenceMap, the company was in the top 10 corporations most actively opposing EU climate regulations.
   *Source: InfluenceMap — "Gas industry lobbying in Europe" (2021)*

2. **Lobbying for gas inclusion in the EU sustainable investment taxonomy (2020–2022)** — Gazprom, through industry associations (Eurogas, Gas Infrastructure Europe), actively lobbied for the recognition of natural gas as a "transitional" fuel within the European Green Deal taxonomy. In 2022, the European Commission partially satisfied this demand, including gas in the taxonomy as a "transitional" investment — a decision that the Climate Action Network called "capitulation to the gas lobby".
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory — "How the gas lobby won the taxonomy battle" (2022)*

3. **"Gas and ecology: facts" disinformation campaign (2019–2021)** — Gazprom launched an information campaign in Russian and European media that exaggerated the climate advantages of natural gas and downplayed the problem of methane emissions. The IEA and several European regulators publicly contested the factual basis of the campaign.
   *Source: IEA — "Methane and the global energy sector" (2022)*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Mass harm to human lives?** — **YES** — Pipeline and compressor station explosions claimed workers' and civilians' lives; gas conflicts (2009) left millions of Europeans without heat in winter, resulting in deaths.

2. **Major environmental disasters?** — **YES** — Large-scale methane emissions over Western Siberia; Yamal condensate spills; Baltic seabed ecosystem damage during Nord Stream 2 construction; catastrophic methane release after 2022 pipeline explosions.

3. **Greenwashing?** — **YES** — Positioning natural gas as "clean" fuel; declaring "carbon-neutral" LNG on the basis of questionable offsets; concealment and understatement of methane emission data.

4. **ESG marketing?** — **YES** — Publishing ESG reports; declaring climate targets and OGCI participation without allowing independent verifiers; funding symbolic environmental programmes.

5. **Wins in ESG competitions?** — **YES (limited)** — High scores in Russian RAEX-Europe ESG rating (2021) and nomination for a GRI Russia corporate award (2019) — without recognition from international investors.

6. **Eco-labels on harmful products?** — **YES** — LNG labelled as "clean fuel" at filling stations; green branding when selling natural gas in Europe; "green city" certificates.

7. **Negligible eco-events?** — **YES** — Tree planting, one-day shoreline clean-up events and ecological education schools in Yamal — incommensurate with the damage caused.

8. **Lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?** — **YES** — Funding European think tanks; active lobbying for gas inclusion in EU taxonomy as "transitional" fuel; large-scale media campaign exaggerating climate advantages of gas.
