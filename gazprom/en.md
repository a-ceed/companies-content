Gazprom: Siberia's Methane Bomb

Gazprom — the world's largest gas company — operates the world's longest gas pipeline network. Methane leaks from its ageing infrastructure raise the most serious concerns.

Satellite observations (GHGSat, ESA Sentinel-5P) detect anomalously high methane concentrations above Gazprom's pipelines and compressor stations in Western Siberia. Independent estimates indicate that leaks in Russia's gas system amount to 1–2% of total production — billions of cubic metres per year.

Construction of Gazprom facilities has disrupted traditional reindeer migration routes, and condensate spills at the Bovanenkovo fields have been recorded by Rosprirodnadzor.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Explosions at Gazprom pipelines and facilities have repeatedly caused fatalities.

1. **Gas pipeline explosion in Nizhny Novgorod Region (2004)** — An explosion on a Gazprom underground pipeline killed 4 people and destroyed several residential buildings. Rostekhnadzor's investigation established corrosion damage to the pipe.
   *Source: Rostekhnadzor — "Report on accidents at gas supply facilities 2004"*

2. **Explosion at a compressor station in the Yamalo-Nenets Autonomous Okrug (2014)** — A pipeline rupture and fire at a Gazprom compressor station killed 3 employees and left 5 others with burns. The incident occurred at the Urengoy field.
   *Source: RIA Novosti — "Accident at Gazprom facility at Urengoy" (2014)*

3. **Residential gas explosions caused by faulty infrastructure (2018–2021)** — Gazprom supplies gas through ageing distribution networks. A series of domestic gas explosions in Russian cities (Magnitogorsk, 2018; Shakhty, 2019; Orekhovo-Zuyevo, 2021) collectively killed more than 40 people. The emergency condition of the networks was recorded by Rostekhnadzor.
   *Source: Rostekhnadzor — "Analysis of accidents on gas distribution networks 2018–2021"*

## 2. Has the corporation been responsible for major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Gazprom bears responsibility for large-scale methane emissions, condensate spills in the Arctic, and ecosystem destruction during infrastructure construction.

1. **Gas flaring in Western Siberia (2000s — present)** — Gazprom annually flares 13 to 20 billion cubic metres of associated gas across Russia.
   *Source: World Bank — "Global Gas Flaring Tracker Report 2021"*

2. **Condensate spills at the Bovanenkovo field (2012–2019)** — Rosprirodnadzor recorded numerous gas condensate spills on the Yamal Peninsula. Contamination affected tundra ecosystems and spawning rivers that are habitats for whitefish. According to environmental experts, the contaminated area exceeded 2,000 hectares.
   *Source: WWF Russia — "Environmental Risks of Yamal Development" (2019)*

3. **Laying of Nord Stream 2 across the Baltic Sea (2018–2021)** — Pipeline construction damaged Baltic Sea benthic ecosystems: ancient seabed areas were destroyed and habitats of Baltic cod and mussels were disturbed. Swedish and Finnish environmental agencies recorded alarming sediment suspension in the laying zones.
   *Source: Swedish Environmental Protection Agency — "Nord Stream 2 Environmental Impact" (2021)*

4. **Nord Stream explosions and large-scale methane release (2022)** — Following the sabotage of the Nord Stream and Nord Stream 2 pipelines in September 2022, between 75,000 and 500,000 tonnes of methane (according to various estimates) were released into the atmosphere — one of the largest single greenhouse gas leaks in history. The gas belonged to Gazprom.
   *Source: IEA — "Nord Stream Methane Leak" (2022); Science — "Quantifying the Nord Stream methane release" (2023)*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Gazprom systematically positions natural gas as a "green" fuel while concealing the true scale of methane leaks.

1. **"Gas — the fuel of the future" campaign (2015–2020)** — Gazprom actively promoted natural gas as an "environmentally clean" alternative to coal and oil in advertising campaigns across Europe. The company did not disclose data on methane leaks.
   *Source: Global Witness — "Fossil fuel industry greenwashing" (2020)*

2. **Concealment of methane emission data (2019–2022)** — Gazprom repeatedly disputed satellite observation data (GHGSat, Sentinel-5P) showing significant methane leaks above its infrastructure. The IEA's "Methane Tracker 2022" report noted that official Russian reporting understates actual methane emissions in the oil and gas sector by a factor of 2–3.
   *Source: IEA — "Methane Tracker 2022"*

---

## 4. Does the company participate in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Gazprom publishes ESG reports and creates an appearance of sustainable development.

1. **Publication of ESG reports and a "Sustainable Development Strategy" (2020–2022)** — Gazprom issued annual sustainability reports declaring emission-reduction targets and social responsibility.
   *Source: Climate Action 100+ — "Benchmark Assessment: Gazprom" (2022)*

2. **Funding of "Gazprom for Children" and "New Impetus" eco-grants (2010–2021)** — Through corporate foundations, Gazprom funded environmental education programmes worth thousands of dollars, positioning them as contributions to sustainable development — while the company's revenues ran to tens of billions of dollars, making the share negligible.
   *Source: Gazprom — "Sustainability Report 2020"*

3. **Membership in OGCI — Oil and Gas Climate Initiative (2017–2021)** — Gazprom declared participation in the OGCI industry climate initiative, publishing targets for reducing methane emission intensity. The company did not allow independent data verification and did not disclose its measurement methodology.
   *Source: OGCI — "Annual Progress Report 2021"; Carbon Tracker — "Gazprom assessment" (2022)*

---

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG competition nominations?

**Answer: YES (with reservations)**. Gazprom received corporate ESG awards primarily from Russian rating agencies, which are not recognised by the international investment community.

1. **RAEX-Europe ESG rating (2021)** — Gazprom received relatively high scores on certain indicators in the Russian ESG rating by agency RAEX-Europe. Critics noted that the rating methodology does not account for methane emissions.
   *Source: RAEX-Europe — "ESG Country Risk Atlas: Russia" (2021)*

2. **"Best Sustainable Development Practice" nomination at Russian GRI Week (2019)** — Gazprom was nominated for a corporate award for ESG reporting under GRI standards. The company positioned this as recognition by the international community, although the nomination itself was local in nature.
   *Source: GRI Russia — "Sustainability Reporting Week 2019"*

---

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Gazprom used environmental labelling and positioning for products whose production causes significant damage to the climate and ecosystems.

1. **Labelling LNG as "clean fuel" at Gazprom Neft stations (2018–2022)** — Gazprom Neft deployed a network of gas motor fuel stations, actively using the slogan "Clean Fuel" and green imagery.
   *Source: Transport & Environment — "Gas trucks: a dead end for climate" (2020)*

---

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Gazprom conducted environmental events incomparably small in scale relative to the damage caused, using them exclusively for PR purposes.

1. **"Tree Planting Day" at Gazprom facilities (2015–2021)** — Gazprom employees annually planted trees near corporate facilities as part of the "Green Planet" initiative. Corporate data indicated 50,000–80,000 trees planted per year. Meanwhile, the company's methane emissions alone are equivalent to the emissions of tens of millions of cars per year.
   *Source: Gazprom — "Sustainability Report 2020"*

2. **Baikal shoreline clean-up expedition (2018)** — Gazprom sponsored a one-day clean-up of a section of the Baikal shoreline with 200 volunteers, widely covered in corporate media. The volume of litter collected was approximately 2 tonnes.
   *Source: Gazprom-Media — "Gazprom for Baikal" (2018)*

3. **"Environmental Education Schools" in Yamal (2016–2020)** — Gazprom funded a series of educational events for schoolchildren in Yamal under the banner of caring for Arctic nature. The programme budget was several million roubles per year, against company profits of hundreds of billions of roubles.
   *Source: Gazprom — "Social Report 2019"*

## Summary

1. **Mass harm to people?** — **YES**
   Explosions at pipelines and compressor stations have killed workers and local residents.

2. **Major environmental disasters?** — **YES**
   Large-scale methane emissions over Western Siberia, condensate spills in Yamal, catastrophic methane release following the pipeline explosions in 2022.

3. **Greenwashing?** — **YES**
   Positioning natural gas as "clean" fuel; concealing and underreporting methane emission data.

4. **ESG marketing?** — **YES**
   Publishing ESG reports, declaring climate targets and participating in OGCI without allowing independent verification; funding symbolic environmental programmes.

5. **ESG award wins?** — **YES (limited)**
   High scores in the Russian RAEX-Europe ESG rating (2021) and nomination for a GRI Russia corporate award (2019) — without recognition from international investors.

6. **Eco-labels on harmful products?** — **YES**
   Labelling LNG as "clean fuel" at filling stations; using green branding when selling natural gas in Europe.

7. **Negligible eco-events?** — **YES**
   Tree-planting days, one-day shoreline clean-up events, and Yamal environmental education schools — incommensurate with the damage caused.

8. **Lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?** — **NO**
