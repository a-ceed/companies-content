Glencore: Cobalt at the Cost of Children's Health

In Katanga Province (DR Congo), Glencore's cobalt mines concentrate heavy metals in soil and air near populated areas. A Stanford University study (2020) found cobalt concentrations in the urine of children living near Glencore's mines 43 times above normal. Cobalt dust causes lung diseases, and lead causes irreversible neurological damage.

In Zambia, the Mopani copper smelter regularly exceeded permissible sulphur dioxide emission limits, causing acid rain and destroying crops within a 50 km radius. In 2015, Zambian authorities demanded $1.5 billion in compensation from Glencore.

In Colombia, the Prodeco coal mine is linked to large-scale displacement of indigenous communities and river contamination with coal dust. In 2022, a UK court ruled that local residents have the right to sue Glencore under British law.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Glencore systematically endangered the health and lives of people in mining countries — DR Congo, Zambia and Colombia. Children grew up in zones of heavy metal intoxication, miners worked in dangerous conditions, and communities lost access to water and food.

1. **Cobalt poisoning of children in DR Congo (2015–2020)** — A Stanford University study (2020) established that children living near Glencore's mines in Katanga Province (Kolwezi, Lubumbashi) have cobalt concentrations in their urine 43 times above normal. Many were found to have chronic lung diseases, birth defects and developmental delays. Amnesty International documented the use of child labour at mines supplying cobalt to Glencore's supply chain.
   *Source: Stanford University / Amnesty International, "This Is What We Die For" (2016)*

2. **SO₂ emissions at Mopani Copper plant, Zambia (2000–2015)** — The Mopani Copper Mines plant in Mufulira (majority-owned by Glencore) for years discharged sulphur dioxide at levels several times exceeding permissible standards. Local residents reported mass cases of bronchitis, asthma and chemical burns to respiratory tracts. In 2010, Zambia's Environmental Management Agency recorded SO₂ levels 1,000 times above permissible limits.
   *Source: Zambia Environmental Management Agency; The Guardian (2011)*

3. **Displacement and miner deaths in Colombia, El Cerrejón mine (2000s–2020s)** — Glencore held a stake in the El Cerrejón coal mine, where Wayuu indigenous communities were forcibly displaced. Miners regularly died in accidents. In 2021, the UN Human Rights Commission identified systemic violations linked to the mine's operations.
   *Source: UN Human Rights Council (2021); Business & Human Rights Resource Centre*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Glencore's activities in several countries were accompanied by large-scale contamination of rivers, soil and atmosphere, the consequences of which persist for decades.

1. **Acid rain in Zambia (2000–2015)** — The Mopani plant in Mufulira discharged sulphur dioxide into the atmosphere, causing acid rain within a radius of up to 50 km. Crops of thousands of small farmers were destroyed, soils degraded and rivers acidified. A World Bank inspection in 2008 found serious violations of environmental standards.
   *Source: World Bank / IFC environmental audit (2008); Earthjustice*

2. **Heavy metal river contamination in DR Congo (chronic)** — Katanga Mining (a Glencore subsidiary) mines in Kolwezi discharged effluent containing copper, cobalt and uranium into the Luvua and Lukula rivers. According to a 2017 study in Science of the Total Environment, uranium concentrations in the water exceeded WHO standards by hundreds of times. Fishing was effectively destroyed.
   *Source: Science of the Total Environment (2017); UNEP*

3. **Contamination in Peru, Espinar region (2012)** — At the Antapaccay copper mine (Glencore/Xstrata) in Espinar Province, local residents recorded contamination of rivers and water bodies with heavy metals — arsenic, lead and chromium. In 2012, mass protests erupted and the Peruvian government declared a state of emergency in the region. Independent water samples confirmed standard exceedances.
   *Source: Peru Ombudsman's Office (Defensoría del Pueblo, 2012)*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Glencore actively promoted the narrative of its role in the "green transition" through cobalt and copper supplies for batteries, while continuing to harm ecosystems and human health in mining countries.

1. **Positioning cobalt as a "green future metal" (2018–2022)** — Glencore ran major PR campaigns presenting its DRC cobalt as "responsibly sourced" and necessary for electric vehicles. Meanwhile, in 2021, the company settled criminal charges in the USA and UK, paying $1.1 billion — including for corruption in Congo mining.
   *Source: US DoJ, UK Serious Fraud Office (2022)*

2. **Sustainability reports vs. actual emissions (2015–2021)** — In annual reports, Glencore declared reductions in greenhouse gas emissions and "responsible extraction". However, in 2019, the company was one of the world's largest coal producers and actively invested in expanding coal assets, contradicting the Paris Agreement.
   *Source: Global Coal Exit List; Carbon Tracker Initiative (2020)*

3. **Marketing "ethical" cobalt despite documented child labour (2016–2020)** — Glencore declared zero tolerance for child labour in its public supply chain standards. Simultaneously, Amnesty International and RAID documented children working at informal mines in Glencore's operational zone in Kolwezi, whose ore entered the company's supply chain.
   *Source: RAID / Amnesty International (2018)*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Glencore actively uses ESG rhetoric to attract investors, despite its real indicators significantly diverging from declared standards.

1. **ESG bonds and "green" financing (2021–2023)** — Glencore actively attracted ESG-labelled financing, citing its position as a key metals supplier for the energy transition. A number of institutional investors, including Norway's Government Pension Fund, subsequently excluded Glencore from their portfolios due to coal assets and corruption charges.
   *Source: Norges Bank Investment Management; Financial Times (2022)*

2. **Publication of "Climate Action Plan" (2021)** — Glencore presented a carbon neutrality plan for 2050 and received support from a number of shareholders. Critics from Climate Action 100+ noted that the plan covers only operational emissions (Scope 1 and 2), but not emissions from burning the coal being sold (Scope 3), which account for 95% of the company's total carbon footprint.
   *Source: Climate Action 100+; InfluenceMap (2021)*

3. **ESG ratings alongside real violations** — Glencore regularly receives mid-to-high scores in MSCI ESG and FTSE4Good ratings, formally meeting information disclosure criteria. This allows ESG funds to hold its shares despite unresolved environmental claims in DR Congo, Zambia and Colombia.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings; ShareAction (2022)*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. Glencore was awarded various prizes and inclusions in sustainability indices, inconsistent with its real environmental and human rights performance.

1. **Inclusion in Dow Jones Sustainability Index (2010s)** — For several years, Glencore was included or considered for inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index family, used as a benchmark for ESG investors. Inclusion in these indices served the company as a marketing argument.
   *Source: S&P Global / RobecoSAM Corporate Sustainability Assessment*

2. **"Best reporting transparency" awards (2014–2017)** — A number of mining industry publications included Glencore in "best practice" lists for non-financial information disclosure, despite ongoing corruption investigations.
   *Source: Mining industry ESG award bodies; ICMM reporting frameworks*

3. **Positive CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project) assessment (2019–2020)** — CDP assigned Glencore moderately positive scores for climate risk disclosure, which the company used in investor communications. This occurred against a backdrop of expanding coal operations in Australia.
   *Source: CDP Climate Scores 2019–2020*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Glencore and its subsidiaries used environmental certification and labelling for metals extracted in violation of environmental standards.

1. **Cobalt certification as "responsibly sourced" through RMAP (2018–2022)** — Glencore obtained Responsible Minerals Assurance Process (RMAP) certification for part of its cobalt production, allowing it to be positioned as "responsible". At the same time, informal suppliers in Glencore's operational zone in the DRC continued operating with violations.
   *Source: Responsible Minerals Initiative; RAID (2020)*

2. **"Cobalt for Generations" programme (2020)** — Glencore launched a marketing programme presenting DRC cobalt extraction as the foundation of "the clean energy future". The programme logo was used in communications with customers — battery manufacturers — despite ongoing human rights investigations.
   *Source: Glencore corporate communications; RAID briefing (2021)*

3. **Copper with "Low Carbon" certificate despite high real footprint (2021)** — Glencore promoted part of its copper production as "low carbon" on the basis of comparatively low Scope 1–2 figures per tonne of metal. Critics pointed out that the calculation deliberately excluded emissions from transport, coal-powered energy at facilities and coal extraction by the same conglomerate.
   *Source: International Copper Association; Carbon Trust analysis*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Against the backdrop of multi-billion revenues and large-scale environmental damage, Glencore conducted symbolic environmental events serving primarily PR purposes.

1. **Tree planting in DRC and Zambia (2019–2022)** — As part of CSR programmes, Glencore funded the planting of several thousand trees near its operations in Congo and Zambia. The scale of planting was incomparable with the destroyed ecosystems and cleared forests in mining zones.
   *Source: Glencore Sustainability Report 2022*

2. **"Environmental days" for employees (2018–2023)** — Glencore annually organised corporate "environment days" with facility clean-ups and waste collection around offices in Zug (Switzerland) and other headquarters. These events had no relevance to the real damage from mining operations.
   *Source: Glencore internal CSR reports*

3. **Biodiversity programmes at mines (2020)** — Glencore reported on "biodiversity programmes" at certain mines, including the creation of small protected zones on the periphery of mine sites. The area of such zones constituted a fraction of a percent of disturbed land in the company's operational zones.
   *Source: Glencore Biodiversity Management Plans 2020; Earthworks critique*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Glencore actively promoted the narrative of "coal necessity" and "responsible extraction" as instruments for combating climate change, opposing more stringent environmental standards.

1. **Lobbying to retain coal assets through "transition fuel" concept (2019–2022)** — Glencore through the World Coal Association and direct contacts with governments in Australia, South Africa and Colombia lobbied for the concept of "coal as a transition fuel", arguing that coal is necessary for the energy security of developing countries. This contradicted the IPCC scientific consensus on the need for immediate coal phase-out.
   *Source: InfluenceMap Corporate Climate Policy Engagement Report (2021)*

2. **Funding "educational" programmes on resource nationalism (2015–2020)** — In several African countries, Glencore funded "educational" initiatives presenting mining as the only path to economic development and deliberately downplaying ecological alternatives — renewable energy and sustainable forestry.
   *Source: Global Witness, "Defenders of the Status Quo" (2020)*

3. **Opposition to tightening waste discharge standards in Indonesia and DRC (2010–2018)** — According to Global Witness investigations, Glencore representatives participated in consultations with DRC and Zambia regulators, seeking to soften or delay the introduction of new environmental standards for the mining sector. The company presented tightening of standards as a threat to the investment climate.
   *Source: Global Witness (2019); Business & Human Rights Resource Centre*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Human health damage**: Glencore chronically poisoned children and adults with heavy metals in DR Congo, Zambia and Peru; child labour in the operational zone was documented by Amnesty International.
2. **Environmental disasters**: Acid rain in Zambia; destruction of fishing in Congolese rivers; severe heavy metal contamination of water bodies across several countries — direct results of the company's operations.
3. **Greenwashing**: Positioning cobalt as a "green metal" while human rights violations continued — a typical example of the gap between marketing and reality.
4. **ESG marketing**: Glencore used ESG instruments to attract financing, while its climate plan deliberately excluded emissions from coal sales.
5. **ESG awards**: Inclusion in sustainability indices occurred in parallel with criminal investigations in the USA and UK.
6. **Eco-labelling**: "Responsible" cobalt certification was used as a marketing tool despite documented supply chain violations.
7. **Symbolic eco-events**: Tree planting and "eco-days" are incomparable in scale with the real damage from operations in several countries.
8. **Lobbying**: The company systematically opposed tightening environmental standards, promoting the "coal as transition fuel" narrative against scientific consensus.
