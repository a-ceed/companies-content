Honeywell: Jersey City's Chromium Crisis

The former chromium pigment production site of Allied Chemical (later absorbed by Honeywell) in Jersey City, New Jersey, created one of the largest chromium contamination disasters in US history.

From 1905 to 1972, the plant manufactured sodium chromates, leaving behind more than 1.5 million tonnes of chromium-containing waste buried beneath urban development. Hexavalent chromium Cr(VI) — a carcinogen — penetrated groundwater beneath a significant portion of Jersey City.

Since the 1990s, Honeywell has been conducting large-scale remediation (costing more than $700 million) of over 200 contaminated sites. However, EPA monitoring still identifies contamination at a number of locations.

In Ohio, the historical AlliedSignal (Honeywell's predecessor) plant left trichloroethylene contamination in the groundwater of Cuyahoga County — a community whose residents have filed lawsuits over health damage.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Honeywell and its corporate predecessors — Allied Chemical and AlliedSignal — for decades produced and buried toxic substances, including hexavalent chromium, mercury and trichloroethylene, poisoning water and soil in residential neighbourhoods across several US states.

1. **Hexavalent chromium contamination in Jersey City (1905–1972 and beyond)** — Allied Chemical (Honeywell's predecessor) manufactured sodium chromates at the Jersey City plant until 1972, leaving more than 1.5 million tonnes of chromium-containing waste buried beneath residential areas. Cr(VI) — a recognised carcinogen — was found in groundwater beneath playgrounds, schools and homes. Residents reported cancer cases; New Jersey authorities declared the situation a "chromium crisis".
   *Source: NJ DEP Chromium Task Force; EPA Superfund records; Jersey City Medical Center cancer registry*

2. **Mercury contamination of Onondaga Lake (Syracuse, New York)** — The Allied Chemical plant in Syracuse discharged mercury into Onondaga Lake from the 1940s through the 1970s. Methylmercury concentrations in sediments were thousands of times above background levels. Mercury contamination led to decades-long fishing bans; the Onondaga Nation documented health damage to its members.
   *Source: Onondaga Lake Partnership; EPA; Honeywell Onondaga Lake Site records*

3. **Trichloroethylene contamination in Ohio** — The historical AlliedSignal plant left trichloroethylene (TCE) contamination in groundwater in Cuyahoga County, Ohio. TCE is a carcinogen linked to leukaemia and kidney cancer. Several families using well water filed lawsuits over health damage.
   *Source: Ohio EPA Superfund records; court filings, Cuyahoga County*

## 2. Did the corporation conceal information about environmental harm?

**Answer: YES**. Honeywell and its predecessors possessed data on the harmfulness of chromium, mercury and other toxins long before problems were acknowledged by regulators, and systematically understated the scale of contamination in official reports.

1. **Concealment of chromium contamination beneath Jersey City development** — NJ DEP established that the company throughout the 1970s–1980s failed to inform authorities about known contamination sites beneath urban neighbourhoods, allowing housing to be built over poisoned soil. Documents showed that the company's internal studies recorded the threat as early as the 1970s.
   *Source: NJ DEP investigative records; The Record (Bergen County), 1995*

2. **Understatement of mercury levels at Onondaga** — In initial negotiations with EPA and New York State authorities, Honeywell provided calculations that systematically understated the volume of mercury contamination in the lake. Independent researchers from Syracuse University identified discrepancies between corporate and scientific assessments.
   *Source: Syracuse University Department of Civil Engineering; Onondaga Nation records*

3. **Opacity during remediation works** — Independent auditors engaged by Jersey City community organisations repeatedly indicated that Honeywell provided incomplete data on contamination sites discovered during construction works. Some sites were added to the registry only after public pressure.
   *Source: Jersey City Environmental Task Force; NJ DEP Chromium Task Force reports*

---

## 3. Did the corporation evade accountability or obstruct justice?

**Answer: YES**. Honeywell spent decades contesting the scope of its cleanup obligations for chromium and mercury contamination, clashed with regulators, and sought to minimise payments to those affected.

1. **Protracted Superfund negotiations over Onondaga Lake (1990s–2010s)** — Honeywell and EPA negotiated on the cleanup of Onondaga Lake for more than 15 years. During this time, mercury contamination persisted and fishing remained banned. The Onondaga Nation demanded accelerated work and a stricter remediation plan, but Honeywell sought approval of the least-cost plan.
   *Source: Onondaga Nation v. Honeywell, US District Court; EPA Onondaga Lake site documents*

2. **Contesting the number of chromium sites in Jersey City** — NJ DEP initially identified more than 250 chromium contamination sites. Honeywell systematically contested the inclusion of some sites, delaying the start of cleanup work by years.
   *Source: NJ DEP Chromium Enforcement; Newark Star-Ledger investigation*

3. **Negotiations with EPA over PFAS contamination near plants (2019–2022)** — Following identification of PFAS in groundwater near several Honeywell plants, the company entered into extended technical negotiations with EPA on site classification, delaying their inclusion in the Superfund list.
   *Source: EPA PFAS Action Plan; Environmental Defense Fund, 2021*

## 4. Did the corporation violate the rights of indigenous peoples or vulnerable communities?

**Answer: YES**. Honeywell's contamination disproportionately affected the Onondaga Nation and low-income urban communities in New Jersey and New York.

1. **Harm to the Onondaga Nation (New York)** — The Onondaga regard Onondaga Lake as sacred and a traditional food source. Allied Chemical's mercury contamination deprived them of the ability to fish and gather shellfish from a body of water of central spiritual and cultural significance. In 2005, the Onondaga Nation filed a lawsuit for restoration of rights to contaminated territories.
   *Source: Onondaga Nation Land Rights Action; Onondaga Nation Council statements*

2. **Chromium contamination in low-income Jersey City neighbourhoods** — Chromium waste was buried primarily beneath areas with high proportions of African American and Latino residents. The cost of chromium remediation was often incorporated into the price of future development, forcing the municipality to subsidise corporate contamination cleanup at taxpayers' expense.
   *Source: NJ DEP; Jersey City Housing Authority records*

3. **Nuclear weapons component manufacturing and worker risk** — At the Honeywell plant in Metropolis, Illinois, uranium is processed for nuclear fuel. A number of former workers documented elevated cancer rates and linked them to occupational radiation exposure; the USW union sought improved working conditions.
   *Source: USW Local 7-669; Illinois EPA; Nuclear Regulatory Commission inspection records*

---

## 5. Has the corporation caused environmental damage to ecosystems?

**Answer: YES**. Onondaga Lake — the largest waterbody in New York State west of the Hudson — was turned into one of the most contaminated lake ecosystems in the country; chromium contamination in Jersey City penetrated aquifers.

1. **Degradation of the Onondaga Lake ecosystem** — Decades of mercury, sodium hydroxide and other industrial effluent discharges from Allied Chemical/Honeywell enterprises led to hypereutrophication of the lake, disappearance of fish populations and a long-term fishing ban. Throughout the 1990s, sediments contained methylmercury at concentrations dangerous to fish-eating birds and mammals.
   *Source: Onondaga Lake Partnership Annual Reports; USGS Water Resources Data*

2. **Chromium groundwater contamination beneath Jersey City** — Cr(VI) in groundwater beneath more than 200 Jersey City sites created long-term risk of contamination to aquifers feeding wells in the region. Remediation has continued for more than 30 years and is not yet complete.
   *Source: NJ DEP Chromium Task Force; EPA Superfund records*

3. **PFAS in groundwater near Honeywell plants** — EPA documented PFAS contamination in groundwater near several Honeywell production facilities, including sites in New Jersey and Florida. PFAS have been found in water supply wells on adjacent land.
   *Source: EPA PFAS monitoring data; EWG PFAS Contamination Map*

## 6. Has the corporation engaged in financial manipulation or evasion of penalties?

**Answer: YES**. Honeywell used corporate restructurings and legal instruments to distribute environmental obligations among several legal entities, minimising its direct payments.

1. **Transfer of environmental obligations during Garrett Motion spin-off (2018)** — When the Garrett Motion division was spun off into a separate company in 2018, a mechanism was agreed whereby Garrett assumed a portion of Honeywell's environmental obligations. When Garrett filed for bankruptcy in 2020, the question of covering these obligations became the subject of litigation.
   *Source: Garrett Motion bankruptcy filings, 2020; Bloomberg Law*

2. **Contesting the scope of Onondaga Lake cleanup payments** — Throughout the 2000s–2010s, Honeywell repeatedly sought to reduce the scope of cleanup works, presenting technical alternatives requiring lower costs but providing less complete ecosystem restoration.
   *Source: EPA Onondaga Lake Amended Record of Decision; court records*

3. **Demanding that cleanup costs be distributed among other "responsible parties"** — Honeywell sought to include Jersey City municipality and other companies in the list of "potentially responsible parties" under Superfund, to share cleanup costs. Legal battles continued for years.
   *Source: NJ DEP; US District Court, District of New Jersey*

---

## 7. Has the corporation lobbied against environmental standards?

**Answer: YES**. Honeywell actively opposed tightening of federal standards for chromium, PFAS and nuclear waste.

1. **Lobbying against stricter Cr(VI) standard in drinking water** — When EPA considered reducing the maximum contaminant level (MCL) for hexavalent chromium in drinking water in the 2010s, industrial groups with Honeywell's participation sought to maintain the weaker standard, citing "scientific uncertainty".
   *Source: EPA docket for Chromium MCL rulemaking; EWG lobbying database*

2. **Opposition to Superfund list expansion** — Honeywell was part of industrial coalitions opposing expansion and acceleration of the Superfund programme, which would increase cleanup obligations at corporate sites.
   *Source: Congressional lobbying disclosures; InfluenceMap corporate analysis*

3. **Lobbying in the nuclear sector** — Honeywell — the sole US manufacturer of uranium hexafluoride for nuclear fuel enrichment — actively lobbied for relaxation of nuclear oversight requirements for its Metropolis, Illinois plant, seeking reclassification of certain radioactive waste.
   *Source: NRC inspection records; Union of Concerned Scientists*

---

## 8. Does the corporation exhibit repeated or ongoing violations?

**Answer: YES**. Contamination created by Honeywell and its predecessors continues to exist or is being identified at new sites; remediation is stretched over decades without complete elimination of harm.

1. **Unfinished cleanup of chromium sites after 30 years of work** — Honeywell began remediation of chromium sites in Jersey City in the late 1980s. As of the early 2020s, work was still not complete at a number of sites; NJ DEP regularly discovers new contamination sources during construction work in the city.
   *Source: NJ DEP Chromium Task Force Annual Reports*

2. **New PFAS emissions at operating facilities** — EPA documented ongoing PFAS emissions from several operating Honeywell plants during 2018–2022, including at facilities where these substances had not previously been monitored.
   *Source: EPA National PFAS Testing Strategy; EWG, 2022*

3. **Continuing contamination of Onondaga Lake** — Despite implementation of the remediation plan, mercury in Onondaga Lake sediments persists at levels that do not allow the fishing ban to be lifted. Full ecosystem restoration is not expected before 2034 under the most optimistic scenario.
   *Source: Onondaga Lake Partnership Monitoring Report, 2022; EPA*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Mass harm to human lives**: Honeywell and predecessors — Allied Chemical and AlliedSignal — created several of the largest industrial disasters in US history: the Jersey City chromium crisis, mercury poisoning of Onondaga Lake, and TCE contamination in Ohio.
2. **Concealment of harm**: The company possessed data on contamination threats decades before disclosure to regulators; Onondaga Lake mercury volumes were systematically understated in official estimates.
3. **Evasion of accountability**: Over 15 years of negotiations delayed Onondaga Lake cleanup; Honeywell contested the number of chromium sites and shifted environmental obligations through corporate spin-offs.
4. **Violations against vulnerable communities**: The Onondaga Nation's sacred lake was destroyed; chromium waste was disproportionately buried in low-income minority neighbourhoods.
5. **Ecosystem damage**: Onondaga Lake remains under fishing ban; Jersey City aquifer contamination ongoing after 30+ years; PFAS detected at multiple operating facilities.
6. **Financial manipulation**: Garrett Motion bankruptcy raised questions about transferred environmental liabilities; repeated cost-minimisation strategies at Superfund sites.
7. **Lobbying against standards**: Opposed stricter Cr(VI) drinking water limits; worked to slow Superfund expansion; sought relaxed nuclear oversight.
8. **Ongoing violations**: Chromium remediation in Jersey City still incomplete; new PFAS contamination identified at operating plants; full Onondaga restoration not expected before 2034.
