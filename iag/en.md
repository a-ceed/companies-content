IAG: Aviation and Ungreen Commitments

International Airlines Group operates one of Europe's largest airline groups. Aviation accounts for approximately 2.5% of direct global CO₂ emissions, but when accounting for NOx, water vapour and contrails, its real climate impact is estimated at 3.5–4.9% of total anthropogenic climate forcing.

British Airways was criticised for "greenwashing" in 2018: the company promised "100% carbon offsetting" of all flights, but the "forest credit" schemes used did not pass independent verification.

IAG is one of the major lobbyists against rapid introduction of a European carbon tax on aviation. In 2023, the EU obliged airlines to increase the proportion of sustainable aviation fuel (SAF), but IAG warned that this would raise ticket prices and reduce the accessibility of air travel.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. IAG's aviation (British Airways, Iberia, Vueling, Aer Lingus) is a major source of CO₂ and NOx emissions contributing significantly to climate change. Climate changes to which the aviation industry is party lead to extreme weather events, droughts and floods that claim human lives.

1. **Large-scale CO₂ and NOx emissions (ongoing)** — IAG annually emits more than 20 million tonnes of CO₂. Aviation NOx emissions at cruise altitudes are 2–4 times more dangerous than ground-level emissions in terms of climate impact. WHO has recognised air pollution from aviation as a health risk factor for populations in airport zones, including near Heathrow and Barajas.
   *Source: Transport & Environment, "European Aviation CO2 Emissions", 2022; WHO Air Quality Guidelines*

2. **Aviation noise and population health near Heathrow (ongoing)** — Residents of west London living under British Airways descent trajectories at Heathrow Airport report elevated levels of cardiovascular disease and sleep disorders. An Imperial College London study (2013) demonstrated a statistically significant link between aircraft noise and heart attacks.
   *Source: Imperial College London, "Aircraft noise and cardiovascular disease", Lancet, 2013*

3. **Participation in failed emission offset programme (2019–2022)** — British Airways operated a "carbon offsets" programme for passengers through forest credit schemes that were subsequently found by The Guardian and Carbon Market Watch to be fictitious: projects either did not exist or protected forests facing no threat. Passengers paid surcharges without receiving any real climate benefit.
   *Source: The Guardian, "Revealed: the 'carbon credit' forest projects that failed", 23 January 2023*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. IAG is one of Europe's largest aviation CO₂ emitters; its activities make a measurable contribution to climate change leading to environmental catastrophes on a global scale.

1. **IAG — largest aviation CO₂ emitter in the EU** — According to Transport & Environment, IAG is among the top three aviation company emitters of CO₂ in Europe. In 2019 (pre-pandemic), the group emitted approximately 21.4 million tonnes of CO₂. Following passenger traffic recovery, emissions returned to pre-pandemic levels.
   *Source: Transport & Environment, "CO2 Emissions from European Flights", 2022*

2. **Soil contamination with aviation fuel near airports** — Aviation fuel leaks and refuelling operations by British Airways at Heathrow have on multiple occasions led to soil and groundwater contamination with kerosene. In 2007, a spill in the fuel supply sector was recorded that affected soil near Thames tributaries.
   *Source: Environment Agency UK, Pollution Incident Register*

3. **Contribution to coral reef degradation through climate change** — Scientific consensus links the rise in global temperatures, to which major aviation emitters contribute, to unprecedented bleaching of the Great Barrier Reef. IAG as a major emitter bears indirect responsibility for these consequences, widely described as a "climate catastrophe" in the scientific community.
   *Source: IPCC AR6 Climate Change Report, 2021; ICCT Aviation Emissions*

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. IAG systematically promotes an image of "green aviation" through declarations about SAF, offsets and climate targets that do not match the real scale of emissions.

1. **British Airways' claim of "carbon neutrality" for domestic flights (2020)** — BA announced that from January 2020 all domestic UK flights would be "carbon neutral" — through offset purchases. Independent analysts from Carbon Brief showed that the offsets used did not deliver the claimed emission reductions; moreover, the programme covered only a small fraction of the company's total emissions.
   *Source: Carbon Brief, "British Airways 'carbon neutral' domestic flights", February 2020*

2. **SAF greenwashing: less than 1% real consumption** — IAG publicly claims "leadership" in the transition to sustainable aviation fuel, but in 2022 the SAF share in the group's fuel mix was approximately 0.3%. Commitments to achieve 10% SAF by 2030 are considered unrealistic by analysts without large-scale government support.
   *Source: ICCT, "SAF Policy Brief", 2023; IAG Sustainability Report 2022*

3. **Advertising the "first zero CO₂ transatlantic flight" (2021)** — In November 2021, BA operated a flight from London to New York on a mix of SAF and conventional fuel, declaring it the "first transatlantic flight on 100% SAF". In reality, the aircraft used only 35% SAF in one engine; the remaining engines ran on conventional kerosene. The advertising campaign was found to be misleading by a number of investigative journalists.
   *Source: The Guardian, "British Airways' misleading SAF claim", 2021; ICCT*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. IAG actively uses ESG reporting and ratings to attract investors, positioning itself as a sustainable development leader in aviation.

1. **Issuance of "green bonds" and "ESG framework"** — IAG developed an ESG framework to attract "responsible investment", citing climate commitments and SAF investments. Critics from Transport & Environment note that IAG's framework documents do not contain binding near-term targets for reducing absolute emissions.
   *Source: IAG ESG Framework 2022; Transport & Environment Analysis*

2. **Inclusion in Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI)** — IAG is in DJSI despite being one of Europe's largest aviation emitters. Index inclusion is used by the company in communications with institutional investors.
   *Source: S&P Global DJSI Yearbook, 2022*

3. **Corporate reports emphasising "progress" while absolute emissions grow** — IAG's annual sustainability reports focus on reductions in specific emissions per passenger-kilometre, obscuring the fact that the group's absolute emissions are growing as the fleet expands and the number of routes increases.
   *Source: IAG Annual Report and Accounts 2022; ICCT analysis*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. IAG and its constituent airlines received industry ESG awards that were criticised by climate organisations.

1. **British Airways — "best airline for sustainable development" in industry ratings** — BA has repeatedly received recognition in industry sustainability rankings, including IATA and Skytrax ratings, despite an extremely low real SAF share in its fuel mix.
   *Source: Skytrax World Airline Awards, 2022; IATA Sustainability Ratings*

2. **IAG inclusion in FTSE4Good index** — IAG is in the FTSE4Good index for "socially responsible investors", despite criticism for greenwashing in relation to offset programmes.
   *Source: FTSE Russell, FTSE4Good Index, 2022*

3. **Iberia — "most eco-friendly transatlantic airline" by Air Transport World (2020)** — Iberia (part of IAG) received an industry award in the environmental sustainability category, widely criticised as a "green showcase" in the absence of real progress in emission reduction.
   *Source: Air Transport World Awards 2020; Transport & Environment critique*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. IAG and its subsidiaries actively use environmental labelling for tickets, fares and emission offset programmes, misleading passengers.

1. **British Airways' "Carbon Zero" fare labelling (2020)** — BA offered fares labelled "Carbon Zero" for flights whose emissions were purportedly fully offset. Audits showed that the offset projects used received low ratings from independent verifiers Gold Standard and VCS.
   *Source: Which?, "Are airline carbon offset schemes worth it?", 2022*

2. **"Eco" labelling for SAF flights with less than 5% SAF content** — A number of IAG flights were labelled as operated using "sustainable fuel" with actual SAF content in the mixture below 5%. The UK aviation regulator (CAA) received complaints on this matter.
   *Source: CAA Consumer Research, 2022; Which? Magazine*

3. **"Green" aircraft liveries in marketing campaigns** — British Airways and Iberia used liveries with environmental symbolism ("green waves", leaves) during selected promotional campaigns. Environmental organisations regarded this as deliberate creation of a "green" company image in the absence of substantial changes in operational activity.
   *Source: Greenpeace UK, "Flying Green: Fact or Fiction?", 2021*

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. IAG organises environmental initiatives whose scale is incomparable with the group's annual emissions of tens of millions of tonnes of CO₂.

1. **"One Young World" programme and sponsorship of climate forums** — IAG sponsors conferences for young climate leaders without underpinning these with real changes to its business model. The annual budget for such events is less than $1 million against group revenue exceeding €20 billion.
   *Source: IAG CSR Report 2022; One Young World Partnership*

2. **Plastic clean-ups at airports** — British Airways conducted plastic litter collection events at Heathrow terminal as part of "Earth Day". Environmentalists characterised the event as a PR exercise diverting attention from the climate impact of flights.
   *Source: BA Newsroom, "Earth Day at Heathrow", April 2022*

3. **British Airways' "climate forest" planting (2021)** — BA announced the planting of 10,000 trees in the UK as an element of its climate strategy. These 10,000 trees absorb less than 200 tonnes of CO₂ per year — a negligible fraction of the group's 21 million tonnes of annual emissions.
   *Source: British Airways Newsroom, 2021; Carbon Brief calculations*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. IAG actively lobbies against strict climate standards for aviation and promotes the narrative that technological solutions (SAF, hydrogen) make immediate regulation unnecessary.

1. **Lobbying against EU aviation carbon tax** — IAG is part of the Airlines for Europe (A4E) coalition, which systematically opposes rapid introduction of a carbon tax on aviation fuel in the EU. In 2021–2022, A4E sought to soften the requirements of the mandatory SAF share directive (ReFuelEU Aviation).
   *Source: InfluenceMap, "Airline Industry Climate Lobbying", 2022; Corporate Europe Observatory*

2. **Promoting SAF as the main "climate solution" for aviation** — IAG actively promotes the concept that SAF can fully decarbonise aviation without reducing flight volumes. Independent scientists and ICCT indicate that under realistic scenarios, SAF will be able to cover no more than 5–10% of aviation fuel by 2030.
   *Source: ICCT, "SAF feasibility analysis", 2022; Nature Climate Change*

3. **Funding "educational" climate programmes for children through the IAG Foundation** — The IAG Foundation supports school ecology and climate programmes. Critics point out that this creates an image of a "responsible" company among young people and their parents without changing the group's real operational activity.
   *Source: IAG Foundation Report 2022; Greenpeace UK analysis*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Human health harm**: IAG's aviation NOx and CO₂ emissions make a measurable contribution to climate change; British Airways aircraft noise near Heathrow is linked to elevated cardiovascular disease rates among west London residents.
2. **Environmental disasters**: IAG is one of Europe's largest aviation CO₂ emitters; its contribution to climate change is a documented fact.
3. **Greenwashing**: BA's "carbon neutrality" claim for domestic flights via fictitious offsets; advertising the "first zero transatlantic flight" with actual SAF content below 35%.
4. **ESG marketing**: "Green" frameworks and bonds attract investment without binding absolute emission reduction targets.
5. **ESG awards**: Industry "sustainability" prizes with SAF share below 1% and growing absolute emissions.
6. **False eco-labels**: "Carbon Zero" fares; "eco" flight labelling with minimal SAF content; green aircraft liveries.
7. **Negligible eco-events**: Planting 10,000 trees; airport plastic clean-ups — incomparable with 21 million tonnes of CO₂ annually.
8. **Lobbying**: IAG through A4E blocks tightening of climate standards for aviation in the EU and promotes SAF as a "complete solution" removing the need to reduce flights.
