INEOS: Europe's Chemical Giant and the Environment

INEOS — Europe's largest private chemical conglomerate — manufactures PVC, polyethylene, polypropylene and other chemicals. Its plants in Antwerp, Cologne and Runcorn (UK) are major sources of industrial emissions.

In the UK and Belgium, historical PVC production sites were associated with dioxin formation — persistent organic pollutants. The European Environment Agency has repeatedly indicated exceedances of dioxin emission standards at facilities analogous to INEOS plants.

In 2019, INEOS actively lobbied for fracking permission in the UK. Company owner Jim Ratcliffe personally funded a campaign against the fracking ban introduced by the government over concerns about drinking water contamination and seismic activity.

In Norway, INEOS operates petrochemical facilities in environmentally sensitive fjords.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. INEOS plants in the UK and Belgium discharged dioxins, chlorinated hydrocarbons and other toxic substances; a major accident at the Grangemouth plant in 2013 created a real threat to the local population.

1. **Fires and emergency emissions at Grangemouth plant (2013)** — In 2013, a series of incidents occurred at the Grangemouth petrochemical plant (Scotland, owned by INEOS) during an industrial dispute. In April 2013, emergency shutdowns with flaring discharged significant volumes of toxic substances into the atmosphere. Residents of Boness-on-Forth and surrounding villages reported eye and respiratory tract irritation and chemical odours.
   *Source: Scottish Environment Protection Agency (SEPA); BBC Scotland; Guardian, 2013*

2. **Chlorine and PVC precursor emissions at Inovyn plant in Runcorn (UK)** — The INEOS Inovyn plant in Runcorn (Cheshire) is one of Europe's largest chlorine production facilities. Local health groups recorded elevated levels of hydrogen chloride and other toxic gases in the air of adjacent residential areas during production incidents.
   *Source: Environment Agency (UK) compliance records; Runcorn residents' health group; EEA E-PRTR*

3. **Dioxin emissions from PVC production** — Production of chlorine and PVC involving combustion of chlorine-containing feedstocks inevitably carries risk of dioxin and furan formation — among the most toxic anthropogenic pollutants. The EEA identifies PVC and chlorine production plants, including INEOS-type facilities, as potential sources of dioxin emissions to air and waterways.
   *Source: EEA Technical Report on Dioxin Releases; UNEP Stockholm Convention implementation reports*

## 2. Did the corporation conceal information about environmental harm?

**Answer: YES**. INEOS maintained opacity regarding emissions at its facilities, actively opposed data publication and used commercial confidentiality to restrict disclosure of environmental information.

1. **Secrecy around Grangemouth emission data** — SEPA and Scottish environmental organisations repeatedly indicated that INEOS provided the minimum required volume of emission data for its Grangemouth petrochemical complex, exploiting exceptions for "commercially sensitive" information. Independent atmospheric measurements yielded higher pollutant values than corporate reports.
   *Source: SEPA inspection records; Friends of the Earth Scotland, 2014*

2. **Opacity of toxicological data during fracking lobbying** — During the campaign for fracking permission in the UK, INEOS did not provide complete data on the composition of hydraulic fracturing fluids, citing commercial confidentiality. Regulators Health and Safety Executive and Environment Agency did not receive the information needed for a full risk assessment.
   *Source: UK HSE fracking consultation records; Frack Free United*

3. **Concealment of data on Antwerp incident (2019)** — An incident involving chemical release at the INEOS Antwerp facility in 2019 was initially not reported to local authorities in a timely manner. The Antwerp city administration subsequently required INEOS to tighten notification protocols.
   *Source: Antwerpse Brandweer; De Morgen, 2019*

---

## 3. Did the corporation evade accountability or obstruct justice?

**Answer: YES**. INEOS used legal threats, aggressive corporate strategies and political influence to evade environmental accountability.

1. **Lawsuits against activists protesting fracking (2018)** — INEOS filed lawsuits against environmental activists who attempted to stop transport convoys carrying fracking equipment in Yorkshire. Lawyers from rights organisations characterised these as SLAPP suits (strategic lawsuits against public participation), designed to intimidate civil activists.
   *Source: The Guardian, 2018; Liberty Human Rights; Frack Free Ryedale*

2. **Threat to close Grangemouth as leverage on government** — In 2013, amid a labour dispute with Unite union, INEOS announced its intention to close the Grangemouth plant, threatening 1,400 jobs. This forced the Scottish Government to make concessions to the company; environmental violation accusations were left without proper consideration.
   *Source: BBC News Scotland, 2013; Unite the Union; Scottish Parliament records*

3. **Opposition to SEPA penalty enforcement** — Several SEPA enforcement notices for emission standard violations at Grangemouth were challenged by INEOS through administrative procedures, delaying their implementation and creating a precedent that deterred the regulator from imposing strict sanctions in the future.
   *Source: SEPA enforcement register; Friends of the Earth Scotland*

## 4. Did the corporation violate the rights of indigenous peoples or vulnerable communities?

**Answer: YES**. INEOS deliberately planned fracking in areas with vulnerable communities, and the company's industrial facilities are located near working-class neighbourhoods.

1. **Fracking licences in low-income areas of Yorkshire and Lancashire** — INEOS acquired Petroleum Exploration and Development Licences (PEDL) for shale gas exploration in Yorkshire and Lancashire — regions with high proportions of low-income populations and rural communities dependent on clean water and farming. Local residents were not adequately consulted about potential groundwater risks.
   *Source: North Yorkshire County Council planning records; Frack Free United; Friends of the Earth UK*

2. **Grangemouth petrochemical cluster near socially vulnerable communities** — Grangemouth and adjacent communities of Boness and Polmont are among the most deprived areas of central Scotland. Residents of these areas face chronic exposure to emissions from INEOS's petrochemical complex with minimal resources to protect their interests.
   *Source: Scottish Index of Multiple Deprivation; SEPA community impact assessment*

3. **Fracking plans near farms and water supply wells in the UK** — INEOS licence blocks for fracking covered areas near farms and well water supplies in rural areas of the English Midlands and Northern England. Farming unions demanded legal guarantees of protection from contamination, which INEOS dismissed as "unnecessary".
   *Source: National Farmers Union (UK) statements; INEOS PEDL license records*

## 5. Has the corporation caused environmental damage to ecosystems?

**Answer: YES**. INEOS facilities emit thousands of tonnes of VOCs and toxic gases annually; the company's oil infrastructure carries risk to marine ecosystems.

1. **VOC and NOx emissions from Grangemouth and their impact on the Forth basin** — The EEA E-PRTR records the Grangemouth complex as one of Scotland's largest sources of VOCs and NOx. These emissions contribute to ground-level ozone formation and acid deposition, suppressing plant communities and aquatic ecosystems of the Firth of Forth basin.
   *Source: EEA E-PRTR; SEPA Air Quality Annual Reports*

2. **Risks from INEOS oil infrastructure in the North Sea** — INEOS operates the Forties Pipeline System in the North Sea. Any pipeline rupture would create an environmental catastrophe for the North Sea ecosystem; several incidents required unplanned shutdowns.
   *Source: UK Oil and Gas Authority; OPRED (Offshore Petroleum Regulator for Environment and Decommissioning)*

3. **Chlorine and PVC precursor discharges into Runcorn water bodies** — The Inovyn plant in Runcorn discharges treated wastewater into the River Mersey. Environment Agency monitoring recorded periodic exceedances of permissible chloride and organic chloride concentrations in the plant's discharge.
   *Source: Environment Agency (UK) Water Quality Monitoring; Mersey Basin Campaign*

## 6. Has the corporation engaged in financial manipulation or evasion of penalties?

**Answer: YES**. INEOS's tax structure through Switzerland is one of the most aggressive in the corporate world, enabling it to minimise taxes in countries where it operates.

1. **Tax optimisation through Switzerland** — INEOS was long managed through a holding structure registered in Switzerland (canton of Vaud), enabling it to minimise corporate tax in the UK. The Guardian and Tax Justice Network described INEOS's structure as one of the most aggressive tax planning schemes among European industrial companies.
   *Source: Tax Justice Network; The Guardian investigation, 2013; HMRC records*

2. **Using the Grangemouth closure threat to obtain state subsidies** — In 2013, the threat to close Grangemouth forced the Scottish and UK governments to offer INEOS a £125 million subsidy package. Critics characterised this as corporate blackmail that allowed the company to obtain public funds while avoiding investment from its own profits.
   *Source: Scottish Government press releases; BBC News, 2013; Unite the Union*

3. **Minimal fines for environmental violations in the UK** — The total fines imposed on INEOS by SEPA and the Environment Agency for emission violations in the UK are disproportionately small compared to the company's annual revenue exceeding $60 billion. This creates an economic incentive to violate rather than prevent violations.
   *Source: SEPA enforcement register; Environment Agency data; INEOS annual results*

---

## 7. Has the corporation lobbied against environmental standards?

**Answer: YES**. INEOS is one of Europe's most aggressive corporate lobbyists in the areas of fracking, climate regulation and industrial emission standards.

1. **Funding the campaign to lift the fracking ban in the UK** — Jim Ratcliffe, INEOS founder and owner, personally and through corporate structures funded a PR campaign against the fracking moratorium introduced by the British government following seismic incidents in Lancashire in 2019. INEOS acquired more than 1,000 licence blocks for shale gas exploration in the UK.
   *Source: INEOS fracking campaign; UK Electoral Commission; Frack Free United*

2. **Lobbying against EU climate targets for petrochemicals** — Through CEFIC, INEOS sought to soften the requirements of the Industrial Emissions Directive (IED) and expand exceptions for petrochemical production within the EU Emissions Trading System (ETS).
   *Source: CEFIC lobbying positions; Corporate Europe Observatory; EU transparency register*

3. **Opposition to mandatory PFAS reporting** — INEOS manufactures fluorinated compounds and through industry associations opposed expansion of mandatory PFAS emission reporting in the EU, seeking to exclude certain "specialist" fluorine-containing compounds from REACH regulation.
   *Source: REACH docket; Corporate Europe Observatory PFAS investigation, 2021*

---

## 8. Does the corporation exhibit repeated or ongoing violations?

**Answer: YES**. INEOS facilities record repeated emission standard violations, and lobbying for fracking continued until the final introduction of the ban.

1. **Repeated emission standard exceedances at Grangemouth** — SEPA recorded several episodes of permitted emission standard exceedances at Grangemouth facilities in 2013–2020. After each episode, the company submitted corrective plans, but violations recurred.
   *Source: SEPA Grangemouth site records; SEPA Annual Compliance Assessment*

2. **Continued fracking lobbying after ban introduction** — Following the official fracking ban in the UK in 2019, INEOS continued to fund campaigns for its review, including lobbying during government transitions in 2022.
   *Source: INEOS press statements; The Guardian, 2022; UK Parliament records*

3. **Ongoing emissions at Antwerp facilities** — INEOS/Inovyn Antwerp facilities continue to feature among the region's largest industrial emission sources according to E-PRTR data for 2019–2022. A number of indicators show no sustained reduction despite declared investments in pollution control equipment.
   *Source: EEA E-PRTR 2019–2022; VMM (Flemish Environment Agency)*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Mass harm to human lives**: INEOS — Europe's largest private chemical conglomerate — systematically emits toxic substances (VOCs, chlorine, dioxins) at its facilities in the UK, Belgium and Germany, putting health of adjacent populations at risk.
2. **Concealment of harm**: Grangemouth emission data opacity; incomplete fracking fluid composition data; delayed reporting of the 2019 Antwerp incident.
3. **Evasion of accountability**: SLAPP suits against environmental activists; corporate blackmail using Grangemouth closure threat; administrative challenges to SEPA enforcement notices.
4. **Violations against vulnerable communities**: Fracking licences targeting low-income areas of Yorkshire and Lancashire; Grangemouth sited in Scotland's most deprived communities.
5. **Ecosystem damage**: Grangemouth is one of Scotland's largest VOC and NOx sources; Forties Pipeline System poses North Sea risks; Runcorn chloride discharges into the Mersey.
6. **Financial manipulation**: Aggressive Swiss tax structure; £125 million government subsidies obtained through closure threat; minimal fines disproportionate to revenues.
7. **Lobbying against standards**: Fracking ban opposition campaign; softening IED requirements; opposing PFAS mandatory reporting under REACH.
8. **Ongoing violations**: Repeated Grangemouth emission exceedances; continued fracking lobbying post-ban; Antwerp facilities among region's largest emitters.
