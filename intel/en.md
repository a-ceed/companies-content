Intel and Toxic Silicon Valley

Semiconductor manufacturing is one of the most chemically intensive industrial processes. Intel's factories use hundreds of toxic chemical substances: solvents, acids, organometallic compounds, fluorinated gases — potent greenhouse substances.

In Silicon Valley (California), historical Intel and other semiconductor company production sites created more than ten EPA Superfund sites. Trichloroethylene and 1,1,1-trichloroethane groundwater contamination beneath Santa Clara was discovered in the early 1980s; in 1986, one of the first major environmental scandals in the technology industry emerged.

Intel's plants in Arizona — one of the driest US states — consume billions of gallons of ultrapure water annually. In conditions of chronic drought, this provokes acute conflicts with local farmers and indigenous peoples over water resources.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Groundwater contamination with toxic chemicals in Silicon Valley is documented and included in the EPA Superfund registry.

1. **Trichloroethylene contamination in Santa Clara (1980s)** — Intel facilities in Santa Clara (California) were among the first "technology" EPA Superfund sites. Groundwater contamination with trichloroethylene (TCE) — a carcinogen — was discovered in the 1980s; TCE was subsequently linked to cancer cases among Silicon Valley residents.
   *Source: EPA Superfund — Intel Sites; Santa Clara EPA records*

2. **Occupational diseases among semiconductor manufacturing workers** — Workers' rights organisations in Silicon Valley (SVTC — Silicon Valley Toxics Coalition) documented elevated rates of reproductive disorders and cancer among women who worked in Intel "clean rooms" and similar facilities.
   *Source: Silicon Valley Toxics Coalition — "Toxic Emissions from the Electronics Industry"*

3. **Water conflict in Arizona** — Massive Intel plant construction in Chandler and Hillsboro (Arizona) creates enormous pressure on water supplies under conditions of chronic drought. The Gila River Indian Community and farmers indicate that Intel's expansion threatens their priority water rights.
   *Source: NRDC; Arizona Republic — Intel water*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Intel Superfund sites in California and standard violations in Israel are documented environmental incidents.

1. **Multiple Superfund sites in Silicon Valley** — EPA included several Intel facilities in the National Priorities List (NPL) Superfund. Cleanup of trichloroethylene and other solvent contamination has continued for decades; remediation costs amount to hundreds of millions of dollars.
   *Source: EPA Superfund NPL — Intel Sites*

2. **Discharge standard violations at Kiryat Gat plant (Israel)** — The Intel Fab 28 plant in Kiryat Gat has repeatedly received enforcement notices from the Israeli Ministry of Environmental Protection for exceeding discharge standards for industrial wastewater containing heavy metals.
   *Source: Israel Ministry of Environmental Protection records*

3. **Perfluorocompound (PFC) emissions — potent greenhouse gases** — Intel chip manufacturing involves PFC emissions (CF₄, C₂F₆) with global warming potential 7,000–12,000 times higher than CO₂. Absolute PFC emission volumes are declining but remain significant.
   *Source: Intel ESG Report; Semiconductor Climate Consortium data*

---

## 3. Has the corporation engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Intel declares climate targets while remaining silent about PFC emissions and the embedded carbon in chips.

1. **"Net Zero Greenhouse Gas Emissions by 2040" with growing PFC volumes** — Intel declared achievement of zero GHG emissions by 2040, but the Scope 3 target (embedded carbon in the supply chain) remains undefined. PFC emissions from the most advanced manufacturing processes may grow proportionally to volumes.
   *Source: Intel ESG Report 2023; Semiconductor Climate Consortium*

2. **"100% Renewable Electricity" through RECs** — Intel declares 100% renewable electricity through purchase of Renewable Energy Credits in the US, but in Israel and Ireland, where its largest plants operate, the grid renewable share is significantly lower.
   *Source: Intel ESG Report; EirGrid Ireland; Israel Electric Corporation*

3. **"Conflict-Free Minerals" labelling without full chain transparency** — Intel labels its products as using "conflict-free" minerals, creating an impression of a fully responsible supply chain, whereas complete transparency across the entire chain is practically unachievable.
   *Source: Intel Conflict Minerals Reports*

## 4. Does the corporation engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Intel actively publishes ESG reports and participates in industry climate initiatives.

1. **Annual Corporate Responsibility Reports** — Intel publishes detailed ESG reports, including data on emission intensity per $1 million revenue, water consumption and waste.
   *Source: Intel Corporate Responsibility Report 2023*

2. **Semiconductor Climate Consortium** — Intel is a co-founder of the SCC — an industry initiative for decarbonising the semiconductor industry.
   *Source: Semiconductor Climate Consortium*

3. **"RISE Strategy" (Responsible, Inclusive, Sustainable, Enabling)** — The RISE corporate strategy is Intel's umbrella ESG brand, used to attract investors and communicate sustainability commitments.
   *Source: Intel RISE Strategy*

## 5. Has the corporation won awards in global ESG competitions?

**Answer: YES**. Intel regularly receives ESG recognition.

1. **Inclusion in DJSI World** — Intel is in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index World as one of the technology sector leaders, used in communications with institutional investors.
   *Source: S&P Global DJSI*

2. **CDP A-List Climate** — Intel has repeatedly received CDP's highest "A" rating in the Climate Change category, used to attract ESG-mandated investors.
   *Source: CDP Climate Scores — Intel*

3. **FTSE4Good Index inclusion** — Intel is included in the FTSE4Good index for socially responsible investors, despite ongoing Superfund remediation at legacy California sites and water conflict in Arizona.
   *Source: FTSE Russell, FTSE4Good Index*

## 6. Has the corporation placed eco-labels on products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Intel chips are labelled in the context of "energy-efficient" and "green" technologies.

1. **"Energy Efficient" and "Low Power" processor labelling** — Intel Core processors are labelled as "energy-efficient", reducing device power consumption. However, manufacturing each chip requires significant water, chemical and energy resources that are not reflected in the labelling.
   *Source: IEEE Spectrum — embedded carbon in semiconductors*

2. **"Conflict-Free Minerals" labelling** — Intel labels its products as using "conflict-free" minerals, creating an impression of a fully responsible supply chain while complete transparency across the entire supply chain is practically unachievable.
   *Source: Intel Conflict Minerals Reports*

3. **"Eco-friendly" semiconductor manufacturing branding** — Intel markets its manufacturing facilities as among the most advanced environmentally, citing water recycling rates and energy efficiency metrics, while the environmental footprint of each new fab construction — costing $20+ billion — is enormous.
   *Source: Intel ESG Reports; Nature Electronics, embodied carbon analysis*

---

## 7. Has the corporation organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Intel's water restoration and tree-planting programmes are negligible compared to actual water consumption.

1. **"Net Positive Water" programme in Arizona** — Intel declares a goal of becoming a "Net Positive Water" company by 2030, supporting water body restoration projects in Arizona amounting to several thousand acre-feet. Meanwhile, the Chandler plant consumes millions of gallons of water per year.
   *Source: Intel ESG Report; Arizona Republic*

2. **Tree planting near plants** — The company funds tree planting and desert ecosystem restoration near its plants in Arizona and Oregon. The scale is incomparable with the ecological footprint of plant construction and operation.
   *Source: Intel Community Programs*

3. **Employee volunteer environmental days** — Intel organises corporate environmental volunteering events at its campuses worldwide. These events are marketed as part of Intel's community responsibility while having no bearing on the Superfund contamination from decades of semiconductor production.
   *Source: Intel Corporate Responsibility Report*

## 8. Has the corporation lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Intel lobbies for semiconductor subsidies as "climate infrastructure".

1. **Lobbying the CHIPS Act as a "climate" law** — Intel actively lobbied for the CHIPS and Science Act (2022), positioning US plant construction as a climate-responsible decision (reducing transport emissions from Asian imports). Meanwhile, the construction of each new plant costing $20+ billion carries an enormous environmental footprint.
   *Source: Federal lobbying disclosures; CHIPS Act advocacy*

2. **"Chips = climate solution" narrative** — Intel through the Semiconductor Industry Association (SIA) promotes the thesis that semiconductors are a key decarbonisation tool (smart grids, AI for climate, EV chips), pushing aside discussion of its own manufacturing environmental footprint.
   *Source: SIA policy papers; Intel Government Affairs*

3. **Presenting Arizona expansion as "green manufacturing" despite water crisis** — Intel's public communications around its massive Arizona expansion framed the multi-billion dollar investment as a model of sustainable manufacturing, without adequately addressing the water security concerns of the Gila River Indian Community and local farmers operating under prior appropriation water rights.
   *Source: Arizona Republic; Gila River Indian Community statements; Intel press releases*

7. **Negligible eco-events**: "Net Positive Water" projects incomparable with Arizona plant water consumption; tree-planting dwarfed by fab construction footprint.
8. **Lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories**: CHIPS Act as "climate" legislation; "chips = decarbonisation" narrative deflecting from manufacturing footprint; Arizona expansion framed as "green manufacturing" during water crisis.
