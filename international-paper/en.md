International Paper: Pulp, Paper and Dioxins

International Paper — the world's largest paper company — processes millions of tonnes of timber annually. The traditional chlorine pulp bleaching process creates dioxins — highly toxic persistent organic pollutants that accumulate in fish and cause cancer.

In states of the American South (Alabama, Mississippi, Georgia), International Paper's pulp mills have historically been located near low-income African American communities. EPA monitoring recorded exceedances of permissible dioxin levels in fish from adjacent rivers.

In Brazil, an IP subsidiary manages eucalyptus plantations in São Paulo state. Monoculture eucalyptus plantations consume enormous quantities of water and do not support biodiversity. Local communities accuse the company of draining springs and streams.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Dioxin contamination of rivers near International Paper plants caused direct harm to the health of thousands of people, primarily low-income communities in the American South.

1. **Dioxin contamination of the Pigeon River (North Carolina — Tennessee, 1970s–1990s)** — The Champion International plant (later absorbed by IP) in Canton, North Carolina discharged pulp effluent into the Pigeon River, turning it brown and contaminating it with dioxins. Residents downstream in Tennessee for decades reported rising cancer rates and skin problems. Tennessee regulators repeatedly documented exceedances of dioxin standards in fish tissue.
   *Source: EPA Enforcement Records; Knoxville News-Sentinel*

2. **Drinking water contamination near the Louisiana plant (1990s–2000s)** — The IP plant in Bascom, Louisiana discharged into the Red River wastewater with elevated chlorophenol and AOX content. African American communities whose water supply depended on surface water intakes documented skin diseases in children and deteriorating water quality.
   *Source: Louisiana DEQ Records; NAACP Environmental Justice Report*

3. **Fish dioxin contamination in states of the American South** — EPA repeatedly issued warnings limiting fish consumption from rivers near IP plants in Alabama, Mississippi and Georgia due to dioxin standard exceedances. For low-income families relying on fish as a primary protein source, this meant direct health damage.
   *Source: EPA Fish Advisory Program; Environmental Working Group, 2001*

---

## 2. Did the corporation deliberately conceal information about environmental or social harm?

**Answer: YES**. International Paper used legal mechanisms and settlement procedures to restrict public disclosure of harm to the environment and human health.

1. **Years-long denial of Pigeon River contamination** — For more than twenty years, Champion International (the future part of IP) officially denied any link between plant emissions and the deteriorating health of Tennessee residents. The company challenged scientific data, hired independent consultants to refute environmental monitoring results, and delayed legal proceedings.
   *Source: Knoxville News-Sentinel, investigative series; EPA Administrative Record*

2. **Confidential settlements with affected communities** — IP and affiliated companies repeatedly settled lawsuits from affected communities with non-disclosure conditions, obstructing the accumulation of public evidence and the informing of other communities about risks.
   *Source: Environmental Justice Law Review; EWG analysis*

3. **Underreporting emissions in TRI (Toxic Release Inventory) reporting** — EPA audits identified discrepancies between company-declared hazardous compound emissions and independent measurements. Toxic Release Inventory data systematically did not reflect the real scale of contamination.
   *Source: EPA TRI Enforcement Actions; EWG Scorecard*

---

## 3. Were the corporation's activities associated with violations of the rights of indigenous or local communities?

**Answer: YES**. International Paper and its predecessors' plants disproportionately affected low-income African American communities — a classic manifestation of environmental injustice.

1. **"Cancer Alley" — plant siting in African American areas of Louisiana** — IP plants in Louisiana are located in the so-called "Cancer Alley" — an industrial corridor along the Mississippi with a disproportionately high proportion of African American residents. Studies record significantly higher cancer rates in these areas compared to white suburbs.
   *Source: EPA EJScreen; Tulane Environmental Law Clinic*

2. **Deforestation on territories with historical tribal claims in the American South** — IP's logging in Alabama and Mississippi affected territories with historical indigenous claims, including the Chickasaw and Choctaw tribes. Prior community consultations were systematically ignored.
   *Source: NRDC Forest Report; Earthjustice submissions*

3. **Brazil: displacement of smallholder farmers by plantations** — IP's Brazilian subsidiary (São Paulo state) expanded eucalyptus plantations at the expense of smallholder farmers' land, using lease and purchase mechanisms under pressure. Local agricultural unions documented cases of forced resettlement.
   *Source: Via Campesina Brazil; CPT, 2015*

---

## 4. What is the corporation's contribution to climate change?

**Answer: SIGNIFICANT**. International Paper is one of the world's largest consumers of forest resources; the felling and processing of timber creates a significant carbon footprint.

1. **Felling of Canadian boreal forests** — IP purchased pulp and wood chips from Canadian suppliers logging in the boreal forests of Ontario and Quebec. The boreal forest is the largest terrestrial carbon reservoir: each felled hectare releases dozens of tonnes of carbon.
   *Source: NRDC Boreal Forest Campaign; ForestEthics*

2. **Emissions from energy-intensive pulp production** — Pulp and paper production is one of the most energy-intensive industrial processes. IP plants collectively emit millions of tonnes of CO₂ annually. According to CDP reporting data, IP's direct and indirect emissions exceeded 10 million tonnes of CO₂ equivalent annually during the 2010s.
   *Source: CDP Climate Disclosure; IP Sustainability Report*

3. **Loss of carbon sequestration capacity from felled forests** — IP managed or licensed logging on millions of hectares of forest in the US. Forest loss means not only the release of stored carbon but also the loss of future ecosystem sequestration capacity — a "double" climate impact.
   *Source: Greenpeace USA Forest Campaign; American Forests Report*

---

## 5. How significant was the ecosystem destruction?

**Answer: SIGNIFICANT**. International Paper damaged forest and water ecosystems across vast territories in the US, Canada and Brazil.

1. **Superfund sites — contaminated industrial areas in the US** — IP and predecessor companies are responsible parties at several EPA Superfund sites — the most contaminated areas in the US. In particular, PCB (polychlorinated biphenyl) contamination at sites in Alabama and Tennessee required large-scale remediation.
   *Source: EPA Superfund Site Database; CERCLA Records*

2. **Destruction of bottomland forests in the American South** — IP's logging in river basins in Alabama, Mississippi and Arkansas destroyed significant areas of bottomland forests — among the most biodiverse ecosystems in North America, critically important for migratory bird wintering.
   *Source: Audubon Society; U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service Bottomland Hardwood Reports*

3. **River contamination with pulp effluent** — AOX (adsorbable organic halogens), suspended solids and wastewater with high biological oxygen demand (BOD) from IP plants led to fish kills, algal blooms and chronic oxygen depletion in rivers near production facilities.
   *Source: EPA Effluent Monitoring Reports; Clean Water Act Enforcement*

---

## 6. Did the corporation use toxic substances posing long-term hazards?

**Answer: YES**. International Paper's production processes historically created and discharged a wide range of persistent toxic compounds.

1. **Dioxins and furans from chlorine bleaching** — Before transitioning to ECF and TCF (totally chlorine-free bleaching) technologies, IP plants used molecular chlorine, producing dioxins and chlorinated furans. These compounds are among the most toxic substances known to science: carcinogens, endocrine disruptors, accumulating in food chains.
   *Source: EPA Dioxin Reassessment; IARC Monographs*

2. **PCBs at historical IP sites** — At a number of IP and predecessor company sites, soil and groundwater contamination with PCBs (polychlorinated biphenyls) has been identified — persistent organic pollutants banned in most countries but persisting in the environment for decades.
   *Source: EPA Superfund Database; ATSDR Site Assessments*

3. **Heavy metals in river sediments** — Monitoring of river sediments near IP plants in Alabama and Georgia detected elevated concentrations of mercury, cadmium and lead — by-products of manufacturing processes and runoff from waste storage sites.
   *Source: USGS Sediment Survey; EPA Region 4 Reports*

---

## 7. What actions have authorities and regulators taken against the corporation?

**Answer: PARTIAL**. A number of significant fines and agreements were reached, but systemic accountability for accumulated environmental damage did not follow.

1. **EPA consent decree and Clean Water Act penalties (2000s)** — As a result of EPA investigations, International Paper paid tens of millions of dollars for violations of the Clean Water Act (CWA) and Clean Air Act (CAA). Plants in Louisiana and Alabama received orders to install additional treatment systems.
   *Source: EPA Enforcement and Compliance Database*

2. **Settlement of the Pigeon River contamination lawsuit (1998)** — After years of litigation, Champion International (the future part of IP) paid compensation to Tennessee residents and funded Pigeon River monitoring, but with the condition that the payment was not an admission of liability for health harm.
   *Source: Tennessee Department of Environment and Conservation; AP, 1998*

3. **Inclusion of IP sites in the EPA Superfund list** — Several sites associated with IP are on the EPA National Priorities List (NPL). This entails a remediation obligation, but the process stretches over decades during which contamination continues to affect adjacent areas.
   *Source: EPA National Priorities List; CERCLA Enforcement*

---

## 8. What is the corporation's position on sustainable development?

**Answer: DECLARATORY**. International Paper publicly adopts sustainable forestry and emission reduction targets, but real results significantly lag behind stated commitments.

1. **FSC/SFI certification without real harm reduction** — IP positions a significant portion of its forest holdings as "responsibly certified". Critics, however, note that the SFI (Sustainable Forestry Initiative) standard — preferred by IP — is significantly less rigorous than FSC and allows practices incompatible with biodiversity conservation.
   *Source: NRDC Forest Certification Report; Dogwood Alliance*

2. **Carbon neutrality targets that exclude Scope 3** — IP publicly declared emission reduction targets, but these covered only direct emissions (Scope 1) and indirect energy emissions (Scope 2), leaving outside their scope the large-scale Scope 3 emissions from forest harvesting, transport and product disposal.
   *Source: IP Sustainability Report; CDP Forest Questionnaire*

3. **Continuing purchases from unsustainable suppliers** — Despite declared responsible procurement policies, NGO investigations documented that IP's supply chain included suppliers logging in ecologically valuable forests of Canada and Russia.
   *Source: ForestEthics Supply Chain Report; Greenpeace Canada*

---

## Summary of Answers

1. **Dioxin contamination**: Decades of chlorine pulp bleaching poisoned rivers with dioxins and chlorophenols, directly harming thousands of people, primarily low-income African American communities.
2. **Environmental injustice**: IP plants were systematically located in "cancer alleys" — industrial zones with a disproportionately high proportion of non-white population lacking political resources to protect their rights.
3. **Superfund contamination**: PCBs, heavy metals and other toxic compounds at historical IP sites require decades of costly remediation.
4. **Ecosystem destruction**: Logging of bottomland forests in the American South and boreal forests of Canada destroyed critically important habitats and released stored carbon into the atmosphere.
5. **Concealment of information**: Years-long denial of the link between emissions and health harm, confidential settlements and underreporting of data obstructed corporate accountability.
6. **Community rights violations**: Disproportionate impact on African American and indigenous communities without adequate compensation or consultation.
7. **Climate damage**: IP-scale forest resource consumption creates millions of tonnes of CO₂ emissions annually, with no Scope 3 targets.
8. **Declaratory sustainability**: Preference for weaker certification standards (SFI over FSC) and incomplete emission target coverage evidence a systemic gap between stated and real commitments.
