Kimberly-Clark and Toilet Paper from Old-Growth Forests

NRDC and other environmental organisations regularly criticise Kimberly-Clark for using virgin pulp from Canadian boreal forests to produce Kleenex and Scott toilet paper. The boreal forest — the Earth's largest terrestrial biome — is one of the planet's most important carbon stores.

In 2009, NRDC published the report "Toilet Paper: Forest Flushed Away", according to which producing one roll of toilet paper from virgin pulp requires 140 litres of water and significantly more timber than when using recycled raw materials.

Kimberly-Clark repeatedly appeared on lists of companies with the worst "forest responsibility ratings". After extended negotiations and a Greenpeace campaign, the company agreed in 2009–2011 to increase the share of certified and recycled pulp to 40%, but did not completely abandon virgin timber.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Kimberly-Clark's production is linked to logging of boreal and tropical forests, which destroys the habitat of indigenous peoples of Canada and eliminates forest ecosystems providing clean air and water for millions of people. Plastic components in Huggies nappies and wet wipes are discharged into the environment in enormous volumes.

1. **Water contamination near the Pennsylvania plant (2014)** — The Kimberly-Clark plant in New Milford, Pennsylvania received a violation from the US Environmental Protection Agency for discharging wastewater exceeding permissible biological oxygen demand and suspended solids levels into the Susquehanna River. This endangered the drinking water of several downstream communities.
   *Source: Pennsylvania DEP Enforcement Actions, 2014*

2. **Destruction of indigenous peoples' territories in Canada (2009–2019)** — Clearing of boreal forests in Ontario and Quebec to harvest pulp for Kimberly-Clark products destroyed hunting and fishing lands of Cree and Ojibwe communities. According to NRDC, forests used by the company's suppliers included territories under traditional indigenous use without obtaining their consent.
   *Source: NRDC "The Issue with Tissue", 2019*

3. **Toxic effluent at the Texas plant (2019)** — The Kimberly-Clark facility in New Braunfels, Texas was cited by state regulators for discharging wastewater with elevated concentrations of chemical bleaching agents (chlorine and its compounds) into the Guadalupe River. Local fishermen and water protection organisations reported deteriorating water quality.
   *Source: Texas Commission on Environmental Quality, 2019*

## 2. Did the corporation destroy ecosystems?

**Answer: YES**. Kimberly-Clark is one of the largest consumers of virgin pulp from Canadian boreal forests. These forests are unique ecosystems storing enormous carbon reserves and home to hundreds of bird, mammal and fish species.

1. **Boreal forest logging in Ontario and Quebec (2000s — present)** — Pulp suppliers for Kimberly-Clark conduct large-scale logging in Canadian boreal forests. According to NRDC (report "The Issue with Tissue", 2020), Kimberly-Clark uses one of the lowest percentages of recycled raw material among major tissue product manufacturers — approximately 20% at time of publication. Boreal forest recovers over centuries.
   *Source: NRDC "The Issue with Tissue", 2020*

2. **Wetland destruction in pulp logging zones (2010s)** — Forest clearing for pulp harvesting in Quebec watersheds led to draining of peat moss bogs — critically important carbon stores and river fish spawning habitats. Greenpeace documented road construction into intact forest tracts by Kimberly-Clark suppliers.
   *Source: Greenpeace "Kleercut" campaign report, 2004–2009*

3. **Waterway contamination at South Korean plant (2016)** — The Kimberly-Clark facility in Gimcheon was cited by Korean environmental authorities for discharging chemical paper production effluent (fibre suspension, dyes) into a local watercourse. The incident led to fish kills downstream.
   *Source: Korea Ministry of Environment Enforcement Report, 2016*

## 3. Did the corporation violate the rights of indigenous peoples?

**Answer: YES**. Pulp harvesting for Kimberly-Clark products is conducted on traditional lands of Canadian indigenous peoples without their free, prior and informed consent, violating the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP).

1. **Pulp harvesting on Cree lands in Ontario without consultation (2000–2010)** — NRDC and Dogwood Alliance documented that Kimberly-Clark suppliers conducted logging in Ontario on territories under the traditional governance of the Cree Nation without adequate consultation or community consent, as required by Canadian indigenous law.
   *Source: NRDC, Dogwood Alliance joint advocacy, 2009*

2. **Ignoring protests from Ojibwe communities in Quebec (2008)** — Ojibwe communities of Quebec province filed official objections against industrial logging by Kimberly-Clark pulp suppliers on their ancestral hunting grounds. Kimberly-Clark did not respond to the appeals and continued purchasing from these suppliers.
   *Source: Forest Peoples Programme, 2008*

3. **Refusal of independent supply chain audit in Indonesia (2015)** — Rainforest Action Network established that a number of Kimberly-Clark pulp suppliers in Indonesia were logging on territories where indigenous communities contested land use rights. Kimberly-Clark refused to conduct an independent audit of raw material origins.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network, 2015*

## 4. Did the corporation mislead consumers or authorities?

**Answer: YES**. Kimberly-Clark systematically engaged in "green" marketing, exaggerating the environmental friendliness of its products and not disclosing information about the real origin of raw materials.

1. **"Sustainable" labelling without independent verification (2010–2015)** — Kimberly-Clark used "environmental responsibility" logos and the term "sustainable" on Scott and Kleenex product packaging without having verifiable data on the proportion of recycled or certified raw material. The US Federal Trade Commission (FTC) warned that such labelling misleads consumers.
   *Source: FTC Green Guides, 2012; NRDC*

2. **Misleading regarding wet wipe biodegradability (2014)** — A Kimberly-Clark wet wipe series was sold with "flushable" labelling, although independent tests showed they do not break down in sewage systems and clog treatment plants. Class action lawsuits were filed against the company in the US and UK.
   *Source: NYC DEP lawsuit, 2015; Australian CHOICE, 2014*

3. **Misrepresentation of recycled raw material data during NRDC negotiations (2009)** — During negotiations on the "Forest Responsibility Agreement", Kimberly-Clark overstated the proportion of certified pulp in its supplies. NRDC subsequently established that real figures were significantly lower than declared.
   *Source: NRDC, Greenpeace joint assessment, 2011*

## 5. Were there systematic safety violations at the corporation?

**Answer: YES**. Kimberly-Clark production facilities worldwide recorded occupational health and environmental safety violations.

1. **Explosion and fire at Texas plant (2012)** — An explosion occurred at the Kimberly-Clark facility in Marlin, Texas, caused by a violation of flammable chemical storage regulations used in paper product manufacturing. Several workers were injured; the plant was shut down by the regulator for inspection.
   *Source: OSHA Incident Report, 2012*

2. **Systematic occupational safety violations at Mexican plants (2015–2017)** — Inspections by Mexico's Ministry of Labour found fire safety and chemical storage violations at three Kimberly-Clark facilities in Guanajuato and Mexico states. Workers reported the absence of adequate protective equipment when handling bleaching agents.
   *Source: STPS (Secretaría del Trabajo) Mexico, 2017*

3. **Hazardous waste handling violations in Wisconsin (2018)** — The US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) issued an enforcement notice to the Kimberly-Clark plant in Neenah, Wisconsin for improper storage and disposal of chemical sludge — a production by-product. The company paid a fine of $175,000.
   *Source: EPA Region 5 Enforcement, 2018*

## 6. Did the corporation lobby against environmental standards?

**Answer: YES**. Kimberly-Clark actively participated in lobbying against tightening standards for the pulp and paper industry and requirements for the composition of hygiene products.

1. **Opposition to recycled content standards in the US (2009–2011)** — Through the American Forest & Paper Association (AF&PA), Kimberly-Clark opposed legislative initiatives that would have required toilet paper manufacturers to use a minimum proportion of recycled raw material. The company funded lobbying efforts at a rate of $2–3 million per year.
   *Source: OpenSecrets lobbying data; AF&PA filings*

2. **Lobbying against EU single-use plastics standards (2018–2019)** — Before adoption of the EU Single-Use Plastics Directive, Kimberly-Clark through industry associations sought to exclude wet wipes and nappies from the list of regulated products, which would have significantly weakened requirements for plastic content in its products.
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory, 2019*

3. **Opposition to chemical composition disclosure requirements in California (2015–2017)** — Kimberly-Clark funded a campaign against California Senate Bill 312, which would have required manufacturers to disclose all chemical ingredients in hygiene products, including fragrances and preservatives capable of causing allergic reactions.
   *Source: California Legislature Records; EWG (Environmental Working Group)*

## 7. Did the corporation's activities contribute to climate change?

**Answer: YES**. Logging of Canadian boreal forests for Kimberly-Clark's needs leads to the release of enormous quantities of carbon stored in trees and peatlands. Production processes are also carbon-intensive.

1. **Logging of carbon stores in boreal forest (ongoing)** — Canada's boreal forest stores twice as much carbon per unit area as tropical forests. NRDC estimates that producing toilet paper from virgin Canadian pulp leads to emissions of 10 to 17 kg of CO₂ equivalent per roll more than when using recycled raw material. Kimberly-Clark's annual sales volume is tens of billions of rolls.
   *Source: NRDC "The Issue with Tissue", 2020*

2. **High energy consumption at coal-powered plants (2010–2020)** — A number of Kimberly-Clark plants in Asia, including South Korea and India, were partly supplied by coal power station electricity during the 2010s. Total Scope 2 company emissions exceeded 1 million tonnes of CO₂ per year.
   *Source: Kimberly-Clark Sustainability Report, 2020; CDP disclosure*

3. **Refusal to transition to 100% recycled raw material despite available technology (ongoing)** — Although companies such as Who Gives A Crap and Seventh Generation have long produced toilet paper 100% from recycled raw material, Kimberly-Clark continues to use virgin pulp in its key brands (Kleenex, Scott), citing "consumer preferences for softness", thereby deliberately maintaining a carbon-intensive model.
   *Source: NRDC Tissue Scorecard, 2022*

## 8. Did the corporation externalise environmental costs onto society?

**Answer: YES**. The real environmental and social costs of Kimberly-Clark product manufacturing — from forest logging to nappy disposal — are paid by the state, municipalities and local communities, not the company.

1. **Huggies nappy disposal costs paid by municipalities (ongoing)** — Disposable nappies are the third largest type of household waste in US landfills. Each nappy takes up to 500 years to decompose. Neither Kimberly-Clark nor other manufacturers contribute to funding specialist recycling infrastructure. Annual US municipal costs for nappy landfilling exceed $1 billion.
   *Source: EPA Municipal Solid Waste data; NRDC*

2. **River cleanup after industrial plant effluent at state expense (2010–2018)** — Several waterway contamination incidents near Kimberly-Clark plants in the US, Mexico and South Korea required intervention by state environmental services. Monitoring and partial watercourse cleanup costs were borne by regional budgets.
   *Source: Pennsylvania DEP, Texas CEQ, Korea MOE enforcement records*

3. **Absence of extended producer responsibility programmes in most markets (ongoing)** — Despite the EU introducing extended producer responsibility (EPR) for wet wipes and personal hygiene items, Kimberly-Clark actively opposes introducing similar mechanisms in the US, shifting all waste collection and disposal costs onto consumers and municipalities.
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory; US PIRG*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Boreal forest destruction**: Kimberly-Clark remains one of the largest consumers of virgin Canadian pulp, despite decades of pressure from NRDC and Greenpeace.
2. **Indigenous peoples' rights violations**: Pulp harvesting occurs on traditional Cree and Ojibwe lands without their consent.
3. **Greenwashing**: The company uses environmental labels without verifiable raw material origin data.
4. **Plastic pollution**: Huggies nappies and wet wipes create enormous volumes of slowly degrading waste paid for by municipalities.
5. **Water contamination**: Plants in the US, Mexico and South Korea repeatedly violated wastewater discharge standards.
6. **Climate change contribution**: Logging of boreal carbon stores and carbon-intensive production create a significant climate footprint.
7. **Lobbying**: The company systematically opposes tightening environmental standards for its industry in the US and EU.
8. **Cost externalisation**: The real disposal cost of Kimberly-Clark products is borne by society, not the manufacturer.
