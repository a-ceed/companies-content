Soy and palm oil trading from active deforestation regions in South America.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Grain trading under conditions of armed conflict, water pollution at production facilities, and manipulation of food markets have directly harmed human lives and health.

1. **Grain trading from Ukrainian territory during the war via offshore chains (2022–2023)** — Journalists from Reuters and Politico documented that Louis Dreyfus continued export operations with Ukrainian grain through offshore trading structures, part of whose logistics indirectly passed through jurisdictions linked to Russian counterparties, despite international sanctions.
   *Source: Reuters, "Grain traders and the war in Ukraine", 2023; Politico, 2023*

2. **Water pollution at processing facilities in Argentina** — Louis Dreyfus plants in Rosario and Santa Fe province received orders from Argentine environmental authorities for contaminating drainage systems with petroleum products and soy processing waste. The Paraná River and its tributaries are used by local communities for fishing and water supply.
   *Source: Secretaría de Ambiente de Santa Fe; FARN (Fundación Ambiente y Recursos Naturales), 2016*

3. **Manipulation of prices on food exchanges** — Louis Dreyfus — a private company with minimal transparency — has repeatedly been at the centre of regulatory suspicions of manipulation of wheat and corn futures contracts. In 2012, the US Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC) investigated LDC's positions on grain markets during the price spikes of 2010–2011.
   *Source: CFTC investigation records; FAO Food Price Index analysis, 2011*

## 2. Has the corporation been responsible for major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Louis Dreyfus, through its soy trade and subsidiary Calyx Agro, directly participates in the destruction of forests in the Cerrado, Gran Chaco, and Chiquitania.

1. **Calyx Agro and land acquisitions in the Cerrado (2018–2022)** — Calyx Agro, Louis Dreyfus's land fund, had by 2022 acquired more than 200,000 hectares of land in Brazil, a significant portion of which is located in the Cerrado biome. A Global Witness investigation (2022) showed that some purchased plots included territories cleared of forest.
   *Source: Global Witness, "Deforestation Dividends", 2022*

2. **Connection to fires in Bolivia (2019)** — Louis Dreyfus is among the top five exporters of Bolivian soy. The fires of 2019, which destroyed approximately 4 million hectares of Chiquitania and Amazon forests, were deliberately set to clear land for soy plantations. Bolivian authorities opened criminal cases against a number of landowner-suppliers.
   *Source: CEDHA, "Fuego y Soja en Bolivia", 2020; La Razón (Bolivia), 2019*

3. **Destruction of the Gran Chaco through Argentine soy exports** — Louis Dreyfus is a major processor and exporter of Argentine soy. Soy production in the Argentine Chaco annually destroys hundreds of thousands of hectares of one of the world's largest dry forests. LDC's infrastructure (ports, grain elevators) provides the economic viability of this expansion.
   *Source: WWF, "Gran Chaco Deforestation", 2021; Trase.earth*

## 3. Has the corporation applied or tolerated forced or child labour?

**Answer: YES**. Forced and child labour have been recorded in the cotton and sugarcane supply chains traded by Louis Dreyfus.

1. **Forced labour in cotton harvesting in Uzbekistan** — Louis Dreyfus is a major trader of Uzbek cotton. Throughout the 2010s, Uzbekistan systematically used a state system of forced labour during cotton harvesting, mobilising teachers, doctors, and students. Despite international campaigns, LDC continued trading operations with Uzbek cotton.
   *Source: Cotton Campaign; Human Rights Watch, "Uzbekistan Forced Labor", 2014–2019*

2. **Child labour in sugarcane supply chains in Brazil** — Louis Dreyfus is one of the world's largest sugar traders. Reporter Brasil (2015) documented cases where children worked in cane harvesting at suppliers of Brazilian sugar mills linked to LDC.
   *Source: Reporter Brasil, "O Açúcar e o Trabalho Infantil", 2015*

3. **Labour conditions at ports and grain storage facilities** — In Argentina, the port workers' union (SOEA) documented Louis Dreyfus's use of subcontractors who violated labour law: workers without official registration, night shifts without overtime pay, undocumented migrants.
   *Source: SOEA (Sindicato de Obreros y Empleados Aceiteros), Argentina, 2017*

## 4. Has the corporation violated the rights of indigenous peoples or local communities?

**Answer: YES**. The subsidiary Calyx Agro acquired land in zones of unresolved land conflicts, and soy trading from Bolivia fuelled fires on indigenous territories.

1. **Calyx Agro and land grabs in conflict zones** — Global Witness (2022) established that a number of plots acquired by Calyx Agro in Brazil were located in zones of active land conflicts where local communities disputed ownership. The practice of "grilagem" (forging land documents) is widespread in Brazil's agricultural sector.
   *Source: Global Witness, "Deforestation Dividends", 2022*

2. **Fires in Bolivia and the territory of the Chiquitano people** — The 2019 fires on lands of LDC suppliers destroyed forests that are the traditional territories of the Chiquitano people. CEDHA (Centre for Human Rights and Environment) documented that communities received no compensation and were deprived of their traditional sources of sustenance.
   *Source: CEDHA, 2020; Amnesty International Bolivia, 2020*

3. **Monopsony and price pressure on smallholder farmers in Brazil** — In regions where LDC is one of the few buyers of soy, the company holds a monopsonistic position. Studies by the Universidade Estadual de Campinas (UNICAMP, 2020) recorded that purchase prices in such areas are systematically below market rates, leading to the displacement of small farmers and concentration of land.
   *Source: UNICAMP, "Market Power in Brazilian Soy", 2020*

## 5. Has the corporation concealed information, falsified data, or misled the public?

**Answer: YES**. Louis Dreyfus uses its private company status as a tool for systematic evasion of public accountability.

1. **Opacity as a corporate strategy** — Louis Dreyfus is the only company among the ABCD group that has never been publicly listed. The company publishes a minimum of financial and operational information. In the Forest 500 transparency ranking, LDC consistently receives some of the lowest scores for supply chain disclosure.
   *Source: Forest 500 Annual Rankings, 2021–2023*

2. **Procurement without verification of soy origin** — Global Witness (2022) showed that LDC claims to have "tracking systems" but refuses to publish the methodology or verification results. Independent researchers have no way of checking whether procurement meets declared standards.
   *Source: Global Witness, 2022*

3. **Contradictions between ESG declarations and Calyx Agro's practices** — LDC's public materials present Calyx Agro as a "responsible investor" in "sustainable agriculture". Meanwhile, the fund's strategy — buying "raw" unprocessed land in the Cerrado and capitalising it through conversion to arable land — is in essence a financial instrument for stimulating deforestation.
   *Source: Global Witness, 2022; Mighty Earth Soy Scorecard, 2022*

## 6. Has the corporation evaded taxes or used illegal financial schemes?

**Answer: YES**. Registration in the Netherlands and active use of offshore trading structures allow LDC to minimise its tax burden in producing countries.

1. **Dutch holding structure** — Louis Dreyfus Company B.V. is registered in the Netherlands, benefiting from the preferential tax regime for holding companies. Trading profits are accumulated at the Dutch structure level, minimising taxes in Brazil, Argentina, and other operating countries.
   *Source: Tax Justice Network, 2022; LDC Annual Report (Dutch incorporation)*

2. **Transfer pricing in Argentine operations** — AFIP (Argentina) included Louis Dreyfus among companies under investigation on suspicion of using intra-corporate transfer pricing to understate taxable profits from grain and oilseed exports.
   *Source: AFIP Argentina; Reuters, 2013*

3. **Minimal tax burden relative to the scale of operations** — According to Tax Justice Network (2022), Louis Dreyfus's effective tax rate is significantly lower than nominal rates in countries of operation — a sign of systematic use of tax optimisation mechanisms.
   *Source: Tax Justice Network, "Big Ag, Big Tax Avoidance", 2022*

## 7. Has the corporation exerted unlawful or unethical influence on policy and regulators?

**Answer: YES**. LDC influences trade and agricultural policy in Brazil, Argentina, and on international platforms through industry associations.

1. **Lobbying against the EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR)** — Louis Dreyfus, through industry associations (ABIOVE, ANEC in Brazil; FEDIOL in Europe), actively lobbied to limit the scope of the EUDR, seeking to exclude the Cerrado biome from the definition of protected ecosystems.
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory, "Agribusiness lobbying on EUDR", 2022*

2. **Influence through the "Bancada Ruralista" in Brazil** — According to Transparência Brasil, structures affiliated with Louis Dreyfus financed deputies in the agricultural lobby who voted against tightening land legislation and expanding controls over soy trading.
   *Source: Transparência Brasil, 2018; TSE electoral financing data*

3. **Opposition to mandatory supply chain traceability** — In WTO negotiations and bilateral trade forums, Louis Dreyfus, through trade associations, opposed the introduction of mandatory traceability systems for agricultural products, citing "trade barriers".
   *Source: WTO Trade Policy Review; Global Witness, 2022*

## 8. Is there evidence of a systemic character of violations — repeated, recurrent, rather than isolated incidents?

**Answer: YES**. Louis Dreyfus has reproduced the model of "opaque private company + offshore structures + minimal public commitments" for many years.

1. **Consistently low transparency scores in all key ratings** — Forest 500, Mighty Earth Soy Scorecard, Chain Reaction Research — all leading independent assessments consistently assign LDC some of the lowest scores among ABCD traders. This is not a one-off failure, but a persistent characteristic.
   *Source: Forest 500, 2020–2023; Mighty Earth Soy Scorecard*

2. **Recurring tax investigations in multiple jurisdictions** — Tax claims against LDC have been recorded in Argentina, Brazil, and India in different years. The same transfer pricing mechanism is applied systematically, regardless of jurisdiction.
   *Source: Tax Justice Network; AFIP; Receita Federal*

3. **Long-term participation in the destruction of Bolivian forests** — Louis Dreyfus has been trading Bolivian soy since the early 2000s. Over this time, Bolivia has lost millions of hectares of forest in soy-producing areas. The 2019 fires were the culmination of this process, yet the company did not change its procurement policy.
   *Source: CEDHA, 2020; Trase.earth Bolivia data*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Deforestation through Calyx Agro**: LDC's subsidiary land structure deliberately purchases forested territories in the Cerrado, converting them into arable land — a financial model built into ecosystem destruction.
2. **Bolivian fires**: Trading soy from zones where the deliberate fires of 2019 destroyed 4 million hectares of forest, without changing procurement policy.
3. **Forced labour**: Participation in trading cotton from Uzbekistan with a state system of forced labour, and links to the "Lista Suja" in Brazil.
4. **Maximum opacity**: The only non-public company in the ABCD group — deliberately used to evade accountability.
5. **Tax schemes**: Dutch holding structure and transfer pricing minimise taxes in countries of actual production.
6. **Rights of indigenous peoples**: Procurement from areas where fires destroyed the territories of the Chiquitano people; land conflicts in Calyx Agro's operating zones.
7. **Political lobbying**: Opposition to the EUDR and expansion of forest protection norms in Brazil and the EU.
8. **Systemic character**: Consistently low transparency scores, recurring tax investigations, and years-long participation in forest destruction across all regions of operation.
