LyondellBasell: Refineries in Residential Neighbourhoods

LyondellBasell — one of the world's largest polypropylene and polyethylene manufacturers — owns the major Houston Refining plant in Houston, Texas. The facility is located in the core of Houston, where predominantly Spanish-speaking and African American residents with lower incomes live.

According to the Texas Commission on Environmental Quality, LyondellBasell's refinery repeatedly exceeded permissible benzene emission limits — a recognised carcinogen. In 2010, the company paid $700 million in fines and compensation under environmental lawsuits following bankruptcy.

In Rotterdam, the company's petrochemical facilities are among the largest sources of industrial emissions in Western Europe. The European Environment Agency regularly includes the Rotterdam industrial cluster in the list of areas with the highest industrial burden.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. LyondellBasell's Houston refinery and Rotterdam petrochemical facilities systematically emitted benzene, 1,3-butadiene, ethylene oxide and other carcinogens into residential air, elevating cancer risks for hundreds of thousands of people.

1. **Chronic benzene emission standard exceedances in Houston (2000s–2010s)** — The Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (TCEQ) documented numerous Houston Refining benzene emission violations. Benzene is a recognised carcinogen causing leukaemia; residents of Manor, East Houston and adjacent areas faced chronic exposure.
   *Source: TCEQ compliance records; EPA National Air Toxics Assessment; Houston Chronicle*

2. **"Accidental" emergency emissions from the refinery** — TCEQ data shows that Houston Refining recorded dozens of emergency events (flares, leaks, spills) in the 2000s–2010s, each time accompanied by multiple exceedances of toxic substance limits in atmospheric air. In 2012, a major emergency flare event led to resident health complaints.
   *Source: TCEQ event reports; ProPublica Air Pollution Monitor*

3. **Rotterdam cluster: VOC emissions and health damage** — LyondellBasell petrochemical facilities in Rotterdam and Maasluis annually emit thousands of tonnes of volatile organic compounds (VOCs). The European Environment Agency links chronic industrial VOC exposure to elevated respiratory disease and cancer risks in the Rotterdam industrial district population.
   *Source: EEA E-PRTR industrial emissions database; RIVM Rotterdam air quality studies*

## 2. Did the corporation conceal information about environmental harm?

**Answer: YES**. LyondellBasell and its predecessors did not fully disclose data on emergency emissions and standard violations, exploiting loopholes in reporting systems.

1. **Underreporting emergency emission volumes in TCEQ reporting** — A ProPublica and Texas Tribune investigation showed that a number of Houston petrochemical companies, including the Lyondell refinery, systematically understated emergency emission volumes in mandatory reports, exploiting vague definitions of "emergency" events and measurement imprecision.
   *Source: ProPublica/Texas Tribune "Poisoned Place", 2012; TCEQ audit records*

2. **Opacity during bankruptcy (2009)** — When Lyondell Chemical filed for bankruptcy in 2009, documents on the company's environmental obligations were submitted to the court incompletely. A number of environmental NGOs and EPA stated the difficulty of obtaining a reliable picture of the company's environmental commitments.
   *Source: US Bankruptcy Court, Southern District of New York; EPA comments on Lyondell bankruptcy*

3. **Delayed notification of residents about violations** — Several emergency emission events at Houston Refining were not reported in a timely manner to local authorities and residents of adjacent neighbourhoods, violating emergency notification legislation requirements.
   *Source: Harris County Pollution Control; Texas RRC; local advocacy group records*

---

## 3. Did the corporation evade accountability or obstruct justice?

**Answer: YES**. LyondellBasell's 2009 bankruptcy was used to minimise environmental obligations, and the company delayed compliance with regulatory enforcement notices.

1. **2009 bankruptcy as an instrument for evading environmental obligations** — LyondellBasell filed for bankruptcy in January 2009, which immediately froze a number of EPA and TCEQ environmental enforcement notices. On emerging from bankruptcy in May 2010, the company secured court restructuring of some environmental obligations.
   *Source: US Bankruptcy Court, SDNY; EPA comments on reorganization plan*

2. **Protracted settlement of EPA lawsuit over benzene emissions** — EPA initiated proceedings over systematic benzene emission violations at Houston Refining. Negotiations stretched over several years; the final agreement provided for relatively small fines.
   *Source: EPA enforcement action records; DOJ press releases*

3. **Contesting Houston resident claims** — Several class action lawsuits from residents of adjacent neighbourhoods claiming health damage from chronic benzene emissions were contested by the company, which sought dismissal on procedural grounds or minimal payments.
   *Source: Harris County District Court records; Earthjustice litigation files*

## 4. Did the corporation violate the rights of vulnerable communities?

**Answer: YES**. Houston Refining is located in a neighbourhood with a predominantly Spanish-speaking and African American population — a classic example of environmental injustice.

1. **Environmental racism: refinery in Houston's "Manhattan Corridor"** — The Ship Channel area of Houston, where LyondellBasell facilities are concentrated, is populated predominantly by Latinos and African Americans with below-average incomes. EPA EJScreen studies confirm that residents of this area bear a disproportionately high share of industrial pollution compared to white neighbourhoods of the same city.
   *Source: EPA EJScreen; Texas Environmental Justice Advocacy Services (TEJAS)*

2. **Absence of Spanish-language notifications during emergencies** — TEJAS documented that notifications of emergency emissions from Ship Channel facilities, including the Lyondell refinery, were systematically not translated into Spanish, leaving a significant proportion of the Spanish-speaking population without timely information about the threat.
   *Source: TEJAS reports; Harris County emergency notification records*

3. **Harm to low-income homeowners** — The decline in property values in areas adjacent to the Ship Channel petrochemical cluster deprived low-income homeowners of accumulated equity. Independent appraisers engaged in litigation confirmed the "pollution discount" in housing values.
   *Source: Texas Southern University Environmental Justice research; Harris County Appraisal District data*

## 5. Has the corporation caused environmental damage to ecosystems?

**Answer: YES**. Air emissions and water discharges around LyondellBasell facilities in Houston and Rotterdam caused long-term damage to water and coastal ecosystems.

1. **Buffalo Bayou and San Jacinto River contamination** — Oil product and chemical spills from Houston Refining entered Buffalo Bayou and the Ship Channel, from which they flowed into Galveston Bay. Texas CEQ recorded several waterway contamination incidents requiring emergency response.
   *Source: TCEQ spill records; Texas Water Development Board*

2. **Industrial discharges into the Maas estuary (Rotterdam)** — Wastewater from LyondellBasell petrochemical facilities in the Rotterdam port industrial cluster enters the Maas delta. The EEA identifies this cluster as one of the largest point sources of industrial discharges into the North Sea.
   *Source: EEA E-PRTR; Rijkswaterstaat Rhine-Meuse Delta monitoring*

3. **VOC emissions and tropospheric ozone formation** — Mass VOC emissions from LyondellBasell's Houston and Rotterdam facilities contribute to ground-level ozone (smog) formation, damaging vegetation and suppressing marine and coastal ecosystems. Houston ranks among the highest for VOC pollution in the US.
   *Source: EPA National Emission Inventory; TCEQ Air Quality data*

## 6. Has the corporation engaged in financial manipulation or evasion of penalties?

**Answer: YES**. The 2009 bankruptcy enabled LyondellBasell to restructure some environmental obligations, and the company's offshore corporate structure raised questions about tax transparency.

1. **Incorporation in the Netherlands with offshore management** — LyondellBasell Industries N.V. is registered in the Netherlands, while its management is conducted from the US and UK. Several analysts pointed to this structure as a tax optimisation instrument enabling minimisation of US tax payments, where the company earns significant profits.
   *Source: LyondellBasell 10-K filings; Bloomberg Tax analysis*

2. **Reducing environmental obligations through bankruptcy court** — During the 2009–2010 reorganisation, a number of environmental claims were reclassified as "unsecured claims" and settled at reduced value. EPA documented a reduction in the total volume of environmental obligations upon emerging from bankruptcy.
   *Source: US Bankruptcy Court, SDNY; EPA response to reorganization plan, 2010*

3. **Dividend payments with outstanding environmental obligations** — During 2012–2019, LyondellBasell paid shareholders more than $10 billion in dividends and share buybacks. Environmental organisations pointed out that during this same period a number of environmental enforcement notices remained unfulfilled.
   *Source: LyondellBasell annual reports; Earthjustice filings*

---

## 7. Has the corporation lobbied against environmental standards?

**Answer: YES**. LyondellBasell through industry associations actively opposed tightening VOC emission standards, plastic waste regulation and climate regulation for the petrochemical industry.

1. **Lobbying against plastics regulation** — LyondellBasell — one of the largest polyethylene and polypropylene manufacturers — participates in lobbying through the ACC (American Chemistry Council) against strict plastic recycling requirements and extended producer responsibility (EPR) introduction.
   *Source: ACC lobbying disclosures; InfluenceMap corporate assessment; Senate Finance Committee records*

2. **Opposition to tightening VOC standards in Texas** — Through the Texas Chemical Council, the company sought to soften or delay tightening of VOC regulations in the Houston industrial area, citing economic costs to the industry.
   *Source: Texas Chemical Council lobbying filings; TCEQ public comment records*

3. **Lobbying against CO₂ emission quotas for petrochemicals in the EU** — In Rotterdam and Brussels, LyondellBasell through CEFIC and PlasticsEurope sought additional free ETS quotas for petrochemical production, slowing implementation of EU climate legislation.
   *Source: EU transparency register; Corporate Europe Observatory; PlasticsEurope positions*

---

## 8. Does the corporation exhibit repeated or ongoing violations?

**Answer: YES**. Emission standard violations at LyondellBasell facilities were systemic over many years, and a number of problems remain unresolved today.

1. **Systematic TCEQ violations over more than 10 years** — TCEQ's violation database records dozens of enforcement notices and fines issued to Houston Refining during 2000–2020. A number of violations recurred at the same points in the production process, indicating systemic rather than accidental character.
   *Source: TCEQ enforcement database; EPA ECHO compliance database*

2. **Repeated emergency emissions after fine imposition** — After paying EPA fines in the 2010s, Houston Refining continued to record emergency flaring events exceeding permitted emission volumes. This indicated the insufficiency of corrective measures taken.
   *Source: TCEQ event database; Harris County Pollution Control*

3. **Ongoing climate violations at Rotterdam facilities** — LyondellBasell Rotterdam facilities annually feature in the EU's list of largest industrial CO₂ emitters (E-PRTR registry), exceeding certain declared VOC standards and maintaining high industrial pollution levels in the Maas delta.
   *Source: EEA E-PRTR 2019–2022; RIVM*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Environmental injustice**: LyondellBasell operates one of the US's largest refineries directly adjacent to residential neighbourhoods with predominantly low-income and non-white populations.
2. **Bankruptcy as evasion tool**: The 2009 bankruptcy enabled the company to restructure environmental obligations with lower settlement costs, while shareholders subsequently received billions in dividends.
3. **Systematic emission violations**: Benzene and VOC emission standard exceedances at the Houston refinery have been documented for more than 15 years.
4. **Corporate tax structure**: Netherlands incorporation with US/UK management creates tax optimisation opportunities.
5. **Rotterdam emissions**: LyondellBasell Rotterdam facilities rank among Western Europe's largest industrial emission sources.
6. **Lobbying**: Opposition to plastics regulation, VOC controls and petrochemical climate restrictions conducted through ACC, CEFIC and PlasticsEurope.
7. **Language injustice**: Emergency emission notifications from Houston Refining were systematically not provided in Spanish for Spanish-speaking neighbourhood residents.
8. **Disproportionate penalties**: Total financial sanctions against the company are dwarfed by dividend payments and profits earned during the period of environmental violations.
