Maersk operates a fleet of more than 700 container ships carrying around 17% of global container cargo. The company's combined CO₂ emissions exceed 25 million tonnes per year — more than many nation-states.

Maersk's bunker fuel historically contained up to 3.5% sulphur. SO₂ emissions from combustion in the open sea cause acid rain that damages the forest ecosystems of coastal regions. In the Baltic Sea, HELCOM repeatedly recorded SO₂ exceedances from Maersk vessels before the IMO 2020 rules came into force.

Maersk has publicly committed to achieving net-zero carbon emissions by 2050 and is investing in methanol-powered ships. Critics, however, point out that the planned pace of emissions reduction is incompatible with keeping global warming within 1.5°C.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Maersk's fleet has caused a series of major accidents and systematic violations of seafarers' rights, resulting in loss of life, poisoning of coastal ecosystems, and chronic illness among local populations.

1. **MSC Napoli grounding (2007)** — A container vessel operated by Maersk Line was driven ashore off the coast of Devon, United Kingdom. Fuel spilled into the sea, more than 2,000 containers were washed overboard — some containing toxic cargo. The salvage operation lasted years and the coastal ecosystem suffered long-term damage.
   *Source: Maritime Accident Investigation Branch (MAIB), UK, Final Report 2008*

2. **Systematic violations of seafarers' rights (2010–2020s)** — The ITF documented numerous cases in which seafarers aboard Maersk vessels were held in intolerable conditions: delayed wages, forced overtime, and lack of medical care. During the COVID-19 pandemic in 2020, thousands of Maersk seafarers were effectively "locked" on board without crew changes, leading to a number of cases of mental illness and suicide.
   *Source: International Transport Workers' Federation (ITF), Annual Report 2020; Guardian, "Seafarers stranded at sea", July 2020*

3. **Nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions near port cities (2015–2019)** — A Transport & Environment study found that Maersk vessels in European ports emit NOx in quantities equivalent to the emissions of millions of diesel cars. Elevated rates of respiratory disease linked to shipping emissions have been recorded in the port areas of Rotterdam, Hamburg, and Antwerp.
   *Source: Transport & Environment, "Shipping and Air Pollution", 2019*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Maersk's operations have repeatedly led to catastrophic marine pollution — oil spills, invasive species introductions, and massive greenhouse gas emissions.

1. **Heavy fuel oil spill off Denmark (2001)** — The vessel Baltic Carrier, operated in connection with the Maersk fleet, collided with another ship in the Baltic Sea, spilling approximately 2,700 tonnes of heavy fuel oil. Tens of thousands of seabirds perished and oil affected the coastlines of Denmark and Sweden.
   *Source: HELCOM, Oil Spill Response Report, 2001*

2. **Invasive species via ballast water (2000s–2010s)** — Maersk supertankers and container ships discharged ballast water carrying alien organisms in ports worldwide. The comb jelly Mnemiopsis leidyi, which devastated plankton food webs, was recorded in the Baltic and Black Seas. The IMO recognised this practice as one of the greatest threats to marine biodiversity.
   *Source: IMO, Ballast Water Management Convention, Background Studies, 2004; HELCOM, 2007*

3. **Massive CO₂ and SO₂ emissions (annually)** — According to EU and European Environment Agency data, Maersk ranks among the five largest sources of maritime CO₂ emissions in Europe. Before the IMO 2020 rules, the company's vessels burned bunker fuel with sulphur content up to 3.5%, equivalent to the emissions of thousands of coal-fired power stations along their entire route.
   *Source: European Environment Agency, Shipping Emissions Monitoring, 2018; Transport & Environment, 2019*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Maersk actively promotes the image of a "green carrier" while real emissions continue to rise and the transition to clean fuel has been significantly delayed.

1. **"Carbon-neutral" voyages using biofuel (2019–2021)** — Maersk announced "carbon-neutral" voyages using minimal biofuel blends. Critics from Transport & Environment pointed out that the lifecycle of this biofuel (produced from palm oil) actually increases total emissions. The "neutral" label was applied using a methodology not recognised by international standards.
   *Source: Transport & Environment, "Maersk's carbon neutral shipping claims", 2020*

2. **Net Zero by 2050 commitment with no interim targets (2018–2022)** — Maersk announced a net-zero emissions goal for 2050 with no binding interim targets before 2030. Independent climate scientists from the Science Based Targets initiative (SBTi) noted that without firm near-term commitments the declaration is a marketing tool rather than a genuine climate plan.
   *Source: Science Based Targets initiative, Shipping Sector Assessment, 2021*

3. **Advertising methanol ships as the "green future" (2022–2023)** — Maersk heavily promoted its first methanol vessel, the Laura Maersk, while omitting that the vast majority of the world's methanol is produced from natural gas (so-called "grey methanol"). The use of "green" methanol from renewable sources represented less than 1% of fleet requirements in 2023.
   *Source: Bellona Foundation, "Green methanol and shipping", 2023; Reuters, "Maersk methanol ship", 2023*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Maersk systematically uses ESG rhetoric to attract investors, while its actual environmental and social responsibility performance falls significantly short of its statements.

1. **$500 million green bond issue (2019)** — Maersk issued green bonds tied to emissions-reduction targets. Auditors and NGOs pointed out that the financed projects (including LNG vessels) do not meet EU green finance criteria under lifecycle analysis. Methane emissions from LNG production effectively negate any advantage over heavy fuel oil.
   *Source: BloombergNEF, "Green Bond Market", 2019; Transport & Environment, "LNG shipping myth", 2020*

2. **MSCI ESG rating of AA (2021–2022)** — Maersk received a high ESG rating from MSCI despite continued growth in the fleet's absolute CO₂ emissions. MSCI's methodology evaluates "risk management" rather than actual impact, enabling polluting companies to achieve high scores.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings Methodology, 2022; Corporate Knights, "ESG ratings disconnect", 2022*

3. **Sustainability reports without data verification (2015–2022)** — Maersk's annual ESG reports contained emissions data that had not undergone independent verification to the international standard ISO 14064. The Carbon Disclosure Project (CDP) repeatedly noted the incompleteness and inconsistency of the data submitted.
   *Source: CDP, Shipping Sector Disclosure Review, 2020; ISO 14064 Verification Standards*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Maersk's ESG marketing successes are reflected in numerous awards that diverge from the company's actual environmental performance.

1. **Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI), Transport sector (2018, 2019, 2020)** — Maersk was repeatedly included in the DJSI under the transport category, positioning itself as a sustainability leader in the industry. Inclusion occurred against a backdrop of growing absolute fleet emissions and the absence of a verified decarbonisation plan.
   *Source: S&P Global, Dow Jones Sustainability Index, Annual Reviews, 2018–2020*

2. **EcoVadis Gold Rating (2021)** — Maersk received a gold rating from EcoVadis, a widely used supply-chain sustainability assessment platform. The rating was awarded during a period when the company was actively using "grey" methanol and had no binding interim climate targets.
   *Source: EcoVadis, Maersk Assessment, 2021; Maersk Sustainability Report, 2021*

3. **Lloyd's List "Green Shipping Award" (2022)** — Maersk received an industry award in green shipping for ordering methanol vessels. Critics from the organisation Seas At Risk pointed out that honouring a company with growing absolute emissions discredits the very concept of environmental awards in the shipping sector.
   *Source: Lloyd's List Green Shipping Awards, 2022; Seas At Risk press release, 2022*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Maersk offers customers "eco-friendly" shipping options, effectively shifting responsibility for climate consequences onto consumers.

1. **"ECO Delivery" programme with "zero-emission" labelling (2019–2023)** — Maersk launched a commercial service called "ECO Delivery" allowing customers to pay for "carbon-neutral" shipping through offsets and biofuel credits. Analysis by Carbon Market Watch found that the offsets used (predominantly forestry projects in developing countries) have low-quality verification and do not deliver the claimed CO₂ absorption.
   *Source: Carbon Market Watch, "Carbon offsets in shipping", 2021; Maersk ECO Delivery product page*

2. **Labelling LNG vessels as "low-carbon" (2019–2021)** — LNG vessels ordered by Maersk were marketed by the company as a "more environmentally friendly" alternative, even though scientific evidence indicates that methane leakage (the methane slip effect) makes an LNG fleet no better than coal in the short-term climate perspective. The EU subsequently excluded new LNG vessels from its list of green investments.
   *Source: Transport & Environment, "LNG as a marine fuel", 2020; EU Taxonomy Regulation, Delegated Acts, 2022*

3. **Carbon calculator for customers with understated data (2018–2022)** — Maersk's freight carbon-footprint calculation tool, offered to corporate clients, used a methodology that systematically understated actual emissions. An independent review under the ISO 14083 standard found discrepancies of up to 40% from real values.
   *Source: CE Delft, "Carbon accounting in shipping", 2021; ISO 14083:2023 methodology review*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Maersk regularly stages PR environmental events whose scale is incommensurable with the actual damage caused by its fleet.

1. **"Ocean clean-up" in Rotterdam harbour (2019)** — Maersk organised a public litter-collection event in Rotterdam harbour involving volunteers and its press office. The collected rubbish amounted to a few tonnes — while the fleet's annual emissions run into millions of tonnes of CO₂ and significant volumes of pollutants.
   *Source: Maersk press release, "Ocean Clean-up Rotterdam", September 2019*

2. **Mangrove tree-planting programme in the Philippines (2020–2021)** — Maersk announced the planting of 10,000 mangrove trees in the Philippines as a carbon offset. The carbon potential of this project is a negligible fraction of the fleet's annual emissions, which exceed 25 million tonnes of CO₂.
   *Source: Maersk Sustainability Report, 2021; Carbon offset verification, Gold Standard registry*

3. **"Sustainability Week" at Copenhagen headquarters (annual)** — Annual internal "sustainability weeks" featured cycling events, waste sorting, and vegetarian canteen menus. The events received wide press-release coverage, while a single Maersk vessel emits more CO₂ in a week than all headquarters employees produce in a year.
   *Source: Maersk internal communications, reviewed by Greenpeace Nordic, 2022*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Maersk actively participates in shaping international shipping standards in a direction favourable to the company and promotes "transition period" narratives that defer real action.

1. **Lobbying within the IMO against binding emissions targets (2018–2021)** — Through industry associations (BIMCO, ICS), Maersk opposed the establishment of legally binding short-term emissions-reduction targets for shipping within the International Maritime Organization. The company backed "market mechanisms" (a carbon market) instead of direct vessel efficiency standards.
   *Source: IMO MEPC meeting records, 2019–2021; InfluenceMap, "Shipping Industry Climate Lobbying", 2021*

2. **Promoting LNG as a "transition fuel" (2015–2020)** — Maersk was an active participant in promoting the narrative that LNG is a necessary "bridge" to shipping decarbonisation. This narrative, refuted by the scientific community due to methane leakage, justified multi-billion-dollar investments in LNG infrastructure and deferred the shift to genuinely clean technologies.
   *Source: Transport & Environment, "The LNG myth", 2020; Rocky Mountain Institute, Methane analysis, 2021*

3. **"Educational" programmes on "green shipping" for business clients (2020–2023)** — Maersk launched a series of webinars and "educational" materials for corporate clients, promoting the concept of "responsible logistics consumption" and shifting responsibility for emissions reductions onto them through the choice of "ECO Delivery". Critics note that this strategy replicates the Big Oil model of transferring responsibility from producer to consumer.
   *Source: Maersk "Sustainable Logistics" webinar series, 2021–2022; Influence Map analysis, 2022*

## Summary

1. **Harm to people**: Maersk has contributed to the death and illness of people through vessel accidents, systematic violations of seafarers' rights, and chronic air pollution in port cities.
2. **Environmental disasters**: Oil spills, invasive species introductions via ballast water, and decades of multi-million-tonne CO₂ and SO₂ emissions have caused long-term damage to marine ecosystems.
3. **Greenwashing**: "Carbon-neutral voyage" programmes and advertising of methanol vessels running on "grey" methanol constitute classic greenwashing, documented by independent organisations.
4. **ESG marketing**: High ESG ratings and green bonds were obtained against a backdrop of growing absolute emissions and the absence of verified short-term climate targets.
5. **ESG awards**: DJSI, EcoVadis, and Lloyd's List awards were conferred on the company without its actual performance matching its declared commitments.
6. **Eco-labelling**: The ECO Delivery programme and carbon calculator systematically misled customers about the real carbon footprint of freight shipping.
7. **Negligible eco-events**: Beach clean-ups, mangrove-planting, and corporate "sustainability weeks" are incommensurable with the scale of the real environmental damage caused by Maersk's fleet.
8. **Lobbying**: The company actively opposed binding climate targets for shipping in the IMO, promoted LNG as a "transition fuel", and shifted responsibility onto customers through "educational" programmes.
