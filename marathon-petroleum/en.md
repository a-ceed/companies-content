Marathon Petroleum operates one of the largest oil refineries in the USA — a plant in Detroit located immediately adjacent to low-income, predominantly African-American neighbourhoods in zip code 48217. According to EPA and ProPublica data, residents face a combined toxic industrial burden ranking in the top 10% of the most polluted areas in the United States.

In 2013, petroleum coke waste from the Marathon refinery formed a massive "petcoke pile" on the banks of the Detroit River — a by-product of processing oil extracted from Canadian oil sands. Black dust spread across residential neighbourhoods, triggering a sharp political debate on environmental justice.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Marathon Petroleum's facilities have systematically poisoned air and water in low-income US communities, creating elevated risks of cancer and respiratory disease.

1. **Air pollution in Detroit, zip code 48217 (2000s–2020s)** — Marathon's Detroit refinery is the single largest source of toxic emissions in an area where the majority of residents are African-Americans with below-average incomes. According to EPA data, residents experience a combined toxic burden ranking in the top 10% nationally. Cancer rates in the area significantly exceed the Michigan state average.
   *Source: EPA EJScreen Data, 2020; ProPublica, "Sacrifice Zones", 2021; Michigan Department of Health and Human Services, Cancer Incidence Report*

2. **Petcoke pile on the Detroit River (2013)** — Petroleum coke — a by-product of processing oil-sands crude at the Marathon refinery — accumulated in mounds up to five metres high on the bank of the Detroit River. Fine black dust spread across residential neighbourhoods of Boult Harbour and Riverside, causing acute respiratory reactions. The coke contains vanadium, nickel, and sulphur.
   *Source: Detroit Free Press, May 2013; Michigan Department of Environmental Quality investigation, 2013; Natural Resources Defense Council, "Detroit Petcoke", 2013*

3. **Sulphur dioxide (SO₂) emissions exceedances at the Galveston City refinery (2019–2022)** — The Galveston City refinery in Texas repeatedly recorded SO₂ emissions above permissible limits. According to the TCEQ, residents of nearby areas with a predominantly Hispanic population reported eye irritation, breathing problems, and headaches during exceedance periods.
   *Source: Texas Commission on Environmental Quality (TCEQ) Violation Records, 2019–2022; EarthJustice, "Texas Refinery Pollution", 2021*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Oil product spills, systematic releases of toxic substances, and water-body contamination have caused long-term environmental damage in several US states.

1. **Oil spill into the Kanawha River, West Virginia (1994)** — A Marathon Pipe Line LLC pipeline ruptured near the Kanawha River, spilling thousands of gallons of crude oil into the waterway. The spill destroyed fish populations over an extended river section and required years of remediation.
   *Source: EPA National Response Center records; West Virginia Department of Environmental Protection, 1994*

2. **Groundwater contamination near the Kentucky refinery (2000s)** — The EPA documented contamination of groundwater with benzene and other hydrocarbons near Marathon's Catlettsburg, Kentucky refinery. The company was required to carry out a multi-year remediation programme, yet residents continued to report contamination of private wells years after work began.
   *Source: EPA Superfund Site Records, Catlettsburg Refinery; Kentucky Energy and Environment Cabinet reports*

3. **Chronic contamination of the Detroit River with petroleum products (2010–2020)** — Michigan state regulators repeatedly recorded discharges of oil-containing wastewater from the Marathon Detroit refinery into the Detroit River and its tributaries. The contamination threatened water-intake stations and fish resources.
   *Source: Michigan Department of Environment, Great Lakes, and Energy (EGLE), Inspection Records; EPA Great Lakes Restoration Initiative data*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Marathon Petroleum presents minimal environmental initiatives as systemic change while simultaneously opposing tighter environmental regulations.

1. **Positioning the Detroit plant as "modern and clean" (2013–2015)** — After the petcoke scandal, Marathon conducted public plant tours and issued materials portraying the facility as "advanced" and "environmentally responsible", ignoring systemic air-quality problems in surrounding communities.
   *Source: Marathon Petroleum Community Relations materials, 2013–2015; NRDC analysis, 2014*

2. **"Environmental" investments alongside growing oil-sands processing capacity (2012–2018)** — Marathon announced investments in emissions reduction while simultaneously expanding processing of Canadian oil-sands crude — one of the most carbon-intensive forms of oil. Total greenhouse gas emissions grew alongside capacity.
   *Source: Marathon Petroleum Annual Reports; Oil Change International, "Tar Sands Refining", 2016*

3. **Declaring "emissions intensity" reductions while absolute figures remained high (2020–2023)** — Marathon's sustainability reports emphasised reductions in specific (per-unit-of-production) emissions while absolute GHG volume remained at a high level. This technique is a standard element of greenwashing in the refining sector.
   *Source: Marathon Petroleum Sustainability Report, 2022; Carbon Disclosure Project (CDP) submission analysis*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Marathon uses ESG instruments to attract investors while its fundamental business model remains incompatible with climate goals.

1. **Inclusion in ESG funds despite violations (2018–2022)** — Marathon Petroleum was included in ESG funds run by major asset managers (BlackRock, Vanguard) despite documented violations of environmental law and environmental-justice complaints, reflecting a systemic flaw in ESG rating methodologies.
   *Source: As You Sow, "Fossil Fuels in ESG Funds", 2021; Sierra Club ESG analysis, 2022*

2. **TCFD climate-risk reports without a decarbonisation plan (2021–2023)** — Marathon published TCFD reporting without a binding plan to phase out refining capacity. The reports analysed climate risks to the business, but not the business's impact on the climate.
   *Source: Marathon Petroleum TCFD Report, 2022; Carbon Tracker Initiative analysis*

3. **"Diversity & Inclusion" programme alongside systematic environmental racism (2019–2023)** — Marathon promoted corporate diversity and inclusion programmes as an ESG element while remaining the primary source of toxic emissions in communities of colour in Detroit and Texas.
   *Source: Michigan Environmental Justice Coalition, 2021; Marathon Petroleum DEI Annual Report, 2022*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Marathon received industry ESG recognition, illustrating the gap between rating methodologies and real environmental impact.

1. **Inclusion in the S&P 500 ESG Index (2020–2022)** — Marathon Petroleum was included in the S&P 500 ESG Index despite documented violations of environmental standards. The index is used by billions of dollars in passive investment as a "responsible" placement.
   *Source: S&P Dow Jones Indices, ESG Index methodology; Bloomberg ESG data, 2021*

2. **National Safety Council "Industry Leader" award (2019)** — The company received recognition for industrial safety performance, actively used in ESG communications, while environmental violations and their community health impact outside plant boundaries were not considered.
   *Source: National Safety Council, Industry Award records, 2019; Marathon Petroleum press release*

3. **CDP Climate rating of "B" (2021)** — Marathon received a "B" ("management") rating from CDP, cited in investor materials as evidence of climate responsibility. A "B" rating merely indicates the presence of management processes, not alignment with a 1.5-degree pathway.
   *Source: CDP Climate Scores, 2021; Marathon Petroleum Investor Presentation, 2022*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Marathon applied "environmental" labelling and positioning to fuel products despite their fundamentally negative climate impact.

1. **Marketing of Speedway "clean" fuel (2015–2020)** — The Speedway retail fuel chain used advertising materials emphasising "clean" and "improved" fuel formulations, creating a false impression of reduced environmental harm from burning petrol.
   *Source: Speedway marketing materials; Federal Trade Commission Green Guides review, 2016*

2. **MPLX — "Clean energy infrastructure" (2019–2022)** — Pipeline subsidiary MPLX positioned part of its infrastructure as "clean energy" assets, including transportation of "low-carbon" fuel derived from oil sands. Independent analysts classified these claims as greenwashing.
   *Source: MPLX investor presentations, 2020–2022; Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis (IEEFA) analysis*

3. **Biodiesel blend labelling as "renewable fuel" (2017–2021)** — Marathon labelled blends with minimal biodiesel content (B5–B10) as "renewable" fuel, creating a distorted consumer impression. The real carbon footprint of such blends differs insignificantly from conventional diesel.
   *Source: EPA Renewable Fuel Standard data; Consumer Reports fuel labeling analysis, 2019*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Marathon systematically stages local environmental events in affected communities that are incommensurable with the scale of the damage caused.

1. **"Greening" Detroit neighbourhoods while pollution continued (2014–2018)** — Marathon sponsored tree-planting and park improvements in Detroit neighbourhoods adjacent to the refinery. Community organisations noted the cynicism of the initiative: trees were planted in areas suffering from chronic air pollution caused by the very same plant.
   *Source: Marathon Petroleum Community Investment Report, 2016; Michigan Environmental Justice Coalition response, 2016*

2. **"Environmental" grants to schools in adjacent neighbourhoods (2015–2020)** — Marathon's grant programme for school environmental projects amounted to a few hundred thousand dollars annually — while the company's tax breaks ran into billions. The programme was widely covered by PR as evidence of "environmental responsibility".
   *Source: Marathon Petroleum Corporate Social Responsibility Report, 2018; EJScreen community analysis*

3. **Participation in Earth Day events (annual)** — Marathon's annual Earth Day activities — site clean-ups and educational stalls — were organised against a backdrop of continuing environmental violations at production facilities. Activists from Detroiters Working for Environmental Justice repeatedly held counter-demonstrations at these events.
   *Source: Detroiters Working for Environmental Justice, press releases 2016–2019; Marathon Petroleum Earth Day reports*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Marathon actively lobbied against tighter environmental regulations, funded organisations opposing climate regulation, and promoted "energy realism" narratives.

1. **Lobbying against the Renewable Fuel Standard (2013–2019)** — Marathon was part of refinery-industry coalitions seeking to reduce or repeal mandatory biofuel blending requirements (RFS), citing "technical constraints" and "threats to consumers". According to InfluenceMap, Marathon spent millions of dollars annually lobbying against climate regulation.
   *Source: InfluenceMap, "Oil Refining Industry Climate Policy Engagement", 2019; OpenSecrets lobbying data*

2. **Funding the American Petroleum Institute (API) and ALEC (2010–2022)** — Marathon is a major member of API and a sponsor of ALEC, organisations that systematically oppose a carbon tax, tighter emissions standards, and climate legislation at the state level.
   *Source: API membership dues disclosures; ALEC donor database; Center for Media and Democracy, 2019*

3. **"Energy security" narrative as justification for environmental degradation (2019–2023)** — Marathon actively used "energy independence" and "energy security" rhetoric to justify expanding oil-sands processing capacity and opposing environmental regulation, framing lower environmental standards as a patriotic measure.
   *Source: Marathon Petroleum lobbying disclosures, 2019–2022; DeSmog, "Energy Security Framing", 2022*

## Summary

1. **Harm to people**: Marathon refineries systematically poison air in low-income communities of colour in Detroit and Texas, creating zones of elevated cancer burden — a textbook case of environmental racism.
2. **Environmental disasters**: Oil product spills into the Kanawha and Detroit Rivers, and chronic groundwater contamination near refineries, have caused long-term damage to aquatic ecosystems across several states.
3. **Greenwashing**: Positioning the plant as "modern" after the petcoke scandal, and reporting on emissions "intensity" while absolute figures remained high, constitute documented greenwashing.
4. **ESG marketing**: Marathon was included in ESG funds and indices and published TCFD reports without a real decarbonisation plan, while simultaneously expanding oil-sands refining capacity.
5. **ESG awards**: Inclusion in the S&P 500 ESG Index and CDP ratings illustrates systemic shortcomings in ESG methodologies that allow refining companies to obtain "responsible" status.
6. **Eco-labelling**: Marketing of Speedway "clean" fuels and biodiesel blends as "renewable" misled consumers about the real carbon footprint.
7. **Negligible eco-events**: Tree-planting and school grants in polluted neighbourhoods are incommensurable with the scale of harm caused and serve a PR function.
8. **Lobbying**: Marathon systematically opposes climate regulation through API, ALEC, and direct lobbying, using "energy security" narratives to block environmental reforms.
