Mars produces billions of units in multi-layer packaging that is nearly impossible to recycle.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. For years Mars profited from cocoa supply chains in which child and forced labour in West Africa is documented, as well as from pet food production with sanitary violations.

1. **Child labour on West African cocoa plantations (2001–2023)** — According to a U.S. Department of Labor report (2020), more than 1.56 million children in Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire are engaged in hazardous work in cocoa cultivation. Mars is among the largest buyers of cocoa from these regions. The company repeatedly pledged to eradicate child labour by 2005, 2010, and 2020 — failing each time.
   *Source: U.S. Department of Labor, "2020 Findings on the Worst Forms of Child Labor"; Washington Post investigation, 2019*

2. **Pet food poisoning (2007)** — In the largest pet food recall in U.S. history, products under the Pedigree brand and others, manufactured using melamine-contaminated vegetable protein from China, led to the deaths of thousands of dogs and cats. Mars Pet Care was among the companies affected by the scandal.
   *Source: FDA Recall Notice, March–April 2007; Associated Press*

3. **Forced labour in chocolate raw material harvesting (2015–2021)** — An investigation by International Rights Advocates documented cases in which children trafficked from Burkina Faso and Mali were held in effective slavery on Mars supplier farms in Côte d'Ivoire. In 2021 a class-action lawsuit against Mars was filed in a U.S. court.
   *Source: International Rights Advocates, lawsuit filed in D.C. district court, 2021*

## 2. Has the corporation been responsible for major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Mars's supply chains are linked to the destruction of tropical forests through palm oil and cocoa purchases, and its packaging generates billions of non-recyclable plastic units every year.

1. **Palm oil and deforestation in Borneo/Sumatra (2010–2020)** — Greenpeace established that Mars's palm oil suppliers were destroying peat forests in Sumatra and Kalimantan, releasing enormous volumes of CO₂ and destroying the habitat of the Sumatran orangutan. In 2010 Greenpeace staged a direct action against Mars in the UK, releasing the video "Killer".
   *Source: Greenpeace International, "Have a Break?" campaign, 2010; Rainforest Action Network, 2018*

2. **Plastic packaging in the world's oceans (2010–2023)** — Mars packaging (Snickers, M&M's, Twix) annually appears in the top 10 brands recorded by volunteers during beach cleanups worldwide under the Break Free From Plastic initiative. Multi-layer wrappers made of aluminium and polyethylene are not accepted for recycling in any country at scale.
   *Source: Break Free From Plastic Brand Audit, 2019, 2020, 2021*

3. **Water pollution in cocoa-growing zones (2015–2022)** — Pesticides (including endosulfan and chlorpyrifos) are used on Mars supplier cocoa plantations in Côte d'Ivoire; they run off into rivers and kill aquatic life. Mighty Earth researchers (2018) documented the clearing of forest areas for Mars supplier plantations inside protected zones.
   *Source: Mighty Earth, "Chocolate's Dark Secret", 2018*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Mars systematically made environmental commitments — "net-zero emissions", "sustainable palm oil", "elimination of child labour" — while its actual performance diverged radically from its declarations.

1. **"Zero-deforestation" palm oil commitments (2014–2020)** — In 2014 Mars publicly signed the New York Declaration on Forests, promising to source only "sustainable" palm oil by 2020. Yet its supply chains continued to include suppliers clearing primary forests. Independent checks by the Rainforest Alliance found systematic gaps between declarations and practice.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network, "Still Failing Forests", 2019*

2. **"Sustainable cocoa" while child labour persisted (2012–2022)** — Mars is a member of the CocoaAction consortium and promotes its "Cocoa for Generations" programme, marketing them as "sustainable" solutions. Yet independent auditors found that the share of child labour in Mars's supply chain had not decreased by 2022.
   *Source: Washington Post, "Cocoa's Child Laborers", 2019; NORC University of Chicago, 2020*

3. **"Sustainable in a Generation" campaign without verifiable results (2017–2022)** — In 2017 Mars announced a $1 billion sustainability strategy branded "Sustainable in a Generation". External experts from Climate Action 100+ and the Science Based Targets initiative noted that the corporation does not publish a methodology for calculating Scope 3 emissions and does not provide verified progress data.
   *Source: Climate Action 100+ Assessment, 2021*

## 4. Does the company participate in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Mars actively promotes ESG narratives through corporate reports, advertising campaigns and partnerships with international organisations.

1. **Annual ESG reports "The Mars Sustainability Report"** — Since 2011 Mars has published annual sustainability reports, actively covering climate goals, human rights and animal welfare. The reports are produced in high-quality print and widely distributed at business forums.
   *Source: Mars Incorporated, ESG/Sustainability Reports, 2018–2023*

2. **Partnership with WWF and eco-themed advertising (2020–2022)** — Mars funded joint projects with WWF on "biodiversity conservation" in Indonesia, using them in PR materials. At the same time the company's palm oil supply chains remained linked to the destruction of tropical forests.
   *Source: WWF Partnership Report 2021; RAN Corporate Accountability Report 2021*

3. **M&M's chocolate advertising with an ecological message (2022)** — In 2022 Mars launched a limited-edition M&M's packaging with messages about "sustainable ingredients", without disclosing that most ingredients still come from non-certified sources.
   *Source: The Guardian, "M&M's sustainability claims", 2022*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG competition nominations?

**Answer: YES**. Mars has repeatedly received high ratings and awards from organisations assessing corporate sustainability.

1. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI) (2015–2020)** — Mars was included in the DJSI for several consecutive years, which was actively used in the company's marketing materials as confirmation of "sustainability leadership".
   *Source: S&P Dow Jones Indices, DJSI Yearbooks*

2. **CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project) "A" rating (2018–2020)** — CDP awarded Mars the highest level of climate information disclosure, although verification of actual emissions reductions in the supply chain remained limited. The award was actively mentioned in press releases.
   *Source: CDP Scores 2018–2020*

3. **EcoVadis Gold Rating (2019–2021)** — The EcoVadis platform, which assesses ESG practices of suppliers, awarded Mars a Gold category, which was used in retailer negotiations as evidence of "sustainable practices".
   *Source: EcoVadis Scorecard Mars, 2019–2021*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Mars products carry "sustainable" standard labels despite documented harm to ecosystems.

1. **Rainforest Alliance label on chocolate with documented child labour (2015–2022)** — Some of Mars's chocolate products (Dove, Galaxy) carry the Rainforest Alliance logo. However, repeated independent investigations show that certification does not exclude child labour at the smallholder level.
   *Source: NORC University of Chicago Cocoa Report, 2020; The Guardian, 2021*

2. **RSPO logo on palm oil products (2013–2022)** — Mars uses the RSPO (Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil) label, yet the Rainforest Action Network has repeatedly documented deforestation by suppliers holding active RSPO certificates within Mars's supply chain.
   *Source: RAN "The Last Forest", 2018*

3. **"Responsibly sourced" wording on chocolate bar packaging (2020–2023)** — Packaging for Snickers, Bounty and Milky Way began carrying the phrase "responsibly sourced cocoa", which has no mandatory independent verification and contradicts NGO reports on the actual state of affairs in the supply chain.
   *Source: Mars Incorporated packaging, 2020–2023; Mighty Earth Cocoa Tracker*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Mars regularly announced environmental initiatives whose scope is disproportionate to the scale of actual damage.

1. **Planting 1 million trees under the "Forests for Our Future" programme (2020)** — Mars announced the planting of a million trees, whereas a single forest-clearing operation for one supplier's plantation in Indonesia destroys tens of thousands of hectares of tropical forest. The scale of the initiative amounts to a negligible fraction of the damage caused.
   *Source: Mars Press Release, 2020; Global Forest Watch data 2019–2021*

2. **Litter clean-ups at Mars offices on Earth Day (2019–2022)** — Every year Mars employees take part in corporate clean-up days at headquarters, while the company continues to produce billions of non-recyclable packaging units per year.
   *Source: Mars Corporate Social Responsibility Reports 2019–2022*

3. **"Cocoa Village Dashboards" programme — monitoring without a reduction in child labour (2018–2022)** — Mars launched a digital child-labour monitoring system on supplier farms, but the scale of coverage (a few thousand farms out of hundreds of thousands) makes it a declarative measure. NORC recorded a rise in the number of working children.
   *Source: NORC University of Chicago, 2020; Washington Post, 2019*

## 8. Has the company lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories or greenwashing outreach?

**Answer: YES**. Mars actively promoted the concept of "sustainable production" as an alternative to systemic change, lobbied for weak industry standards, and participated in initiatives that substitute real regulation with voluntary declarations.

1. **Lobbying CocoaAction against mandatory regulation (2014–2019)** — The CocoaAction industry consortium, established in part with Mars's participation, promoted the concept of voluntary "sustainable cocoa" as an alternative to legislative mechanisms for combating child labour in the EU and USA. Critics noted that the consortium deliberately delayed the adoption of binding norms.
   *Source: Environmental Food Crisis Report; Corporate Accountability International, 2016*

2. **Participation in lobbying against the EU Deforestation Regulation (2021–2022)** — Mars, together with other confectionery giants, participated in lobbying efforts aimed at weakening the requirements of the EU Deforestation Regulation (EUDR), seeking to extend transition periods and dilute traceability requirements.
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory, 2022; Global Witness Lobbying Report, 2022*

3. **Promoting "responsible consumption" instead of systemic change (2015–2023)** — Mars makes active use of educational campaigns ("Mars Education", "Cocoa School") that shift responsibility for environmental harm onto consumers, focusing on personal ecological habits rather than changes in the production chain.
   *Source: Mars Incorporated Education Program materials, 2017–2022*
