Meta (Facebook) operates more than 20 major data centres. The company's total water consumption in 2021 exceeded 3.5 billion litres. Particularly acute problems exist in Arizona and New Mexico, where new data centres are being built: both states are suffering from severe, prolonged drought.

In the city of Mesa, Arizona, Meta has already built a large data centre and continues to expand, despite the fact that the Salt River Project aquifer is being drawn down faster than it is replenished. Local indigenous communities — particularly the Akimel O'otham and Pee Posh — warn of a threat to their historic water rights.

In the Netherlands, Meta's data centre in Zevenaar consumes as much water annually as 20,000 households. In 2022 a Dutch court heard an appeal by environmental organisations demanding limits on data-centre water consumption in the region.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Meta has been directly implicated in the genocide in Myanmar, in the mass leaking of personal data of millions of people, and in systematic psychological harm to content moderation workers.

1. **Facebook's role in the genocide of the Rohingya in Myanmar (2017–2018)** — A UN Fact-Finding Mission concluded that Facebook played a "determining role" in spreading hate content against Rohingya Muslims, contributing to ethnic cleansing that killed thousands and forced 700,000 people to flee to Bangladesh. Meta acknowledged that the platform's algorithms amplified the virality of hate content, but attributed the delayed response to a shortage of moderators fluent in Burmese.
   *Source: UN Independent International Fact-Finding Mission on Myanmar report, 2018; Reuters, 2018*

2. **Cambridge Analytica scandal — data leak of 87 million users (2018)** — It emerged that the political consulting firm Cambridge Analytica obtained data on 87 million Facebook profiles without user consent via a third-party app. The data was used to build psychographic profiles and micro-targeted political advertising — including in the 2016 US presidential campaign and the Brexit campaign. The FTC fined Meta a record $5 billion.
   *Source: New York Times, The Guardian, "Facebook and Cambridge Analytica", 2018; FTC decision, 2019*

3. **Psychological harm to content moderators (2020–2022)** — Thousands of employees of Meta contractors (Accenture, Teleperformance) in Nairobi, Barcelona, and other cities reviewed hundreds of items of extreme content daily. A 2022 collective lawsuit by Kenyan moderators documented cases of PTSD, depression, and a lack of adequate psychological support. Workers were paid between $2 and $3 per hour.
   *Source: Time Magazine, "Facebook's African Sweatshop", 2022; lawsuit of Traumatised Workers v. Meta, 2022*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Meta's data centres consume water in regions suffering from chronic drought, and the carbon footprint of the server infrastructure continues to grow.

1. **Water crisis in Arizona: Mesa data centre (2019–2023)** — Meta's data centre of over 200,000 sq m in Mesa, Arizona consumes hundreds of millions of litres of water annually from the state's water supply system, which is under a water emergency. In 2023 the Bureau of Reclamation cut Colorado River water allocations for Arizona, but industrial facilities including data centres continued to receive water on a priority basis.
   *Source: Arizona Central, 2022; High Country News, 2023*

2. **Netherlands pollution: conflict with local authorities (2022)** — Meta's data centre in Zevenaar (Gelderland province) consumed water above agreed limits, prompting a warning from the Rijn en IJssel water board. Local farmers reported falling groundwater levels and worsening conditions for irrigating farmland.
   *Source: De Gelderlander, 2022; Data Center Dynamics, 2022*

3. **Energy footprint: consumption growth despite climate goals (2020–2023)** — Despite claims of 100% renewable energy, Meta's total energy consumption grew from 7.2 TWh in 2019 to more than 10 TWh in 2022. Expansion in Oregon increased load on the regional grid, requiring increased capacity from gas peaker plants.
   *Source: Meta Sustainability Report 2022; Oregon Department of Energy*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Meta systematically overstates its environmental achievements in public communications, using the same "paper green" mechanisms as its industry competitors.

1. **Claims of "net zero" using low-quality offsets (2020–2022)** — Meta announced achieving a net-zero carbon footprint in 2020 by purchasing offset credits. A Bloomberg Green investigation found that a significant portion of the purchased offsets related to avoided deforestation projects (REDD+) whose verification was seriously questioned by Carbon Market Watch.
   *Source: Bloomberg Green, "Carbon Offset Market Has a Serious Fraud Problem", 2021*

2. **Advertising the "sustainable" virtual world of Metaverse (2021–2023)** — Meta promoted the metaverse concept, including references to the potential "environmental benefit" of replacing physical travel with virtual meetings. Research by RWTH Aachen University found that a one-hour VR session consumes as much electricity as a smartphone over several days of active use, undermining the Metaverse's "green" narrative.
   *Source: RWTH Aachen University study, 2021; Ars Technica analysis, 2022*

3. **Membership in climate coalitions while real footprint grew (2019–2023)** — Meta is a member of RE100 and the Climate Pledge, yet the company's actual energy consumption grew faster than the share of genuinely generated green energy. An independent audit found that a significant proportion of the "green" kilowatt-hours in Meta's reports came from capacity built long before the company signed corporate climate agreements.
   *Source: RE100 Progress Report 2022; Carbon Disclosure Project Meta report, 2022*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Meta actively uses ESG frameworks to improve its reputation, attract investment, and soften regulatory pressure.

1. **Annual ESG reports with a focus on the positive (2019–2023)** — Meta publishes detailed sustainability reports in which environmental indicators are described in sections preceding the social and governance sections (including moderation, genocide, and Cambridge Analytica). Investors cite ESG reports when assigning ratings, while human-rights organisations point to a gap between declared values and actual business practices.
   *Source: Meta Sustainability Report 2022; Amnesty International report on Meta, 2022*

2. **Meta Open Compute Project as a "green" initiative (since 2011)** — Meta promotes the Open Compute Project (data-centre efficiency hardware standards) as its flagship contribution to reducing the energy intensity of the IT industry. This positioning is actively used in ESG communications, despite the fact that reductions in energy per unit of computation are accompanied by a many-fold increase in total computational volume.
   *Source: Meta Open Compute Project, annual reports*

3. **Public commitment to water neutrality by 2030 (2021)** — Meta announced a goal to become "water neutral" by 2030. The commitment was widely cited in ESG ratings. However, the water-neutrality metrics do not disclose how consumption is being offset in specific crisis regions (Arizona, New Mexico) where data centres are located directly in areas of water scarcity.
   *Source: Meta Water Stewardship Strategy, 2021*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Meta has received recognition in ESG ratings despite well-known human rights and environmental violations.

1. **Inclusion in the MSCI ESG Leaders Index (2019–2021)** — Rating agency MSCI assigned Meta ratings of "BB" and "BBB", including the company in sustainable investment indices — even after the 2017–2018 Rohingya genocide. Human-rights defenders and academic researchers criticised MSCI's methodology for giving insufficient weight to social and human-rights risks.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings Meta Platforms, 2019–2021; Harvard Business School correlation analysis, 2022*

2. **Newsweek "America's Most Responsible Companies" nomination (2021)** — Meta was listed in Newsweek's "America's Most Responsible Companies" in the social responsibility category, despite numerous documented cases of harm to users.
   *Source: Newsweek America's Most Responsible Companies, 2021*

3. **Energy efficiency award from The Green Grid (2018)** — Meta's data centre in Prineville, Oregon received recognition from The Green Grid association for a high Power Usage Effectiveness coefficient (PUE close to 1.1). The award was widely circulated in ESG communications, although the PUE metric reflects only internal efficiency and does not account for the total environmental footprint.
   *Source: The Green Grid Award 2018; Meta Engineering Blog*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Meta Quest headsets and other Reality Labs devices were promoted with environmental claims that do not reflect the full lifecycle.

1. **Meta Quest 2 and "sustainable packaging" claims (2021–2022)** — Meta promoted Quest 2 headsets in packaging made from recycled materials, emphasising "sustainable design". However, the device's repairability was rated 4/10 by iFixit: the battery and display cannot be replaced independently, condemning the device to early obsolescence and electronic waste.
   *Source: iFixit Meta Quest 2 Teardown, 2020; Meta Sustainability packaging claims*

2. **Promoting Ray-Ban Stories as "smart glasses for sustainable living" (2021)** — The Ray-Ban Stories smart glasses were promoted with an emphasis on "reducing the number of devices" in the user's life, thereby implicitly positioning them as an environmentally responsible choice. Production included components from China through unverified supply chains.
   *Source: Meta Ray-Ban Stories marketing materials, 2021*

3. **"Green" Meta HQ campus as an example of "sustainable architecture" (2015–2022)** — The MPK20 campus in Menlo Park was promoted as "the world's largest open office space" with a green roof and LEED environmental certifications. However, the total carbon footprint from campus operations and the daily commuting of thousands of employees by car was not disclosed in marketing materials.
   *Source: Frank Gehry / MPK20 press materials; US Green Building Council LEED*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Meta staged symbolic environmental events incommensurable with the scale of the actual harm caused by its operations.

1. **Corporate "Earth Days" with volunteer clean-ups (2018–2022)** — Meta employees participated in annual clean-ups around campuses in Menlo Park and New York. A few hundred employees participated in each event, picking up predominantly plastic litter. The total weight of the collected waste amounted to a few tonnes — while one hour of Meta's server infrastructure generates equivalent emissions of tens of tonnes of CO₂.
   *Source: Meta Newsroom, Earth Day posts 2019–2022*

2. **Coral reef restoration initiative via VR (2021)** — Meta announced a partnership with a reef conservation organisation, promoting VR's capabilities for "environmental education" about threats to coral ecosystems. Funding amounted to a few hundred thousand dollars — a negligibly small sum compared to the company's multi-billion-dollar revenues and environmental footprint.
   *Source: Meta Reality Labs Blog, 2021*

3. **Meta Sustainability Challenge programme for employees (2020)** — Meta launched an internal gamified app encouraging employees to reduce their personal carbon footprint (avoiding single-use packaging, cycling, etc.). The programme covered a few thousand participants and created the impression of a corporate environmental culture while having no effect on the systemic sources of harm.
   *Source: Meta Sustainability Report 2021, Employee Engagement section*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Meta promotes the narrative that digital technologies and the metaverse are environmental goods, ignoring or concealing their real resource footprint.

1. **Promoting the metaverse as a "green" alternative to physical travel (2021–2022)** — During the rebranding launch, Meta employees in media comments and at conferences articulated the thesis that virtual meetings in the metaverse would sharply reduce air travel and business trips, cutting the carbon footprint. This narrative was spread without any independent research into the real energy consumption of VR infrastructure.
   *Source: Mark Zuckerberg, Founders Letter, 2021; critical analysis: Gizmodo, 2021*

2. **Lobbying against end-to-end encryption under the guise of protecting children (2022–2023)** — Meta actively participated in debates on encryption regulation, evading mandatory privacy-by-design measures. Under the pretext of combating child abuse, the company lobbied to retain access to user data for advertising algorithms. This indirectly supported the advertising business model that incentivises maximum content consumption and growth in computing power.
   *Source: Electronic Frontier Foundation, 2022; Access Now analysis, 2023*

3. **Funding "carbon-neutral" advertising industry through Ad Net Zero (2021–2023)** — Meta joined the Ad Net Zero initiative promoting decarbonisation of the advertising industry. Yet Meta's core business — serving personalised advertising — stimulates production and consumption of goods that are one of the largest sources of the global carbon footprint. Participation in Ad Net Zero was used to divert attention from this systemic contradiction.
   *Source: Ad Net Zero members list, 2022; Badvertising campaign analysis, 2022*

## Summary

1. **Mass harm to people**: Facebook directly contributed to the Rohingya genocide in Myanmar (recognised by the UN); 87 million users' data leaked through Cambridge Analytica; psychological harm to thousands of low-paid moderators.
2. **Environmental disasters**: Water consumption in crisis zones (Arizona, Netherlands); many-fold growth in energy consumption while declaring climate commitments.
3. **Greenwashing**: Net-zero claims using low-quality carbon offsets; promoting the metaverse as a "green" alternative without scientific basis.
4. **ESG marketing**: Participation in RE100 and Climate Pledge while real consumption grew; ESG reports emphasising positives and concealing systemic violations.
5. **ESG awards**: Inclusion in MSCI ESG Leaders after the Rohingya genocide; Newsweek Most Responsible Companies in the year an investigation into moderator working conditions was published.
6. **False eco-labels**: Quest 2 headsets with "sustainable packaging" but zero repairability; LEED-certified campus without accounting for employees' transport footprint.
7. **Negligible eco-events**: Volunteer clean-ups and VR coral-reef education — symbolic gestures against a multi-billion-dollar carbon footprint.
8. **Lobbying and pseudo-education**: The metaverse-as-climate-solution narrative; participation in Ad Net Zero while systemically stimulating consumerism through the advertising business.
