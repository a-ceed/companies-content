Michelin — the world's largest tyre manufacturer — opened a 15,000-hectare natural rubber plantation in Laos in 2013. Investigations by NGOs FERN and Forest Peoples Programme found that land clearance for the plantation proceeded without adequate information and consent of local communities practising slash-and-burn agriculture.

In Indonesia and Thailand, Michelin's supply chains are linked to plantations located on land cleared from tropical forests. Michelin introduced a "Sustainable Natural Rubber Policy" in 2016, but tracing rubber to its ultimate source in complex multi-tier supply chains remains technically challenging.

Tyres generate enormous amounts of "tyre dust" — one of the largest sources of microplastics in the urban environment. According to Swiss researchers, each car loses approximately 1 kg of rubber dust per year from its tyres, which enters water bodies through storm drains.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Michelin's rubber plantations in Cambodia and Laos led to forced community displacement, destruction of livelihoods, and documented human rights violations.

1. **Forced displacement in Cambodia (2008–2015)** — Michelin subsidiary KPC (Kampong Pouk Plantation Co.) in Kampong Thom province received land concessions covering more than 24,000 hectares. According to Amnesty International (2015), more than 1,000 families were evicted from land they had occupied for generations. Residents lost agricultural land, fruit gardens, and access to water sources without adequate compensation.
   *Source: Amnesty International, "Cambodia: Rubber, Land and People", June 2015; Forum for Democracy in Burma/LICADHO Cambodia*

2. **Workers' rights violations on plantations in Laos (2013–2018)** — FERN and Forest Peoples Programme documented the absence of Free, Prior and Informed Consent (FPIC) from communities when the plantation opened in Salavan province. Workers were paid wages below subsistence level; contracts were concluded only in languages incomprehensible to the local population.
   *Source: FERN, "Michelin in Laos", 2013; Forest Peoples Programme, Laos Mission Report, 2014*

3. **Air pollution at the tyre manufacturing plant in South Carolina (2005–2015)** — Michelin's plant in Greenville, South Carolina repeatedly exceeded permissible emissions levels for volatile organic compounds (VOCs) and heavy metals. Residents of adjacent low-income areas complained of respiratory illnesses. The EPA issued several notices of violation.
   *Source: EPA Air Enforcement Records, Michelin, Greenville SC; South Carolina Department of Health and Environmental Control, inspection reports*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Michelin's natural rubber supply chain is linked to large-scale tropical deforestation, and synthetic tyre production is the largest source of microplastics in aquatic environments.

1. **Tropical deforestation for rubber plantations in Cambodia (2008–2015)** — Land clearance for KPC plantations in Cambodia involved the destruction of significant areas of tropical forest and degradation of water catchment basins. Satellite analysis by Global Forest Watch recorded forest cover loss on concession land directly linked to Michelin supplies.
   *Source: Global Forest Watch satellite analysis, 2015; Rainforest Alliance Supply Chain Assessment, 2016*

2. **Tyre-derived microplastic contamination of rivers (global)** — A study by the Swiss Federal Institute of Aquatic Science and Technology (Eawag) established that tyre rubber is the largest source of microplastics in European urban rivers. A single car loses up to 1 kg of rubber crumb per year, which enters water bodies through storm drains. The chemical compound 6PPD-quinone from tyres has been identified as highly toxic to coho salmon.
   *Source: Eawag, "Tire rubber as a source of microplastics", 2020; Science, "A ubiquitous tire rubber–derived chemical induces acute mortality in coho salmon", 2021*

3. **Peatland destruction in rubber supply chains (Indonesia, Thailand, 2010s)** — Independent supply-chain audits of Michelin identified links to processors purchasing rubber from plantations located on former peatland in Sumatra. Draining and burning peatland for rubber cultivation releases carbon accumulated over millennia.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network, "Who's Behind the Wheel", 2016; Supply Change / Forest 500 assessment, Michelin, 2017*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Michelin declares "sustainable" supply chains while independent audits record violations in key supply links.

1. **"Sustainable Rubber Policy" (2016) against a backdrop of violations in Cambodia** — Michelin adopted a sustainable natural rubber policy in 2016, but the evictions and violations were documented by Amnesty International in 2015. Critics noted that the policy appeared as a reaction to reputational damage rather than a proactive measure, and compensation to affected families was never paid in full.
   *Source: Amnesty International, 2015; FERN response to Michelin policy, 2016*

2. **RSPO certification while retaining problematic suppliers (2018–2022)** — Michelin demonstrated commitment to sustainable production standards while continuing to purchase from processors linked to deforestation in Indonesia. Chain Reaction Research identified direct links between Michelin suppliers and deforestation in "high-risk" categories.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research, "Michelin Supply Chain Deforestation Risk", 2019*

3. **Advertising "eco-friendly" tyres without disclosing full production lifecycle (2015–2022)** — Michelin actively advertised ENERGY Saver tyres as "fuel-saving" and "CO₂-reducing" in use, while not disclosing the full carbon footprint of production: crude oil extraction for synthetic rubber, the energy-intensive vulcanisation process, and disposal of used tyres.
   *Source: Michelin marketing materials, 2015–2022; European Tyre and Rubber Manufacturers' Association lifecycle analysis critique*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Michelin actively uses ESG instruments to attract investors and corporate clients, positioning itself as a responsible industry player.

1. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index Europe (2018–2022)** — Michelin regularly featured in the European DJSI as a model of sustainable manufacturing, despite ongoing human rights violations in the supply chain and the absence of a systemic solution to the microplastics problem.
   *Source: S&P Global DJSI Assessment, Michelin, 2019–2022*

2. **ESG-linked bonds to finance "sustainable" production (2021)** — Michelin issued ESG-linked bonds tied to emissions-reduction and "sustainable" sourcing targets. Independent analysts from Planet Tracker noted the absence of verifiable metrics for ending forest-linked rubber purchasing.
   *Source: Planet Tracker, "Michelin Sustainability Bond Analysis", 2021; Michelin bond prospectus*

3. **MSCI ESG rating of "A" (2020–2022)** — Michelin received an MSCI rating of "A" during a period when the company's supply chains were still linked to deforestation in Indonesia and Thailand. MSCI's methodology assessed management processes rather than actual outcomes in preventing deforestation.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Research, Michelin Rating Report, 2021*

---

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Michelin's ESG marketing strategy has earned a number of international awards that contrast with the actual situation in its supply chain.

1. **CDP "A" for forest management (2019)** — Michelin received the highest CDP rating in the forest-risk management category ("Forests A-List"), positioning itself as a leader in the fight against deforestation. The award was given despite documented supply-chain links to tropical deforestation.
   *Source: CDP Forests Score, Michelin, 2019; Chain Reaction Research critique, 2019*

2. **EcoVadis Platinum Rating (2021)** — Michelin received an EcoVadis Platinum rating — the highest level of supply-chain sustainability assessment. The rating was actively used in B2B client and investor communications as evidence of supplier reliability.
   *Source: EcoVadis, Michelin Scorecard, 2021; Michelin Supplier Sustainability presentation*

3. **Annual recognition in Ethisphere "World's Most Ethical Companies" (2016–2021)** — Michelin was repeatedly included on the Ethisphere list of the world's most ethical companies — during a period when documented violations of the rights of displaced Cambodian residents were ongoing.
   *Source: Ethisphere Institute, World's Most Ethical Companies list, 2016–2021; Amnesty International ongoing monitoring*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Michelin widely applies environmental labels to its tyre products, emphasising operational characteristics while omitting production impacts.

1. **EU Tyre Label "A" for fuel efficiency (2012–present)** — Michelin Energy Saver and similar lines receive the highest EU Tyre Label scores for low rolling resistance, which the company positions as an "environmental" advantage. The label entirely ignores the environmental harm from tyre production, the rubber lifecycle, and microplastic generation.
   *Source: EU Regulation 1222/2009 on tyre labelling; ETRMA lifecycle analysis, 2018*

2. **"Green Performance" logo on corporate materials (2015–2022)** — Michelin used its own "Green Performance" mark in advertising materials without verification by an independent third party. The mark was applied to products whose manufacture involves petrochemical raw materials and potential deforestation.
   *Source: Michelin marketing and product catalogues, 2015–2022; EU Unfair Commercial Practices Directive analysis*

3. **"Sustainable rubber" labelling on products without full supply-chain traceability (2018–2022)** — Michelin labelled certain products and corporate lines as containing "sustainable rubber" without full traceability to plantation level. Independent auditors noted that tracing rubber origin in multi-tier chains down to farm level is technically impossible with existing systems.
   *Source: Rainforest Alliance Supply Chain Audit, 2019; Global Canopy Forest 500 assessment*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Michelin regularly stages environmental events incommensurable with the damage caused by tyre manufacturing and the rubber supply chain.

1. **Used-tyre collection and recycling programme (France, 2014–2020)** — Michelin participated in used-tyre collection programmes in France under extended producer responsibility. However, most collected tyres were incinerated as fuel for cement plants (so-called "energy recovery"), which is not genuine recycling and is accompanied by toxic gas emissions.
   *Source: Aliapur (French tyre recycling scheme) Annual Report, 2018; European Environment Bureau critique*

2. **Corporate "Clean Air" events in Clermont-Ferrand (annual)** — Michelin organised cycling days and supported air-quality initiatives in its headquarters city of Clermont-Ferrand. Meanwhile, production emissions from the plant remained significant, and tyre microplastics entered the Allier River.
   *Source: Michelin CSR reports, 2016–2020; Institut national de l'environnement industriel et des risques (INERIS), air quality data*

3. **Tree planting under a "biodiversity" programme near the plant in Chennai, India (2019)** — Michelin India organised the planting of several thousand trees near its Chennai plant under a "greening" programme that received wide press-release coverage. The scale of the event is incommensurable with the volume of deforestation in the rubber supply chain.
   *Source: Michelin India press release, "Biodiversity plantation drive", 2019*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Michelin actively shapes the "green transport" narrative, focusing attention on tyre efficiency in use and diverting it from production and supply-chain problems.

1. **Lobbying at EU level for a tyre labelling methodology favourable to manufacturers (2009–2012)** — During the development of EU tyre labelling regulations, manufacturers including Michelin, through the ETRMA industry association, sought to limit the label to operational efficiency parameters (rolling resistance, braking distance) and exclude lifecycle parameters including the production carbon footprint and microplastics.
   *Source: European Parliament legislative records, Regulation 1222/2009; ETRMA lobbying disclosure, Brussels*

2. **Promoting "mobility without harm" as a response to criticism (2017–2022)** — Michelin actively promoted the concept of "All Sustainable Mobility", presenting the tyre industry as a partner in solving climate challenges rather than a source of problems. The concept emphasised the role of tyres in reducing fuel consumption while concealing the microplastics problem.
   *Source: Michelin "All Sustainable Mobility" white papers, 2018–2022; InfluenceMap transport sector analysis*

3. **"Educational" campaigns on "correct tyre pressure" to reduce emissions (2015–2022)** — Michelin funded global "Michelin Tyre Pressure" (correct tyre pressure) campaigns, positioning them as a climate initiative. The campaigns shifted responsibility for CO₂ emissions onto drivers, while fundamentally more significant problems — microplastics and deforestation — were kept in the background.
   *Source: Michelin "Be tyre smart" campaign; Consumer advocacy analysis, 2019*

## Summary

1. **Harm to people**: Forced displacement of more than 1,000 families in Cambodia, violation of labour rights in Laos, and air pollution near factories in the USA are documented by international human-rights organisations.
2. **Environmental disasters**: Deforestation for rubber plantations in Cambodia and Indonesia, and the release of tyre microplastics — the largest anthropogenic source of contamination in urban water bodies — constitute long-term systemic harm.
3. **Greenwashing**: The "Sustainable Rubber Policy" was adopted retrospectively after human-rights scandals; the advertised "eco-friendly" tyres are evaluated solely on operational, not lifecycle, criteria.
4. **ESG marketing**: High MSCI and EcoVadis ratings and ESG-linked bonds were obtained while supply-chain violations continued and verifiable metrics for ending deforestation were absent.
5. **ESG awards**: CDP, Ethisphere, and EcoVadis awards were conferred despite documented human rights violations and environmental problems in supply chains.
6. **Eco-labelling**: EU Tyre Label "A", the "Green Performance" logo, and "sustainable rubber" labelling were applied to products without full lifecycle analysis and independent verification.
7. **Negligible eco-events**: Collecting tyres for incineration, cycling days at headquarters, and tree-planting are incommensurable with the scale of microplastic pollution and deforestation in the supply chain.
8. **Lobbying**: Michelin, through ETRMA, succeeded in limiting tyre labelling to operational parameters, excluding production and lifecycle parameters, and actively promotes "green mobility" narratives that shift responsibility onto drivers.
