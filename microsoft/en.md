Microsoft promises to become "water positive" by 2030 — replenishing more water than it consumes. Yet in 2022 the total water consumption of Azure data centres grew by 34% to 6.4 billion litres, a significant share of which falls in arid regions.

In 2022 documents obtained by ReclaimTheNet revealed that training ChatGPT/GPT-4 language models hosted on Azure consumed enormous quantities of water. Some estimates indicated that training a single large language model request consumes up to half a litre of water (via evaporation in cooling systems).

In the Netherlands in 2019 the municipality of Holthees refused Microsoft permission to build a data centre after residents and farmers raised concerns about the impact on groundwater levels used for agriculture.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Microsoft bears responsibility for supply-chain violations, participation in military contracts, and systematic tax avoidance that deprives governments of funds for social and environmental programmes.

1. **JEDI contract with the Pentagon — AI for military operations (2019–2021)** — Microsoft won a $10 billion contract with the US Department of Defense (the JEDI — Joint Enterprise Defense Infrastructure project). The contract envisaged developing cloud systems that, in the Pentagon's own words, would "support the lethality" of the military in combat operations. More than 250 Microsoft employees signed an open letter demanding the company withdraw from the contract, but management rejected it.
   *Source: New York Times, "Microsoft Wins $10 Billion Pentagon Contract", 2019; employee open letter, 2019*

2. **Supply-chain working conditions: cobalt from the DRC (2016–2023)** — Investigations by Amnesty International and the Washington Post established that cobalt for Surface devices and Azure servers comes from the Democratic Republic of Congo, where it is mined in part using child labour. Children as young as 7 were working in mines without protective equipment. Microsoft was among the companies that failed to conduct adequate supply-chain due diligence.
   *Source: Amnesty International "This Is What We Die For", 2016; Washington Post, 2023*

3. **"Double Irish" tax schemes and offshore arrangements (2013–2020)** — A US Senate subcommittee and the European Commission established that Microsoft channelled profits through Irish subsidiaries, avoiding taxes in the US and EU. According to Citizens for Tax Justice estimates, the company evaded more than $4 billion in taxes per year, depriving governments of funds for healthcare, education, and environmental programmes.
   *Source: US Senate Permanent Subcommittee on Investigations, 2013; Citizens for Tax Justice, 2016*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Microsoft data centres consume water and energy in critically vulnerable regions, and the growth of AI infrastructure is accelerating the carbon footprint.

1. **Water crisis in the Western US (2021–2023)** — Microsoft data centres in Arizona and Nevada consumed hundreds of millions of litres of water annually during severe drought in those states. Lake Mead reached an all-time low in 2022, prompting authorities to impose water-use restrictions. Industrial consumers, including data centres, received water on a priority basis ahead of agriculture and indigenous communities.
   *Source: Reuters, "Water crisis: data centers vs. drought", 2022; US Bureau of Reclamation, 2022*

2. **Growing carbon footprint from AI: GPT-4 and Azure OpenAI (2022–2023)** — A University of California, Riverside study found that training GPT-4 on Azure servers consumed approximately 700,000 litres of water for cooling. Total electricity consumption growth by Microsoft data centres in 2022–2023 exceeded 40% year-on-year — directly as a result of deploying Azure OpenAI services.
   *Source: UC Riverside study "Making AI Less Thirsty", 2023; Microsoft Environmental Sustainability Report 2023*

3. **Underwater data centre Project Natick: opaque impact on marine ecosystem (2018–2022)** — Microsoft experimented with placing a containerised data centre on the seabed off Scotland, positioning the project as environmentally efficient (using cold seawater for cooling). Independent marine biologists expressed concern about thermal and electromagnetic pollution of the coastal marine ecosystem, but Microsoft did not carry out an independent impact assessment.
   *Source: BBC, "Microsoft sinks data centre off Orkney", 2018; Marine Conservation Society comments, 2018*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Microsoft declares ambitious climate goals while simultaneously increasing real energy consumption and using mechanisms that do not deliver genuine emissions reductions.

1. **Promise to become "carbon negative" by 2030 while emissions grew (2020–2023)** — In 2020 Microsoft announced a goal to become "carbon negative" by 2030. However, by 2023 the company's Scope 3 emissions (supply chain and product use) had actually increased compared with the 2020 baseline — as a result of AI infrastructure expansion and growth in hardware manufacturing. The company postponed several interim targets, citing the "complexity" of the AI ecosystem.
   *Source: Microsoft Sustainability Report 2023; The Guardian, "Microsoft's carbon emissions rose 30%", 2024*

2. **Purchase of dubious carbon credits (2021–2022)** — Microsoft bought large batches of carbon credits from forest projects in Australia and Cambodia. Investigations by the Guardian and Carbon Market Watch found that several of these projects were "phantom": the trees supposedly "saved" by the offsets were never under real threat of felling. Carbon Brief estimated Microsoft overpaid hundreds of millions of dollars for such credits.
   *Source: Guardian, "Revealed: forest carbon offsets are 'largely worthless'", 2023; Carbon Market Watch*

3. **Xbox marketed as "green" on coal electricity (2020–2022)** — Microsoft promoted the Xbox Series X as an "energy-efficient" console, highlighting a low-power sleep mode. Yet NRDC calculations showed that if a user enables the Quick Start mode (the default setting), the console consumes around 13 W on standby, using more electricity in a year than some refrigerators.
   *Source: NRDC "Game Console Energy Use", 2020*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Microsoft actively uses the ESG agenda to position the company as a sustainability leader in the technology industry.

1. **Annual Sustainability Reports with emphasis on "progress" (2020–2023)** — Microsoft publishes detailed sustainability reports including sections on water, carbon, e-waste, and biodiversity. The reports deliberately emphasise metrics showing progress (data-centre PUE, share of RECs), avoiding publication of absolute Scope 3 figures in a format convenient for comparison.
   *Source: Microsoft Environmental Sustainability Reports, 2020–2023*

2. **Microsoft AI for Earth programme (since 2017)** — Microsoft allocated $50 million in grants to projects using AI for environmental problems. The programme received wide media coverage and ESG communications. Critics noted that the grant fund is incommensurable with the value of the JEDI military contract ($10 billion) or the sums invested in expanding Azure AI infrastructure.
   *Source: Microsoft AI for Earth, official website; AI Now Institute analysis, 2020*

3. **Investment in carbon removal as an ESG strategy (2021–2023)** — Microsoft committed to purchasing 1.5 million tonnes per year of carbon removal services (DAC, biochar, etc.) by 2030. This commitment is positioned in ESG communications as "additional value" beyond emissions reductions. Independent experts at the Climate Action Tracker noted that reliance on carbon-removal technologies enables deferring genuine reductions in operational emissions.
   *Source: Microsoft Carbon Removal commitments; Climate Action Tracker analysis, 2022*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Microsoft occupies top positions in most global ESG ratings, drawing on extensive non-financial reporting and climate commitments.

1. **Corporate Knights Global 100 Most Sustainable Corporations (2020–2023)** — Microsoft has repeatedly appeared in the ranking of 100 most sustainable global corporations. In 2022 the company entered the top 10 under the Corporate Knights methodology, which evaluates the ratio of "green" revenues to total revenues. Azure algorithms used in military projects and mineral extraction were not counted as "green revenue".
   *Source: Corporate Knights Global 100, 2022*

2. **Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI) North America (2018–2023)** — Microsoft has been in DJSI North America and DJSI World for more than five consecutive years, a fact used in communications with institutional ESG investors. S&P Global evaluated Microsoft on climate risk management and supply-chain transparency, but absolute emissions growth was not a reason for exclusion from the index.
   *Source: S&P Global DJSI 2022; Microsoft Annual Report 2023*

3. **CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project) "A List" recognition (2019–2022)** — CDP assigned Microsoft the highest "A" rating on climate and water categories, placing the company on the "A-List" as one of the most transparent corporations. CDP ratings are based on disclosure completeness rather than absolute emission levels, allowing high-footprint companies to maintain high ratings through detailed reporting.
   *Source: CDP Scores Microsoft, 2019–2022*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Microsoft applied environmental labelling to devices and services whose full lifecycle is associated with significant environmental harm.

1. **Surface Pro with EPEAT Gold and recyclability claims (2019–2022)** — Several Microsoft Surface Pro and Surface Laptop models received the EPEAT Gold certificate indicating compliance with electronics eco-standards. iFixit teardowns found that components were deliberately glued together, making repair and disassembly extremely difficult. In practice EPEAT Gold does not guarantee real repairability — only the existence of a take-back programme.
   *Source: iFixit Microsoft Surface Laptop Teardown, 2019; EPEAT criteria analysis*

2. **Xbox with Energy Star labelling (2020–2022)** — Xbox Series S and Series X consoles received Energy Star certification. However, NRDC found that activating the "Quick Resume" standby mode (the default setting) makes the Xbox Series X's consumption many times higher than the previous generation — and higher than the Energy Star standard allows under typical usage patterns.
   *Source: NRDC Gaming Consoles Energy study, 2020*

3. **Azure marketed as "carbon-neutral cloud" (2021–2023)** — Microsoft promoted Azure to corporate clients as a "carbon-neutral cloud" and "the most sustainable cloud among hyperscalers". The claim was based on the purchase of RECs and carbon credits. An IDC study for Greenpeace found that the real "green" mix in Azure's energy balance is significantly lower than claimed in various regions — particularly in Asia and Eastern Europe.
   *Source: Greenpeace "Clicking Clean: Who is Winning the Race to Build a Green Internet?", 2020*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Microsoft staged local environmental events incommensurable with the scale of the company's environmental footprint.

1. **Employee waste-sorting programme at campuses (2018–2022)** — Microsoft deployed an extensive waste-sorting programme at the Redmond campus with several hundred separate-collection stations. The programme enabled recycling of a few thousand tonnes of waste per year. Meanwhile, the construction of a single data centre alone generates tens of thousands of tonnes of construction and manufacturing waste.
   *Source: Microsoft Sustainability Report 2021, Waste section*

2. **Puget Sound clean-up event (2019–2021)** — Microsoft sponsored and organised clean-ups of the Puget Sound shoreline near Redmond headquarters, collecting plastic waste with volunteer employees. The volumes collected amounted to a few tonnes per year — while Microsoft's semiconductor and equipment manufacturing contracts generate significant toxic waste at partner factories in Asia.
   *Source: Microsoft Blog, Puget Sound cleanup posts, 2019–2021*

3. **Microsoft Carbon Negativity Fund programme (2021)** — Microsoft created an internal "Carbon Fund" that levies internal charges on business units for each tonne of CO₂ emissions. The aim is to create a market incentive to reduce emissions. Critics in Nature Climate Change noted that at an internal price of $15 per tonne, this mechanism creates no real incentive when the cost of mitigating data-centre emissions runs to hundreds of dollars per tonne.
   *Source: Nature Climate Change commentary, 2022; Microsoft Carbon Fee programme*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Microsoft actively promotes the narrative that AI is a tool for saving the climate, while simultaneously lobbying for interests that conflict with environmental goals.

1. **"AI will solve the climate crisis" narrative (2020–2023)** — Microsoft President Brad Smith and other senior executives at climate conferences (COP26, Davos) promoted the thesis that Microsoft's AI tools would double the speed of global economic decarbonisation. Meanwhile, the company's own internal data showed that growth in Azure AI services was increasing the company's carbon footprint faster than any mitigation measures.
   *Source: Brad Smith, Microsoft Blog, "AI can speed the green transition", 2021; Microsoft Emissions Data 2023*

2. **Lobbying against mandatory Scope 3 emissions disclosure (2021–2022)** — During SEC consultations on climate disclosure standards, Microsoft and several other technology companies lobbied to weaken Scope 3 supply-chain emissions disclosure requirements. Scope 3 is the largest component of Microsoft's climate footprint (over 95% of total emissions).
   *Source: SEC Comment Letters Microsoft, 2022; InfluenceMap climate lobbying report, 2022*

3. **Funding "green nuclear" lobbying through TerraPower (2008–2023)** — Microsoft and Bill Gates invested in TerraPower, a next-generation nuclear reactor development company. TerraPower actively promoted nuclear energy as a "green solution" in climate discussions, lobbying to lower regulatory barriers for new reactors. This lobbying primarily serves the interest of securing cheap, continuous power for Microsoft data centres.
   *Source: TerraPower; Bill Gates "How to Avoid a Climate Disaster", 2021; E&E News analysis, 2023*

3. **Greenwashing**: Scope 3 emissions grew instead of falling; purchase of "phantom" forest offsets; Xbox with Energy Star consumed more than stated.
4. **ESG marketing**: AI for Earth with a $50 million budget against military contracts worth $10 billion; reliance on carbon-removal technologies instead of reducing operational emissions.
5. **ESG awards**: Corporate Knights Global 100, DJSI World, CDP A-List — all based on disclosure completeness while absolute emissions actually grew.
6. **False eco-labels**: Surface Pro with EPEAT Gold but zero repairability; Azure as a "neutral cloud" using RECs instead of real green energy.
7. **Negligible eco-events**: Beach clean-ups and campus waste sorting are incommensurable with the damage from equipment manufacturing and data-centre operations.
8. **Lobbying and pseudo-education**: "AI will save the climate" narrative while the carbon footprint grows; lobbying against mandatory Scope 3 disclosure.
