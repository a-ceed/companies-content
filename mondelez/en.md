Mondelez International generates enormous quantities of multi-layer plastic packaging that cannot be recycled. The company is also linked to deforestation in Borneo and Sumatra through its palm oil supply chain, threatening orangutans and other endangered species.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Mondelez profits from cocoa supply chains where child and forced labour are documented, and employs aggressive marketing of unhealthy products to children.

1. **Child labour on West African cocoa plantations (2001–2023)** — According to a NORC University of Chicago report (2020), commissioned by the U.S. Department of Labor, more than 1.56 million children are engaged in hazardous labour in cocoa production in Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire. Mondelez is one of the world's largest buyers of cocoa from these regions. The company pledged to eliminate child labour from its supply chain by 2010, then by 2020 — both commitments were broken.
   *Source: NORC University of Chicago, 2020; Washington Post Cocoa Investigation, 2019*

2. **Tax schemes depriving countries of resources to protect labour rights (2012–2017)** — According to ActionAid's investigation (2016), Mondelez used aggressive transfer pricing schemes, shifting profits to low-tax jurisdictions. As a result, the Netherlands and Switzerland became centres of tax optimisation, while the budgets of cocoa-producing countries were deprived of funds for worker social protection.
   *Source: ActionAid "Sweet Nothings", 2016; European Commission State Aid Investigation*

3. **Forced labour in the cocoa supply chain (2019–2021)** — In 2021 the U.S. Supreme Court heard the case of Nestle USA and Cargill v. former child slaves (Doe v. Nestle); analogous suits against Mondelez were filed in U.S. courts by a group of NGOs that documented forced labour on farms supplying Cadbury and Oreo.
   *Source: International Rights Advocates; Human Rights Watch, "Child Labor in Cocoa", 2020*

## 2. Has the corporation been responsible for major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Mondelez's palm oil purchases are linked to the destruction of Indonesia's peatland forests, and product plastic packaging generates billions of units of non-recyclable waste.

1. **Palm oil and destruction of peatland forests in Sumatra and Borneo (2010–2021)** — Rainforest Action Network reports in 2018 and 2020 established that Mondelez's palm oil suppliers were clearing peatland forests in Sumatra. Peatlands are the largest carbon depositories: their drainage and burning releases thousands of years of accumulated CO₂. The Sumatran tiger and orangutan are under threat.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network "Conflict Palm Oil", 2018; Greenpeace "Oreo: Last Forest Standing", 2018*

2. **Plastic packaging in the top ocean polluters (2018–2022)** — Mondelez products (Oreo, Cadbury, Milka) annually rank among the most commonly found brands in Break Free From Plastic beach litter audits. Multi-layer wrappers made of aluminised film and polypropylene cannot be mechanically recycled.
   *Source: Break Free From Plastic Brand Audit 2019–2022*

3. **Water pollution on cocoa plantations of suppliers (2015–2020)** — Mighty Earth (2018) documented cases of Mondelez purchasing cocoa from suppliers operating within specially protected natural areas of Côte d'Ivoire. Pesticide use on illegal plantations led to pollution of waterways.
   *Source: Mighty Earth "Chocolate's Dark Secret", 2018*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Mondelez systematically proclaimed commitments on sustainable palm oil and cocoa while supply chains continued to demonstrate deforestation.

1. **"Deforestation-free palm oil sourcing" policy (2014–2020)** — In 2014 Mondelez signed the New York Declaration on Forests, committing to fully transition to "sustainable" palm oil sourcing by 2020. In 2019, Rainforest Action Network recorded deforestation by suppliers listed on Mondelez's approved list.
   *Source: RAN "Failing Forests", 2019; Mondelez Palm Oil Policy Assessment*

2. **"Cocoa Life" programme — environmental marketing without verification (2012–2022)** — Mondelez advertises the Cocoa Life programme as a "sustainable" alternative covering 200,000 farmers. An independent audit (KPMG, 2019) found that the programme has no verifiable indicators for reducing child labour, and some "covered" farms continue to use child labour.
   *Source: KPMG Cocoa Life Audit, 2019; Washington Post, 2019*

3. **"Sustainable packaging" with actual growth in plastic (2020–2022)** — Mondelez announced a goal to make all packaging "recyclable" by 2025. Meanwhile, according to Break Free From Plastic (2022), the volume of non-recyclable Mondelez packaging has not decreased, and the company does not disclose its methodology for calculating "recyclability".
   *Source: Break Free From Plastic Brand Audit 2022; Mondelez Packaging Commitments*

## 4. Does the company participate in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Mondelez actively uses ESG narratives through annual reports, partnerships and themed advertising campaigns.

1. **Annual "Snacking Made Right" ESG report (2018–2023)** — Mondelez publishes substantial ESG reports under the "Snacking Made Right" brand, covering climate targets, social programmes and packaging initiatives. The reports are distributed to investors and partners as evidence of "sustainable transformation".
   *Source: Mondelez International ESG Reports 2018–2023*

2. **Partnership with RSPO and marketing of "sustainable palm oil" (2010–2022)** — Mondelez has been an RSPO member since 2010 and actively uses its association with the organisation in corporate materials, while independent checks record deforestation in supply chains.
   *Source: RSPO Annual Reports; Rainforest Action Network, 2020*

3. **"Oreo — More Sustainably Sourced" campaign (2021)** — Mondelez released promotional materials claiming "responsibly sourced" ingredients in Oreo, while palm oil and cocoa supplies from deforestation regions continued. The campaign was criticised by Greenpeace as misleading.
   *Source: Greenpeace International statement, 2021*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG competition nominations?

**Answer: YES**. Mondelez received high ESG ratings and awards from ESG organisations over a number of years.

1. **Inclusion in Dow Jones Sustainability Index World (2017–2021)** — Mondelez was repeatedly included in DJSI World in the "Food Products" category, which was used in investor communications as confirmation of ESG leadership.
   *Source: S&P Global DJSI Yearbooks 2018–2021*

2. **Rating of "A-" from CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project) (2019–2021)** — CDP awarded Mondelez a high score for climate data disclosure. The company actively cited this score in press releases, while keeping silent about limited Scope 3 coverage of supply chain emissions.
   *Source: CDP Scores 2019–2021*

3. **Recognition as "Industry Leader" in sustainable cocoa by the VOICE Network (2020)** — The industry association VOICE Network recognised Cocoa Life as "best practice" among corporate cocoa programmes, even though independent auditors recorded continuing child labour on covered farms.
   *Source: VOICE Network Annual Report, 2020; KPMG Cocoa Life Audit*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Mondelez products carry certification marks that create a false impression of environmental responsibility.

1. **Fairtrade logo on Cadbury chocolate with documented child labour (2009–2023)** — Part of the Cadbury range (Dairy Milk) is labelled Fairtrade. However, investigations by journalists and NGOs recorded child labour in Cadbury's supply chain, which Fairtrade certification technically allows at the smallholder level.
   *Source: The Guardian "Fairtrade chocolate and child labour", 2019; BBC Panorama, 2010*

2. **RSPO labelling on products containing palm oil, while deforestation continues (2013–2022)** — Oreo, belVita and other Mondelez products carry the RSPO label. At the same time, Rainforest Action Network documented peatland clearing by suppliers within Mondelez's supply chain holding valid RSPO certificates.
   *Source: RAN "Conflict Palm Oil", 2018*

3. **"Cocoa Life certified" inscription on Milka and Oreo packaging (2020–2022)** — Mondelez's own "Cocoa Life" certification is placed on packaging as if it were an independent eco-standard. Independent experts point out that "Cocoa Life" is an internal corporate programme without external independent verification of child labour incidents.
   *Source: KPMG Cocoa Life Audit, 2019; NORC University of Chicago, 2020*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Mondelez conducted environmental actions that are incomparable in scale to the real damage caused by its activities.

1. **"Plant a tree" under Cocoa Life (2018–2022)** — Mondelez announced tree planting on cocoa farms as part of the Cocoa Life programme. The scale of planting achieved amounted to several hundred thousand trees, while the area of tropical deforestation in cocoa-sourcing regions amounts to hundreds of thousands of hectares annually.
   *Source: Mondelez Cocoa Life Progress Report, 2021; Global Forest Watch*

2. **Corporate litter-collection days near offices (2016–2022)** — Mondelez employees participate annually in environmental clean-up days. The total volume of litter collected is incomparable to the daily volume of non-recyclable packaging produced by the company.
   *Source: Mondelez CSR Reports 2018–2022*

3. **"Reduce packaging" — selective lightweighting while overall volume grows (2020–2022)** — Mondelez announced a 10–15% weight reduction for certain packaging lines in several European markets. Meanwhile the total volume of packaging sold grew proportionally to sales growth, and the total weight of packaging waste increased.
   *Source: Mondelez Packaging Reduction Announcement, 2021; WRAP UK Analysis*

## 8. Has the company lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories or greenwashing outreach?

**Answer: YES**. Mondelez participated in lobbying for voluntary industry standards against binding regulation and promoted narratives of individual consumer responsibility.

1. **Lobbying through the World Cocoa Foundation against mandatory standards (2014–2021)** — Mondelez is a major donor to the World Cocoa Foundation, which promoted voluntary industry initiatives as an alternative to legislative regulation of child labour in the EU and USA, slowing the adoption of mandatory due-diligence mechanisms.
   *Source: World Cocoa Foundation Annual Reports; Corporate Accountability International, 2016*

2. **Opposition to the EU Deforestation Regulation (2021–2022)** — Mondelez, together with other confectionery companies, lobbied for the softening of the EU Deforestation Regulation requirements, pushing for extended transition periods and a weakening of traceability obligations for cocoa and palm oil supplies.
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory, 2022; Global Witness Lobbying Report, 2022*

3. **"Choose Wisely" educational campaign — shifting responsibility to the consumer (2019–2022)** — Mondelez funded educational materials under the slogan "make a conscious choice", emphasising the role of the individual consumer in environmental problems rather than systemic change in the corporation's production practices.
   *Source: Mondelez Marketing Materials, 2019–2022; Break Free From Plastic Analysis*
