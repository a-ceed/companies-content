Monsanto (now part of Bayer) produced Roundup, the world's most widely used herbicide. Glyphosate contamination has been linked to destruction of biodiversity, soil microbiome collapse, and water pollution across agricultural regions worldwide.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Monsanto's products caused documented harm to human health: from cancer linked to glyphosate to large-scale poisoning of Anniston, Alabama residents with polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs).

1. **PCB contamination of Anniston (1935–2002)** — For decades Monsanto's Anniston, Alabama plant discharged polychlorinated biphenyls into Choccolocco Creek and surrounding soils. Residents were not warned of the danger. Internal company documents revealed in litigation showed that Monsanto knew of PCB toxicity as early as the 1960s. In 2003 Monsanto and Solutia paid $700 million to Anniston residents in a class-action settlement.
   *Source: Anniston Star court reporting; Grist, "Monsanto's Toxic Legacy", 2002; Abernathy v. Monsanto, 2003*

2. **Roundup lawsuits and non-Hodgkin's lymphoma (2015–2022)** — After the IARC classified glyphosate as "probably carcinogenic" (Group 2A) in 2015, more than 100,000 Americans filed lawsuits against Monsanto/Bayer. Courts awarded multi-million-dollar damages: in 2018 — $289 million (Dewayne Johnson), in 2019 — $2 billion (the Pilliod couple). By 2022 Bayer had paid more than $10 billion in settled claims.
   *Source: IARC Monograph 112, 2015; Reuters, "Bayer settles Roundup claims", 2020*

3. **Dioxin poisoning of Nitro, West Virginia residents (1949)** — After an explosion at Monsanto's Nitro, WV plant in 1949, plant workers and local residents were exposed to dioxin produced during manufacture of the herbicide 2,4,5-T. Monsanto concealed data about dioxin contamination. Long-term health consequences included chloracne, neurological disorders, and cancer.
   *Source: Environmental Working Group, "Monsanto's Decades of Deception", 1995; EPA dioxin records*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Monsanto is responsible for large-scale PCB contamination in the USA, destruction of biodiversity by glyphosate, and ecosystem degradation through GMO monocultures.

1. **PCB contamination of Choccolocco Creek and the Anniston ecosystem (1929–1971)** — Desoto Park Lake in Anniston, Alabama was so contaminated with PCBs from Monsanto's plant that the fish in it were literally decomposing alive — internal documents described "fish dying with deformed organs". PCB concentrations in fish tissue exceeded permissible limits by hundreds of times. The lake was closed to fishing for decades.
   *Source: Monsanto internal memos, 1966 (released in litigation); EPA Site Assessment, Anniston, 1997*

2. **Destruction of the monarch butterfly population in the USA (1990s–2010s)** — Widespread application of Roundup on RoundupReady GMO fields destroyed milkweed — the only host plant for monarch butterfly reproduction. According to WWF and Xerces Society data, the monarch population declined by 80–90% between 1994 and 2014. The destruction of pollinators threatens ecosystem services worth billions of dollars.
   *Source: WWF / Monarch Watch annual census; Xerces Society, "Milkweed, Monarchs and More", 2014*

3. **Super-resistant "superweeds" contamination (2000s–2010s)** — Monoculture application of Roundup led to the evolution of 38 glyphosate-resistant "superweed" species across more than 30 million hectares in the USA. To combat them, farmers were forced to use even more toxic herbicides including dicamba, leading to mass destruction of neighbouring farms' crops through wind drift.
   *Source: Weed Science Society of America, 2016; NRDC, "Superweeds: How Biotech Crops Bolster the Pesticide Industry", 2013*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Monsanto actively promoted GMO technologies as an environmental solution while increasing pesticide use and suppressing independent scientific research.

1. **Positioning Roundup Ready as "eco-friendly" farming (1996–2010)** — Monsanto promoted Roundup Ready GMO crops with the marketing narrative "fewer pesticides, less tillage — better for nature". In reality, glyphosate use in the USA grew from 12 million kg in 1994 to 125 million kg in 2014 — a tenfold increase directly linked to the spread of Roundup Ready crops.
   *Source: USGS pesticide use data; Environmental Science & Technology, Benbrook, 2016*

2. **Concealment of PCB toxicity data (1950–1970)** — Internal Monsanto documents revealed in litigation showed that the company knew of the carcinogenicity and ecotoxicity of PCBs no later than 1956, but continued to manufacture and sell them for another 15 years, while publicly declaring their safety.
   *Source: Monsanto internal memo, "Confidential — Aroclors", 1966; Environmental Defense Fund archive*

3. **Monsanto Fund "Environmental Education" programme (1990s–2000s)** — Monsanto Fund funded biotechnology education programmes in US schools, presenting GMOs exclusively in a positive light. The programmes were distributed without any mention of environmental risks and were developed by the company's public relations agency.
   *Source: Center for Food Safety, "Monsanto vs. U.S. Farmers", 2005; PR Watch analysis of Monsanto Fund grants*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Before the Bayer acquisition, Monsanto positioned itself as a leader in "sustainable agriculture" through aggressive ESG marketing that did not correspond to its actual activities.

1. **"Pledge to Farmers" and food security narrative (2012–2018)** — Monsanto launched a "Pledge to Farmers" campaign and publicly positioned itself as a company providing "food security for 9 billion people". At the same time the company was suing thousands of farmers for "patent violations" — replanting their own seeds — undermining the food sovereignty of small farms.
   *Source: Center for Food Safety, "Monsanto's Patent Enforcement", 2013; NYT, "Monsanto's Seed Patents May Trump Jevons Paradox", 2013*

2. **Inclusion in Dow Jones sustainability indices (2010–2016)** — Monsanto was repeatedly included in DJSI as one of the agribusiness sector's sustainability leaders. Independent NGOs including Friends of the Earth and Center for Food Safety publicly challenged the accuracy of these ratings.
   *Source: DJSI Monsanto entries, 2010–2016; Friends of the Earth critique, 2015*

3. **Carbon farming narrative for GMO crops (2014–2018)** — Monsanto promoted the concept of "carbon farming", claiming that no-till practices based on Roundup Ready crops sequester carbon in soils. Independent research showed that degradation of the soil microbiome under the influence of glyphosate reduces soils' ability to sequester CO₂.
   *Source: Soil Science Society of America Journal; Monsanto carbon farming white paper, 2015*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Monsanto received corporate ESG awards that contrasted sharply with its reputation in the environmental community.

1. **Inclusion in "Global 100 Most Sustainable Corporations" (2005, 2011)** — Corporate Knights included Monsanto in its list of the hundred most sustainable global corporations. This triggered a wave of protests from farming organisations and environmental NGOs, who called the award scandalous given the patent wars against farmers and glyphosate contamination.
   *Source: Corporate Knights Global 100, 2005, 2011; La Via Campesina response, 2011*

2. **Nomination for "Best ESG Practice in Agribusiness" by Business Ethics Network (2013)** — Monsanto was nominated for an industry ESG award by Business Ethics Network for farmer engagement programmes. Opponents pointed out that "engagement" included aggressive patent litigation against farmers.
   *Source: Business Ethics Network annual report, 2013*

3. **Fortune "Best Corporate Citizen" rating (2014)** — Fortune included Monsanto among "Best Corporate Citizens" on several sustainability metrics. That same year the company was conducting more than 140 lawsuits against farmers for seed patent violations.
   *Source: Fortune Magazine, Corporate Citizenship rankings, 2014; Center for Food Safety litigation tracker*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Monsanto's products were marketed with environmental claims that did not correspond to their real impact on nature.

1. **Roundup labelled as "biodegradable" (1990s–2009)** — Monsanto labelled Roundup as "biodegradable" and "environmentally safe" on packaging sold in France. In 2009 a French court found these claims to be false and ordered Monsanto to remove them. Similar lawsuits were filed in New York.
   *Source: French court ruling, Tribunal de Grande Instance de Lyon, 2009; NY Attorney General consent order, 1997*

2. **GMO seeds as a "biodiversity solution" (2000s)** — Monsanto promoted Bt crops (cotton, maize) as a solution for reducing insecticide loads and "protecting biodiversity". Long-term studies recorded the development of pest resistance and the need for increasingly intensive application of additional insecticides.
   *Source: Nature Biotechnology, Tabashnik et al., 2013; USDA Economic Research Service*

3. **Positioning Roundup Ready crops as a "no-till solution" for soil protection (1998–2015)** — Monsanto actively promoted no-till farming based on Roundup Ready crops as an "eco-friendly" alternative to conventional tillage. Independent research showed that prolonged glyphosate use in no-till systems leads to degradation of the soil biome.
   *Source: Applied Soil Ecology journal; Monsanto no-till marketing materials, archived by EWG*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Monsanto funded conservation projects of negligible scale incommensurable with the environmental damage from its activities.

1. **Monarch Butterfly Fund programme (2015–2017)** — Monsanto announced a $4 million donation to restore the monarch population — the very butterfly destroyed by Roundup herbicides. The monarch population had declined by 90% by that point. $4 million represented less than 0.002% of Monsanto's annual revenue.
   *Source: Monsanto press release, Monarch Butterfly Fund, 2015; Xerces Society grant database*

2. **Tree-planting sponsorship for Earth Day (2010–2016)** — Monsanto annually sponsored tree-planting events on Earth Day at corporate campuses and surrounding areas. Events received wide press-release coverage; the area of restored land amounted to a few hectares.
   *Source: Monsanto Corporate Responsibility reports, 2010–2016*

3. **"Bee Smart" beekeeping programme in the USA (2014)** — Monsanto launched a beekeeper support programme called "Bee Smart", offering free consultations and "biodiversity guides" for farmers. The programme was not accompanied by any change in the formulation or sales volume of the neonicotinoid pesticides applied to the same fields.
   *Source: Monsanto Bee Smart programme website, archived 2014; Friends of the Earth, "Monsanto's Bee-Washing", 2014*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Monsanto invested significant resources in manufacturing "scientific doubt" around proven risks of its products and in lobbying against regulation.

1. **Discrediting Gilles-Éric Séralini's research (2012–2014)** — French scientist Gilles-Éric Séralini published a study on the toxicity of GMO maize NK603 in rats. Monsanto coordinated a campaign to refute it: funded counter-articles, pressured the editorial board of the journal Food and Chemical Toxicology, which eventually retracted the article. It subsequently emerged that the journal editor who decided on the retraction was a former employee of a biotechnology company.
   *Source: Le Monde, "L'affaire Séralini", 2013; Retraction Watch, 2013; The Guardian investigation*

2. **Funding the "third wave" of GMO education through front NGOs (2010s)** — Monsanto funded a network of agricultural bloggers and "grassroots" organisations defending biotechnology. Links to Monsanto were not disclosed. Journalists at U.S. Right to Know uncovered email correspondence proving coordination between Monsanto's PR department and "independent" scientists.
   *Source: U.S. Right to Know, "Monsanto's Academic Shills", 2015; The Intercept, "Food Industry Studies", 2016*

3. **Lobbying against GMO labelling (2012–2016)** — Monsanto spent more than $8 million on a campaign against the mandatory GMO product labelling initiative in California (Proposition 37, 2012). The company funded advertising claiming that labelling "misleads consumers" and is "anti-science", promoting the narrative that GMOs are completely identical to conventional products.
   *Source: California Secretary of State campaign finance filings; Just Label It campaign reports, 2012*
