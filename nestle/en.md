Nestlé produces instant coffee, mineral water, chocolate, ice cream, broths, dairy products, baby food, pet food, pharmaceuticals, and cosmetics. Sub-brands: KitKat, Maggi, Nescafe, Nesquik, Nestea, and others.

In 1977 Nestlé was boycotted for its aggressive advertising of baby formula in developing countries. In 2008 Nestlé products at Chinese factories were found to contain melamine — a toxic substance added to inflate protein readings — causing kidney failure in thousands of infants. The Break Free From Plastic movement found Nestlé-branded waste in 37 countries across its global audit.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Nestlé has for decades harmed human health through aggressive infant formula marketing, use of dangerous substances in products, and support of forced child labour in its supply chains.

1. **Baby formula scandal and infant deaths (1974 — present)** — From the 1970s Nestlé aggressively promoted powdered formula as a replacement for breastfeeding in developing countries. Lack of clean water for mixing formula and widespread illiteracy caused thousands of children to die of intestinal infections and malnutrition. WHO issued the International Code of Marketing of Breast-milk Substitutes in 1981 specifically in response to Nestlé's practices. UNICEF estimates that inappropriate formula use still causes more than 800,000 child deaths per year globally.
   *Source: WHO/UNICEF — "International Code of Marketing of Breast-milk Substitutes"; War on Want — "The Baby Killer", 1974*

2. **Melamine contamination of dairy products, China (2008)** — Toxic melamine was found in Nestlé products at Chinese factories, added to artificially inflate protein levels during quality tests. Taiwan's Ministry of Health detected melamine in six product lines. Across China more than 294,000 children were sickened by contaminated dairy products, 54,000 developed kidney failure, and several deaths were recorded.
   *Source: WHO — "Melamine contamination in China", 2008; Reuters — "China milk scandal"*

3. **Child labour and child trafficking on cocoa plantations (2000s — present)** — The documentary "The Dark Side of Chocolate" (2010) and International Labour Organization investigations documented the use of child slave labour in Nestlé's cocoa supply chains in Côte d'Ivoire and Ghana. Children aged 10–15 worked in dangerous conditions with machetes and pesticides without pay. In 2021 the US Supreme Court heard a case brought by eight child slavery survivors against Nestlé and Cargill.
   *Source: ILO — "Child Labour in Cocoa Sector"; US Supreme Court — Nestlé USA v. Doe, 2021*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Nestlé has systematically depleted water resources in arid regions, purchased palm oil from suppliers destroying tropical forests, and been one of the largest sources of plastic pollution.

1. **Water extraction during drought: California (2015–2021)** — During California's worst drought on record Nestlé continued pumping water in the San Bernardino Mountains under a permit that had expired in 1988. The company extracted tens of millions of litres annually, bottling it under the Arrowhead and Pure Life brands. In 2021 the California State Water Resources Control Board ruled that Nestlé had no legal right to most of the water it was taking.
   *Source: California State Water Resources Control Board — ruling 2021; The Desert Sun — "Nestlé water pumping" investigation*

2. **Kalimantan deforestation through palm oil supply chains (2010–2020)** — Greenpeace's 2010 investigation showed that Nestlé was purchasing palm oil from Sinar Mas, a company linked to illegal deforestation in Indonesia's Riau province and destruction of peatlands — critical carbon deposits. The clearance took place in part on land that is habitat for the Sumatran orangutan.
   *Source: Greenpeace — "Caught Red-Handed: Nestlé's Palm Oil", 2010*

3. **Aquifer depletion in Pakistan (2000s–2010s)** — Nestlé's Pure Life plant in Sheikhupura, Pakistan pumped millions of litres of groundwater annually in a region suffering chronic water stress. Local farmers reported falling water table levels and rising soil salinity. According to the Pakistan Council of Research in Water Resources, the district falls within a zone of critical groundwater depletion.
   *Source: Pakistan Council of Research in Water Resources — report 2018; The News International — "Nestlé water extraction in Punjab"*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Nestlé publicly committed to sustainability while continuing practices that cause environmental damage, and used vague language in environmental statements.

1. **Failure on palm oil commitments (2010–2020)** — After the 2010 scandal Nestlé made a public commitment to source "sustainably produced" palm oil with zero deforestation by 2020. A Rainforest Action Network investigation in 2018 found that Nestlé's supply chains still included suppliers linked to deforestation on Borneo and Sumatra. The company repeatedly pushed back the deadline.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network — "Nestlé's Palm Oil Scorecard", 2018*

2. **"Zero waste" pledges while increasing plastic use (2018–2022)** — In 2018 Nestlé announced that all its packaging would be recyclable or reusable by 2025. According to Changing Markets Foundation, by 2022 the company had not only failed to meet the commitment but had increased its use of hard-to-process multilayer packaging.
   *Source: Changing Markets Foundation — "Nestle Sustainability Report Audit", 2022*

3. **Vittel rebranding without changing the substance (2017–2022)** — Residents and scientists in the Vosges region of France documented a critical drop in the Vittel aquifer level caused by Nestlé's water extraction. The company proposed a pipeline to import water from other sources while continuing to market Vittel as the region's "natural mineral water". Local residents filed lawsuits and French environmental organisations characterised the situation as ecological deception.
   *Source: Le Monde — "Nestlé vide la nappe de Vittel", 2018; France Nature Environnement*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Nestlé actively uses ESG rhetoric in investor communications, publishes voluminous sustainability reports, and positions itself as a leader in "nutrition, health and wellbeing".

1. **Creating Shared Value (CSV) framework** — Nestlé introduced the Creating Shared Value concept as an alternative to traditional CSR, actively promoting it through Harvard Business School and Professor Michael Porter. Critics at Corporate Watch pointed out that the concept allows the company to set the terms of its own "responsibility", avoiding binding standards.
   *Source: Corporate Watch — "Nestlé and Creating Shared Value", 2015*

2. **ESG reports with unmet health-product targets (2020–2023)** — After an internal Nestlé study (leaked to the Financial Times in 2021) showed that more than 60% of the company's products failed to meet healthy-eating criteria, Nestlé announced a sweeping "portfolio reform". The portfolio restructuring was promoted as an ESG initiative, although the volume of sales of unhealthy products declined only marginally.
   *Source: Financial Times — "More than 60% of Nestlé's mainstream food and drink products fail to meet health threshold", 2021*

3. **Nestlé Cocoa Plan as PR cover for child labour (2012 — present)** — Nestlé launched the Cocoa Plan, committing tens of millions of dollars to combating child labour and improving farmers' livelihoods. An independent audit commissioned by a US court in 2019 found that the programme covered less than 50% of suppliers and had not led to a systemic reduction in child labour.
   *Source: International Labor Rights Forum — "Nestlé Cocoa Plan Audit", 2019*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Nestlé regularly received recognition in ESG ratings, which the company used in its marketing despite systemic violations.

1. **Dow Jones Sustainability Index (annually, 2000s–2020s)** — Nestlé was repeatedly included in the DJSI as a leader in the food-products category. The company used this inclusion in investor presentations and corporate communications as proof of ESG credibility, while in the same periods it was involved in litigation over water rights and child labour.
   *Source: S&P Global — DJSI annual compositions; Nestlé corporate reports*

2. **Access to Nutrition Index — high positions (2016–2021)** — Despite criticism of its product portfolio quality, Nestlé periodically appeared in the upper ranks of the Access to Nutrition Foundation Index, creating a contradiction with the company's own data on the share of unhealthy products.
   *Source: Access to Nutrition Foundation — reports 2016, 2018, 2021*

3. **CDP climate awards (2019–2022)** — CDP assigned Nestlé high scores for its climate and water policy. Changing Markets Foundation published an analysis showing that CDP's methodology does not account for the real impact of palm oil and water supply chains on local ecosystems.
   *Source: CDP — ratings 2019–2022; Changing Markets Foundation — methodology analysis*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Nestlé used environmental and "natural" designations on products whose production is linked to deforestation, aquifer depletion, and child labour.

1. **Rainforest Alliance mark on KitKat while violations continued (2010–2020)** — Nestlé secured Rainforest Alliance certification for part of its cocoa and prominently used this mark on KitKat packaging. Investigations simultaneously showed that uncertified cocoa from conflict supply chains continued to be used in production. Certifier Rainforest Alliance was criticised for insufficiently rigorous auditing.
   *Source: Mighty Earth — "Nestlé & Rainforest Alliance certification audit", 2017*

2. **"Nestlé Pure Life" brand — an image of purity against environmental harm (global)** — The Nestlé Pure Life drinking water brand was built on images of natural purity and sustainability, while water extraction for this brand in California and Pakistan was carried out in environmentally harmful conditions. Packaging was decorated with leaves and water imagery without any real environmental certification.
   *Source: Food & Water Watch — "Nestlé: the most aggressive water grabber", 2018*

3. **RSPO certification of palm oil as cover (2012–2020)** — Nestlé obtained RSPO certification for part of its palm oil supply and used this status in marketing. However, investigations by the Rainforest Action Network and Global Witness showed that RSPO certification did not guarantee zero deforestation, and the certification system itself suffered from conflicts of interest.
   *Source: Global Witness — "Certifying Destruction", 2013; Rainforest Action Network — "Palm Oil Scorecard"*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Nestlé regularly staged public environmental initiatives whose scale was incommensurable with the harm caused.

1. **Watershed restoration programme (Nestlé Waters, 2010s)** — Nestlé Waters funded local watershed restoration projects near its water-extraction sources, positioning them as "water conservation" initiatives. Investment volumes ran to hundreds of thousands of dollars, while the value of commercial water extraction was in the hundreds of millions. Several environmental organisations regarded these programmes as an attempt to legitimise extraction in contested regions.
   *Source: Food & Water Watch — "Nestlé's Conservation Washing", 2016*

2. **Tree-planting in Central Africa (2020–2022)** — Under the Nestlé Cocoa Plan, the company organised tree-planting on farms in Ghana and Côte d'Ivoire. The declared target — 10 million trees by 2030 — appeared negligible against the area of forest destroyed in the region due to cocoa plantation expansion, including that linked to Nestlé's supply chains.
   *Source: Nestlé Cocoa Plan — Annual Report 2022; Mighty Earth — "Deforestation in Cocoa"*

3. **"Love Food Hate Waste" food waste reduction initiative (2015–2018)** — Nestlé supported a series of food waste reduction campaigns in the UK and Australia, positioning this as a contribution to sustainable consumption. Critics pointed out that the campaigns shifted consumers' attention away from producer responsibility for packaging and onto buyers' behaviour at home.
   *Source: Changing Markets Foundation — "Producer vs Consumer responsibility in food waste"*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Nestlé funded academic research and industry organisations promoting concepts that served corporate interests in the areas of water use, nutrition, and plastic pollution.

1. **Lobbying for water privatisation through "water economics" theory (1990s–2000s)** — Nestlé Chairman Peter Brabeck publicly championed the concept of water as a market commodity rather than a public good. Nestlé funded academic conferences and think tanks promoting the idea that only market mechanisms can ensure efficient water resource management. This rhetoric was used to justify the corporation's right to extract water in arid regions.
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory — "Nestlé and the Water Privatization Agenda"; The Guardian — interview with Brabeck*

2. **Funding ILSI to promote "healthy lifestyle" narratives (1990–2010s)** — Nestlé was one of the key sponsors of the International Life Sciences Institute (ILSI) — an organisation funded by the food industry that promoted scientific concepts downplaying the role of ultra-processed products in rising obesity rates. ILSI was repeatedly accused of systematically influencing WHO and national regulators.
   *Source: BMJ — "ILSI: industry's weapon against public health", 2019*

3. **Promoting RSPO as a "sufficient" sustainability standard (2004 — present)** — Nestlé was among the founders of the Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil (RSPO) and actively promoted the organisation as a comprehensive solution to deforestation. Independent researchers at Leipzig University showed that RSPO certification did not prevent deforestation in a statistically significant way. Nestlé used RSPO to deflect stricter legislative requirements on supply chains.
   *Source: University of Leipzig — "Does RSPO Certification Reduce Deforestation?", 2018*

## Summary

1. **Mass harm to people?** — **YES** — Aggressive infant formula marketing led to thousands of infant deaths in developing countries. Melamine contamination in China (2008) sickened 294,000 children. Child slave labour on cocoa plantations documented in Nestlé's supply chains.
2. **Major environmental disasters?** — **YES** — Illegal water extraction in California during drought. Aquifer depletion in Pakistan. Purchase of palm oil from suppliers destroying forests in Borneo and Sumatra.
3. **Greenwashing?** — **YES** — Palm oil commitments repeatedly missed. Growth of non-recyclable packaging alongside pledges to transition to sustainable materials. Ecological deception with the Vittel brand in France.
4. **ESG marketing?** — **YES** — Creating Shared Value framework as a substitute for binding standards; sustainability reports with 60%+ unhealthy product share; Cocoa Plan with minimal real coverage.
5. **ESG award wins?** — **YES** — Years of DJSI inclusion, high CDP scores, Access to Nutrition Index positions — all actively used in marketing while systemic violations continued.
6. **Eco-labels on harmful products?** — **YES** — Rainforest Alliance mark on KitKat alongside uncertified supply chains; "natural purity" image on Pure Life brand despite environmentally harmful extraction; RSPO certification as a cover.
7. **Negligible eco-events?** — **YES** — Watershed restoration programmes with funding incommensurable with extraction volumes; 10 million trees as PR cover while large-scale deforestation continued in the cocoa supply zone.
8. **Lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?** — **YES** — Promoting water privatisation as "ecologically rational"; funding ILSI to neutralise criticism of ultra-processed products; using RSPO to block stricter legislation.
