Nestlé produces instant coffee, mineral water, chocolate, ice cream, broths, dairy products, baby food, pet food, pharmaceuticals, and cosmetics. Sub-brands: KitKat, Maggi, Nescafe, Nesquik, Nestea, and others.

As is typical of a corporation: the greed for profit at any cost is evident in all aspects of its management's activities, not just in its approach to the environment.

A brief historical note:

— In 1977, Nestlé was boycotted for its aggressive advertising of baby formula. Many countries still refuse Nestlé products to this day because of what happened. The advertising claimed that powdered formula was the only correct option for babies. As a result, a huge number of children died or fell ill after switching to formula. The exact number of victims of Nestlé's advertising campaign remains unknown to this day.

In Hong Kong in September 2008, children began dying — for the same reason: baby formula. Six infants suffered kidney failure, and a total of 860 children were hospitalized. Those very formulas were supplied to Hong Kong by Nestlé. It was later revealed that the formula contained melamine — a mildly toxic substance added to the product to reduce production costs. Nestlé denied everything at first, but in October 2008, Taiwan's Ministry of Health conducted an inspection and found this substance in six types of Nestlé products at Chinese factories.

The director of the documentary "The Dark Side of Chocolate," Miki Mistrati, exposed in his film Nestlé's use of child slave labor. Released in 2010, it showed the world at what price cocoa beans are harvested. Children are stolen, used as slaves, poorly fed, and treated like animals. The average age of the slaves is 11–15 years, and this abuse of children is widespread across West Africa. Interpol and local police freed 50 children from captivity in 2009, but the situation has not changed.

In 2008, Nestlé launched its bottled water advertising campaign under the name Nestlé Pure Life. According to the advertisement, Nestlé was supposed to collect used bottles and recycle them so as not to harm the environment. But some time later, the company reported in its annual report that all water bottles were sent to landfill.

"Access to water should not be a public right," — Nestlé Chairman of the Board Peter Brabeck
https://www.vesti.ru/article/2006854

Under this manifesto, the corporation takes precious water from local residents. It pumps water to bottle it, depleting local wells. This also damages the environment. Where the land was once wet, it becomes dry. And plastic bottles are added to the mountains of waste that already exist in abundance in the world.
The corporation does this not only in poor villages of Pakistan, but also in "developed" countries such as Canada and America.

Nestlé is responsible for the mass deforestation of orangutan jungles.

Throughout the history of the corporation, we see that Nestlé crosses all moral boundaries, including in the area of environmental safety.

The Break Free From Plastic movement published the results of brand waste audits conducted worldwide. Over two months, volunteers examined city streets, parks, forests, and the shores of water bodies in 55 countries. In 51 of them, waste with Coca-Cola branding was found. PepsiCo packaging was found in 43 countries, and Nestlé in 37.
