Newmont — the world's largest gold mining company — operates on six continents. At the Yanacocha mine in Peru, the local communities of Cajamarca accuse the company of contaminating four mountain lakes on which the water supply of 50,000 people depends.

In Ghana, Newmont's Ahafo mines are located in the Subri River basin. Discharges of mine drainage water have repeatedly caused fish kills and water turbidity. In 2009 a cyanide solution leak reached the river and caused a mass fish die-off.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Newmont's operations have been accompanied by the killing of protesters in Peru, forced displacement of communities in Ghana and Suriname, and chronic poisoning of water bodies on which tens of thousands of people depend.

1. **Deaths of protesters at the Conga mine conflict, Peru (2012)** — Local communities in the Cajamarca region opposed the construction of Newmont's new Conga mine, fearing contamination of four mountain lakes. In July 2012 clashes between protesters and police left five people dead. The Peruvian government declared a state of emergency. The Conga project was frozen indefinitely.
   *Source: Peru Ombudsman (Defensoría del Pueblo, 2012); BBC Mundo*

2. **Forced displacement of communities near the Ahafo mine, Ghana (2005–2010)** — Construction of the Ahafo mine required the resettlement of several hundred families. Human rights organisations documented inadequate resettlement conditions, insufficient compensation, and the destruction of the traditional way of life of rural communities.
   *Source: Oxfam America, "Newmont in Ghana" (2010)*

3. **Cyanide poisoning of water bodies and fish kills in Ghana (2009)** — In 2009 a cyanide solution leak at the Ahafo mine (Ghana) reached the Subri River. Mass fish kills deprived hundreds of local fishermen of their source of food and income. Newmont paid compensation to some of the victims after public pressure.
   *Source: Ghanaian Environmental Protection Agency; Earthworks (2010)*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Newmont has caused large-scale environmental damage in multiple countries — from cyanide leaks in Ghana to groundwater contamination in Nevada and tropical forest destruction in Suriname.

1. **Groundwater contamination at the Yanacocha mine, Peru (1990s–2010s)** — Yanacocha — Latin America's largest gold mine — uses heap-leach cyanide extraction in a high-altitude zone that serves as a watershed for several rivers. Independent studies recorded elevated mercury and arsenic levels in waterways near the mine; in 2000 a major mercury spill occurred at Choropampa.
   *Source: PUCP (Pontificia Universidad Católica del Perú) water studies; Oxfam America*

2. **Mercury spill at Choropampa, Peru (2 June 2000)** — A truck contracted by the Yanacocha mine spilled 151 kg of mercury near the villages of Choropampa, Magdalena, and San Juan. Around 1,000 local residents sought medical attention with poisoning symptoms. Newmont was found partially responsible for the consequences.
   *Source: Peruvian Ministry of Health (2000); Oxfam*

3. **Tropical forest clearance in Suriname for the Merian mine (2010–2015)** — Development of the Merian mine required clearing a significant area of tropical forest. Resettlement of the indigenous Ndjuka Maroon community was carried out in violation of free, prior, and informed consent requirements. Environmentalists documented the degradation of watercourses in the development zone.
   *Source: Forest Peoples Programme; Amazon Watch (2014)*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Newmont actively promoted a narrative of "responsible mining" and "traceable-supply-chain gold" while continuing environmentally harmful operations.

1. **"Water stewardship" narrative while polluting rivers in Ghana and Peru (2010–2020)** — Newmont positioned itself as a company "responsibly managing water resources", publishing water stewardship reports. At the same time cyanide leaks at Ahafo and water-quality concerns near Yanacocha did not cease.
   *Source: Newmont Water Stewardship Reports; Earthworks critique (2018)*

2. **"Net zero deforestation" declarations while forest clearing continued (2014–2020)** — Newmont announced its commitment to "net zero deforestation", pledging to offset every hectare of forest destroyed. Environmentalists pointed out that compensatory planting in other locations is not equivalent to preserving intact tropical forests in biodiversity hotspots.
   *Source: Newmont Forest Policy 2014; Rainforest Alliance assessment*

3. **Positioning as a "climate leader" despite a high real footprint (2020–2023)** — Newmont publicly set ambitious CO₂ reduction targets and declared "climate leadership" in the mining industry. Independent analysis showed that the targets did not include full coverage of supply-chain emissions (Scope 3).
   *Source: Climate Action 100+; InfluenceMap (2021)*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Newmont is one of the most active advocates of ESG in the gold mining industry, using high ratings to attract institutional investors.

1. **DJSI leader position in mining (2018–2022)** — Newmont occupied first place in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index in the "gold and precious metals" category for several consecutive years and actively used this in investor and client communications. Meanwhile human rights violations in Peru and Ghana remained unresolved.
   *Source: S&P Global Corporate Sustainability Assessment; Newmont Investor Presentation 2021*

2. **Sustainability-Linked Bonds (2021)** — Newmont issued bonds linked to ESG performance indicators, committing to reduce greenhouse gas emissions. The bond structure was criticised for the absence of strict penalties for non-performance.
   *Source: Newmont SLB Prospectus 2021; Bloomberg*

3. **ESG integration into senior management compensation (2019–2023)** — Newmont announced that a portion of management compensation would be tied to ESG metrics, presenting this as "confirmation of serious intent". Critics noted that the chosen metrics are oriented towards disclosure rather than real outcomes for ecosystems and communities.
   *Source: Newmont Proxy Statement 2022; Institutional Shareholder Services*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Newmont has amassed an extensive collection of ESG awards and occupied leading positions in several sustainability ratings.

1. **First place in DJSI, "gold and silver" category (2019, 2020, 2021)** — Newmont occupied first place in its Dow Jones Sustainability Index category for three consecutive years and actively used this achievement in marketing for investors and clients.
   *Source: S&P Dow Jones Indices, DJSI 2019–2021*

2. **Inclusion in FTSE4Good Index and Bloomberg Gender-Equality Index (2020–2022)** — Alongside human rights violations in Peru and Suriname, Newmont was recognised as a "socially responsible" company by the criteria of several sustainability indices.
   *Source: FTSE Russell; Bloomberg GEI*

3. **CDP "A-List" award for water management (2021)** — CDP assigned Newmont an "A" rating for water management disclosure — despite ongoing concerns about water quality near the company's key mines in Peru and Ghana.
   *Source: CDP Water Security Scores 2021*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Newmont used environmental certification and labelling to promote "responsible" gold without eliminating systemic environmental violations.

1. **Certification under the Responsible Gold Mining Principles (RGMP) (2019–2023)** — Newmont was among the first companies certified under RGMP and actively used this status in marketing for jewellery retailers and electronics manufacturers. The standard was developed by the industry and does not include independent verification of water-resource impacts in mining zones.
   *Source: World Gold Council RGMP List; Earthworks analysis*

2. **"Newmont Responsibly Sourced Gold" programme (2021)** — The company launched a "responsibly sourced" gold brand for B2B clients, certifying individual mines under its own programme. The Yanacocha mine in Peru, which has been the subject of prolonged human rights and environmental claims, was not included in the programme — but the existence of the programme created an "umbrella" marketing effect.
   *Source: Newmont corporate communications*

3. **"Water-neutral mine" labelling for Ahafo (Ghana) (2018)** — Newmont declared "water neutrality" at the Ahafo mine and used this status in environmental reporting. Independent observers noted that the "neutrality" calculation did not include diffuse pollution of adjacent watercourses from mine drainage water.
   *Source: Newmont Water Reports; Oxfam America water analysis*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Newmont conducted environmental actions that served primarily PR purposes and were incommensurable with the scale of ecological damage.

1. **"Plant a Tree" reforestation programme in Suriname (2016–2020)** — Newmont funded the planting of several thousand trees in Suriname as compensation for clearance for the Merian mine. The area planted represented a small fraction of the primary tropical forest destroyed; the biological value of the compensatory plantings is incomparable to primary forest.
   *Source: Newmont Merian Biodiversity Reports; Forest Peoples Programme*

2. **"Biodiversity Days" at mines in Ghana (2012–2019)** — Newmont organised public tree-planting and riverbank clean-up events near the Ahafo mines, inviting local schoolchildren and officials. The events were actively covered in corporate reports and social media.
   *Source: Newmont Ghana Community Reports*

3. **"Water for life" educational programmes in Peru (2013–2017)** — Against the backdrop of the Conga project conflict, Newmont funded educational programmes about "the importance of water" for schools in Cajamarca. Local activists regarded this as an attempt to improve the company's image in communities that were opposing the mine.
   *Source: La República (Peru); CooperAcción (2015)*

---

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Newmont participated in lobbying for a regulatory framework favourable to the gold mining industry and funded pseudo-independent research.

1. **Lobbying against independent monitoring at Yanacocha mine, Peru (2010–2014)** — During the Conga project conflict Newmont, through its Peruvian subsidiary Minera Yanacocha, contested the results of an independent environmental assessment (Peritos Especiales) commissioned by the government. The company promoted alternative studies prepared by consultants it had hired.
   *Source: Peru Ombudsman; CooperAcción (2012)*

2. **Participation in lobbying for weak heap-leach regulation in the USA (2000–2010s)** — Through the National Mining Association (NMA) Newmont participated in opposing the tightening of environmental requirements for heap-leach facilities in western US states. The company portrayed the technology as "safe when properly managed" despite accumulated evidence of groundwater contamination.
   *Source: National Mining Association lobbying disclosures; Earthworks*

3. **Funding "independent" research on the benefits of gold mining for Ghana's development (2008–2015)** — Newmont commissioned economic studies on gold mining's contribution to Ghana's development from affiliated academic structures. The results were used to resist demands for stricter environmental standards and increased payments to local communities.
   *Source: Business & Human Rights Resource Centre; Oxfam America (2010)*

## Summary

1. **Mass harm to people?** — **YES** — Newmont bears responsibility for deaths during suppression of protests in Peru (2012), mercury poisoning of Choropampa residents, and deprivation of livelihoods of thousands through water body contamination.
2. **Major environmental disasters?** — **YES** — Mercury spill of 2000 in Peru, cyanide leak of 2009 in Ghana, and primary tropical forest clearance in Suriname — all results of the company's operations.
3. **Greenwashing?** — **YES** — Declarations of "water neutrality" and "zero deforestation" used while actual pollution and ecosystem destruction continued.
4. **ESG marketing?** — **YES** — DJSI leadership actively exploited in marketing while human rights violations remained unresolved.
5. **ESG award wins?** — **YES** — CDP "A-List" rating for "water management" while water-quality concerns at key mines continued.
6. **Eco-labels on harmful products?** — **YES** — RGMP certification and "responsible" gold programme created a marketing cover without real elimination of violations.
7. **Negligible eco-events?** — **YES** — Tree-planting and educational programmes in conflict zones served as tools for managing public opinion.
8. **Lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?** — **YES** — Opposition to independent monitoring, funding of convenient research, and lobbying for weak heap-leach regulation.
