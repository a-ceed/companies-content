Nornickel: Decades of Environmental Destruction Across the Arctic

MMC Norilsk Nickel — the world's largest producer of palladium and high-grade nickel — has become one of the most prominent symbols of unchecked industrial environmental damage on Earth. Operating out of Norilsk on the Taimyr Peninsula deep inside the Arctic Circle, the company has spent decades poisoning the air, rivers, and soil of Siberia, leaving behind a legacy of contamination that stretches all the way to the Arctic Ocean.

SO2 Emissions: A Permanent Chemical Siege

The most pervasive environmental harm caused by Nornickel is the continuous release of sulfur dioxide (SO2) from its smelting operations. A NASA satellite study found that in 2018, Nornickel's Polar Division discharged approximately 1.9 million tonnes of SO2 — making it the single largest man-made point source of sulfur dioxide pollution on the planet, surpassing any active volcano. On individual days, concentrations near the plant perimeter exceeded the maximum allowable limit by a factor of 4.5.

In the atmosphere, SO2 combines with moisture to form sulfuric acid, which falls as acid rain across the tundra. Nornickel's smelters have destroyed an estimated 5.9 million acres (2.4 million hectares) of boreal forest within a 100-kilometre radius. Within 30 kilometres of the plants, a 300,000-hectare zone has been stripped bare of trees and lichens entirely — a dead landscape visible from orbit.

The smelters also release heavy metal particulates including copper, nickel, cobalt, lead, cadmium, arsenic and selenium. More than half of ambient air samples taken in Norilsk exceed maximum permissible concentrations for copper and nickel. In 2019, more than 15,000 residents signed an open letter stating: "We are sick and dying."

The 2020 Diesel Catastrophe: Rivers Turned Red

On May 29, 2020, a fuel storage tank at Thermal Power Plant No. 3 — operated by Nornickel's subsidiary NTEK — catastrophically failed. The tank's bottom had been weakened by corrosion, compounded by the thawing of the permafrost beneath it. Approximately 21,163 tonnes of diesel fuel flooded the Daldykan River, turning its water a vivid red, before the contamination spread into the Ambarnaya River and across 350 square kilometres. This was the largest Arctic fuel spill ever recorded.

The company did not report the spill to authorities. Officials learned of the disaster two days later from videos on social media. President Putin declared a state of emergency and publicly upbraided Nornickel's management: "Are we going to learn about emergency situations from social media?" Containment booms were overwhelmed by drifting ice. The spill reached Lake Pyasino and tracked the entire 900-kilometre length of the Pyasina River to the Kara Sea. Researchers found diesel and heavy metals in the tissue of seven freshwater fish species. Indigenous communities were warned that local fish were unsafe to eat.

In February 2021, the Krasnoyarsk Arbitration Court ordered Nornickel to pay 146.2 billion rubles (approximately USD 2 billion) — the largest environmental fine in Russian history.

Chronic Water Contamination

The 2020 spill occurred against a backdrop of chronic pollution. WWF Russia's satellite imagery analysis for 2017–2020 confirmed that Nornickel had been routinely discharging industrial waste into rivers. Concentrations of iron, nickel, copper and other heavy metals in waterways near Nornickel's facilities averaged 9 to 10 times the maximum permissible concentration.

Unfulfilled Promises and the Sulfur Programme

Facing regulatory pressure, Nornickel launched its "Sulfur Programme" to capture SO2 at its Nadezhda Metallurgical Plant. The company originally pledged a 90% reduction in sulfur emissions by 2025 — a deadline since pushed to 2031. As of 2024, emissions stood at approximately 1.27 million tonnes, a 37% reduction from 2015 levels but still among the highest industrial SO2 outputs in the world. Critics note that at the current pace, the ecological recovery of the Taimyr Peninsula will not happen within any near-term timeframe.

Nornickel has repeatedly promoted itself as a responsible supplier of "green metals" for battery technology and the energy transition. Its actual record tells a different story: decades of atmospheric poisoning, systematic water contamination, a catastrophic Arctic spill concealed from authorities, and accountability only when concealment became impossible.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. The residents of Norilsk and workers at the metallurgical plants have for decades suffered from chronic exposure to toxic emissions — sulfur dioxide, heavy metals, and industrial dust.

1. **Norilsk residents' petition "We Are Sick and Dying" (2019)** — More than 15,000 residents of Norilsk signed an open letter to the authorities describing widespread cases of respiratory disease, cancer, and chronic poisoning. According to Rospotrebnadzor data, concentrations of nickel and copper in Norilsk's air systematically exceed maximum permissible levels; the city consistently tops Russian rankings for atmospheric pollution.
   *Source: Novaya Gazeta — "Norilsk: We Are Sick and Dying" (April 2019); Rospotrebnadzor — "State Report on the Sanitary and Epidemiological Welfare of the Population of the Russian Federation" (2019)*

2. **Occupational diseases among smelter workers** — Workers at the Polar Division and Kola MMC are exposed to SO2, heavy metals, and industrial aerosols throughout their working shifts. Medical studies record elevated rates of chronic bronchitis, pneumoconiosis, and cardiovascular disease among metallurgical workers.
   *Source: Rostrud — "Report on Occupational Health and Safety Conditions in the Russian Federation" (2020); Bjørklund G. et al. — "Nickel and Human Health" (Environmental Research, 2017)*

3. **Fish consumption ban for indigenous peoples of Taimyr (2020)** — Following the diesel spill into the Daldykan and Ambarnaya rivers, Rospotrebnadzor prohibited the indigenous peoples of Taimyr — the Dolgans, Nganasans, and Enets — from consuming fish from contaminated waterways. For communities practising traditional ways of life, fishing is the primary source of food and a core cultural practice.
   *Source: Rospotrebnadzor — "Resolution on Restrictions on Fishing in the Pyasina River Basin" (June 2020); Reuters — "Norilsk Nickel spill threatens indigenous food sources in Russian Arctic" (2020)*



## 2. Has the corporation been responsible for major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Nornickel bears responsibility for one of the largest environmental catastrophes in the history of the Arctic, as well as for the chronic industrial-scale destruction of ecosystems.

1. **Spill of 21,163 tonnes of diesel fuel into Arctic rivers (May 2020)** — The failure of the NTEK Thermal Power Plant No. 3 tank contaminated the Daldykan and Ambarnaya rivers, Lake Pyasino, and the Pyasina River (900 km). Oil products reached the Kara Sea. The court ordered the company to pay 146.2 billion rubles — a record environmental fine in Russian history. Scientists detected diesel in the tissue of seven fish species.
   *Source: Reuters — "Russia orders Norilsk Nickel to pay $2 billion in damages" (February 2021); Krasnoyarsk Regional Arbitration Court — ruling in case No. А33-28214/2020; [WWF Russia — "The Norilsk Tragedy" (2020)](https://wwf.ru/resources/ekologicheskaya-katastrofa-v-norilske/)*

2. **Destruction of 2.4 million hectares of forest and a 300,000-hectare "dead zone"** — Acid rain from SO2 emissions destroyed boreal forests across an area comparable to Belgium. Within a 30-kilometre zone around the plants, trees and lichens have completely disappeared; soils have irreversibly degraded.
   *Source: [Fioletov V.E. et al. — "A global catalogue of large SO₂ sources and emissions derived from the Ozone Monitoring Instrument" (Atmospheric Chemistry and Physics, 2016)](https://acp.copernicus.org/articles/16/11497/2016/); [NASA Earth Observatory — "A Manmade Volcano over Norilsk" (2018)](https://science.nasa.gov/earth/earth-observatory/a-manmade-volcano-over-norilsk-92246)*

3. **Chronic heavy-metal contamination of rivers** — Systematic discharges of industrial waste have caused exceedances of maximum permissible concentrations for iron, nickel, and copper in rivers near the facilities by factors of 9 to 10. WWF Russia documented these violations using satellite imagery for 2017–2020.
   *Source: [WWF Russia — "Environmental Consequences of the Oil Product Spill in Norilsk" (2020)](https://wwf.ru/resources/ekologicheskaya-katastrofa-v-norilske/); Rosprirodnadzor — "Materials of the Inspection of PJSC MMC Norilsk Nickel" (2020)*



## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Nornickel systematically uses corporate communications tools to build an image of a "responsible miner", concealing or downplaying the real environmental damage.

1. **Concealment of the 2020 spill — authorities learned from social media** — The company did not report the disaster to the authorities for two days. The information spread via social media videos. This was a direct violation of the environmental disclosure obligations declared in the company's ESG reports.
   *Source: [kremlin.ru — "Meeting on the elimination of the consequences of the accident in Norilsk" (3 June 2020)](http://kremlin.ru/events/president/news/63535); BBC News — "Norilsk Nickel oil spill: Putin angry at 'criminal' delayed response" (2020)*

2. **Postponement of the "Sulfur Programme" from 2025 to 2031** — The publicly announced target of a 90% reduction in SO2 emissions by 2025 was moved to 2031 without any public acknowledgement of the failure. Meanwhile, the company continued to position the "Sulfur Programme" as proof of its environmental responsibility.
   *Source: Kommersant — "Nornickel shifts the timeline of the sulfur programme" (2022); Norilsk Nickel — "Annual Report 2022", section "Environmental Responsibility"*

3. **ESG reports amid chronic pollution** — Nornickel publishes annual ESG reports declaring Scope 1, 2, and 3 targets and membership in international sustainable-development associations — while Norilsk remains one of the most polluted cities on the planet and heavy-metal concentrations in rivers exceed the norm by 9 to 10 times.
   *Source: Norilsk Nickel — "Sustainability Report 2022"; Carbon Disclosure Project (CDP) — "Nornickel Climate Submission" (2022)*



## 4. Does the company participate in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Nornickel actively uses ESG communications tools, publishing reports, declaring climate targets, and participating in international initiatives.

1. **Annual ESG reports and climate targets** — The company publishes annual sustainability reports, declares Scope 1, 2, and 3 emission-reduction targets, and positions itself as a responsible producer of "energy-transition metals" — nickel and palladium needed for electric vehicles and hydrogen energy.
   *Source: Norilsk Nickel — "Sustainability Report 2021"; Norilsk Nickel — "Metals for Green Economy" (corporate brochure, 2021)*

2. **Membership in RSPP and international associations** — Nornickel is a member of the Russian Union of Industrialists and Entrepreneurs (RSPP), participates in international metallurgical associations, and declares compliance with UN sustainable-development principles.
   *Source: RSPP — "Member organisations" (2023); Norilsk Nickel — "Membership in industry associations", official company website*

3. **Positioning as a supplier of "green metals"** — The company actively promotes the narrative that the nickel and palladium it produces are critically necessary for the energy transition and decarbonisation of the global economy, while suppressing its own environmental footprint.
   *Source: Norilsk Nickel — "Investor Presentation" (2022); Bloomberg — "Norilsk Nickel pitches itself as green metals champion" (2021)*



## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG competition nominations?

**Answer: YES**. Nornickel was included in international ESG indices and received awards for "best practices" in sustainability in Russian ratings.

1. **Inclusion in MSCI ESG and Bloomberg ESG indices** — Prior to 2022, Nornickel's shares were present in the MSCI ESG and Bloomberg ESG indices, which the company used as a marketing argument in favour of its "sustainability".
   *Source: MSCI — "ESG Ratings: MMC Norilsk Nickel" (data up to February 2022); Bloomberg Intelligence — "ESG Data: Norilsk Nickel" (2021)*

2. **Awards from Russian "best practices" ratings** — The company received recognition in Russian ESG ratings and competitions for "best sustainability practices" in the mining sector — against the backdrop of a record environmental fine and chronic pollution.
   *Source: RAEX-Europe — "ESG rating of Russian issuers" (2021); Russian GRI Week — "Best practices in non-financial reporting" (2020)*

3. **Self-positioning through the "Sulfur Programme"** — Nornickel used the "Sulfur Programme" as grounds for claiming the title of "environmentally responsible" producer when participating in industry forums and sustainability ratings.
   *Source: Norilsk Nickel — "Sulfur Programme" (official website, 2021); address by Nornickel President at the "Mining and Sustainable Development" forum (Moscow, 2021)*



## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: NO**. Nornickel is a B2B company and does not apply eco-labelling to consumer goods. Metals (nickel, palladium, copper, cobalt) are sold to corporate buyers without retail packaging.

However, the company uses similar practices in corporate communications: positioning the metals it produces as "green" and "necessary for the energy transition" without disclosing the full environmental footprint of the production cycle.
*Source: Norilsk Nickel — "Metals for a Green Economy" (corporate brochure, 2021)*



## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Against the backdrop of millions of tonnes of industrial emissions and destroyed ecosystems, Nornickel conducts local initiatives that are incomparable in scale to the damage caused.

1. **Volunteer clean-ups near Norilsk** — The company organises annual "Clean Days" involving several thousand volunteers collecting household waste. The volume of waste collected amounts to a few thousand kilograms — against annual industrial emissions of more than one million tonnes of SO2 and heavy metals.
   *Source: Norilsk Nickel — "Social Report 2021", section "Environmental initiatives"; Norilsk Nickel — press release "Clean Day 2021"*

2. **Funding of a small nature reserve** — Nornickel supports the Putorana State Nature Reserve (a UNESCO World Heritage site). The reserve covers approximately 1.9 million hectares; meanwhile, the company's acid rain has destroyed 2.4 million hectares of forest in the same region.
   *Source: UNESCO — "Putorana Plateau" (World Heritage site, inscribed 2010); Norilsk Nickel — "Corporate partnership with the Putorana Reserve" (2020)*

3. **Small-scale land remediation programmes** — The announced remediation plans for contaminated territories cover insignificant areas relative to the total scale of degradation: the 300,000-hectare "dead zone" cannot be restored within any foreseeable timeframe.
   *Source: Norilsk Nickel — "Programme for the elimination of accumulated environmental damage" (2021); Greenpeace Russia — "Assessment of Nornickel's remediation programme" (2022)*



## 8. Has the company lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories or greenwashing outreach?

**Answer: YES**. Nornickel actively lobbies for the relaxation of environmental requirements while simultaneously positioning itself as a participant in the "green transition".

1. **Lobbying for the postponement of the "Sulfur Programme" (2025 → 2031)** — Under pressure from the company, the timeline for the SO2 capture project was moved back by six years. This deprived Norilsk of six years of relatively cleaner air amid already-high levels of population illness.
   *Source: Kommersant — "Nornickel asks to delay completion of the sulfur programme" (2021); Interfax — "Norilsk Nickel: postponement of the sulfur programme timeline agreed with the Ministry of Natural Resources" (2022)*

2. **Lobbying through RSPP against tightening SO2 standards** — Through the Russian Union of Industrialists and Entrepreneurs, Nornickel participates in opposing tighter maximum permissible concentrations for industrial atmospheric emissions.
   *Source: InfluenceMap — "Corporate Lobbying on Environmental Regulation in Russia" (2022); RSPP — "Position on the regulation of industrial emissions" (2021)*

3. **Opposing tightening of Russian environmental legislation** — Through industry associations, the company lobbied against mandatory real-time monitoring of industrial emissions proposed in draft amendments to the Federal Law "On Environmental Protection".
   *Source: Rossiyskaya Gazeta — "Industrialists object to online emissions monitoring" (2021); Bellona — "Nornickel and environmental regulation in Russia" (2022)*
