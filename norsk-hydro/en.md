Aluminium smelting and bauxite processing generates toxic red mud and air pollution.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. The catastrophic red mud discharge in 2018 poisoned drinking water sources for tens of thousands of residents in the Brazilian state of Pará.

1. **Red mud discharge in Barcarena, Brazil (February 2018)** — Overflow of tailings storage facilities during heavy rains caused toxic alkaline slurry (red mud) to enter the Murucupi River and nearby water bodies. Brazilian authorities reported that water sources used by approximately 40,000 residents were contaminated. Local residents reported skin rashes, eye irritation and gastrointestinal disorders.
   *Source: IBAMA (Brazilian Institute of Environment), 2018; Reuters, February 2018*

2. **Chronic air pollution near the Alunorte plant (2010–2023)** — The Alunorte alumina plant emits SO₂, dust and alkali vapors. Residents of settlements near the facility complained of respiratory illnesses for many years. The Brazilian Ministry of Health documented elevated bronchitis rates within a 10 km radius of the plant.
   *Source: Brazilian Ministry of Health, 2019; Conectas Human Rights*

3. **Contamination of drinking water in Barcarena municipality (2018–2021)** — After the 2018 accident, thousands of families were without safe water supplies for several months. Independent well water tests showed concentrations of caustic soda, aluminium and heavy metals exceeding permissible levels. Affected communities filed a class action lawsuit that ended in a $110 million settlement.
   *Source: Conectas Human Rights, 2020; Norsk Hydro Settlement Agreement 2021*

## 2. Has the corporation destroyed ecosystems and biodiversity?

**Answer: YES**. Bauxite mining and alumina production caused serious damage to rainforests and river ecosystems of the Amazon.

1. **Deforestation from bauxite mining in Trombetas (Pará)** — Bauxite extraction at the Trombetas deposit, linked to Norsk Hydro's supply chain, led to clearing of significant areas of Amazon rainforest. The area of disturbed forest land amounted to several thousand hectares. The Trombetas River — habitat of endemic river turtles — was contaminated with suspended particles.
   *Source: IMAZON (Amazon Institute of People and the Environment), 2017*

2. **Contamination of the Murucupi River by red mud (2018)** — The entry of red mud into the Murucupi River caused a sharp rise in water pH. The high alkalinity destroyed a significant portion of the bottom fauna and aquatic vegetation. Fishermen reported mass fish kills. Biologists estimate ecosystem recovery will take several decades.
   *Source: IBAMA Environmental Assessment, 2018; WWF Brazil*

3. **Tailings storage facilities as a permanent threat to water systems** — Red mud stored in open lagoons near the Alunorte plant continues to threaten Amazon water systems. The volume of accumulated sludge runs into hundreds of millions of tonnes. The risk of repeat accidents during extreme rainfall remains high, as acknowledged by Brazilian regulators.
   *Source: SEMAS Pará (State Environment Secretariat), 2020*

## 3. Has the corporation violated the rights of indigenous peoples and local communities?

**Answer: YES**. The company's activities violated the rights of Amazon indigenous peoples and traditional fishing communities.

1. **Threat to Trombetas indigenous territories** — Bauxite mining in the Trombetas River basin was conducted near the territories of the Kaxuyana and Tiriyó indigenous peoples. Indigenous organizations reported contamination of fishing grounds and disruption of river regimes used for traditional water use. Consultation with indigenous communities was conducted as a formality.
   *Source: FUNAI (Brazilian Indigenous Peoples Foundation), 2016; Survival International*

2. **Loss of livelihood for fishing communities after 2018** — The Alunorte accident completely destroyed the fishing livelihoods of families living along the Murucupi River. Fishermen could not sell their catch due to reputational damage and actual contamination. Some families lost their only source of income for periods ranging from six months to two years.
   *Source: Conectas Human Rights, 2019; Imaflora*

3. **Insufficient compensation for those affected (2018–2021)** — Despite the $110 million settlement, human rights organizations noted that many directly affected families received unjustly small payments or were excluded from the compensation process. Community organizations accused Norsk Hydro of using legal procedures to minimize payments.
   *Source: Conectas Human Rights, settlement analysis 2021*

## 4. Has the corporation systematically violated environmental legislation?

**Answer: YES**. IBAMA and Brazilian regional authorities documented serious and systematic violations of environmental requirements.

1. **Violation of environmental licensing conditions at Alunorte (2018)** — Brazilian agency IBAMA established that the Alunorte plant was operating a second tailings storage reservoir without proper environmental authorization. This illegally commissioned reservoir was the cause of the emergency discharge in 2018. IBAMA imposed a fine of 41 million Brazilian reais.
   *Source: IBAMA, Auto de Infração 2018; Reuters*

2. **Mandatory 50% production reduction (2018–2019)** — Brazilian authorities required Alunorte to reduce production by exactly half and maintain this restriction for 15 months — an unprecedented measure for a plant of this scale. The restriction was lifted only after the company presented evidence of violation remediation.
   *Source: SEMAS Pará decision, 2018; Norsk Hydro press releases*

3. **Occupational safety violations at Norwegian facilities (2019–2022)** — The Norwegian Labor Inspection Authority (Arbeidstilsynet) identified violations in chemical safety management at several Norsk Hydro facilities in Norway, including aluminium plants in Årdal and Sunndalsøra.
   *Source: Arbeidstilsynet, inspection reports 2020*

## 5. Has the corporation concealed information or misled the public?

**Answer: YES**. The company denied contamination and downplayed its scale in the critical days following the accident.

1. **Initial denial of mud discharge (February 2018)** — In the first days after the accident, Norsk Hydro publicly denied any emergency discharge beyond the facility's boundaries, despite visual evidence and water analyses conducted by independent organizations. The company insisted that the violations were solely related to rainwater runoff.
   *Source: Norsk Hydro press statement February 2018; IBAMA counter-statement*

2. **Understatement of health impacts on the population** — In official communications, Norsk Hydro consistently denied any causal link between plant operations and residents' health complaints. Independent epidemiologists (Universidade Federal do Pará) found this position contradicted available medical data.
   *Source: UFPA Public Health Study, 2019; Conectas*

3. **Lack of transparency about tailings storage data** — Prior to the 2018 accident, the volume and condition of tailings storage facilities were not publicly disclosed and were not part of the plant's official environmental reporting. The post-accident investigation revealed that the company was aware of overflow risks in the second reservoir but did not inform regulators.
   *Source: IBAMA investigation report, 2018*

## 6. Has the corporation opposed regulation and climate policy?

**Answer: YES**. Norsk Hydro delayed implementation of necessary technical improvements and challenged directives from Brazilian authorities.

1. **Challenge to the production reduction order (2018)** — After Brazilian authorities imposed a mandatory 50% production restriction, Norsk Hydro appealed in court, attempting to have the measure overturned or softened. The attempt to challenge the order provoked a wave of public condemnation in Brazil and Norway.
   *Source: Reuters, March 2018; Norsk Hydro legal filings*

2. **Delays in construction of a new tailings storage facility** — The need to build a new, safer red mud storage facility was known to the company's engineers years before the accident. The investment decision was repeatedly delayed to save costs. Brazilian prosecutors considered this a deliberate disregard for safety.
   *Source: Ministério Público Federal do Pará, 2019*

3. **Opposition to stricter Brazilian bauxite residue storage standards (2019–2022)** — Following dam disasters in Brazil (Mariana 2015, Brumadinho 2019), the government developed stricter standards for tailings storage. The industry association that includes Norsk Hydro lobbied against applying these standards to alumina plants.
   *Source: Instituto Brasileiro de Mineração (IBRAM), 2020*

---

## 7. Has the corporation violated workers' rights?

**Answer: YES**. Labour rights violations were documented at both Brazilian and Norwegian facilities.

1. **Hazardous working conditions at Alunorte (2010–2022)** — Workers at the Alunorte plant in Barcarena worked in contact with toxic substances with inadequate protection. The metallurgical workers' union of Pará state reported chronic dermatitis, respiratory diseases and alkali burns among workers. Staff turnover remained abnormally high.
   *Source: Sinteticom (Sindicato dos Trabalhadores), 2019*

2. **Discrimination against local workers in hiring** — Local communities in Barcarena complained that well-paying positions at Alunorte were traditionally filled by outside specialists, while residents received unskilled temporary work with minimal social guarantees.
   *Source: Imaflora social audit, 2018*

3. **Industrial accidents at Norwegian plants (2019–2022)** — At the aluminium plant in Årdal (Norway) in 2020, a molten aluminium spill occurred, injuring several workers with burns. Norwegian trade unions Metal and Industri Energi criticized management's insufficient investigation into the accident's causes.
   *Source: Arbeidstilsynet incident report, 2020; Industri Energi*

## 8. Is the corporation a significant greenhouse gas emitter?

**Answer: YES**. Aluminium production is an energy-intensive process with a high carbon footprint, despite declarations about hydropower use.

1. **High emissions from alumina production in Brazil** — The Alunorte plant uses natural gas and petroleum coke to roast bauxite, generating significant CO₂ emissions. According to the company's own data, the bauxite-to-aluminium supply chain in Brazil has a substantially higher carbon footprint than equivalent capacity in Norway.
   *Source: Norsk Hydro Sustainability Report 2022*

2. **Perfluorocarbon (CF₄, C₂F₆) emissions during electrolysis** — Norsk Hydro's aluminium plants emit CF₄ and C₂F₆ during anode effects — super-potent greenhouse gases with global warming potentials 7,390 and 12,200 times higher than CO₂ respectively. Despite progress in reducing anode effect frequency, absolute PFC emissions remain significant.
   *Source: Norsk Hydro Annual Report 2022; IEA Aluminium Technology Roadmap*

3. **Group's total carbon footprint exceeds 10 million tonnes CO₂-equivalent** — According to Norsk Hydro's own reporting, the group's direct (Scope 1) and indirect (Scope 2) emissions total more than 10 million tonnes CO₂-eq. per year. Including indirect supply chain emissions (Scope 3) significantly increases this figure. The pace of carbon intensity reduction is assessed as insufficient to meet the 1.5°C target.
   *Source: Norsk Hydro Sustainability Report 2022; Carbon Tracker*

## Summary of Answers

1. The 2018 Alunorte plant accident poisoned drinking water for approximately 40,000 residents of the Brazilian state of Pará and caused long-term damage to Amazon river ecosystems.
2. IBAMA established that the plant was operating an illegally commissioned tailings reservoir — the direct cause of the accident — without the necessary authorization.
3. The company initially denied the discharge, then systematically downplayed health impacts, denying any link between its operations and documented illnesses among residents.
4. Bauxite mining in the Amazon region threatens the territories of the Kaxuyana and Tiriyó indigenous peoples and destroys tropical forests.
5. The $110 million settlement did not cover all affected families, and many received unjustly small compensation.
6. The Alunorte plant maintains enormous tailings storage facilities that continue to threaten Amazon water systems during extreme rainfall.
7. Perfluorocarbon (CF₄, C₂F₆) emissions from aluminium electrolysis make a significant contribution to the greenhouse effect.
8. Brazilian prosecutors considered the delay in upgrading tailings storage facilities to be a deliberate disregard for safety in the interest of cost savings.
