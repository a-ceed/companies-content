Cocoa, coffee and palm oil trading from active deforestation zones in West Africa.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Olam's activities are linked to child labour on cocoa plantations, hazardous working conditions at processing facilities and displacement of local communities from their lands.

1. **Child labour on cocoa plantations in Côte d'Ivoire (2010s — present)** — Investigations by the International Labour Organization and NGO Mighty Earth documented child labour in Olam's cocoa supply chain. Children aged 10–14 work on plantations in dangerous conditions: applying pesticides without protective equipment, carrying heavy bags, using machetes. Many are denied the opportunity to attend school.
   *Source: Mighty Earth, "Dirty Chocolate", 2018; ILO report on child labour in cocoa production, 2020*

2. **Working conditions at cashew processing plants in Côte d'Ivoire and Nigeria (2017)** — International human rights organization Human Rights Watch documented cases of chemical burns among workers at cashew processing facilities — including at facilities in Olam's supply chain. Workers processed nuts by hand without gloves, being exposed to toxic cashew shell liquid (cardol oil).
   *Source: Human Rights Watch, "Peeling Back the Layers", 2017*

3. **Displacement of communities in Gabon (2010s)** — The expansion of Olam's oil palm plantations in Gabon was accompanied by the seizure of community lands without adequate consultation with local populations. Cases are documented of families losing their livelihoods who were engaged in subsistence farming and fishing.
   *Source: Global Witness, Gabon reports, 2019–2021*

## 2. Has the corporation systematically destroyed natural ecosystems?

**Answer: YES**. Olam's operations are directly or through its supply chain linked to deforestation across three continents.

1. **Cocoa purchases from Côte d'Ivoire national parks (2017–2018)** — A Mighty Earth investigation showed that Olam was purchasing cocoa from farmers illegally farming inside Marahoué National Park and other protected areas. The area of Côte d'Ivoire's forests declined from 16 to 3.4 million hectares over several decades.
   *Source: Mighty Earth, "Cocoa's Dark Secret", 2017*

2. **Deforestation for palm plantations in Gabon (2012 — present)** — Olam Palm Gabon received concessions covering more than 50,000 hectares in one of the world's most pristine tropical forest zones. Independent satellite data recorded clearing of primary forest despite the company's declared "no deforestation" commitments.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network, Olam palm oil supply chain, 2020*

3. **Destruction of gorilla habitat in Cameroon (2019–2020)** — Olam's palm oil suppliers in Cameroon were clearing forests in buffer zones around Lobeke Park — a key habitat of the western lowland gorilla. A Rainforest Action Network investigation documented new clearings in 2020.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network, "The Olam Problem", 2020*

## 3. Has the corporation concealed information or misled the public?

**Answer: YES**. Olam declared a zero-deforestation policy while in practice continuing to source raw materials from cleared territories.

1. **RSPO certification while violations continued (2012–2020)** — Olam received Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil (RSPO) certification for some plantations, while simultaneously continuing to clear forest in Gabon. Independent auditors and NGOs documented the discrepancy between stated standards and actual practices.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research, Olam asset analysis, 2019*

2. **"No Deforestation, No Peat, No Exploitation" (NDPE) declaration (2019)** — Despite public NDPE commitments, Global Forest Watch satellite monitoring recorded forest cover loss in Olam's concessions in Gabon and procurement regions in West Africa during the period these commitments were in force.
   *Source: Global Forest Watch, concession monitoring, 2019–2022*

3. **Response to criticism — threats of legal action (2012)** — When analyst Madhav Datar published a critical report on Olam's finances in 2012, the company filed a defamation lawsuit. The action was perceived as an attempt to silence independent analysis of the company's business practices.
   *Source: Financial Times, "Olam versus Muddy Waters", December 2012*

## 4. Has the corporation violated the rights of indigenous peoples and local communities?

**Answer: YES**. The expansion of Olam's plantations in Africa has repeatedly violated land rights and the principle of free, prior and informed consent.

1. **FPIC violations in Gabon (2013–2019)** — Local communities in Ngounié Province reported that they were not informed about the seizure of lands for Olam plantations. Lands traditionally used for agriculture and hunting were incorporated into concession areas without community consultations.
   *Source: Global Witness, "Holding Corporations Accountable", 2019*

2. **Land conflicts in Cameroon (2016–2020)** — The expansion of Olam's suppliers' operations in Cameroon was accompanied by documented conflicts with local farmers whose fields were destroyed or seized during land clearing for plantations.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network, 2020*

3. **Displacement of fishing communities in Nigeria (2010s)** — The construction of Olam infrastructure near water bodies in Nigeria damaged traditional fishing grounds. Local communities documented declining catches and restricted water access after the company began operations.
   *Source: NGO reports on cocoa supply chains, 2015–2018*

## 5. Has the corporation evaded taxes or participated in corruption?

**Answer: YES/PARTIALLY**. There are documented cases of non-transparent financial transactions and questions about tax structures.

1. **Non-transparent concession deals in Gabon (2010s)** — Global Witness documented the conditions of Olam's concession agreements in Gabon, concluded with the involvement of state structures with minimal public transparency. Contract conditions, including tax incentives, were not publicly disclosed.
   *Source: Global Witness, Gabon investigations, 2019–2021*

2. **Financial questions raised by Muddy Waters analyst (2012)** — Muddy Waters Research analysts published a report questioning Olam's accounting practices and the sustainability of its business model. They pointed to an atypical structure for reflecting capital expenditures. Olam disputed the findings in court.
   *Source: Muddy Waters Research, November 2012; Financial Times*

3. **Subsidies and state incentives in Nigeria** — Olam received state subsidies and incentives in Nigeria against a backdrop of environmental violations. Independent analysts pointed to the non-transparency of distribution of state support to agro-industrial companies.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research, Olam profile, 2019*

## 6. Has the corporation's activity accelerated the climate crisis?

**Answer: YES**. Olam's cocoa and palm oil procurement is directly linked to tropical deforestation — one of the main sources of carbon emissions.

1. **Primary forest destruction in Côte d'Ivoire** — Côte d'Ivoire has lost approximately 80% of its forest cover primarily due to the expansion of cocoa production. Each hectare of tropical forest stores hundreds of tonnes of carbon. As a major cocoa trader, Olam bears direct responsibility for these emissions through its supply chain.
   *Source: Mighty Earth, Satellite monitoring, 2017–2019*

2. **Peatland drainage in Southeast Asia** — Olam's palm oil operations in regions with peat soils lead to the release of carbon accumulated over millennia. Peatlands are among the largest terrestrial carbon stores on the planet.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network, palm oil supply chain analysis, 2020*

3. **Deforestation of Gabon's forests for plantations** — Gabon holds approximately 25% of Central Africa's forest cover. Olam Palm Gabon's concessions created a threat to one of the planet's key carbon reservoirs. Gabon's forest legislation was partially modified under pressure from investors.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research, 2019; Global Forest Watch*

## 7. Has the corporation impeded justice or environmental defenders?

**Answer: YES**. Olam used lawsuits to suppress criticism and opposed independent monitoring.

1. **Lawsuit against Muddy Waters analysts (2012)** — In response to a critical public report, Olam filed a defamation lawsuit in a Singapore court against the analysts. The case was assessed by experts as an example of a strategic lawsuit against public participation (SLAPP) aimed at silencing criticism.
   *Source: Financial Times, Bloomberg, December 2012*

2. **Restricting NGO access to plantations in Gabon** — Independent researchers and journalists reported difficulties obtaining access to Olam's concession territories in Gabon for independent environmental audits.
   *Source: Global Witness, investigation methodology, 2021*

3. **Opposition to supply chain data disclosure requirements** — Despite pressure from investors and consumers, Olam for many years did not publish a complete registry of first- and second-tier suppliers, making independent verification of zero-deforestation claims substantially more difficult.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research, 2018–2020*

## 8. Are there systemic, recurring patterns of violations?

**Answer: YES**. The violations are systematic in nature and have repeated over decades.

1. **Recurring gap between declarations and practice** — Olam has repeatedly made public sustainability commitments (NDPE, RSPO, No Deforestation), while independent monitoring has time and again recorded continued deforestation in procurement zones. This pattern has been observed from 2012 to 2022.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research, 2019; Rainforest Action Network, 2020*

2. **Child labour in cocoa supply chain — a chronic problem** — Despite years of anti-child labour commitments (Harkin-Engel protocol, 2001), by 2020, according to ILO data, the number of children employed in cocoa production in West Africa increased by 14% over the decade. Olam as the largest trader has not changed this trend.
   *Source: ILO, Report on Child Labour in Cocoa Production, 2020*

3. **Expansion into new regions while violations remain unresolved** — As oversight intensified in one region, Olam expanded operations into other countries with less stringent regulation — from Côte d'Ivoire to Cameroon and Gabon, maintaining the same behavioural patterns.
   *Source: Global Witness, Rainforest Action Network, consolidated analysis, 2021*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Child labour and worker exploitation** — Olam is linked to the use of child labour on West African cocoa plantations, chemical burns to cashew plant workers and violations of migrant workers' rights.
2. **Systemic destruction of tropical forests** — Through direct operations and its supply chain, Olam is implicated in the clearing of protected forests in Côte d'Ivoire, Cameroon and Gabon.
3. **Violation of community rights** — Plantation expansion was accompanied by displacement of local communities without FPIC compliance in Gabon, Cameroon and Nigeria.
4. **Double standards in certification** — Obtaining RSPO certificates and adopting NDPE declarations did not prevent continued deforestation, demonstrating the systemic failure of voluntary instruments.
5. **Suppression of criticism** — The lawsuit against Muddy Waters analysts and restriction of access for independent researchers demonstrate a strategy of concealing information.
6. **Climate footprint** — Olam's operations are linked to emissions from the clearing of primary forests and peatland drainage in key carbon reservoirs on the planet.
7. **Recurring patterns** — Violations have been documented over more than twenty years despite public commitments, indicating a structural rather than accidental nature of the problems.
8. **Impunity** — None of the documented violations has resulted in significant legal consequences for Olam; voluntary accountability mechanisms have proven ineffective.
