Clear-cutting of ancient redwood forests in California destroying irreplaceable ecosystems.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. PALCO's aggressive logging caused landslides that destroyed homes and contaminated drinking water sources in settlements of Northern California.

1. **Landslide in Ridle Creek — destruction of a settlement (1997)** — After intensive logging on steep hillsides in 1997, a powerful landslide destroyed homes and property of residents in Ridle Creek settlement in Humboldt County. State geologists established a direct link between PALCO's clear-cutting and the destabilization of slope soil cover.
   *Source: California Department of Forestry and Fire Protection, 1998 report; Save the Redwoods League*

2. **Sebastian-Cooper landslide and contamination of water intakes (1997)** — A series of landslides in PALCO's operational area in the Eel Creek basin led to silting and contamination of watercourses serving as drinking water sources for farming households. Local residents were forced to temporarily forgo well water.
   *Source: NRDC, "Logging and Landslides in Northern California", 1999*

3. **Flood threat to Arcata — flooding after logging (1990s)** — The destruction of forest canopy over catchment basins led to accelerated surface runoff during winter rains, increasing flood risks in settlements downstream. Municipalities initiated lawsuits against PALCO.
   *Source: Earthjustice, lawsuits against PALCO, 1996–2001*

---

## 2. Has the corporation systematically destroyed natural ecosystems?

**Answer: YES**. PALCO under MAXXAM's management destroyed millennia-old coastal redwood forests at a pace unprecedented in California's history.

1. **Shift to clear-cutting of old-growth redwoods (1986–2000)** — After the MAXXAM takeover in 1985, logging rates were sharply increased: the company shifted from selective logging to clear-cutting. In 15 years, as much forest was logged as in the previous 80 years of the company's existence.
   *Source: Save the Redwoods League; NRDC, "Headwaters Forest" reports*

2. **Destruction of Headwaters Forest groves** — The Headwaters conservation zone contained one of the last large stands of undisturbed old-growth coastal forest. PALCO planned to log several hundred hectares of this unique stand. Part was saved in 1999 thanks to a deal with the federal government for $480 million.
   *Source: U.S. Department of the Interior, Headwaters Agreement 1999*

3. **Salmon die-off in basin rivers (1990s)** — Clear-cutting destroyed the forest canopy over spawning streams. Without shade, water temperatures rose above the survival threshold for spawning Pacific salmon (Oncorhynchus spp.) and steelhead, listed as threatened species.
   *Source: NOAA Fisheries, Northern California spawning river studies, 1997–2001*

---

## 3. Has the corporation concealed information or misled the public?

**Answer: YES**. PALCO manipulated permitting procedures and concealed environmental assessments.

1. **Manipulation of Timber Harvest Plans (THP) (1988–2000)** — PALCO systematically divided large planned logging operations into several smaller projects to avoid the threshold for mandatory environmental review under California's Environmental Quality Act (CEQA). Courts subsequently ruled several such plans unlawful.
   *Source: California Court of Appeal, PALCO case rulings, 1990s*

2. **Understating reserve volumes in financial reporting** — After the MAXXAM takeover, the company financed the deal through "junk" bonds, creating an incentive to extract forest reserves as quickly as possible. Public statements about "rational" forest management diverged from actual logging volumes.
   *Source: Los Angeles Times, investigation of MAXXAM and PALCO, 1996*

3. **Disinformation about landslides** — After the catastrophic landslides of 1997, PALCO publicly insisted that the cause was abnormal precipitation, not logging. Independent geological surveys commissioned by the state refuted this version.
   *Source: California Department of Conservation, geological reports, 1998*

## 4. Has the corporation violated the rights of indigenous peoples and local communities?

**Answer: YES**. Traditional lands of the Wiyot people and other Northern California indigenous peoples were harmed by PALCO's activities.

1. **Violation of Wiyot people's treaty rights** — Logging in the Meadow Creek basin affected lands traditionally used by the Wiyot people for food gathering, ceremonies and hunting. The Tribal Council submitted objections to the California Department of Forestry.
   *Source: Wiyot Tribe, statements to CDF, 1990s*

2. **Destruction of traditional fishing grounds** — The contamination of spawning rivers and silting of streams damaged the traditional fishing practices of indigenous communities of Humboldt County, for whom salmon has cultural and nutritional significance.
   *Source: Earthjustice, indigenous rights reports, 1997–2001*

3. **Ignoring community objections during permitting** — Residents of settlements near PALCO's operational areas repeatedly submitted objections to timber harvest plans. State regulatory authorities in the majority of cases approved plans while ignoring community concerns.
   *Source: NRDC, chronology of lawsuits against PALCO*

## 5. Has the corporation evaded taxes or participated in corruption?

**Answer: YES**. PALCO's acquisition by MAXXAM is linked to fraud in the use of government financial guarantees.

1. **Savings and loan crisis fraud (1980s)** — Charles Hurwitz, head of MAXXAM, used California Federal Bank (CalFed) funds to finance the hostile takeover of PALCO. CalFed subsequently went bankrupt, costing taxpayers $1.6 billion. Hurwitz was fined $206 million by the Office of Thrift Supervision.
   *Source: Office of Thrift Supervision, 1995; Los Angeles Times*

2. **Financing through Michael Milken's "junk bonds"** — The PALCO acquisition was carried out using high-risk bonds from Drexel Burnham Lambert, which was subsequently convicted of securities fraud. This mechanism created systemic pressure to exploit forests as quickly as possible.
   *Source: Wall Street Journal, MAXXAM investigation, 1989*

3. **Bankruptcy with asset concealment (2007)** — When filing for bankruptcy in 2007, MAXXAM/PALCO structures were reorganized such that the parent company avoided direct liability. Creditors and affected communities received minimal compensation.
   *Source: U.S. Bankruptcy Court, Northern District of California, PALCO case, 2007*

## 6. Has the corporation's activity accelerated the climate crisis?

**Answer: YES**. The destruction of old-growth redwood forests — among the most carbon-rich repositories on Earth — caused significant climate damage.

1. **Logging of millennia-old carbon-accumulating trees** — Coastal redwoods hold records for carbon accumulation among all trees: a single tree can store up to 500 tonnes of carbon. PALCO logged thousands of such trees, releasing into the atmosphere carbon accumulated over centuries.
   *Source: Prairie Creek Redwoods State Park research; USFS carbon studies*

2. **Destruction of forest litter and soil carbon** — Clear-cutting and heavy logging equipment destroyed the carbon-rich forest litter and soil layer of coastal forests, additionally increasing emissions.
   *Source: UC Berkeley, coastal forest soil studies, 1990s*

3. **Loss of ecosystem carbon sequestration capacity** — The destruction of hundreds of hectares of old-growth forest not only released stored carbon but permanently eliminated the ecosystem's capacity for further CO₂ absorption: secondary forest accumulates carbon tens of times more slowly.
   *Source: Save the Redwoods League, climate and redwood reports*

## 7. Has the corporation impeded justice or environmental defenders?

**Answer: YES**. PALCO and MAXXAM actively opposed environmental activists and regulatory oversight.

1. **Tree spiking for sabotage of protests (1987)** — Metal spikes were found driven into tree trunks in PALCO's forests — a practice posing a deadly threat to sawyers. Although responsibility was never established, the atmosphere of conflict between the company and activists created a real threat of violence.
   *Source: Los Angeles Times, 1987*

2. **Persecution of activists Daryl Cherney and Judi Bari (1990)** — The FBI and Oakland police arrested environmental activists Daryl Cherney and Judi Bari after a bomb exploded in their car, accusing them of transporting explosives. A court subsequently found that the investigation was conducted in violation of civil rights; the federal government paid $4.4 million in compensation.
   *Source: Federal jury verdict, Oakland, 2002*

3. **Threats and pressure on tree-sitting protesters** — PALCO security guards applied physical pressure on activists blocking logging operations. Julia Hill maintained her position for 738 days under constant pressure: operating saws nearby, round-the-clock surveillance, floodlights.
   *Source: Julia Butterfly Hill, "The Legacy of Luna", 2000*

## 8. Are there systemic, recurring patterns of violations?

**Answer: YES**. PALCO/MAXXAM's actions demonstrate a consistent logic of predatory resource exploitation.

1. **"Slash and run" model** — MAXXAM's entire strategy was built on extracting the valuable resource (old-growth forest) as quickly as possible to service the debt accumulated during the acquisition. Long-term sustainability was not intended from the outset: PALCO went bankrupt in 2007, having exhausted reserves.
   *Source: NRDC, analysis of MAXXAM corporate behavior; Wall Street Journal*

2. **Repeated permit condition violations** — The California Department of Forestry repeatedly documented violations of timber harvest plan conditions: non-compliance with stream buffer zones, exceeding permitted volumes, unauthorized logging. Fines were negligible compared to profits.
   *Source: California Department of Forestry, inspection reports, 1990–2000*

3. **Corporate model of predatory acquisition** — The PALCO story became a textbook example of a "leveraged buyout" in which the target company is exploited to service the acquirer's debts through natural resources. This model was replicated by MAXXAM in other sectors.
   *Source: Los Angeles Times, MAXXAM investigations; academic publications on LBO and environment*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Predatory acquisition using government guarantees** — The PALCO acquisition was financed through fraudulent schemes costing taxpayers $1.6 billion, and the corporate debt made forest destruction an economic necessity for MAXXAM.
2. **Accelerated destruction of millennia-old forests** — In 15 years, the company logged as many old-growth coastal redwoods as in the previous eight decades.
3. **Landslides and threat to human lives** — Clear-cutting on hillsides led to catastrophic landslides that destroyed homes in 1997.
4. **Death of spawning salmon** — The destruction of river canopy killed populations of protected salmon species in spawning rivers.
5. **Systematic violations of environmental legislation** — Plan splitting, permit manipulation, ignoring fines — all created a culture of unpunished lawbreaking.
6. **Persecution of activists** — Pressure on forest defenders, including the Cherney-Bari case that ended in $4.4 million compensation for civil rights violations.
7. **Bankruptcy as the outcome of the predatory model** — In 2007, PALCO went bankrupt, leaving devastated forests, unemployed workers and unpaid debts to communities.
8. **Precedent for environmental legislation** — The PALCO case became a catalyst for stricter laws protecting old-growth forests and tighter oversight of hostile acquisitions in resource industries.
