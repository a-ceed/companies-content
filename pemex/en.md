Chronic oil spills and gas flaring in the Gulf of Mexico and southern Mexico.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Over decades of operations, hundreds of people have died as a result of explosions, fires and poisonings, while thousands of residents of coastal and riverside areas have lost their water sources and livelihoods.

1. **Explosion on Abkatun-A Permanente platform (2015)** — In April 2015, an explosion and fire occurred on the Abkatun-A offshore oil platform in the Gulf of Campeche. Four people died and 16 were injured. The fire burned for several days and a significant quantity of oil spilled into the sea. The investigation found violations of maintenance regulations.
   *Source: Reuters, April 2015; Pemex official incident report*

2. **Guadalajara sewer explosions (1992)** — A fuel leak from a Pemex pipeline under the city of Guadalajara led to the accumulation of explosive vapors in the sewer system. On 22 April 1992, a series of explosions destroyed 8 kilometres of city blocks: 206 people died, more than 500 were injured, thousands lost their homes.
   *Source: New York Times, April 1992; Mexican government reports*

3. **Chronic drinking water poisoning in Veracruz** — For decades, ruptures in Pemex pipelines in Veracruz state contaminated the Coatzacoalcos and Papaloapan rivers — the main drinking water sources for hundreds of thousands of residents. Studies document elevated levels of hydrocarbons and heavy metals in the groundwater of surrounding communities.
   *Source: IMSS and local environmental NGOs in Veracruz, 2018–2020*

## 2. Has the corporation systematically destroyed natural ecosystems?

**Answer: YES**. Pemex is the source of chronic environmental pollution covering the coastal, marine and river ecosystems of Mexico.

1. **Ixtoc-I spill — largest oil disaster before Deepwater Horizon (1979–1980)** — On 3 June 1979, the explosion of the Ixtoc-I exploratory well in the Gulf of Campeche led to an uncontrolled oil release. Over 9 months, approximately 475,000 tonnes (3.3 million barrels) of oil spilled into the sea. The oil slick reached the coast of Texas. Fish spawning grounds, mangrove forests and coral reefs were destroyed over an area of thousands of square kilometres.
   *Source: NOAA, oil spill history; National Geographic*

2. **Chronic contamination of Laguna de Términos (1970s — present)** — Laguna de Términos in Campeche state — Mexico's largest lagoon biosphere reserve — has experienced chronic oil contamination from Pemex offshore platforms and pipelines for decades. Scientists document the accumulation of petroleum products in bottom sediments and fish tissue.
   *Source: UNAM, Laguna de Términos environmental studies, 2000–2020*

3. **Contamination of the Coatzacoalcos River (1990s — present)** — The Coatzacoalcos River in Veracruz has repeatedly been recognized as one of Mexico's most polluted rivers. Pemex petrochemical plants and pipelines are the main pollution sources. Fish populations in the river have catastrophically declined; mangrove forests at the river mouth are partially destroyed.
   *Source: SEMARNAT, annual water body condition reports; ProNatura*

## 3. Has the corporation concealed information or misled the public?

**Answer: YES**. Pemex systematically understated the scale of oil spills and delayed public disclosure of accident information.

1. **Understating Ixtoc-I spill volumes** — Initially, Pemex understated the daily oil output volume several-fold. Independent assessments by NOAA scientists conducting aerial measurements systematically exceeded the company's official figures. The full scale of the catastrophe only became known months later.
   *Source: NOAA Oil Spill Investigative Reports, 1979–1980*

2. **Delays in reporting pipeline accidents** — Numerous investigations by Mexican journalists and NGOs documented cases where Pemex notified authorities of pipeline accidents with delays of a day or more, depriving local communities of the opportunity to protect themselves from contamination in time.
   *Source: Animal Político, investigations 2016–2019*

3. **Non-transparency of methane emission data** — Independent satellite studies (TROPOMI/Sentinel-5P data) have repeatedly recorded large methane emissions over Pemex facilities, significantly exceeding the company's official reporting data to Mexican regulatory authorities.
   *Source: Environmental Defense Fund, Global Methane Tracking, 2019–2022*

## 4. Has the corporation violated the rights of indigenous peoples and local communities?

**Answer: YES**. Pemex's oil infrastructure has been damaging the territories of Mexico's indigenous peoples for decades without adequate consultation and compensation.

1. **Contamination of Chontal people's territories in Tabasco** — Tabasco state — one of Pemex's main oil-producing regions — is home to the Chontal Maya indigenous people. Chronic oil spills destroyed the agricultural lands and fishing grounds of communities. Compensation claims were ignored for years.
   *Source: Centro de Derechos Humanos Miguel Agustín Pro Juárez, reports 2010–2015*

2. **Violation of Veracruz community rights during pipeline construction** — Farming and fishing communities in Veracruz repeatedly reported that Pemex infrastructure was being laid through their lands without full consultations as provided for by ILO Convention No. 169 on indigenous peoples.
   *Source: Red Mexicana de Afectados por la Minería (REMA), 2014–2018*

3. **Oil contamination of Campeche community fishing grounds** — Coastal fishing communities of Campeche state, traditionally engaged in octopus and fish catching in the Gulf of Campeche, as a result of years of contamination from Pemex offshore platforms lost a significant portion of their commercial catch without compensation being paid.
   *Source: CONAPESCA, Campeche fishing output reports; local fishing cooperatives*

## 5. Has the corporation evaded taxes or participated in corruption?

**Answer: YES**. Pemex became the centre of large-scale corruption schemes linked to the oil union, state procurement and political financing.

1. **Corruption scandal involving union leader Carlos Romero (2008–2012)** — Long-time head of the STPRM oil union Carlos Romero Deschamps was arrested in 2009 on charges of embezzling union funds — by some estimates, more than $2 billion. Pemex was the de facto source of these funds through inflated contracts with union companies.
   *Source: Reuters, 2009; Mexican Attorney General's Office*

2. **Inflated procurement contracts** — Long-running investigations by Mexico's Supreme Audit Institution (ASF) revealed systematic overpricing in Pemex's state contracts for equipment, services and construction. Damage amounted to billions of pesos annually.
   *Source: Auditoría Superior de la Federación, annual reports*

3. **Financing political parties through Pemex** — Investigations by Mexican investigative journalists (MCCI) documented schemes for channeling Pemex funds to the Institutional Revolutionary Party (PRI) funds through dummy contractor companies.
   *Source: Mexicanos Contra la Corrupción y la Impunidad (MCCI), 2016–2018*

## 6. Has the corporation's activity accelerated the climate crisis?

**Answer: YES**. Pemex is one of the world's largest sources of methane emissions and a significant CO₂ emitter.

1. **Anomalous methane emissions — among the largest in the industry** — According to satellite observations by EDF (Environmental Defense Fund), Pemex is among the oil and gas companies with the highest specific methane emissions in the world. Gas flaring at Mexican oilfields significantly exceeds indicators of Western majors.
   *Source: EDF, "Permian Basin Methane" and global reports; Sentinel-5P data*

2. **Large-scale associated gas flaring** — The volume of gas flared at Pemex facilities places the company in the top five global polluters by this indicator. Billions of cubic metres of gas are released into the atmosphere annually — equivalent to the CO₂ emissions of several large coal power plants.
   *Source: World Bank Global Gas Flaring Tracker, 2019–2022*

3. **Resistance to the climate agenda** — Despite Mexico's adoption of climate commitments under the Paris Agreement, Pemex lobbied for the preservation of fossil fuel subsidies and increased oil production. Under President López Obrador (2018–2024), the priority of oil production over renewable energy was enshrined at the state level.
   *Source: Climate Action Tracker, México; Financial Times*

---

## 7. Has the corporation impeded justice or environmental defenders?

**Answer: YES**. Pemex uses its state status to evade legal liability and pressure critics.

1. **State company immunity from lawsuits** — As a state company, Pemex enjoys a special legal status that substantially complicates filing a lawsuit for compensation for contamination damage. Numerous lawsuits from fishing communities and farmers were dismissed on procedural grounds.
   *Source: CEJIL, Centro de Justicia y Derecho Internacional, reports*

2. **Absence of compensation for Ixtoc-I victims** — Despite the Ixtoc-I spill causing significant damage to fishermen and coastal communities in Texas and Mexico, Pemex has never paid full compensation. Appeals to diplomatic protection at the level of US–Mexico relations produced no results.
   *Source: Houston Chronicle, investigation of Ixtoc-I compensation*

3. **Pressure on investigative journalists** — Mexican journalists investigating Pemex corruption and environmental violations in Veracruz and Tabasco states have repeatedly reported threats and intimidation. Several journalists working on oil scheme investigations have been victims of attacks.
   *Source: Reporters Without Borders, Mexico press freedom index; CPJ*

---

## 8. Are there systemic, recurring patterns of violations?

**Answer: YES**. Pemex's violations span more than half a century and are replicated through all changes of the country's political leadership.

1. **Permanence of environmental violations regardless of presidential cycles** — Oil spills, river contamination and above-standard methane emissions are recorded under all Mexican presidents from the 1960s through the 2020s, indicating the institutional rather than situational nature of violations.
   *Source: Consolidated analysis: SEMARNAT, UNAM, ProNatura*

2. **Repetition of major accidents while lessons are ignored** — Major accidents (1979 Ixtoc-I, 1992 Guadalajara, 2015 Abkatun-A) occurred despite the experience of previous catastrophes, indicating a chronic deficit of investment in safety and maintenance.
   *Source: Pemex safety reports; STPS inspection data*

3. **Structural corruption reproduced over decades** — The Pemex–union–political parties nexus was reproduced over decades, accumulating systemic damage to state finances and the company's environmental standards.
   *Source: MCCI, chronology of Pemex corruption investigations, 1980–2020*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Ixtoc-I — one of the largest environmental catastrophes of the 20th century** — The 1979–1980 spill of 475,000 tonnes of oil destroyed marine ecosystems of the Gulf of Campeche and damaged the coastlines of two countries without any significant compensation.
2. **Guadalajara explosions (1992)** — A Pemex fuel leak killed 206 people in city blocks; this is one of the largest corporate-caused disasters in Mexico's history.
3. **Chronic poisoning of water ecosystems** — Veracruz rivers and Laguna de Términos have been receiving oil pollution from Pemex infrastructure for decades.
4. **Violation of indigenous peoples' rights** — Chontal Maya communities and other indigenous peoples of Tabasco and Veracruz have borne environmental damage without compensation for generations.
5. **Systemic corruption** — Multi-billion-dollar schemes through the oil union and procurement contracts undermined safety and environmental standards funding.
6. **Among the world's largest methane emitters** — Pemex is among the global leaders in specific methane emissions, aggravating the climate crisis.
7. **Legal impunity** — Pemex's state status effectively shields the company from environmental damage compensation lawsuits.
8. **Recurrence of disasters** — Half a century of accidents and contamination without systemic changes indicates the structural rather than accidental nature of violations.
