Oil spills and gas flaring along the Brazilian coast and in the Amazon region.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Accidents at Petrobras facilities have claimed the lives of workers and contaminated water sources and fishing grounds that coastal communities depended on for survival.

1. **Explosion on platform P-36 (2001)** — On 15 March 2001, two explosions occurred on the P-36 oil platform in the Atlantic Ocean. Eleven people died and the platform sank several days later. It was the world's largest floating oil production platform at the time.
   *Source: BBC News, March 2001; Petrobras official report; Reuters*

2. **Contamination of Guanabara Bay (2000)** — In January 2000, a Petrobras pipeline rupture spilled approximately 1.3 million liters of oil into Guanabara Bay near Rio de Janeiro. Mangrove forests were destroyed, hundreds of local fishermen lost their livelihoods. Fish populations in the bay declined several-fold.
   *Source: Greenpeace Brasil, 2000; IBAMA penalty ruling*

3. **Spill in Paraná state (2000)** — In the same year 2000, a Petrobras pipeline leak near Curitiba spilled more than 4 million liters of oil into the Iguaçu and Barigui rivers, supplying drinking water to 2.5 million residents. The region's water supply was interrupted; fishing was banned for several months.
   *Source: IBAMA, incident report, 2000; BBC Brasil*

## 2. Has the corporation systematically destroyed natural ecosystems?

**Answer: YES**. Petrobras operations are associated with chronic pollution of unique ecosystems of the Atlantic coast and Brazil's continental shelf.

1. **Years of Guanabara Bay pollution** — Despite publicly-stated commitments to restore the bay after the 2000 spill, by the 2020s Guanabara Bay was still among the most polluted coastal waters of Brazil. Independent studies document chronic oil and chemical pollution from multiple sources, including Petrobras infrastructure.
   *Source: Instituto Estadual do Ambiente (INEA), 2019–2021*

2. **Oil leaks in the Atlantic Forest (Mata Atlântica)** — Petrobras pipelines pass through remnants of Mata Atlântica — one of the world's most endangered biomes (less than 12% of its original area remains). IBAMA documented numerous leaks that caused damage to specially protected areas.
   *Source: IBAMA, environmental violations database*

3. **Deep-water Frade field pollution (2011)** — An emergency oil spill at the deep-water Frade field near Cabo Frio affected an important spawning area for commercial fish. Brazilian authorities fined Chevron (the operator), but the ecosystem damage remained uncompensated.
   *Source: Ibama; O Globo, November 2011*

---

## 3. Has the corporation concealed information or misled the public?

**Answer: YES**. The "Lava Jato" corruption scandal and understatement of spill volumes exposed a systemic culture of non-transparency.

1. **"Lava Jato" corruption scandal — concealment of financial abuses (2014–2018)** — Operation "Lava Jato" ("Car Wash") revealed a long-running bribery scheme by Petrobras top executives through contractors. More than $2 billion in bribes were concealed in financial reporting. Management deliberately concealed the scheme from shareholders, the board of directors and regulators.
   *Source: Brazil Federal Prosecution Service; Wall Street Journal; The Economist*

2. **Understating spill volume in Paraná state** — After the 2000 spill into the Iguaçu River, Petrobras's initial estimates of oil volume substantially understated the actual figures. Independent measurements by IBAMA produced significantly higher numbers.
   *Source: IBAMA, final report on the Paraná spill, 2000*

3. **Asset price manipulation in the bribery scheme** — The "Lava Jato" investigation established that Petrobras deliberately overpaid for asset acquisitions (for example, the Pasadena refinery in the USA for $820 million at a fraction of the real value), concealing the difference in the form of bribes.
   *Source: CNN Brasil; Folha de São Paulo, Pasadena case investigation*

## 4. Has the corporation violated the rights of indigenous peoples and local communities?

**Answer: YES**. Petrobras's oil infrastructure affected the territories of indigenous peoples and coastal fishing communities.

1. **Damage to Guanabara Bay fishing communities** — The 2000 spill destroyed the income sources of hundreds of artisanal fishing families living around the bay. Compensation from Petrobras proved negligibly small; many fishermen were forced to change their profession.
   *Source: Rede Brasileira de Justiça Ambiental, 2001*

2. **Violation of community rights during GASBOL pipeline construction** — During the construction of the Bolivia-Brazil gas pipeline (GASBOL) through Mato Grosso and other regions, community lands were affected without full consultations as provided for by Brazilian law.
   *Source: Instituto Socioambiental (ISA), 1999–2002*

3. **Contamination of Paraná community water sources** — The 2000 spill into the Barigui River deprived thousands of families in Curitiba of access to drinking water. Low-income communities dependent on municipal water supply suffered disproportionately.
   *Source: Prefeitura de Curitiba, crisis reports 2000*

---

## 5. Has the corporation evaded taxes or participated in corruption?

**Answer: YES**. Operation "Lava Jato" exposed one of the largest corruption schemes in corporate history.

1. **"Lava Jato": $2+ billion in bribes (2004–2014)** — Petrobras top executives received bribes from major construction and oilfield service companies (Odebrecht, OAS, Andrade Gutierrez) in exchange for inflated state contracts. The total documented bribe amount exceeded $2 billion. Dozens of company executives and businessmen were convicted.
   *Source: Brazil Federal Prosecution; US Department of Justice*

2. **Shareholder losses — more than $100 billion** — When the scheme came to light in 2014, Petrobras's market capitalization collapsed by more than $100 billion. Thousands of small investors, including pension funds of state enterprise workers, lost their savings.
   *Source: Bloomberg, December 2014; Reuters*

3. **Financing political parties** — The investigation established that part of the corrupt funds were directed to financing the Workers' Party (PT) and other Brazilian political parties, turning Petrobras into a mechanism for covert political financing.
   *Source: Brazil Electoral Court; Folha de São Paulo*

## 6. Has the corporation's activity accelerated the climate crisis?

**Answer: YES**. Petrobras actively expanded fossil fuel production, opposed climate regulation and generated significant methane emissions.

1. **Deep-water oil production expansion against Brazil's climate commitments** — While Brazil adopted international climate commitments, Petrobras expanded investments in developing the giant deep-water "pre-salt" layer deposits, production from which will require decades and will release billions of tonnes of CO₂ into the atmosphere.
   *Source: Carbon Tracker, "Flying Blind" report; Climate Action Tracker*

2. **Methane emissions at offshore fields** — Independent studies recorded methane leaks at Petrobras offshore facilities significantly exceeding the company's official corporate reporting data. Methane is a greenhouse gas 80 times more potent than CO₂ in the short term.
   *Source: Climate & Clean Air Coalition; Global Methane Pledge data*

3. **Lobbying against climate regulation** — Petrobras actively lobbied to preserve fuel subsidies and opposed the introduction of a carbon tax in Brazil, directing significant resources to work with legislators through industry associations.
   *Source: InfluenceMap, corporate lobbying database; Reuters*

---

## 7. Has the corporation impeded justice or environmental defenders?

**Answer: YES**. Petrobras used political connections and legal mechanisms to evade full accountability.

1. **Years of resistance to compensation for the 2000 spill** — After the Guanabara Bay and Iguaçu River spills, affected communities spent years seeking compensation through the courts. Petrobras contested damage amounts in years-long legal proceedings, ultimately paying sums significantly less than the actual harm.
   *Source: Rede Brasileira de Justiça Ambiental; Paraná state court documents*

2. **Political cover for corruption schemes** — The "Lava Jato" investigation established that high-ranking politicians connected to Petrobras actively obstructed investigations, interfered in prosecutors' appointments and pressured investigative bodies.
   *Source: Federal Prosecution Service of Brazil, public case materials*

3. **Pressure on Judge Moro** — Subsequently leaked correspondence showed that former President Lula and his entourage exerted pressure on the course of the "Lava Jato" investigation. Brazil's Constitutional Court considered the question of the impartiality of case conduct.
   *Source: The Intercept Brasil, 2019; Supreme Federal Tribunal proceedings*

---

## 8. Are there systemic, recurring patterns of violations?

**Answer: YES**. Petrobras's environmental and corruption violations have a sustained, decade-long character.

1. **Recurring oil spills without systemic improvement** — Major spills in 2000 (Guanabara, Paraná), 2001 (P-36) and 2011 (Frade) occurred despite promises to improve safety standards after each previous catastrophe.
   *Source: IBAMA, chronology of Petrobras environmental incidents*

2. **Corruption as institutional practice** — "Lava Jato" showed that corruption at Petrobras was organized as a systematic corporate system for at least 10 years, with clear procedures and role distribution among executives.
   *Source: Federal Prosecution Service materials*

3. **Weak environmental accountability under state control** — As a company with a state controlling stake, Petrobras de facto benefited from mild environmental regulation: IBAMA fines were repeatedly challenged in courts and reduced.
   *Source: IBAMA enforcement data; TCU audit reports*

## Summary of Answers

1. **"Lava Jato" — the largest corruption scandal in history** — More than $2 billion in bribes, dozens of convicted executives, more than $100 billion in shareholder losses and systemic destruction of state institutions.
2. **Deaths on platform P-36 (2001)** — 11 killed in explosions on the world's largest floating platform.
3. **Environmental catastrophe in Guanabara Bay (2000)** — Destruction of mangrove forests, collapse of fishing, deprivation of livelihoods for hundreds of fishing families.
4. **Contamination of drinking water for 2.5 million Paraná residents (2000)** — The oil spill into the Iguaçu River interrupted the region's water supply for several months.
5. **Chronic pollution of unique ecosystems** — Mata Atlântica, Guanabara Bay and the Atlantic shelf suffered from years of pipeline leaks.
6. **Climate irresponsibility** — Expansion of deep-water oil production, methane leaks and lobbying against climate regulation.
7. **Violation of community rights** — Fishing and coastal communities systematically received inadequate compensation for damage caused by spills.
8. **Impunity through political connections** — Petrobras's state status and political connections ensured mild legal regulation for decades.
