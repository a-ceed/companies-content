Philip Morris is a leading tobacco empire.

The company encompasses many popular cigarette brands, including Marlboro. By promoting smoking and crafting "cool" images in films and advertising that children aspire to imitate, the company has buried more than one actor who played the Marlboro cowboy. Four, to be precise.

Three of the actors died of lung cancer; the fourth, Eric Lawson, died of respiratory failure. Ironically, most of the "Marlboro cowboys" spent their final years repenting their roles and becoming fierce anti-smoking advocates, taking the corporation to court. The company has repeatedly paid tens of millions of dollars to settle lawsuits related to concealing the harm caused by tobacco smoke.

In pursuit of profit, the company shows no reluctance in the face of millions of premature deaths, and year after year it wraps the advertising of its addictive products in ever more deceptive ideas tailored to every taste, age, and background.

Smoking is one of the leading causes of death worldwide, so before turning to the environmental issue, we must at least briefly mention the nature of this bloody business and the lies of the Philip Morris corporation that have accompanied it throughout its entire history.

Smoking harms not only human health, but also the environment!

Cigarette butts pollute the land and water with plastic particles and harmful chemicals — and today this problem is reaching catastrophic proportions.

According to preliminary estimates by the Ocean Conservancy research group, cigarette filters make up the largest share of litter in the world's oceans. Cigarette butts are the most frequently encountered type of waste. Approximately 4.5 trillion cigarette butts are discarded worldwide every year, contaminating 180 trillion litres of water.

Cigarette filters are made of plastic, and by the time they are disposed of they contain thousands of chemicals, including arsenic, lead, copper, and nicotine. Rain and dew wash out these toxic substances, which then seep into the soil and contaminate groundwater. Cigarette butts also reach the ocean through drainage systems and rivers.

The problem lies not only in the harmful substances contained in cigarette butts — the filters themselves damage marine life because they are made of plastic. A vast number of underwater creatures swallow pieces of plastic, mistaking them for food. Birds also mistake cigarette butts for food and ingest both plastic and toxins.

Researchers from Ocean Conservancy found traces of chemicals in 70% of the seabirds they caught. Furthermore, more than 30 percent of sea turtles and many species of marine fish are also loaded with toxins whose composition matches the chemicals used in cigarette manufacturing.

As the number of megacities and the world's population grows, so does the volume of waste discharged into the land and ocean ecosystems. Natural resources are not unlimited, and they cannot process excessive amounts of plastic, cigarette butts, and toxic substances. These discharges poison flora and fauna and destroy ecosystems that form the very foundation of our life on this planet.

The blame for this destruction rests first and foremost with producers like Philip Morris, who show no concern for the basic or environmental safety of their product, and secondly with the politicians they have bought — whether those politicians promote cigarettes through their authority or remain inactive on environmental legislation, rolling out the red carpet of impunity for these waste monsters.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Philip Morris International bears direct responsibility for millions of deaths annually and for decades concealed information about the lethal nature of its products.

1. **Master Settlement Agreement of US States (1998)** — As a result of years-long litigation, Philip Morris and other major tobacco companies paid US states **$246 billion** to settle claims related to health harm and concealment of data on cigarette toxicity. Documents disclosed during the proceedings showed that the company had known about the carcinogenic properties of tobacco smoke since the 1950s and deliberately withheld this information.
   *Source: National Association of Attorneys General — "Master Settlement Agreement"*

2. **Death of four "Marlboro Cowboys"** — Wayne McLaren, David McLean, and Dick Hammer — actors who portrayed the Marlboro cowboy — died of lung cancer. Eric Lawson died of a pulmonary disease. Three of them openly condemned Philip Morris in their final years and participated in anti-tobacco campaigns; McLaren gave testimony against the company before his death.
   *Source: The Guardian — "The Marlboro Men who died of smoking"*

3. **WHO: 8 million deaths per year from tobacco** — According to the World Health Organization, smoking kills more than 8 million people annually. Philip Morris — the world's largest cigarette manufacturer — plays a central role in sustaining the global tobacco epidemic, including through aggressive marketing in African and Asian countries.
   *Source: WHO — "Tobacco Fact Sheet" (2023)*



## 2. Has the corporation been responsible for major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Philip Morris's activities are linked to large-scale environmental pollution — from cigarette butts in the oceans to deforestation for tobacco plantations.

1. **4.5 trillion cigarette butts per year** — According to Ocean Conservancy, approximately **4.5 trillion cigarette butts** are discarded worldwide each year, making them the largest category of plastic waste by volume. A single manufacturer (Philip Morris) accounts for around 28% of global cigarette sales. Chemicals from cigarette butts (arsenic, lead, nicotine) contaminate **180 trillion litres of water** annually.
   *Source: Ocean Conservancy — "Cigarette Butts: The World's Most Collected Trash"*

2. **Deforestation for tobacco plantations** — Tobacco cultivation destroys **600,000 hectares of forest** annually, primarily in Zimbabwe, Malawi, Tanzania, and Indonesia — to create new plantations and to harvest firewood for curing leaves. Philip Morris is the largest buyer of leaf tobacco in Africa.
   *Source: WHO/FCTC — "Tobacco and its environmental impact" (2017)*

3. **Soil and water contamination by pesticides** — Tobacco plantations make intensive use of pesticides (imidacloprid, chlorpyrifos) that contaminate groundwater. Plantation workers in Brazil and India suffer from "green tobacco sickness" (acute nicotine poisoning) when harvesting wet leaves without protective gloves.
   *Source: Human Rights Watch — "Tobacco's Hidden Children" (2014)*



## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Philip Morris actively uses greenwashing, positioning IQOS and other "heated" products as an environmentally safe alternative, thereby misleading consumers.

1. **IQOS as an "eco-friendly alternative" (2016–2024)** — Philip Morris promotes IQOS with the claim that "no combustion means better for the environment," even though Heets sticks are single-use plastic items that cannot be separately collected for recycling. Independent analysis shows that Heets sticks produce a comparable quantity of toxic compounds in their aerosol to conventional cigarettes.
   *Source: Action on Smoking and Health (ASH UK) — "IQOS and environmental claims"*

2. **The "Smoke-Free Future" mission** — PMI actively uses the slogan of delivering "a smoke-free world," presenting itself as a company moving away from cigarettes, while more than 70% of its revenue still comes from conventional cigarette sales. Critics call this "changing the narrative without changing the business."
   *Source: STOP Campaign — "Philip Morris' Smoke Screen" (2021)*

3. **FDA denial of "modified risk product" status** — The US Food and Drug Administration rejected PMI's application for IQOS to be designated a Modified Risk Tobacco Product (MRTP), citing insufficient evidence of reduced harm to public health. Nevertheless, PMI continues to use similar claims in its marketing in other countries.
   *Source: FDA — "FDA Grants IQOS Modified Risk Tobacco Product Authorization with Restrictions" (with restrictions, 2020)*



## 4. Does the company participate in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Philip Morris International actively constructs an ESG narrative around the concept of "harm reduction," publishing extensive sustainability reports.

1. **Annual "Delivering a Smoke-Free Future" report** — PMI publishes a detailed ESG report in which it claims to be transitioning to "heated" products, reducing the carbon footprint of its manufacturing, and running social programmes for plantation workers. The report is widely cited in financial media as an example of "responsible business."
   *Source: Philip Morris International — "Integrated Report 2023"*

2. **Scope 1+2 carbon neutrality target by 2025** — PMI announced it would achieve carbon neutrality in its own operations by 2025. However, Scope 3 emissions (tobacco cultivation, transport, cigarette butt disposal) are excluded from the report and are many times greater than the company's direct emissions.
   *Source: PMI Sustainability Report (2023)*

3. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (2020–2022)** — PMI was listed in the DJSI World, which was used in the company's press releases as confirmation of ESG leadership. Following a public pressure campaign, S&P excluded tobacco companies from several ESG indices in 2022.
   *Source: S&P Global — "Exclusion of tobacco companies from ESG indices" (2022)*



## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG competition nominations?

**Answer: YES**. Despite selling a lethal product, PMI has received recognition in several ESG ratings, which in itself serves as an indicator of systemic flaws in ESG evaluation.

1. **Dow Jones Sustainability Index World (2020–2022)** — PMI was included in the DJSI World, one of the most authoritative global ESG indices, for several consecutive years. This fact was actively used in the company's investor presentations.
   *Source: S&P Global Dow Jones Indices — DJSI compositions for 2020–2022*

2. **CDP Climate Score "B" (2022)** — Philip Morris received a "B" rating from CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project) — the "management" level — which the company presented as evidence of the transparency of its climate policy.
   *Source: CDP — "Philip Morris International climate disclosure score"*

3. **"Employer of Choice" Award (several countries, 2021–2023)** — PMI received HR awards in several countries as a "best employer," which is cited in the company's ESG communications under the "social responsibility" section.
   *Source: PMI — annual ESG reports*



## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Philip Morris actively uses pseudo-ecological labelling on IQOS and related products, misleading consumers about their environmental impact.

1. **IQOS labelling "no combustion — no smoke"** — IQOS devices are sold with an emphasis on the absence of smoke as an environmental advantage. However, Heets sticks are single-use composite items made of plastic, aluminium, and tobacco that cannot be separately recycled. No country in the world has established a separate collection system for them.
   *Source: ASH UK — "Environmental impact of heated tobacco products"*

2. **"Biodegradable" filters in the Bond Street range (selected markets)** — PMI promoted cigarettes labelled "biodegradable filter" in certain markets. Independent tests showed that an acetate filter takes more than **10 years** to break down under natural conditions, even longer in seawater, and releases toxic substances in the process.
   *Source: Tobacco Control — "Biodegradable cigarette filters: marketing vs. reality"*

3. **"Sustainable Packaging"** — Since 2022, PMI has labelled some of its packaging with a claim about using "responsibly sourced" cardboard (FSC-certified), while the primary environmental damage from the product — cigarette butts and nicotine — remains unchanged.
   *Source: PMI — "Our approach to packaging sustainability"*



## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Philip Morris carries out environmental initiatives whose scale is incomparable to the damage caused by its products.

1. **Cigarette butt collection programme in Switzerland (2019–2022)** — PMI sponsored the installation of special cigarette butt disposal units in several Swiss cities. The programme covered a few thousand units while **4.5 trillion** cigarette butts are discarded worldwide every year. Critics called it "a drop in the ocean" and a PR exercise for EU regulators.
   *Source: Tobacco Reporter — "PMI launches cigarette butt collection program"*

2. **Reforestation in Malawi (2020–2023)** — Through the PMI Impact programme, the company funded the planting of several thousand hectares of forest in Malawi. For comparison, tobacco production destroys **600,000 hectares** of forest annually in that same country and its neighbours.
   *Source: PMI Impact — Annual Report (2022)*

3. **Subsidies for farmers on "sustainable practices" (Good Agricultural Practices)** — PMI paid small bonuses to tobacco-supplier farmers for compliance with GAP standards. The programme's budget amounted to a few million dollars against the company's annual revenue of over **$30 billion**.
   *Source: PMI — "Sustainable Agriculture Program"*



## 8. Has the company lobbied for pseudo-ecological theories or greenwashing outreach?

**Answer: YES**. Philip Morris actively lobbies in favour of regulatory approaches that benefit the tobacco industry, promoting the concept of "harm reduction" as a substitute for an actual ban on cigarettes.

1. **Lobbying against plain packaging through ISDS** — PMI initiated international trade arbitrations (ISDS) against Australia (2012) and Uruguay, contesting plain packaging laws as violations of investment agreements. The Australian claim was rejected, but the process took several years and cost the government tens of millions of dollars.
   *Source: The Guardian — "Philip Morris sues Australia over plain packaging laws"*

2. **Promoting the harm reduction concept as an alternative to a cigarette ban** — PMI actively lobbies at WHO, EU, and IQOS-market forums for the idea of "harm reduction," arguing that heated devices should receive a preferential tax and regulatory regime. Critics from the WHO Framework Convention (FCTC) point out that this allows the company to slow the adoption of stricter anti-tobacco measures.
   *Source: FCTC COP — "Tobacco industry interference with FCTC implementation"*

3. **Funding pseudo-scientific organisations** — In the 1990s and 2000s, Philip Morris sponsored The Advancement of Sound Science Coalition (TASSC) — an organisation that publicly disputed scientific data on the harm of passive smoking and the link between tobacco and cancer. It was subsequently revealed that TASSC was directly funded through PMI's PR agencies.
   *Source: UCSF Industry Documents Library — PMI internal documents*
