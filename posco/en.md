POSCO — one of the world's largest steelmakers — sought to build the largest foreign-owned plant in India in the state of Odisha. After 12 years of resistance by local communities, POSCO was forced to abandon the project in 2017. At its home plants in Pohang, South Korea, POSCO has repeatedly exceeded emission limits for sulphur dioxide, nitrogen oxides, and PM2.5 particulates. POSCO is one of the top five largest corporate CO₂ emitters in South Korea.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. POSCO's plants in Pohang cause long-term health damage to residents through air pollution with dioxins, PM2.5, and heavy metals.

1. **Dioxin air pollution in Pohang (2010–2023)** — The coking-chemical production at POSCO's Pohang plant is a source of dioxin and furan emissions — Class 1 hazardous substances. The Korean Environment Institute (KEI) repeatedly recorded dioxin concentrations near the plant exceeding permissible limits. Epidemiological studies found elevated cancer rates among the Pohang population compared with the national average.
   *Source: Korea Environment Institute (KEI), 2019; Korean Cancer Society data*

2. **PM2.5 and respiratory diseases in Gwangyang (2015–2022)** — POSCO's second major complex in Gwangyang (South Jeolla Province) is one of the largest PM2.5 sources in the region. Local authorities recorded exceedances of daily fine-particle concentration norms during steelmaking operations. Residents submitted several collective complaints to the Korean Environmental Dispute Mediation Commission.
   *Source: Ministry of Environment Korea, 2020; Gwangyang City environmental monitoring*

3. **Industrial accidents and deaths at plants (2014–2022)** — According to the Korean Ministry of Labour, POSCO's plants experienced several dozen serious occupational accidents in the period 2014–2022. In 2022 three workers were killed at the Pohang plant in a process gas explosion. Trade unions accused the company of cutting corners on safety.
   *Source: Korean Ministry of Employment and Labor, 2022; KCTU (Korean Confederation of Trade Unions)*

## 2. Has the corporation destroyed ecosystems and biodiversity?

**Answer: YES**. The Odisha project threatened mangrove forests, and industrial activity in South Korea has damaged coastal and marine ecosystems.

1. **Threat to mangrove forests in Odisha, India (2005–2017)** — The planned construction of a POSCO steel plant in Jagatsinghpur (Odisha) threatened several thousand hectares of mangrove forests in the Mahanadi River delta. Mangrove ecosystems are spawning grounds for more than 200 fish species and critical habitat for sea turtles. Environmentalists warned of a threat to the livelihoods of tens of thousands of fishing families.
   *Source: WWF India, Mangrove Assessment Report 2010; Greenpeace India*

2. **Pollution of Yongil Bay near Pohang (1990–2023)** — Decades of industrial water discharges into Yongil Bay have led to accumulation of heavy metals (zinc, lead, cadmium) in bottom sediments. Korean marine biologists recorded a reduction in biodiversity in the coastal zone: the disappearance of several bivalve mollusc species and a significant decline in marine organism numbers.
   *Source: KIOST (Korea Institute of Ocean Science and Technology), 2018; Pohang City environmental audit*

3. **Industrial pollution of the Hyeonsan River in Pohang (2000–2020)** — The Hyeonsan River, which flows near the POSCO complex, was subject to regular discharges of contaminated water from the plants. In 2017 the Korean National Institute of Environmental Research recorded critically high iron and manganese levels in the river water downstream from the plant.
   *Source: National Institute of Environmental Research Korea, 2017*

## 3. Has the corporation violated the rights of indigenous peoples and local communities?

**Answer: YES**. The Odisha project became one of the most documented cases of indigenous peoples' rights violations in the Asian mining industry.

1. **12 years of resistance in Jagatsinghpur, Odisha (2005–2017)** — POSCO planned to build a 12-million-tonne steel plant on land inhabited by fishing and farming communities without obtaining their consent. Local residents formed the POSCO Pratirodh Sangram Samiti (POSCO Resistance Struggle Committee) and for 12 years blocked entry to their lands. In 2017 POSCO was forced to formally abandon the project.
   *Source: Global Witness, "POSCO's Odisha disaster", 2017; Amnesty International*

2. **Police repression against protesters (2011–2013)** — During the dispersal of anti-POSCO protests in Odisha in 2011–2013, police used tear gas and batons against peaceful demonstrators. Several activists were arrested on fabricated charges. Human Rights Watch documented at least 20 cases of arbitrary detention.
   *Source: Human Rights Watch, 2012; Frontline Defenders*

3. **Destruction of fishing communities' livelihoods** — More than 22,000 residents of coastal villages dependent on fishing in the Jagatsinghpur area reported threats from individuals connected to POSCO and pressure to sign resettlement consent forms. Families who refused to leave their land were denied government services.
   *Source: Actionaid India, 2013; UN Committee on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights*

## 4. Has the corporation systematically violated environmental legislation?

**Answer: YES**. Violations of emission standards and waste management requirements have been documented by Korean regulators over many years.

1. **Emission standard violations at the Pohang plant (2010–2022)** — The Korean Ministry of Environment repeatedly recorded exceedances of permissible SO₂ and NOₓ emissions at POSCO's Pohang facilities. In 2017 the plant received a large administrative fine for exceeding particulate emission limits over several consecutive weeks.
   *Source: Ministry of Environment Korea, Enforcement Reports 2017, 2020*

2. **Unlawful industrial water discharges in Gwangyang (2018)** — In 2018 regulators established that POSCO had unlawfully discharged industrial water with elevated zinc and iron levels into the Gwangyang coastal zone. The company paid a fine, but environmental organisations demanded stricter penalties.
   *Source: Gwangyang Environmental Agency, 2018; Yonhap News Agency*

3. **Permit violations at coal mines in Australia (2015–2020)** — POSCO's coal assets in Queensland (Australia) underwent regulatory inspections in connection with exceedances of dust pollution standards and violations of mine water management requirements. In 2019 the Australian regulator issued an official order to suspend part of the operations.
   *Source: Queensland Department of Environment and Science, 2019*

## 5. Has the corporation concealed information or misled the public?

**Answer: YES**. POSCO manipulated environmental reporting and concealed the real scale of health impacts on the population.

1. **Concealment of dioxin emission data (2012–2018)** — Korean environmental activists conducting independent measurements near the Pohang plant found substantial discrepancies with POSCO's official data. The company disputed the results, funding counter-studies. Only after parliamentary hearings in 2019 did the Ministry of Environment acknowledge that the data had been understated.
   *Source: Korea Environment Movement (KFEM), 2018; Korean National Assembly hearings*

2. **Disinformation about the Odisha project (2005–2017)** — Throughout the entire project period, POSCO actively claimed in international investment reports that dialogue with local communities was proceeding successfully, while in practice thousands of people were openly rejecting the project. Global investors (including Norway's Government Pension Fund) excluded POSCO from their portfolios because of the violations.
   *Source: Norges Bank Investment Management, exclusion notice 2015; Global Witness*

3. **Manipulation of climate reporting (2018–2022)** — Analysis by independent researchers (Carbon Tracker) found that POSCO's carbon footprint calculation methodology allowed understating real emissions through incomplete accounting of indirect emissions (Scope 3). The company declared decarbonisation targets without binding interim indicators.
   *Source: Carbon Tracker, "Steel Transition", 2022*

## 6. Has the corporation opposed regulation and climate policy?

**Answer: YES**. POSCO has actively lobbied against climate restrictions both within South Korea and internationally.

1. **Lobbying against introduction of a carbon tax in Korea (2018–2023)** — POSCO, through the Federation of Korean Industries (FKI), actively opposed the expansion of the emissions trading system (K-ETS) and an increase in carbon credit prices. Korean NGOs accused the company of blocking ambitious climate policy.
   *Source: Korea Green Foundation, 2021; Climate Action Tracker Korea*

2. **Opposition to tightening air quality standards (2015–2022)** — Industry associations with POSCO's participation insisted on weakening proposed PM2.5 standards for industrial enterprises, citing economic costs. As a result, several rounds of regulatory reform were delayed.
   *Source: Korea Federation for Environmental Movements, 2020; Ministry of Environment Korea*

3. **Delaying the transition to hydrogen metallurgy (2020–2023)** — Despite announcing the HyREX (hydrogen reduction) plan in 2021, independent analysts noted that real investment in the technology significantly lagged behind the declared ambitions. Critics pointed out that the "green" announcements served as a greenwashing tool to attract ESG investors.
   *Source: Reclaim Finance, 2022; SBTi analysis of POSCO targets*

## 7. Has the corporation violated workers' rights?

**Answer: YES**. The use of contracted labour and unequal treatment of workers has been documented by trade unions and human rights organisations.

1. **Discrimination against contracted workers (2015–2022)** — Thousands of contracted workers at POSCO's plants received significantly lower pay than permanent employees for performing equivalent functions. A Korean court in 2010 found certain POSCO contracting arrangements to be effectively disguised employment relationships and ordered the company to hire the workers directly, but enforcement of the ruling was delayed.
   *Source: Korean Supreme Court ruling 2010; KCTU, 2018*

2. **Suppression of trade union activity (2010–2020)** — For decades POSCO maintained "union-free" status, obstructing workers' organisation. The International Labour Organization (ILO) received complaints from Korean trade union federations about systematic violations of the right to freedom of association.
   *Source: ILO Committee on Freedom of Association, 2016; IndustriALL*

3. **Inadequate protection against occupational injuries (2018–2022)** — According to Korean Ministry of Labour statistics, the occupational injury rate at POSCO's enterprises exceeded the industry average. After the "Chunghon Law" on criminal liability of management for workplace deaths came into force (2022), POSCO came under investigative scrutiny.
   *Source: Ministry of Employment and Labor Korea, 2022*

## 8. Is the corporation a significant greenhouse gas emitter?

**Answer: YES**. POSCO is one of the world's largest corporate sources of CO₂.

1. **Largest industrial CO₂ emitter in South Korea** — According to the Korean emission registry, POSCO annually emits approximately 75–80 million tonnes of CO₂ equivalent — more than any other corporation in the country. This corresponds to approximately 10–12% of South Korea's total greenhouse gas emissions.
   *Source: GHG Inventory Korea, 2022; Climate Action Tracker*

2. **Emission intensity above the industry average** — Specific CO₂ emissions per tonne of steel at POSCO's plants exceed the average for developed countries. The WorldSteel organisation noted that POSCO is showing slower progress in reducing carbon intensity compared with European competitors.
   *Source: WorldSteel Association, Sustainability Indicators 2022*

3. **HyREX plans: insufficient pace of decarbonisation** — POSCO's announced plan for transitioning to hydrogen-based steelmaking foresees climate neutrality only by 2050. SBTi analysis showed that these targets are not compatible with the 1.5°C trajectory. Before 2030 POSCO does not commit to reducing emissions by more than 10% from 2017 levels.
   *Source: Science Based Targets initiative, 2023; Carbon Tracker POSCO profile*

## Summary

1. POSCO's plants in Pohang — South Korea's largest industrial city — produce dioxin, PM2.5, and SO₂ emissions, creating elevated risks of oncological and respiratory diseases for the population.
2. The twelve-year standoff in Odisha became a symbol of corporate pressure on indigenous peoples: POSCO abandoned the project only after international pressure made it unviable.
3. Korean courts recognised POSCO violations in the area of labour relations — in particular, the disguising of actual employment relationships as contracting.
4. POSCO is South Korea's largest corporate CO₂ emitter — approximately 75–80 million tonnes per year, representing 10–12% of the country's total emissions.
5. Pollution of Yongil Bay near Pohang has led to heavy metal accumulation in bottom sediments and a reduction in marine biodiversity.
6. Norway's Government Pension Fund excluded POSCO from its portfolio because of documented human rights violations in Odisha.
7. The company's decarbonisation plans have been found by independent analysts to be incompatible with the 1.5°C targets of the Paris Agreement.
8. Systematic concealment of dioxin emission data and manipulation of climate reporting indicate deliberate misleading of the public.
