Procter & Gamble — one of the world's largest consumer goods manufacturers — annually consumes hundreds of thousands of tonnes of palm oil. In 2020 a Rainforest Action Network investigation found that key P&G suppliers in Indonesia continued clearing forests on peatlands despite the company's declared "zero deforestation" policy.

Pampers disposable nappies are one of the world's largest sources of household solid waste. Around 20 billion nappies are discarded annually in the US alone, occupying approximately 3.5% of all landfill volume. A single nappy takes 200–500 years to decompose.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. P&G's products and its supply chains have been linked to violations of indigenous peoples' rights, exposure of consumers to toxic chemicals, and exploitation of vulnerable communities.

1. **PFAS in laundry detergents (2021–2023)** — Research by the Silent Spring Institute and other independent laboratories detected per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS, "forever chemicals") in Tide and Bounce products. PFAS are linked to cancer, immune system disorders, and reproductive health problems. The US National Institutes of Health (NIH) recognises them as a serious threat to human health.
   *Source: Silent Spring Institute — "PFAS in Consumer Products"; Environmental Working Group (EWG)*

2. **Violation of indigenous peoples' rights through supplier Korindo (Papua New Guinea)** — Investigations by Mighty Earth and the Rainforest Action Network documented that Korindo — a palm oil supplier for P&G — was destroying indigenous peoples' lands in Papua New Guinea without their free, prior, and informed consent. Communities were losing food sources and traditional territories.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network — "The Procter & Gamble Deforestation Report"; Mighty Earth*

3. **Chemicals in Pampers nappies (2019–2022)** — The French Agency for Food, Environmental and Occupational Health & Safety (ANSES) found dioxins, pesticides, and aromatic hydrocarbons in Pampers nappies. The agency recognised the existence of a health risk for newborns from prolonged exposure. P&G disputed the findings, but inspections followed in several European countries.
   *Source: ANSES — "Évaluation des risques liés à la présence de substances chimiques dans les couches"; Le Monde*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. P&G and its suppliers' activities were directly linked to large-scale tropical forest destruction, water body pollution, and accumulation of non-degradable waste.

1. **Tropical forest clearance through Korindo in Indonesia and Papua (2010s–2020)** — A Rainforest Action Network investigation established that Korindo destroyed more than 30,000 hectares of primary tropical forest in Indonesia and Papua New Guinea to create palm oil plantations. P&G was one of Korindo's largest buyers. Habitats of orangutans, birds of paradise, and other endangered species were destroyed.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network — "The Procter & Gamble Deforestation Report" (2020)*

2. **Pollution of the Ohio River from P&G plant chemical effluents (2006)** — A P&G plant in Ivorydale (Ohio) discharged industrial effluent into the Great Miami River — a tributary of the Ohio. The discharge contained surfactants used in the production of Tide and Ariel. The US Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) recorded violations of water quality standards.
   *Source: US EPA — Clean Water Act files; Cincinnati Enquirer*

3. **20 billion Pampers nappies in US landfills annually** — According to EPA data, disposable nappies account for approximately 3.5% of municipal solid waste. P&G as the largest producer (Pampers holds ~30% of the US market) bears disproportionate responsibility for this accumulation of non-degradable waste. Each nappy takes 200–500 years to decompose.
   *Source: US EPA — Municipal Solid Waste data; NRDC*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. P&G's advertising claims have been repeatedly found misleading by regulators and independent organisations.

1. **UK ban on Fairy "kind to wildlife" advertisement (2022)** — The UK Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) banned a television advertisement for Fairy in which the washing-up liquid was described as "kind to wildlife". The ASA ruled that the claim was misleading to consumers because the product contains chemicals harmful to aquatic organisms when it enters water bodies.
   *Source: ASA — Ruling on Procter & Gamble (Fairy), September 2022*

2. **ClientEarth complaint about RSPO green marketing (2021)** — Environmental NGO ClientEarth filed a complaint with the European Commission against several companies, including P&G, for using RSPO certification as evidence of sustainable palm oil production. ClientEarth noted that P&G's RSPO-certified suppliers continued to clear forests and peatlands, making marketing claims about "responsible" palm oil misleading.
   *Source: ClientEarth — "Palm oil: why RSPO certification isn't enough"; The Guardian*

3. **Challenges to the Ariel "cold wash saves the planet" campaign (2023)** — Several European consumer organisations and NGOs questioned the Ariel campaign claiming that washing at low temperatures with their detergent makes the consumer a "climate protector". Critics pointed out that P&G does not disclose the full carbon footprint of the detergent's production and packaging, making the overall climate benefit claim unreliable.
   *Source: Changing Markets Foundation — "Talking Trash" report; European Consumer Organisation (BEUC)*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. P&G actively promotes its sustainability programme "Ambition 2030" through annual reports, NGO partnerships, and public statements by management.

1. **"Ambition 2030" programme** — P&G publicly declared targets to achieve carbon neutrality at manufacturing sites by 2030, transition to 100% renewable electricity, reduce primary plastic use, and incorporate 50% recycled plastic in packaging. The programme is widely covered in press releases and at investor conferences.
   *Source: P&G — "2023 Citizenship Report"; P&G Investor Relations*

2. **TerraCycle partnership for recycling hard-to-process packaging** — P&G announced a programme for recycling Pantene, Oral-B, and other brand packaging through the TerraCycle platform. The programme is promoted on social media and product packaging as confirmation of a "circular" approach to packaging.
   *Source: P&G — press releases on TerraCycle partnership; TerraCycle*

3. **Annual ESG/Citizenship Report** — P&G annually publishes a detailed corporate citizenship report covering emissions, water, waste, and human rights indicators. The report is accompanied by a social media campaign and is a key ESG communication tool for investors and consumers.
   *Source: P&G — "Citizenship Report 2023"*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. P&G regularly receives high ratings in leading ESG indices.

1. **Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI) — World and North America** — P&G is consistently included in the DJSI World and DJSI North America indices, which S&P Global forms based on sustainability assessments of the world's largest companies. Inclusion in DJSI World means being in the top 10% of companies in an industry by ESG indicators.
   *Source: S&P Global — Dow Jones Sustainability Indices*

2. **CDP "A" rating for climate** — P&G has received an "A" rating from CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project) for transparency and ambition of climate targets. This is the highest CDP rating level, assigned to fewer than 2% of disclosing companies.
   *Source: CDP — Annual Scores; P&G Citizenship Report*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Several P&G products carry environmental labelling contested by independent organisations.

1. **RSPO logo on products containing palm oil** — P&G uses the RSPO (Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil) logo as evidence of responsible sourcing. However, investigations by RAN and Mighty Earth showed that the company's RSPO-certified suppliers continued to clear forests. Greenpeace called RSPO "an indulgence for deforestation".
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network; Greenpeace — "Certifying Destruction"*

2. **Tide labelling "recyclable bottle" when actual recycling rates are below 30%** — Tide bottles are labelled "recyclable", but according to EPA data the actual recycling rate of HDPE plastic bottles in the US is less than 30%. Consumer protection organisations point out that the labelling creates a false impression about the actual fate of the packaging.
   *Source: US EPA — Plastics recycling data; Changing Markets Foundation*

3. **Pampers "Plant-based materials" labelling while petrochemical plastic remains the main component** — P&G launched the Pampers Pure line with an emphasis on "plant-based materials" and "chlorine-free". Yet the nappy's core is still made from petrochemical polyester and polyethylene. Independent composition analyses showed that the share of bio-based materials is minimal.
   *Source: NRDC — Diaper analysis reports; Environmental Working Group*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. P&G's environmental initiatives are disproportionately small relative to the scale of pollution created.

1. **Fairy and Gillette bottles from 10% ocean plastic** — P&G released limited editions of Fairy bottles and Gillette razor handles incorporating 10% recycled plastic collected from coastal zones. The initiative received wide media coverage, but these editions represented a negligible fraction of P&G's total plastic packaging output (over 500,000 tonnes of plastic per year).
   *Source: P&G press releases; Greenpeace — comment on the initiative*

2. **Ariel "cold wash" campaign** — P&G invested in a large-scale advertising campaign urging consumers to wash at 30°C, presenting this as a contribution to fighting climate change. However, the production, packaging, and logistics of Ariel detergent itself generates significantly more carbon than is saved through consumers lowering their washing temperature.
   *Source: Changing Markets Foundation — "Talking Trash" (2021)*

3. **Forest restoration initiative targeting 50 million acres by 2030** — P&G announced partnerships with forest restoration organisations as compensation for its suppliers' activities. However, according to Rainforest Action Network estimates, the pace of deforestation in P&G's supply chain far exceeds the volumes of declared restoration.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network — "Bad Bargain" report; P&G Citizenship Report 2023*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. P&G is a member of industry associations that lobby against mandatory plastic regulation, supporting voluntary initiatives in place of legislative standards.

1. **American Chemistry Council (ACC) membership and lobbying against EPR** — P&G is a member of the American Chemistry Council (ACC), which actively lobbies against mandatory extended producer responsibility (EPR) legislation for plastic waste. ACC funds campaigns promoting recycling as an alternative to reducing plastic production.
   *Source: InfluenceMap — "Plastics & Climate: Industry Lobbying"; DeSmog*

2. **Support for voluntary "Plastic Pact" instead of mandatory standards** — P&G is a signatory of voluntary initiatives such as the Ellen MacArthur Foundation's New Plastics Economy Global Commitment, while simultaneously opposing mandatory government standards for plastic reduction in the EU and US. Critics note that participation in voluntary pacts is used as an argument against strict regulation.
   *Source: Ellen MacArthur Foundation — Global Commitment reports; InfluenceMap*

3. **US Congressional lobbying on PFAS regulation (2022–2025)** — According to OpenSecrets data, P&G spent $3–5 million per year on federal lobbying, including on PFAS regulation, packaging standards, and trade policy. The company engaged with Congress through its own lobbyists and industry associations.
   *Source: OpenSecrets — P&G lobbying data; Senate Lobbying Disclosure*
