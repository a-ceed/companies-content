On Bougainville (Papua New Guinea) Rio Tinto's Panguna copper mine caused an environmental catastrophe and an armed conflict that claimed 15,000 lives (1988–1998). Tailings dams were discharging 130,000 tonnes of waste per day into the Jaba River, completely destroying the valley's ecosystem.

In Australia the ERA uranium mine in Kakadu National Park (Northern Territory) suffered thousands of radioactive wastewater leaks into the soil and waterways during its operation. In 2013 there was a major spill of 1 million litres of acid solution containing uranium.

In Madagascar, ilmenite mining destroyed sections of unique coastal forest — habitat for endemic lemurs and chameleons. Local fishermen recorded a sharp decline in catches in adjacent waters due to changes in hydrology.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Rio Tinto's activities are directly linked to loss of life and mass harm to health. The armed conflict on Bougainville, triggered in part by the Panguna mine, claimed 15,000 lives. The destruction of the Juukan Gorge caves inflicted severe cultural and psychological trauma on Australia's indigenous peoples. Radioactive leaks in Kakadu threaten the health of Aboriginal people and the park's ecosystem.

1. **The Bougainville war and the Panguna mine (1988–1998)** — The environmental damage from the Panguna mine (discharging 130,000 tonnes of tailings per day into the Jaba River) triggered an uprising by the local population. The ensuing armed conflict claimed around 15,000 lives. Rio Tinto abandoned the mine in 1989 without remedying the contamination.
   *Source: Human Rights Watch, "Papua New Guinea: Bougainville Mine Legacy", 2000*

2. **Destruction of the sacred Juukan Gorge caves (2020)** — Rio Tinto blew up 46,000-year-old caves of the Martu Aboriginal people in Western Australia in order to expand an iron ore mine. An Australian parliamentary commission found this to be "one of the most devastating acts of destruction of cultural heritage". Three senior executives resigned under public pressure.
   *Source: Australian Parliament, Joint Standing Committee on Northern Australia, 2020*

3. **Radioactive leaks in Kakadu, Australia (1979–2021)** — The ERA Ranger uranium mine suffered more than 200 documented radioactive water leaks. The Mirarr Aboriginal people, whose sacred lands surround the mine, recorded elevated concentrations of uranium and radium in water bodies used for drinking and ritual practices.
   *Source: Australian Conservation Foundation, "Ranger Uranium Mine Incidents", 2020*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Rio Tinto is implicated in several large-scale environmental catastrophes on multiple continents: destruction of Bougainville's river ecosystem, decades of radioactive contamination in Kakadu, and destruction of Madagascar's coastal forests.

1. **Destruction of the Jaba River, Papua New Guinea (1972–1989)** — The Panguna mine discharged 130,000 tonnes of tailings per day into the Jaba River, completely destroying the valley's aquatic ecosystem. Ecologists established that river recovery would take centuries even if pollution ceased immediately. The discharge continued for 17 years.
   *Source: MiningWatch Canada, "Panguna Mine Legacy Assessment", 2003*

2. **Spill of 1 million litres of uranium-containing solution, Kakadu (2013)** — A pipeline failure at the ERA Ranger mine released 1 million litres of acid solution containing uranium into Kakadu National Park. The contamination affected the soil and groundwater of a UNESCO World Heritage site.
   *Source: Australian Radiation Protection and Nuclear Safety Agency (ARPANSA), report 2013*

3. **Destruction of Madagascar's coastal forests (2005 — present)** — Ilmenite mining at the QMM project (Rio Tinto 80%) destroyed hundreds of hectares of the unique Littoral coastal forest — one of the most threatened ecosystems on the planet, and habitat of endemic lemur and chameleon species.
   *Source: WWF Madagascar, "QMM Impact Assessment", 2016*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Rio Tinto actively uses climate rhetoric and sustainability commitments while continuing destructive mining and opposing stringent climate regulation.

1. **"Green" repositioning after Juukan Gorge (2020–2022)** — After the international scandal over the cave destruction, Rio Tinto announced a "transformation of corporate culture" and commitment to indigenous peoples' rights. However, independent monitoring organisations documented the continuation of minimal-compliance practices on community consultation at other sites.
   *Source: Australasian Centre for Corporate Responsibility, "Rio Tinto Cultural Heritage", 2022*

2. **Declaration of climate neutrality while lobbying for coal (2019–2021)** — Rio Tinto announced a target of net zero emissions by 2050, while simultaneously participating through industry associations in lobbying against the introduction of a carbon price in Australia.
   *Source: InfluenceMap, "Rio Tinto Climate Lobbying Scorecard", 2021*

3. **"Sustainable aluminium" marketing while ignoring devastation in Guinea (2020–2023)** — Rio Tinto's Alcan division promoted aluminium as a "low-carbon metal for the green transition". At the same time bauxite mining in Guinea was accompanied by tropical forest destruction and village resettlement without proper compensation.
   *Source: Inclusive Development International, "Rio Tinto Guinea Bauxite", 2021*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Rio Tinto is among the most active participants in ESG communications in the mining industry, publishing voluminous sustainability reports and participating in international climate initiatives.

1. **Annual Sustainability Reports (since 2001)** — Rio Tinto annually publishes Global Reporting Initiative (GRI) reports with detailed environmental performance data. After the Juukan Gorge scandal, the 2020 report included an extensive section on "cultural heritage", but independent auditors noted the absence of verifiable commitments.
   *Source: Rio Tinto Sustainability Report 2022; criticism from Earthjustice*

2. **TCFD participation (2019 — present)** — Rio Tinto discloses climate risks in accordance with Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures recommendations. The company positions this as "leadership in climate transparency", although disclosure of Scope 3 (product use emissions) remains minimal.
   *Source: Rio Tinto, Climate Change Report 2022*

3. **IUCN biodiversity partnership (2010s — present)** — Rio Tinto acts as a corporate partner of the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN), funding specific species conservation projects. Environmentalists criticise this partnership as "reputation laundering", pointing to the continuing destruction of biodiversity at active mines.
   *Source: Global Witness, "Corporate Conservation Partnerships", 2020*

---

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Rio Tinto has repeatedly received high ratings in corporate ESG indices, some of them coinciding in time with major environmental incidents.

1. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (2015–2019)** — Rio Tinto was included in the DJSI for several years, including a period when the company was receiving criticism for its activities in Kakadu and Madagascar. Index inclusion allowed the company to position itself as "sustainable" to institutional investors.
   *Source: S&P Global, DJSI Mining Sector Results, 2015–2019*

2. **MSCI ESG rating — "A" (2020)** — Despite the destruction of the Juukan Gorge caves in May 2020, MSCI maintained a high ESG rating for Rio Tinto for several months after the incident, triggering sharp criticism of rating agency methodology.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings; criticism from Responsible Investment Association Australasia, 2020*

3. **CDP "A-List" award for climate transparency (2018)** — The CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project) included Rio Tinto in its list of companies with the highest level of climate transparency. A year later it emerged that the company had been participating in lobbying against climate legislation through industry associations.
   *Source: CDP Corporate Climate Scores, 2018; InfluenceMap analysis, 2019*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Rio Tinto promotes aluminium, copper, lithium, and other metals as "green" materials necessary for the energy transition, without disclosing the environmental consequences of their extraction in marketing materials.

1. **Marketing of Rio Tinto aluminium as "sustainable" (2020–2023)** — The Rio Tinto Alcan division actively promotes aluminium under the slogan "metal of the green future", pointing to its infinite recyclability. At the same time primary aluminium production from bauxite remains one of the most energy-intensive and destructive extraction processes.
   *Source: Rio Tinto Aluminium, marketing materials 2022*

2. **Promotion of Jadar lithium as a "battery material" (2021–2022)** — Rio Tinto promoted the Jadar project in Serbia as a source of "clean lithium for electric vehicles", triggering large-scale protests by local residents and environmental organisations. The project was temporarily frozen by the Serbian government under public pressure.
   *Source: Reuters, "Serbia Revokes Rio Tinto Jadar Lithium Licence", 2022*

3. **"ResponsibleSteel" certificate for steel raw material (2021)** — Rio Tinto sought certification of its iron ore under the ResponsibleSteel standard, which would have allowed it to be labelled as "responsibly sourced". Critics pointed out that the standard does not account for cultural heritage impacts — precisely the aspect for which Rio Tinto has faced the greatest criticism.
   *Source: Australasian Centre for Corporate Responsibility, 2021*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Rio Tinto regularly conducts environmental initiatives that are incommensurable in scale with the damage from mining at its sites.

1. **Mangrove restoration programme in Madagascar (2010–2020)** — Rio Tinto funded mangrove planting near the QMM project in Madagascar. A few hundred hectares of mangroves were restored, while ilmenite mining had destroyed significantly larger areas of the unique Littoral forest — an ecosystem that takes centuries to recover.
   *Source: Rio Tinto QMM, Environmental Report 2018*

2. **"Zero Waste Day" at Pilbara mines (annually)** — Rio Tinto holds annual waste separation events at its Pilbara mining sites and publishes reports on reductions in domestic waste volumes. Meanwhile the annual volume of mining waste (tailings) from the same mines runs to millions of tonnes.
   *Source: Rio Tinto, Sustainability Report 2021*

3. **Sponsorship of a children's nature drawing competition in Australia (2019)** — Rio Tinto sponsored an all-Australia children's drawing competition on the theme of "The nature we love". The event was held in the year in which preparations were underway for the Juukan Gorge explosion.
   *Source: Rio Tinto, Community Engagement Report 2019*

---

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Rio Tinto has actively participated in shaping the climate and environmental agenda through industry associations, often promoting narratives that delay real change.

1. **Minerals Council of Australia membership — lobbying against a carbon price (2011–2014)** — Rio Tinto funded the Minerals Council's campaign against the introduction of a carbon tax in Australia, while publicly declaring "climate responsibility". The tax was introduced, then repealed following large-scale industry lobbying.
   *Source: InfluenceMap, "Australian Mining Industry Climate Lobbying", 2019*

2. **Promoting "nuclear renaissance" as a climate solution (2020–2023)** — Rio Tinto, as a uranium producer, actively participated in promoting the narrative that "nuclear energy is the key climate solution". Critics pointed out the obvious conflict of interest: the company is lobbying for technologies that increase demand for its own product.
   *Source: Australian Conservation Foundation, "Nuclear Lobby and Mining Interests", 2022*

3. **Funding the "carbon capture technology" narrative (2017–2021)** — Rio Tinto participated in funding research consortia promoting CCS technologies as an alternative to immediate emissions reductions. This allowed the company to postpone real changes to its business model by relying on uncertain technological prospects.
   *Source: Oil Change International, "CCS Lobbying by Mining Sector", 2020*

## Summary

1. Rio Tinto is indirectly implicated in the deaths of approximately 15,000 people during the armed conflict on Bougainville, triggered in part by the environmental damage from the Panguna mine.
2. The destruction of the 46,000-year-old sacred Juukan Gorge caves in 2020 became one of the most high-profile acts of cultural heritage destruction in the history of the mining industry.
3. The ERA Ranger uranium mine in Kakadu suffered more than 200 radioactive leaks, threatening the health of Aboriginal people and the ecosystem of a World Heritage site.
4. Ilmenite mining in Madagascar destroyed sections of the unique Littoral forest — one of the world's most threatened ecosystems.
5. Rio Tinto received high ESG ratings (DJSI, MSCI "A") during periods of active environmental violations, pointing to systemic flaws in ESG methodologies.
6. Lobbying against carbon pricing through the Minerals Council of Australia contradicted the company's public climate commitments.
7. The Jadar lithium mining project in Serbia was frozen under citizen pressure, despite active "green" marketing by the company.
8. Rio Tinto's restoration and philanthropic programmes are systematically incommensurable in scale with the harm caused.
