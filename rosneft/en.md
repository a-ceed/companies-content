According to WWF and Russia's Federal Environmental Supervision Agency (Rosprirodnadzor), Russia is the world leader in the number of onshore oil spills: more than 20,000 incidents are recorded annually. Rosneft — Russia's largest oil company — bears responsibility for a significant share of them.

In the Khanty-Mansiysk Autonomous Region — the oil heartland of Russia — ageing pipeline infrastructure (some pipes unchanged since Soviet times) regularly ruptures. According to Greenpeace Russia estimates, hundreds of thousands of tonnes of oil ended up in Siberian swamps, rivers, and tundra in 2014–2016 alone.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Accidents and fires at Rosneft facilities have cost workers their lives, and contamination of water and soil has caused long-term health damage to indigenous peoples of Siberia.

1. **Fire at the Vankor oil field (2014)** — A pipeline rupture and subsequent fire at Rosneft's Vankor field (Krasnoyarsk Region) killed 4 shift workers. Russia's Ministry of Emergency Situations fought the blaze for two days.
   *Source: Russian Ministry of Emergency Situations — "Information on the Vankor accident" (2014)*

2. **Drinking water contamination in Khanty-Mansiysk — threat to indigenous peoples (2014–2018)** — Oil spills from RN-Yuganskneftegaz (a Rosneft subsidiary) pipelines in the Ob and Irtysh river basins contaminated drinking water sources of Khanty and Mansi communities. Russia's Federal Service for Consumer Rights Protection (Rospotrebnadzor) recorded petroleum product concentrations in drinking water at some settlements up to 30 times the maximum permissible level. Cancer rates in the Khanty-Mansiysk region are statistically higher than the Russian national average.
   *Source: Greenpeace Russia — "Oil in Siberian rivers" (2018); WWF Russia — "Oil pollution in Khanty-Mansiysk" (2017)*

3. **Explosion at the Tuapse refinery (2015)** — A fire and explosion at Rosneft's Tuapse oil refinery injured 6 people (2 fatalities). Russia's Federal Industrial and Environmental Supervision Service (Rostekhnadzor) found violations of equipment maintenance procedures.
   *Source: Rostekhnadzor — "Investigation of the Tuapse refinery accident" (2015)*

4. **The Yukos case and associated social harm (2003–2007)** — The criminal prosecution of Yukos, organised with active state participation and ending in the company's absorption by Rosneft, cost thousands of people their jobs, destroyed the pension savings of small shareholders, and triggered a flight of capital from Russia.
   *Source: ECHR — "Khodorkovsky v. Russia", rulings of 2011 and 2013*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Rosneft bears responsibility for some of Russia's largest onshore oil pollution incidents.

1. **Chronic spills by RN-Yuganskneftegaz in Khanty-Mansiysk (2011–2019)** — Greenpeace Russia documented more than 800 individual oil spills only at RN-Yuganskneftegaz sites in 2016–2018. The total volume of leaks amounted to tens of thousands of tonnes. Contamination covered swamps and river floodplains of hundreds of square kilometres, part of which is a protected area.
   *Source: Greenpeace Russia — "Oil pollution in Khanty-Mansiysk" (2019)*

2. **Oil spill on the Kolva River, Nenets Autonomous Region (2021)** — In May 2021 a spill from a Rosneft subsidiary (Usinsk Oil) polluted the Kolva River. Rosprirodnadzor data indicated several hundred tonnes of oil entered the watercourse. The oil slick spread 30 km downstream.
   *Source: Rosprirodnadzor — "Results of the Kolva inspection" (2021)*

3. **Spills in Samara Region (2019)** — A series of Samaraneftegaz (Rosneft) pipeline ruptures contaminated agricultural land and streams of the Samara Bend. Rosprirodnadzor estimated damages at over 280 million roubles. A significant share of the oil reached the Volga.
   *Source: Rosprirodnadzor — "Spill data for Samara Region" (2019)*

4. **Arctic drilling risks: Rosneft–ExxonMobil project in the Kara Sea (2014)** — In the only exploratory well drilled before US sanctions were imposed, Rosneft and ExxonMobil found oil. Drilling near Arctic ice without an adequate spill response plan was declared environmentally irresponsible by WWF, Oil Change International, and the Bellona Foundation.
   *Source: WWF International — "Arctic Oil Spill Risk Assessment" (2014)*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Rosneft declared climate targets and environmental responsibility while simultaneously expanding production and concealing the scale of pollution.

1. **Underreporting spill volumes in official reporting (systematic)** — Greenpeace Russia and WWF repeatedly documented discrepancies between Rosneft's official spill data and the results of independent inspections. According to Greenpeace, officially reported volumes are understated by a factor of 3–10. In 2019 an investigation by Novaya Gazeta uncovered falsifications in remediation acts for contaminated sites.
   *Source: Greenpeace Russia — "Data gap: official vs. actual spills" (2019)*

2. **"Decarbonisation strategy" declaration with record production (2021)** — Rosneft published its "Rosneft-2030" strategy with a decarbonisation section: promising to reduce carbon emission intensity by 30% by 2035, while planning to increase production from 5.2 to 6.2 million barrels per day. Carbon Tracker described this strategy as incompatible with the Paris Agreement.
   *Source: Carbon Tracker — "Rosneft: stranded asset risk" (2022)*

3. **"Responsible Arctic operator" image (2015–2020)** — In public communications Rosneft positioned itself as an "environmentally responsible" Arctic operator, publishing photos of polar bears and seals in corporate materials. At the same time the company was pressing for the lifting of the moratorium on drilling in sensitive Arctic zones.
   *Source: Arctic Council Observer Reports (2018); Bellona Foundation — "Rosneft Arctic" (2019)*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Rosneft publishes ESG reports and promotes a corporate image of an environmentally responsible company.

1. **Publication of GRI-standard ESG reports (2019–2022)** — Rosneft issued annual sustainability reports verified by auditor Ernst & Young. Independent analysis showed: despite formal compliance with GRI standards, the climate risk section does not include Scope 3 emissions (indirect emissions from the combustion of extracted fuels), which constitute more than 90% of an oil company's climate footprint.
   *Source: InfluenceMap — "Rosneft ESG disclosure gap" (2022)*

2. **Inclusion in MSCI ESG ratings (2018–2021)** — Rosneft was included in MSCI Emerging Markets indices and received an MSCI ESG rating despite chronic spills and lawsuits. After the invasion of Ukraine (2022) several index providers excluded Rosneft.
   *Source: MSCI — "ESG Ratings: Rosneft" (2021)*

3. **Corporate "Rosneft — Fire-Free Zone" programme (2017–2021)** — Rosneft launched a forest fire protection programme as an ESG communication element, covering it widely in annual reports. Critics noted that the programme's scale is incomparable with the damage from flare gas combustion at the company's facilities.
   *Source: Rosneft — "Annual Report 2021"*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES (limited)**. Rosneft received recognition primarily in Russian corporate ratings.

1. **RSPP "Sustainable Development Leaders" rating (2020)** — Rosneft was included in the Russian Union of Industrialists and Entrepreneurs (RSPP) sustainable development leaders list — a domestic corporate rating not recognised by international investors as independent.
   *Source: RSPP — "Sustainable Development Leaders 2020"*

2. **Moscow Exchange Sustainability Index (2018–2021)** — Rosneft was periodically included in the MOEX Sustainability Index, which the company used in ESG communications as evidence of recognition.
   *Source: Moscow Exchange — "MOEX Sustainability Index" (2021)*

---

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Rosneft used green imagery and environmental messages in marketing petroleum products.

1. **Rosneft "Eco-Friendly Choice" motor oil line (2017–2021)** — Rosneft promoted several motor oils with the marketing message of "eco-friendly choice" and "reduced environmental impact", without disclosing specific production footprint indicators. No independent verification of the labelling was conducted.
   *Source: Rosneft — "Lubricants Catalogue" (2019)*

2. **"Green Energy" and "Clean Fuel" branding at petrol stations (2018–2022)** — Rosneft petrol stations displayed banners and fuel labelling with environmental messages, including "improved environmental formula". Environmental organisations noted the absence of certification for these claims.
   *Source: Kommersant — "Oil companies master green marketing" (2019)*

3. **Positioning Euro-5/Euro-6 fuel as "environmentally clean" (2016–2020)** — Rosneft actively promoted Euro-5 standard fuel as "environmentally clean", placing corresponding labels on fuel pumps. Environmental organisations noted that the Euro-5 standard only regulates local exhaust pollutants, not the climate footprint of oil production.
   *Source: WWF Russia — "Green marketing in the oil industry" (2020)*

---

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Rosneft conducted symbolic environmental events used exclusively for PR purposes.

1. **"Save the Polar Bear" campaign (2010–2020)** — Rosneft funded polar bear research and protection programmes together with the Russian Nature Reserve Centre, spending tens of millions of roubles annually. Simultaneously the company was advancing Arctic oil projects threatening key polar bear habitats.
   *Source: WWF Russia — "The polar bear and oil" (2019)*

2. **"Eco-marathon" beach clean-up at Lake Baikal (2016–2019)** — An annual corporate event with several hundred employees collecting litter on the Baikal shoreline was covered as part of the corporate social responsibility programme. The volume of litter collected was approximately 3–5 tonnes.
   *Source: Rosneft — "Sustainability Report 2019"*

3. **Tree-planting in Siberia (annually, 2012–2021)** — Rosneft held annual corporate "forest planting days" in extraction areas in Khanty-Mansiysk and other regions. Reports indicated the planting of up to 100,000 trees per year — a negligible figure compared with the scale of ecosystem degradation from oil pollution.
   *Source: Rosneft — "Sustainability Report 2021"*

---

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Rosneft funded a narrative of Russian oil production safety and lobbied for the relaxation of environmental requirements.

1. **Lobbying against tightening Arctic environmental drilling standards (2014–2022)** — Through industry associations and direct contacts with relevant agencies, Rosneft systematically sought the weakening of environmental requirements for Arctic drilling in Russia. In 2019 Russia's Ministry of Natural Resources relaxed several regulations on spill response in ice conditions following industry lobby pressure.
   *Source: Bankwatch — "Rosneft Arctic lobbying" (2020); Vedomosti — "The Ministry of Natural Resources changes standards" (2019)*

2. **Funding "independent" scientific publications on oil production safety (2015–2020)** — Several Russian academic publications funded through Rosneft-affiliated grants downplayed the environmental damage from chronic small oil spills, describing them as "self-restoring" ecosystem processes. Independent ecologists from Moscow State University published critiques of these works.
   *Source: Ecology and Industry of Russia — "Critique of the oil pollution assessment methodology" (2018)*

3. **"Rosneft — Responsible Arctic Neighbour" campaign (2017–2021)** — As part of corporate communications, Rosneft disseminated materials presenting Arctic oil production as compatible with ecosystem conservation. These materials were distributed in schools in Russia's Arctic regions and at Arctic forums.
   *Source: Arctic Council Observer Documents — forum materials 2018–2020*

## Summary

1. **Mass harm to people?** — **YES** — Fires and explosions at Rosneft facilities have cost workers their lives; chronic oil pollution of rivers in Khanty-Mansiysk has caused long-term health damage to indigenous peoples.
2. **Major environmental disasters?** — **YES** — Thousands of annual oil spills in Western Siberia; contamination of the Ob and Irtysh rivers; Kolva River spills — among the world's largest onshore oil pollution incidents.
3. **Greenwashing?** — **YES** — Systematic underreporting of spill volumes; decarbonisation strategy declared alongside plans to increase production; "responsible Arctic operator" image.
4. **ESG marketing?** — **YES** — GRI-standard reports without Scope 3 emissions; inclusion in MOEX and MSCI indices; corporate environmental programmes.
5. **ESG award wins?** — **YES (limited)** — Inclusion in the RSPP "Sustainable Development Leaders" rating (2020) and MOEX Sustainability Index (2018–2021).
6. **Eco-labels on harmful products?** — **YES** — "Eco-friendly oil" labelling; "clean fuel" banners at petrol stations; Euro-5 positioned as "environmentally clean" without full lifecycle accounting.
7. **Negligible eco-events?** — **YES** — Polar bear protection programme alongside advancement of Arctic projects; Baikal eco-marathons; corporate tree-planting in Siberia.
8. **Lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?** — **YES** — Lobbying to weaken Arctic environmental standards; funding "scientific" publications downplaying spill damage; "responsible Arctic neighbour" campaign for schools.
