At Samsung's South Korean plants, an abnormally high rate of cancer and other serious diseases has been recorded since the 1990s among workers exposed to organic solvents, benzene, and other toxic chemicals in semiconductor manufacturing. The SHARPS organisation documented hundreds of such cases, and in 2018 Samsung issued a formal public apology and established a compensation fund.

Samsung's manufacturing plants in Vietnam (Thai Nguyen province) became sources of groundwater chemical contamination: local farmers reported crop failures and fish kills in adjacent water bodies.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Samsung for decades exposed workers at its semiconductor plants to toxic chemicals, causing mass illness and deaths, while systematically denying the connection.

1. **Leukaemia and cancer in "clean room" workers (1990s–2018)** — According to BBC reporting (2012), at least 70 Samsung plant workers developed leukaemia and other forms of cancer, 22 of whom died. The SHARPS organisation documented hundreds of such cases. Workers — predominantly young women — were exposed to benzene, organic solvents, and other carcinogens in "clean rooms" without adequate protection. Samsung denied any link between the illnesses and working conditions for 11 years.
   *Source: BBC News — "Samsung semiconductor workers and cancer claims" (2012); SHARPS — reports 2007–2018*

2. **Formal apology and compensation fund (2018)** — In November 2018 Samsung Electronics issued a formal public apology to affected workers and their families and established a compensation fund. This acknowledgement came more than 11 years after the first documented complaints. By that point some of those who had fallen ill had already died without receiving compensation.
   *Source: Reuters — "Samsung apologises to workers who fell ill, as it reaches compensation deal" (2018)*

3. **Child labour and violations at Chinese supplier factories (2012–2015)** — Independent China Labor Watch audits at Samsung's Chinese supplier factories found systematic labour law violations: overtime work of up to 100 hours per month, use of child labour, and failure to comply with safety standards. Samsung acknowledged the violations and announced corrective measures, but repeat audits in 2014–2015 found similar problems.
   *Source: China Labor Watch — "Samsung's Supplier Factory Exploiting Child Labor" (2012, 2014)*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Samsung's manufacturing activities are accompanied by chemical contamination of soil and water near plants, and the scale of electronic waste produced represents a global environmental problem.

1. **Groundwater contamination near the Vietnam plant (2014)** — Local farmers in Thai Nguyen province recorded crop failures and fish kills in water bodies adjacent to the Samsung manufacturing complex. Vietnamese media and NGOs reported contamination of groundwater with chemicals from the plant site. Samsung invested significant sums in Thai Nguyen, but did not disclose the nature or scale of the contamination.
   *Source: VietnamNet — publications 2014; reports from local environmental NGOs*

2. **Chemical waste discharge at Samsung SDI in Tianjin (2014)** — An external China Labor Watch audit at Samsung SDI's battery plant in Tianjin found chemical waste being discharged directly into the municipal sewerage system without adequate treatment. Lithium-ion battery production involves the use of toxic solvents and heavy metals.
   *Source: China Labor Watch — "Samsung SDI Factory Audit Report" (2014)*

3. **Electronic waste: 500 million devices per year with a collection rate below 20% (2020s)** — Samsung sells approximately 500 million electronic devices globally each year. According to the Global E-waste Monitor, the rate of official electronics recycling in most countries does not exceed 20%. Samsung declares device take-back programmes, but real coverage remains minimal, and the bulk of e-waste ends up on illegal dumps in Africa and Asia, where toxic substances from circuit boards and batteries contaminate soil and water.
   *Source: Global E-waste Monitor 2020; iFixit — "Samsung's Repair and E-Waste Problem"*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Samsung systematically uses environmental rhetoric and imagery not backed by real results: positioning products as "eco-conscious" with minimal use of recycled materials, and declaring "green" targets without disclosing the full emissions picture.

1. **Galaxy S24 Ultra as "eco-conscious" with minimal recycled content (2024)** — Samsung promotes the Galaxy S24 series with an emphasis on "eco-conscious design", mentioning the use of recycled materials. However, independent analyses show that the share of recycled materials in the finished device remains negligible, and new chip production is extremely energy-intensive and non-decarbonised.
   *Source: Samsung Galaxy S24 Ultra — official press release (2024); iFixit Teardown Report (2024)*

2. **"Carbon neutrality by 2050" declaration with growing Scope 3 emissions (2023)** — In its Sustainability Report 2023 Samsung declares a "Net Zero by 2050" target and refers to reductions in direct emissions. However, Scope 3 emissions — including chip production at third-party fabs, logistics, and consumer product use — grow proportionally to sales volumes and are not fully disclosed in the report.
   *Source: Samsung Sustainability Report 2023; Carbon Tracker Initiative*

3. **"Re:Samsung" campaign covers less than 5% of sold devices (2022–2023)** — Samsung advertises its device take-back and recycling programme "Re:Samsung" as a key element of its environmental strategy. However, the company's own data show that the volume of collected devices amounts to less than 5% of total devices sold in the comparable period. The programme receives extensive press release coverage but has no real impact on the global e-waste stream.
   *Source: Samsung ESG Data Book 2022; Greenpeace — "Guide to Greener Electronics" (2022)*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Samsung actively publishes ESG reports, participates in international sustainability initiatives, and uses the environmental agenda in product marketing.

1. **Annual Sustainability Report and Galaxy for the Planet programme (2021–2024)** — Samsung annually publishes a detailed sustainability report covering emissions targets, waste management, and social responsibility. In 2021 the company launched the "Galaxy for the Planet" programme with targets for recycling and reducing the carbon footprint of the Galaxy product line by 2025.
   *Source: Samsung Sustainability Report 2023; Samsung Newsroom — "Galaxy for the Planet" (2021)*

2. **RE100 target: 100% renewable energy by 2050** — Samsung joined the RE100 initiative, committing to transition all manufacturing facilities to renewable energy sources by 2050 (with interim targets for 2030). The company actively uses this commitment in communications with investors and consumers.
   *Source: RE100 — "Samsung Electronics joins RE100" (2022); Samsung IR materials 2023*

3. **CDP participation and climate data disclosure** — Samsung regularly participates in the CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project) ranking, disclosing emissions and climate risk data. In 2022 the company received a CDP A- rating, which was actively used in press releases as confirmation of environmental responsibility.
   *Source: CDP — Samsung Electronics Climate Disclosure (2022)*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Samsung has repeatedly received recognition in international ESG ratings and corporate governance and sustainability competitions.

1. **Inclusion in DJSI World (2022–2024)** — Samsung Electronics has been included in the Dow Jones Sustainability World Index (DJSI World) for several consecutive years — one of the most authoritative global ESG ratings, covering the top 10% of companies in each industry. DJSI World inclusion is regularly covered in Samsung's corporate communications.
   *Source: S&P Global — DJSI Results 2022, 2023, 2024; Samsung Newsroom*

2. **CDP A- climate rating (2022)** — CDP assigned Samsung an A- rating in the Climate Change category, indicating a high level of climate data disclosure and verified emissions reduction targets. Samsung used this rating in its annual report and investor materials.
   *Source: CDP — "CDP Climate Change Score 2022"*

3. **"Best Corporate Governance in Asia" award (FinanceAsia)** — Samsung Electronics received a FinanceAsia award in the corporate governance category as part of its broader ESG achievements. Corporate governance awards are regularly highlighted by the company as an element of its ESG profile.
   *Source: FinanceAsia — Asia's Best Companies Awards*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Samsung places Energy Star labelling and its own "green" symbols on products with high real energy consumption and a significant environmental footprint.

1. **Energy Star on QLED televisions in "Vivid" mode (2020–2024)** — Samsung QLED series televisions receive Energy Star certification, creating an image of an energy-efficient product among buyers. However, in "Vivid"/"Dynamic" mode — set as the default in stores — energy consumption significantly exceeds the test mode figures. The EU in 2023 tightened television energy labelling requirements precisely because of the discrepancy between test conditions and real-world use.
   *Source: European Commission — Energy Labelling Review 2023; Which? — "TV energy ratings explained"*

2. **"Eco-packaging" labelling while using plastic screen protectors (2021–2023)** — Galaxy S series smartphones are shipped in packaging labelled as "eco" or "sustainable packaging", while the box still contains disposable plastic screen protectors and plastic inserts. iFixit and environmental bloggers documented this discrepancy across several device generations.
   *Source: iFixit — Samsung Galaxy S22 Teardown (2022); The Verge — "Samsung's eco packaging" (2022)*

3. **"Green leaf" logo on home appliances with minimal recycled content** — The Bespoke home appliances line bears Samsung's sustainability badge and references the use of recycled plastic. Independent material composition analysis shows that the share of recycled content in most models does not exceed 5–10% of total product weight, yet the labelling creates an impression of systemic environmental responsibility.
   *Source: Greenpeace — "Guide to Greener Electronics" (2022); Samsung Bespoke product specifications*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Samsung conducts environmental events and programmes whose scale is incommensurable with the volume of waste and emissions produced by the company.

1. **Galaxy Upcycling covers less than 1% of obsolete phones (2021–2024)** — Samsung launched the "Galaxy Upcycling at Home" programme, allowing an old Galaxy smartphone to be converted into a smart device (light sensor, pet monitor, etc.). The programme is positioned as a contribution to the circular economy, but real coverage is less than 1% of the billions of obsolete Samsung phones in the world. Only a limited list of older models is supported.
   *Source: Samsung Newsroom — "Galaxy Upcycling" (2021, 2022); Counterpoint Research — Samsung device sales volumes*

2. **$1 million partnership with Ocean Cleanup against revenues of over $200 billion (2022)** — Samsung announced a partnership with The Ocean Cleanup project for removing plastic from the ocean, allocating approximately $1 million in funding. This amounts to less than 0.0005% of Samsung's annual revenue. The partnership was widely covered in press releases as an element of the company's environmental responsibility.
   *Source: Samsung Newsroom — "Samsung and The Ocean Cleanup" (2022)*

3. **Planting 10,000 trees in South Korea (2023)** — As part of environmental initiatives, Samsung organised the planting of 10,000 trees in South Korea. Meanwhile producing a single smartphone requires substantial energy and water inputs, and the total carbon footprint of Samsung's products amounts to tens of millions of tonnes of CO₂ per year. The absorption potential of 10,000 trees is approximately 500 tonnes of CO₂ per year.
   *Source: Samsung Sustainability Report 2023*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Samsung, through industry associations, lobbies against tightening environmental standards in semiconductor manufacturing and promotes its own version of "green" technological development.

1. **Lobbying through KSIA against chemical disclosure in "clean rooms" (2010s–2020s)** — Samsung, through the Korea Semiconductor Industry Association (KSIA), actively opposed legislative initiatives requiring mandatory disclosure of chemicals used in semiconductor manufacturing. SHARPS documented that Samsung systematically obstructed investigations into worker illnesses, citing trade secrets in production processes.
   *Source: SHARPS — reports 2010–2018; Korea Times — "Samsung and chemical disclosure debate"*

2. **CHIPS Act lobbying in the US with emphasis on subsidies without environmental obligations (2022–2023)** — Samsung actively participated in lobbying the US CHIPS and Science Act, seeking subsidies for the construction of a plant in Taylor, Texas. In negotiations the company emphasised economic benefits and "technological sovereignty" without taking on mandatory environmental obligations regarding water use (chip production is extremely water-intensive) and emissions.
   *Source: The Wall Street Journal — "Samsung Lobbies for Chips Act Subsidies" (2022); Bloomberg — "Samsung Taylor Texas fab" (2023)*

3. **Promoting the concept of "sustainable electronics" through corporate education (2020–2024)** — Samsung funds educational programmes and public campaigns on the "proper disposal" of electronics, shifting responsibility for e-waste onto consumers. Campaigns such as "recycle your phone" create an image of an environmentally responsible company, while systemic solutions — right to repair, increasing device lifespan — are implemented by Samsung to a minimum degree, and the company lobbies against expanded repairability in several jurisdictions.
   *Source: iFixit — "Samsung Repairability Report" (2023); Greenpeace — "Right to Repair" advocacy materials*
