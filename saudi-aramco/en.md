A Carbon Disclosure Project study (2019) established that Saudi Aramco is the largest corporate CO₂ emitter in history: since 1965 the company has been responsible for emitting approximately 59.26 billion tonnes of CO₂ equivalent — roughly 4.4% of all industrial greenhouse gas emissions by humanity.

At extraction sites in Saudi Arabia's Eastern Province, flaring of associated gas and evaporation of light hydrocarbons create significant local pollution. Satellite data from Global Energy Monitor (2022) showed that Saudi Aramco is one of the largest sources of methane emissions in the oil and gas industry.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. In addition to global climate damage, direct incidents at Aramco facilities have cost lives, and air pollution in industrial zones threatens the health of the population.

1. **Drone attacks on Abqaiq and Khurais facilities — environmental and human consequences (2019)** — In September 2019 drone strikes on Saudi Aramco's largest facilities (Abqaiq and Khurais) caused large-scale fires in which approximately 5–6% of global daily oil production was consumed. Although civilian casualties were avoided, the attacks exposed the vulnerability of concentrated oil infrastructure and produced large-scale discharges of toxic gases.
   *Source: Reuters — "Saudi Arabia oil facilities attacked" (2019)*

2. **Occupational diseases and worker deaths in the Eastern Province (systematic)** — Human rights organisations and Saudi activist investigations document chronic occupational diseases (lung damage, oncological conditions) among oil facility workers at Aramco sites in Al-Khobar and Dhahran. Data on industrial deaths in Saudi Arabia are classified; independent monitoring is limited.
   *Source: Human Rights Watch — "Saudi Arabia: Labor Rights" (2020)*

3. **Air pollution and its impact on the health of Eastern Province residents (2010–2020)** — Studies by King Abdulaziz University recorded elevated concentrations of benzene, toluene, and hydrogen sulphide in residential areas near Aramco oil facilities in Dhahran. Rates of asthma and respiratory disease in the region are statistically higher than in other parts of Saudi Arabia.
   *Source: King Abdulaziz University Journal — "Air quality in Eastern Province" (2017)*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Saudi Aramco bears responsibility for several major oil spills in the Persian Gulf and is the largest cumulative source of greenhouse gases in corporate history.

1. **Gulf War oil spill (1991)** — In January–February 1991 Iraqi forces opened the oil valves of the Mina al-Ahmadi terminal (Kuwait) and also struck Saudi Aramco facilities, resulting in the largest deliberate oil spill in history — 4 to 8 million barrels. The Persian Gulf was covered by an oil slick and marine fauna were destroyed along hundreds of kilometres of coastline.
   *Source: NOAA — "Gulf War Oil Spill" (archive 1991); UNEP — "Environmental impacts of Gulf War" (1991)*

2. **Oil spill off the Jeddah coast (2021)** — In October 2021 Saudi Aramco pipelines near Jeddah were damaged in an attack, causing an oil spill in the Red Sea. The oil slick covered approximately 17 km of coastline, polluting mangroves and coral reefs.
   *Source: Al Jazeera — "Jeddah oil spill damage" (2021)*

3. **Largest cumulative contribution to global warming (since 1965)** — According to the Carbon Disclosure Project report (2019), Saudi Aramco has emitted 59.26 billion tonnes of CO₂ equivalent since 1965 — more than any other corporation in history. This is comparable to Germany's total emissions over 150 years.
   *Source: CDP — "Carbon Majors Report 2019" (Griffin, Heede)*

4. **Gas flaring at Hawiyah and Shaybah fields (2000–2020)** — Despite declared gas utilisation programmes, satellite data from Global Energy Monitor and Ember recorded significant volumes of flaring at Aramco fields. Sulphur dioxide emissions were recorded by the Sentinel-5P satellite at levels exceeding the industry average.
   *Source: Global Energy Monitor — "Saudi Arabia Flaring" (2022)*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Saudi Aramco positions itself as a responsible producer of oil with the lowest carbon footprint, while simultaneously being the largest cumulative CO₂ emitter.

1. **"Oil with the lowest carbon footprint" campaign (2020–2023)** — Saudi Aramco claims to produce oil with one of the lowest emission intensity indicators per barrel among major producers (approximately 10.8 kg CO₂/barrel) and actively promoted this narrative at international forums. Critics — including IEEFA — pointed out that only Scope 1 and 2 are included in the calculation, while Scope 3 (combustion of oil by consumers) accounts for 85–90% of the climate footprint.
   *Source: IEEFA — "Saudi Aramco: misleading carbon intensity claims" (2022)*

2. **Promoting "circular carbon economy" instead of energy transition (2020–2022)** — Saudi Aramco and the Saudi government actively promoted the concept of a "circular carbon economy" (CCE), presupposing continued oil combustion with subsequent CO₂ capture. IPCC and Greenpeace critics noted that CO₂ capture technologies do not exist at the necessary scale, and CCE is a rhetorical tool for slowing the energy transition.
   *Source: IPCC AR6 Working Group III (2022); Climate Action Tracker — "CCE assessment" (2021)*

3. **"Operations carbon neutrality" promises by 2050 while planning production growth (2021)** — During the IPO and subsequent investor days, Aramco stated its intention to achieve "carbon neutrality" of operations (Scope 1 and 2) by 2050, while planning to increase production from 12 to 13 million barrels per day. Oil Change International called this "the single largest example of greenwashing in the history of the oil industry".
   *Source: Oil Change International — "Aramco's net zero pledge" (2021)*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Saudi Aramco actively uses ESG communications to maintain its image on international capital markets, especially in the IPO context.

1. **ESG narrative during the largest IPO in history (2019)** — In preparation for the IPO on Tadawul (Riyadh) and during the roadshow for international investors, Saudi Aramco actively used ESG rhetoric, publishing data on low emission intensity and investments in "clean technologies". Several major institutional investors (Norway's Government Pension Fund Global, BlackRock) declined to participate in the IPO, citing climate risks.
   *Source: FT — "Saudi Aramco IPO: ESG concerns" (2019)*

2. **Annual Sustainability Reports (2018–2022)** — Aramco publishes reports to GRI and SASB standards, declaring investments in renewable energy and emissions reductions. Independent auditing of the climate sections of these reports is not conducted.
   *Source: Saudi Aramco — "Sustainability Report 2022"*

3. **Sponsorship of climate summits and COP (2021–2023)** — Saudi Aramco was one of the corporate sponsors of COP27 (Egypt, 2022) and actively participated in business forums at UN climate negotiations. More than 600 NGOs signed an open letter condemning the presence of oil companies at climate negotiations.
   *Source: Global Witness — "Big Oil at COP27" (2022)*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Saudi Aramco has received corporate ESG recognition, primarily related to operational safety and energy efficiency programmes.

1. **IOGP Platinum Award for occupational safety (2020)** — Saudi Aramco received the highest award of the International Association of Oil & Gas Producers (IOGP) in the field of production safety for a record-low injury rate (TRIR) among association members in 2019.
   *Source: IOGP — "Safety Performance Indicators 2020 Report"*

2. **Corporate Knights Global 100 nomination (2018)** — Saudi Aramco was nominated in the annual Corporate Knights ranking, though it did not make the final list of 100 most sustainable companies in the world. The nomination was used in corporate PR materials.
   *Source: Corporate Knights — "Global 100 methodology" (2018)*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Aramco used environmental rhetoric and labelling in promoting petrochemical products and fuel.

1. **Labelling of oil as "low-carbon" in documentation for European buyers (2021–2023)** — Saudi Aramco in commercial documents and presentations for European clients positioned its oil as "low-carbon" based on Scope 1/2 intensity indicators. European NGOs filed complaints with the European Commission about misleading labelling.
   *Source: Transport & Environment — "Aramco low-carbon crude claims" (2022)*

2. **SABIC products with "green" certificates based on mass balance (2021–2022)** — After the acquisition of SABIC (Saudi Basic Industries Corporation), Aramco began marketing polymer products with "green" or "certified" labelling based on mass-balance accounting of bio-feedstock. ChemSec and ClientEarth noted that this scheme does not reduce actual petroleum feedstock use.
   *Source: ChemSec — "Petrochemical greenwashing via mass balance" (2022)*

3. **Aramco "blue hydrogen" — marketing as a clean fuel (2021–2023)** — Saudi Aramco actively promotes "blue hydrogen" (produced from natural gas with CO₂ capture) as the "clean" fuel of the future, planning exports to Japan and South Korea. IEEFA and the Hydrogen Science Coalition noted that CO₂ capture efficiency in blue hydrogen production does not exceed 55–85%, and the product is not "clean".
   *Source: IEEFA — "Blue hydrogen: Aramco's clean fuel claim" (2022)*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Saudi Aramco conducts environmental events that in absolute figures are negligible compared with the scale of damage caused.

1. **"Saudi Green Initiative" tree-planting programme — Aramco's participation (2021–2023)** — Saudi Aramco announced its intention to plant 1 billion trees in Saudi Arabia by 2030 as part of the Saudi Green Initiative, as a contribution to CO₂ absorption. According to Carbon Brief calculations, to offset Aramco's annual emissions would require foresting an area comparable to France and Germany combined.
   *Source: Carbon Brief — "Saudi Green Initiative tree planting" (2021)*

2. **Red Sea coral reef cleaning (2018–2021)** — Aramco funded a Red Sea coral reef monitoring and cleaning programme together with the Saudi Aramco Environmental Protection Department. The programme budget was a few million dollars per year, against annual company revenues of over $300 billion.
   *Source: Saudi Aramco — "Red Sea Coral Conservation Program" (2020)*

3. **"Aramco Volunteers" beach clean-up campaign (2017–2022)** — Annual corporate volunteer events to collect litter on the Persian Gulf coastline, involving company employees, were widely covered in Aramco's corporate media, though the volume of litter collected did not exceed a few tonnes.
   *Source: Saudi Aramco — "Sustainability Report 2021"*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Saudi Aramco and related structures have actively lobbied for slowing the global energy transition under the pretext of "energy security" and "just transition".

1. **Lobbying for the Circular Carbon Economy concept through OPEC and G20 (2020–2022)** — Saudi Aramco and the Saudi Ministry of Energy secured the inclusion of the CCE concept in G20 final documents under the Saudi presidency (2020). CCE presupposes the continuation of oil extraction as "compatible" with climate goals provided CO₂ is captured. The IPCC and climate organisations condemned CCE as a misinformation tool.
   *Source: IPCC AR6 WG3 — "Assessment of CCE" (2022); Corporate Europe Observatory — "G20 Saudi presidency and fossil fuels" (2020)*

2. **Funding climate change denial through the Global Climate Coalition (until 2002) and subsequent structures** — Saudi Aramco was a member of the Global Climate Coalition — an industry group that systematically challenged the scientific consensus on anthropogenic climate change in the 1990s and 2000s. After the GCC was dissolved, similar functions were performed through OPEC+.
   *Source: Climate Investigations Center — "Global Climate Coalition members" (archive)*

3. **Aramco "educational" publications on "reliable energy" (2019–2023)** — Saudi Aramco funded and distributed brochures and online materials for schoolchildren and students about oil's role in providing "reliable" and "affordable" energy, presenting the oil industry as an indispensable foundation of the global economy for decades to come. Materials were distributed through partner universities in Asia and Europe.
   *Source: InfluenceMap — "Aramco climate lobbying" (2022)*

## Summary

1. **Mass harm to people?** — **YES** — Air pollution in Eastern Province industrial zones harms the population's health; explosions and fires at Aramco facilities created immediate threats; global warming from the company's emissions is the largest long-term contribution of any single corporation to harm to human health.
2. **Major environmental disasters?** — **YES** — Participation in the catastrophic Persian Gulf spill (1991); Jeddah spill (2021); the largest cumulative corporate contribution to climate change (59.26 billion tonnes of CO₂ equivalent since 1965).
3. **Greenwashing?** — **YES** — "Oil with the lowest carbon footprint" campaigns; promotion of CCE concept; "operations carbon neutrality" promises alongside plans to increase production.
4. **ESG marketing?** — **YES** — ESG narrative during the 2019 IPO; GRI/SASB-standard reports; sponsorship of COP climate summits.
5. **ESG award wins?** — **YES** — IOGP Platinum Award for occupational safety (2020); Corporate Knights nomination (2018).
6. **Eco-labels on harmful products?** — **YES** — Oil labelled "low-carbon" for European clients; "green" SABIC polymer products via mass-balance scheme; "blue hydrogen" promoted as clean fuel.
7. **Negligible eco-events?** — **YES** — Saudi Green Initiative tree-planting programme; Red Sea coral reef cleaning; volunteer beach clean-ups — negligible compared with global climate damage.
8. **Lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?** — **YES** — Lobbying CCE into G20 documents as a substitute for real energy transition; participation in GCC climate denial; "educational" publications on the indispensability of oil.
