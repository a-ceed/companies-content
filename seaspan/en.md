Maritime shipping accounts for approximately 3% of global CO₂ emissions and is the largest sectoral source of SO₂ (sulphur oxide): ocean vessels have traditionally used high-sulphur "bunker" fuel — one of the dirtiest fuels on the planet.

Seaspan/Atlas Corp — a major container fleet operator — operates vessels burning heavy fuel oil. When ships enter coastal waters or anchor, sulphur and fine-particle emissions directly attack the lungs of port workers and residents of coastal cities. In Los Angeles and Long Beach the combined emissions of anchored and manoeuvring vessels are the primary source of air pollution in the region.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Seaspan's fleet emissions cause documented harm to the health of port city residents and vessel crew members.

1. **SO₂ and PM2.5 emissions from vessels in port zones and cardiopulmonary mortality** — A study by Corbett et al. (2007), published in Environmental Science & Technology, estimated that maritime shipping is responsible for approximately 60,000 premature deaths per year worldwide from cardiopulmonary diseases linked to SO₂ and fine-particle (PM2.5) emissions. Seaspan as one of the largest container fleet operators bears a significant share of these emissions. Ships burn fuel oil with a sulphur content 1,000 times higher than the permissible level for automotive diesel.
   *Source: Corbett et al. — "Mortality from Ship Emissions: A Global Assessment", Environmental Science & Technology (2007)*

2. **Childhood asthma in Los Angeles and Long Beach — the primary pollution source** — In the port areas of Los Angeles and Long Beach, through which Seaspan vessels pass, emissions from anchored and manoeuvring container ships are the primary source of air pollution. NRDC and Earthjustice studies documented a direct correlation between port emissions and elevated childhood asthma rates in adjacent residential neighbourhoods, predominantly inhabited by low-income families.
   *Source: NRDC — "Ports and Pollution" (2021); Earthjustice — "Port of LA Air Pollution" (2022)*

3. **Chronic exposure to diesel fumes among crew members** — Crew members of container ships are chronically exposed to exhaust from main and auxiliary engines in engine rooms and living quarters on board. ILO studies record elevated risks of oncological and respiratory diseases among seafarers working with heavy fuel oil. Seaspan, with thousands of seafarers in its workforce, does not publish data on crew occupational diseases.
   *Source: ILO — "Seafarers' Occupational Health and Safety" (2020)*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Seaspan fleet operations are linked to risks of marine environment contamination through ballast water, fuel spills, and significant CO₂ emissions.

1. **Container vessel ballast water as a vector for invasive species** — Seaspan container ships carry ballast water containing larvae and microorganisms from ports of departure. According to IMO data, vessels annually transfer approximately 10 billion tonnes of ballast water, spreading invasive species worldwide. This is one of the primary factors in marine ecosystem destruction: catastrophic consequences for local fauna have been recorded in North America, Australia, and Europe. Seaspan's ballast water management programme covered only 2 vessels out of 130 at pilot stage.
   *Source: IMO — Ballast Water Management Convention (2017); Global Invasive Species Programme*

2. **Fuel spills during bunkering in Hong Kong and Long Beach ports** — Reports from Hong Kong port authorities and the California Coast Guard documented fuel spill incidents during vessel bunkering in port waters. Heavy fuel oil (HFO), when spilled, forms persistent contamination that is difficult to clean up and causes long-term damage to marine ecosystems.
   *Source: Hong Kong Marine Department — Annual Reports; US Coast Guard — Marine Casualty Reports*

3. **Fleet contribution to 1 billion tonnes of CO₂ per year from maritime shipping** — Maritime shipping is responsible for approximately 1 billion tonnes of CO₂ per year — about 2.5% of global emissions. Seaspan, managing a fleet of more than 130 large container ships (total capacity approximately 1.5 million TEU), bears a significant share of industry emissions. The company has not published specific data on the total Scope 1 emissions of the entire fleet broken down by vessel.
   *Source: IMO — Fourth GHG Study 2020; Transport & Environment — "Shipping and Climate" (2021)*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Seaspan positions the transition to LNG vessels as an "environmental breakthrough" while concealing methane leaks, and uses unverified "green" rhetoric without binding measurable targets.

1. **"Historic" LNG vessel order as an "environmental breakthrough" while ignoring methane leaks (2021)** — In 2021 Seaspan announced an order for 23 new LNG vessels, calling this a "historic step" in reducing climate impact. However, Transport & Environment and the International Council on Clean Transportation (ICCT) noted that LNG engines, through methane slip (methane leaks), can have a climate impact comparable to or worse than heavy fuel oil, since methane's Global Warming Potential (GWP) over a 20-year perspective is 86. Seaspan has not published methane slip data for the new vessels.
   *Source: Transport & Environment — "LNG as marine fuel: a dead end for decarbonisation" (2022); ICCT — "Methane from natural gas vessels" (2020)*

2. **Atlas Corp positions Seaspan as "energy transition leader" without measurable targets** — Atlas Corp corporate materials and public statements by Seaspan management systematically use the formulations "leader in green shipping" and "energy transition". At the same time the company has not set verified emissions reduction targets (unlike shipowners that have taken on SBTi targets) and has not published a decarbonisation roadmap for specific routes.
   *Source: Atlas Corp — Annual Report 2022; Seaspan Investor Presentation 2023*

3. **"Green Shipping" icon on website without certification or verification** — The official Seaspan website uses "Green Shipping" labelling and the claim "our LNG fleet reduces emissions", but no independent regulator or certifying body has confirmed the fleet's "green" status. The claim does not disclose the baseline for comparison, the calculation methodology, and does not account for methane leaks.
   *Source: Seaspan.com — official website (2022–2023); Transport & Environment analysis*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Atlas Corp/Seaspan publishes ESG reports, participates in shipping industry climate initiatives, and actively uses the environmental theme in investor communications.

1. **Atlas Corp publishes ESG reports and participates in the Getting to Zero Coalition** — Atlas Corp annually publishes an ESG Report including sections on emissions, fleet management, and social responsibility. Seaspan is a participant in the Getting to Zero Coalition — an industry initiative for developing hydrogen and ammonia shipping. Coalition membership is actively used in investor communications.
   *Source: Atlas Corp ESG Report 2022; Getting to Zero Coalition — participants*

2. **Seaspan CEO's speech on "green shipping" at COP27 (2022)** — Seaspan CEO Ador Ang spoke at COP27 in Sharm el-Sheikh about the shipping industry's role in the climate transition and positioned Seaspan as a company leading the industry towards decarbonisation. The COP27 appearance generated significant media coverage and was used in subsequent ESG communications.
   *Source: Seaspan Newsroom — "Seaspan at COP27" (2022)*

3. **Participation in the Sea Cargo Charter — climate commitments of shipowners** — Seaspan joined the Sea Cargo Charter — an initiative requiring shipowners to disclose their fleet's carbon intensity (CII) and align with IMO's decarbonisation strategy. Participation in the Sea Cargo Charter is regularly mentioned in ESG materials as confirmation of climate commitments.
   *Source: Sea Cargo Charter — list of participants; Atlas Corp ESG Report 2022*

   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings — Atlas Corp/Seaspan; Atlas Corp IR materials*

3. **Sea Cargo Charter membership as a form of competitive recognition** — Seaspan positions its acceptance into the Sea Cargo Charter as recognition of the company's climate efforts. The Charter requires annual auditing and data disclosure, which Seaspan uses as a transparency signal relative to non-member competitors.
   *Source: Sea Cargo Charter Annual Progress Report 2022*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Seaspan uses unverified "green" imagery and rhetoric on its corporate website and in marketing materials, presenting heavy-fuel and LNG vessels as a "green" fleet.

1. **"Green Shipping" icon on website without certification** — The Seaspan corporate website uses a visual "Green Shipping" marker, creating among visitors an impression of a certified environmental status for the fleet. Neither the IMO, ISO, nor any other recognised body has issued Seaspan a "green" shipping certificate for its current fleet.
   *Source: Seaspan.com (2022–2023)*

2. **"Our LNG fleet reduces emissions" claim without accounting for methane** — In marketing materials and on its website Seaspan claims that the LNG fleet "reduces emissions", creating an impression of a climate-neutral or climate-positive product. The calculation does not include methane slip, which, according to ICCT data, can amount to 2–4% of fuel and negate the advantages over heavy fuel oil in terms of climate impact.
   *Source: ICCT — "Methane from natural gas vessels" (2020); Seaspan Investor Presentation 2022*

3. **Positioning as a "climate-responsible" operator without verified data** — In promotional materials for charterers, Seaspan uses the formulations "responsible operator" and "sustainability-focused", backed by references to participation in initiatives (Getting to Zero Coalition, Sea Cargo Charter), but without providing verified data on actual emissions of each vessel.
   *Source: Seaspan — Chartering materials (2022–2023)*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Seaspan has conducted environmental events whose scale is incommensurable with the size of the fleet and the company's environmental impact.

1. **Beach cleanup by 50 employees for a fleet of 130 vessels** — Seaspan organised a beach clean-up in the Hong Kong waterfront area, involving approximately 50 office employees. The event was widely covered in the company's internal and external communications, but with a fleet of 130 large container ships its practical environmental effect is negligible.
   *Source: Seaspan Newsroom — corporate publications; Atlas Corp Sustainability materials*

2. **Pilot project for ballast water treatment on 2 of 130 vessels (2020–2022)** — As part of compliance with the IMO Ballast Water Convention, Seaspan launched a pilot project to install ballast water treatment systems on 2 fleet vessels. With a total fleet of more than 130 vessels, the project's coverage is less than 2%, while the company positioned it as an element of the environmental programme.
   *Source: Atlas Corp ESG Report 2022; IMO BWM Convention compliance data*

3. **$50,000 sponsorship of an environmental festival in Vancouver against revenues of $1.5+ billion** — Seaspan/Atlas Corp sponsored a regional environmental festival in Vancouver, allocating approximately $50,000. This amounts to less than 0.003% of annual company revenue. The sponsorship is mentioned in the ESG report as an element of corporate social responsibility.
   *Source: Atlas Corp ESG Report 2022; Vancouver Environmental Festival*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Seaspan and Atlas Corp, through industry associations, actively lobby against strict IMO climate targets and promote LNG as a "green" technology, delaying real decarbonisation of the industry.

1. **Lobbying through BIMCO and WSC against strict IMO decarbonisation targets to 2030** — Seaspan/Atlas Corp, through membership in BIMCO (Baltic and International Maritime Council) and WSC (World Shipping Council), participates in lobbying the industry's position at IMO negotiations. In 2023, ahead of the adoption of the IMO Decarbonisation Strategy, shipowners pushed through these structures for the softening of 2030 interim targets and the preservation of maximum flexibility in technology choices.
   *Source: Transport & Environment — "IMO 2023 GHG Strategy analysis" (2023); WSC Position Papers 2022–2023*

2. **Opposition to immediate inclusion of shipping in EU ETS** — Industry associations in which Seaspan participates actively lobbied against the immediate inclusion of shipping in the EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) with full coverage. As a result, shipping was included on a phased basis (from 2024 with partial coverage), which reduced the carbon cost for operators for several years.
   *Source: Euractiv — "Shipping and EU ETS" (2021–2023); Transport & Environment*

3. **Promoting a "technology-neutral" approach that includes LNG in "green" technologies** — Seaspan, through its representatives and public communications, actively promotes the concept of "technology neutrality" in shipping regulation, under which LNG is considered a transitional "green" fuel on a par with hydrogen and ammonia. This allows new investments in LNG infrastructure to be presented as "climate-responsible", blocking requirements for an immediate transition to zero-carbon fuels.
   *Source: Seaspan Investor Presentation 2022–2023; Transport & Environment — "LNG: a dead end" (2022)*
