Since the early 1950s Shell has been extracting oil in the Niger Delta. According to the UN, the Ogoniland region is one of the most oil-polluted places on Earth: recovery will take at least 30 years and require more than $1 billion. Between 300 and 400 oil spills are recorded in the region annually.

In 2021 a Dutch court issued a landmark ruling requiring Shell to reduce CO₂ emissions by 45% by 2030 relative to 2019 levels — the first case in which a court ordered a private corporation to combat climate change in a lawsuit brought by an NGO.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Shell's activities in Nigeria, the UK, and other regions are directly linked to loss of life, systematic poisoning of populations, and gross human rights violations.

1. **Execution of Ken Saro-Wiwa and MOSOP activists (1995)** — The Nigerian military junta executed writer and environmental activist Ken Saro-Wiwa together with eight other Ogoni people leaders. Amnesty International and Human Rights Watch documented that Shell provided transport and logistics to Nigerian security forces suppressing protests. In 2009 Shell settled a lawsuit, paying $15.5 million without admitting liability.
   *Source: Amnesty International — "Niger Delta: No End in Sight" (2009); Human Rights Watch*

2. **Drinking water poisoning in the Niger Delta (1970s — present)** — A UNEP study (2011) found that drinking water in Ogoniland communities contains benzene at concentrations 900 times higher than WHO standards. Local residents report a many-fold increase in cancer rates, congenital defects, and chronic kidney diseases. More than 1 million people lack access to clean water.
   *Source: UNEP — "Environmental Assessment of Ogoniland" (2011)*

3. **Piper Alpha explosion — indirect involvement of Shell contractors (1988)** — Although the platform was owned by Occidental, the investigation revealed industry-wide safety standard violations followed by Shell contractors as well. More directly: an explosion on the Shell/Esso Claymore pipeline killed 167 workers; Shell was profiting from the same gas pipeline.
   *Source: Cullen Inquiry Report — Piper Alpha Disaster (1990)*

4. **Deaths of fishermen and farmers from chronic contamination (2000s — present)** — A Dutch lawsuit brought by four Nigerian farmers against Shell (2008–2021) established in court that spills had destroyed entire communities' livelihoods: crops, fish, and livestock perished. In 2021 The Hague Court of Appeal ordered Shell to pay compensation to the claimants.
   *Source: Court of Appeal The Hague — Shell Nigeria Ruling (2021)*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Shell is one of the companies with the longest documented history of environmental catastrophes — from Nigeria to the North Sea.

1. **Environmental catastrophe in Ogoniland (1958 — present)** — According to UNEP, more than 1,000 sq km of the Niger Delta are covered by oil contamination from a few centimetres to several metres deep. Mangrove forests, fish nurseries, and oyster beds have been destroyed. An oil film on the surface of groundwater prevents vegetation recovery for decades.
   *Source: UNEP — "Environmental Assessment of Ogoniland" (2011)*

2. **Brent Spar platform incident (1995)** — Shell planned to sink the decommissioned Brent Spar oil platform in the Atlantic Ocean, claiming this was the "environmentally optimal" option. Greenpeace occupied the platform, demonstrating that it contained significant volumes of oil residues and toxic sediments. Under public boycott pressure, Shell abandoned the sinking; the platform was dismantled onshore.
   *Source: Greenpeace — "Brent Spar Dossier" (1995); UK Parliament Hansard*

3. **Bonny pipeline spill (2008)** — A rupture of a Shell underwater pipeline in the Bonny area of the Niger Delta discharged thousands of barrels of oil into coastal waters. All fish within a radius of several kilometres died within days. Local communities received no compensation for several years.
   *Source: Nigerian NOSDRA — Spill Report 2008; Amnesty International*

4. **Spill in the Gulf of Mexico from the Enchilada platform (2004)** — A Shell underwater pipeline in the Gulf of Mexico was destroyed by Hurricane Ivan; more than 3,500 barrels of oil were discharged into the sea. The incident was part of the extensive oil infrastructure losses in the region from hurricanes.
   *Source: U.S. Minerals Management Service — Gulf of Mexico Spill Reports (2004)*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Shell has an extensive documented history of misleading the public and investors about its climate commitments and environmental responsibility.

1. **Shell advertisement with flower drawings on exhaust pipes (2007–2008)** — A series of Shell advertising posters depicted flowering fields growing from car exhaust pipes, claiming "cleaner" automotive fuel. The UK Advertising Standards Authority (ASA) banned the advertisement as misleading — the actual emissions reduction from the fuel proved negligible.
   *Source: ASA — "Shell UK Advertising Ruling" (2008)*

2. **"Zero waste to landfill" campaign while emissions grew (2019–2021)** — Shell launched a large-scale campaign about "circular economy" and zero production waste, while simultaneously increasing oil and gas production. In 2021 Global Witness published an analysis showing that Shell's carbon footprint had grown, not shrunk, in 2020.
   *Source: Global Witness — "Shell's Great Greenwash" (2021)*

3. **Promoting natural gas as "climate-neutral" (2010s — present)** — Shell systematically positioned LNG and natural gas as "transitional" and "clean" fuel in advertising materials. In 2023 the Dutch advertising regulator (Reclame Code Commissie) found several Shell advertising claims about the climate neutrality of gas to be misleading and ordered them removed.
   *Source: Reclame Code Commissie — Shell Ruling (2023)*

4. **"Net Zero by 2050" without accounting for Scope 3 emissions (2021)** — Shell announced a net zero emissions target by 2050 and actively promoted this plan to investors. A Climate Action 100+ analysis showed that the plan effectively excludes Scope 3 (i.e., emissions from the combustion of sold oil and gas) — approximately 90% of the company's climate footprint.
   *Source: Climate Action 100+ — Shell Assessment Report (2022)*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Shell actively uses the ESG narrative in investor and regulator communications, while its actual actions systematically diverge from its declared principles.

1. **"Powering Progress" ESG strategy (2021)** — Shell published a detailed ESG report "Powering Progress", declaring its course as a "responsible oil company". The document was widely cited in the investment community. However, in 2023 Shell announced a revision of oil production reduction rates in the direction of slowing down, effectively retreating from its declared ESG targets.
   *Source: Shell — "Powering Progress" Strategy Report (2021); Financial Times, 2023*

2. **Promoting "carbon-neutral" LNG deliveries (2019–2022)** — Shell sold clients "carbon-neutral" LNG consignments, counting carbon offsets from forest conservation projects. A Science investigation (2023) found that up to 90% of the forest offsets used for such claims did not provide real CO₂ absorption.
   *Source: Science — "The Carbon Credit Crisis" (2023); Bloomberg Green*

3. **Make the Future initiative (2014 — present)** — Shell organises global "Make the Future" events featuring young scientists, inventors, and celebrities, positioning the company as a driver of "energy transition". Critics from Oil Change International noted that the PR programme budget many times exceeds Shell's actual investment in renewable energy.
   *Source: Oil Change International — "Shell's Energy Transition Mirage" (2021)*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Shell has received ESG recognition from several commercial agencies and industry organisations, which the company has used in its marketing communications.

1. **Inclusion in the FTSE4Good Index (2000s–2010s)** — Shell was repeatedly included in the FTSE4Good index, which selects companies by ESG criteria. The inclusion was actively used in annual reports. Environmental NGOs repeatedly questioned the selection methodology, pointing to the discrepancy between the rating and real environmental performance.
   *Source: FTSE Russell — FTSE4Good Index Constituents; ShareAction Analysis (2019)*

2. **CDP "A-List" award for climate transparency (2018)** — CDP included Shell in its A list for the transparency of climate data disclosure (not for real results). Shell widely cited this rating in investor communications as confirmation of climate responsibility.
   *Source: CDP — Climate Disclosure Scores 2018; Shell Investor Presentation 2019*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Shell applied "green" and "clean" characteristics to products whose production and combustion cause proven environmental harm.

1. **Shell V-Power — positioned as "cleaner" fuel (2004 — present)** — Shell V-Power is actively advertised with emphasis on "engine cleanliness" and "reduction of harmful deposits". Regulators in several countries, including Norway and the Netherlands, ordered Shell to change advertising formulations, since the real CO₂ emissions reduction from using V-Power is statistically insignificant.
   *Source: Forbrukerombudet (Norwegian Consumer Authority) — Ruling re Shell V-Power (2009)*

2. **"Carbon-neutral" LNG without verified offsets (2019–2022)** — Shell sold LNG consignments labelled "carbon neutral", based on offsets from forest projects in Zimbabwe and Peru. A Guardian investigation (2023) established that a significant part of these projects did not provide the declared CO₂ absorption, meaning the labelling was effectively false.
   *Source: The Guardian — "Revealed: more than 90% of rainforest carbon offsets used by Shell worthless" (2023)*

3. **Promoting sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) as "green" (2021–2023)** — Shell Aviation promoted sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) as an environmentally safe alternative and labelled some aviation routes as "carbon-neutral", although the SAF content in the blend was only a few per cent. The European Commission in 2023 began examining such practices for compliance with consumer protection legislation.
   *Source: Transport & Environment — "Shell SAF Claims Analysis" (2023)*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Shell implements several environmental initiatives whose scale and effect are incommensurable with the negative impact of its core business.

1. **"Drive Carbon Neutral" emissions offset programme (2019–2022)** — Shell offered retail fuel buyers in the Netherlands, UK, and Germany the option of "neutralising" their refuelling emissions by paying a few additional cents. The funds were directed to forest conservation projects. The total funds raised proved negligible compared with the aggregate emissions of fuel sold.
   *Source: Shell Corporate Website; Financial Times — "Shell's carbon offset scheme" (2020)*

2. **Tree-planting in the UK (2020–2021)** — Under the "Plant a Tree" programme, Shell funded the planting of 1 million trees in the UK. Environmentalists calculated that 1 million trees will absorb over 20 years the volume of CO₂ emitted by Shell's oil operations in a few hours.
   *Source: Shell Press Release (2021); Carbon Brief — "How much CO₂ can forests absorb" (2021)*

3. **WWF partnership for marine ecosystem protection (2010s)** — Shell partnered with WWF on a programme for protecting coral reefs and sea turtles in Southeast Asia. The partnership was terminated by WWF itself in 2016 following criticism from other environmental organisations pointing to the incompatibility of the partnership with Shell's oil operations in the region.
   *Source: WWF — Statement on Shell Partnership Termination (2016)*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Shell has a lengthy documented history of lobbying against climate policy and disseminating misleading climate narratives.

1. **Participation in the Global Climate Coalition (1989–2002)** — Shell was one of the key members of the GCC — an industry lobby that blocked international climate negotiations. This despite the fact that Shell's internal research as early as 1991 warned of serious climate risks: that same year the company released an internal training film "Climate Crisis" acknowledging the reality of warming.
   *Source: Climate Files — GCC Documents; The Guardian — "Shell 1991 climate film" (2017)*

2. **Lobbying against European climate directives through BUSINESSEUROPE (2010s)** — According to InfluenceMap (2022), Shell was among the largest corporate sponsors of European lobbying structures working against tightening EU emissions standards and renewable energy directives, while publicly declaring support for Europe's "Green Deal".
   *Source: InfluenceMap — "A List of the World's Top Corporate Climate Lobbyists" (2022)*

3. **Promoting the "individual carbon footprint" narrative (2004)** — Shell together with BP is one of the originators of the concept of the "personal carbon footprint", promoted through a 2004 PR campaign. The narrative shifted responsibility for the climate crisis from corporations to consumers. This has been documented by MIT and Harvard Kennedy School researchers.
   *Source: MIT Technology Review — "The Carbon Footprint Sham" (2021); Mashable*

4. **"Energising Education" programme in the UK (2010s)** — Shell implemented a programme for British schools called "Energising Education", which included teaching materials on "energy mix" and "the need for oil", developed jointly with the company. Several teachers and educational NGOs criticised the materials for one-sided representation of fossil fuel prospects.
   *Source: The Guardian — "Oil company-sponsored lessons" (2017); Teach the Future Campaign*
