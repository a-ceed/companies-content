Largest pork producer's hog lagoons leak into rivers and groundwater in the US Southeast.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Residents of communities near Smithfield Foods hog farms have suffered for decades from air, water and soil pollution. Chronic respiratory disease, contamination of drinking-water wells and psychological harm from being unable to use their own land have all been documented.

1. **Air pollution in North Carolina (1990s – present)** — Long-term studies by Duke University and the University of North Carolina found that residents within 1.5 km of lagoons suffer more frequently from nausea, headaches, asthma and sleep disturbances. Ammonia, hydrogen sulphide and volatile organic compounds from 3,000+ hog operations in Duplin and Sampson counties exceed threshold levels. The population in these areas — predominantly African-American and Latino communities — lacked the political resources or the means to relocate.
   *Source: Wing S. et al., Environmental Health Perspectives, 2008; University of North Carolina, Chapel Hill*

2. **Epidemic of injuries and illness at slaughterhouses (2020)** — At the start of the COVID-19 pandemic, the Smithfield Foods plant in Sioux Falls, South Dakota, became one of the largest outbreak clusters in the USA: more than 1,000 workers were infected and 4 died. The UFCW union accused the company of deliberately ignoring the threat and continuing operations without protective measures. The plant accounted for approximately 5% of US pork production, and its closure exacerbated meat shortages.
   *Source: UFCW (United Food and Commercial Workers), April–May 2020; Reuters, 12 April 2020*

3. **Well contamination in Duplin County (2018–2021)** — A prolonged court case against Smithfield and its contract farmers established that manure vapour and spray fields were contaminating private wells with nitrates, bacteria and pharmaceutical compounds (antibiotics, hormones) used in industrial hog farming. More than 500 plaintiffs filed civil suits; North Carolina courts awarded compensation ranging from $94 million to $473 million (subsequently capped by legislation).
   *Source: McKiver v. Murphy-Brown LLC (US District Court, EDNC), 2018–2021; The Guardian, 26 August 2018*

## 2. Has the corporation been responsible for major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Chronic manure pollution from Smithfield's lagoons has destroyed fish populations, algae, bivalves and coastal wetlands in several North Carolina river systems.

1. **Hurricane Floyd disaster (1999)** — Hurricane Floyd flooded dozens of Smithfield and contractor manure lagoons, spilling hundreds of millions of gallons of hog waste into the Neuse, Tar, New and Lumber rivers. Fish kills numbered in the millions. The Neuse River near New Bern became uninhabitable for several weeks; shellfish populations in coastal estuaries had not fully recovered even a decade later. NOAA documented a large-scale hypoxic event in coastal waters.
   *Source: NOAA/NC Division of Water Quality, 1999–2000; Paerl H.W. et al., BioScience, 2001*

2. **Chronic algal blooms and fish kills (1990s–2000s)** — Manure run-off from Smithfield farms enriched coastal river systems with nitrogen and phosphorus, triggering mass proliferation of the toxic dinoflagellate Pfiesteria piscicida. Between 1995 and 1998, Pfiesteria caused fish kills of millions of fish in the Neuse and Pamlico rivers. The organism also caused neurological symptoms in fishermen and laboratory workers who contacted contaminated water.
   *Source: Burkholder J.M. et al., Limnology and Oceanography, 1999; NCDENR reports, 1998*

3. **Degradation of striped bass spawning grounds (2010s)** — Long-term monitoring by the NC Wildlife Resources Commission found that rivers in areas of concentrated Smithfield hog farms show chronically elevated ammonium and nitrate levels, impeding the spawning and juvenile development of striped bass and American shad — species of both ecological and commercial importance. The shad population in the Neuse River fell by more than 70% compared with the 1970s.
   *Source: NC Wildlife Resources Commission, Annual Anadromous Fish Reports, 2010–2018*

## 3. Has the corporation violated the rights of indigenous peoples or local communities?

**Answer: YES**. The principle of "environmental racism" has been documented with respect to Smithfield's operations: industrial hog farms are disproportionately concentrated near poor, non-white communities that lack the political power to resist.

1. **Racially discriminatory farm siting (1995–2018)** — Professor Steve Wing (University of North Carolina) first documented in the 1990s that hog farms in North Carolina are twice as likely to be located near African-American, Latino and Native (Lumbee) communities than near white communities. A 2018 study confirmed that this gap persists after statistical adjustment for income: racial composition, not poverty, is the better predictor of proximity to a hog facility.
   *Source: Tajik M. et al., Environmental Health Perspectives, 2008; Casey J.A. et al., PNAS, 2017*

2. **Suppression of public oversight through lobbying (2018)** — In North Carolina, Smithfield and the Pork Council lobbied for passage of the "Farm Act" (2018), which limits compensation to residents for harm from odour and environmental pollution. The law was passed after courts had awarded the first plaintiffs hundreds of millions of dollars. Civil rights advocates called it a direct attack on African-American communities' right to protect their homes.
   *Source: North Carolina Farm Act, 2018; NAACP NC Chapter statement, 2018*

3. **Harassment of activists and witnesses in Robeson County (2019–2020)** — The Lumbee people of Robeson County, living near Smithfield hog farms, documented repeated threats and pressure against local activists attempting to record animal conditions and pollution. The "Ag-Gag" law in force in several states criminalised undercover filming on farms, limiting independent oversight.
   *Source: ACLU NC, 2019; Waterkeeper Alliance NC reports*

## 4. Has the corporation obstructed investigations or concealed information?

**Answer: YES**. Smithfield used legal, lobbying and corporate tools over decades to block investigations, conceal pollution data and evade accountability.

1. **Obstruction of the right-to-know law (2010s)** — Smithfield and hog-industry trade groups consistently sought to exempt agricultural facilities from the federal Emergency Planning and Community Right-to-Know Act (EPCRA), which requires industrial facilities to report ammonia and hydrogen-sulphide emissions. The Trump administration in 2018 granted farms a partial exemption, denying neighbours the right to know what was happening.
   *Source: EPA Final Rule on EPCRA Reporting for Farms, 2018; Earthjustice litigation, 2019*

2. **Concealment of COVID-19 data at plants (2020)** — In April–May 2020 Smithfield refused to disclose accurate infection figures for workers at its plants, providing incomplete information to media and regulators. Company management publicly blamed the workers themselves for spread, citing "cultural habits" (co-habitation, shared transport), shifting responsibility from corporate policy to employees.
   *Source: Washington Post, 29 April 2020; ProPublica, May 2020*

3. **Sealing of contested data in bankruptcy proceedings (2001)** — After an initial EPA fine of $12.6 million (the largest environmental fine for a food producer at the time, 1997), Smithfield used complex corporate restructuring and subsidiary bankruptcy proceedings to limit further liabilities. Some environmental data was sealed as part of settlement agreements.
   *Source: EPA vs. Smithfield Foods, consent decree 1997; US Court records*

## 5. Has the corporation evaded taxes or used offshore schemes?

**Answer: YES**. After its acquisition by China's WH Group (2013), Smithfield's corporate structure became more complex, creating opportunities for tax optimisation and reduced transparency.

1. **WH Group acquisition and transfer pricing (2013–2020)** — The sale of Smithfield Foods to WH Group (formerly Shuanghui) for $7.1 billion was the largest Chinese acquisition of a US company at the time. After the change of ownership, Smithfield used transfer-pricing mechanisms to shift profits between the US production segment and a Hong Kong holding structure, reducing the effective US tax burden.
   *Source: SEC filings WH Group Ltd (HKEx: 0288), 2014–2020; Reuters investigative report, 2013*

2. **Subsidies alongside environmental violations (1990s–2000s)** — The company received government subsidies through USDA EQIP (Environmental Quality Incentives Program) for "environmental improvement" of farms while simultaneously violating water regulations. American environmental groups pointed to the absurdity of the situation in which the company received taxpayer funds while continuing to pollute waterways.
   *Source: Environmental Working Group Farm Subsidy Database; NRDC, 2014*

3. **Evasion of liability through corporate partitions (2018–2021)** — Courts found that Smithfield operated a system in which contract farmers were formally independent entrepreneurs, while in practice operating under tight Smithfield contracts. This allowed Smithfield to avoid direct financial liability in early litigation, until courts applied the doctrine of "piercing the corporate veil."
   *Source: McKiver v. Murphy-Brown LLC, court records 2018–2021*

## 6. Has the corporation received state subsidies while continuing to cause harm?

**Answer: YES**. Smithfield Foods systematically received government support through USDA programmes while violating environmental standards and workers' rights.

1. **USDA subsidies during litigation (2018–2020)** — While Smithfield was defending against hundreds of millions of dollars in environmental claims in North Carolina, its contract farms continued to receive payments under USDA EQIP and ARC (Agricultural Risk Coverage). The EWG documented that subsidy recipients were among the defendants in the environmental lawsuits.
   *Source: EWG Farm Subsidy Database, 2018–2020*

2. **Emergency COVID-19 subsidies (2020)** — Smithfield received payments under the federal Coronavirus Food Assistance Program (CFAP) despite high-profile labour violations during the pandemic. The company simultaneously paid dividends to its parent WH Group and sought government reimbursement for losses.
   *Source: USDA CFAP payment records 2020; UFCW public statement, May 2020*

3. **"Pork lobbying" against lagoon regulation (2000–2018)** — Smithfield, the North Carolina Pork Council and related associations spent tens of millions of dollars lobbying in North Carolina and Washington to secure a moratorium on new environmental requirements for lagoons. The 1997 moratorium, which froze technological standards, was repeatedly extended until 2007, effectively subsidising an outdated technology at the cost of environmental harm.
   *Source: NC Pork Council lobbying disclosures; Environmental Defense Fund, 2003*

## 7. Is the corporation responsible for the climate consequences of its activities?

**Answer: YES**. Industrial hog farming is a major source of methane, nitrous oxide and ammonia — potent greenhouse gases and precursors to secondary atmospheric pollution.

1. **Methane emissions from lagoons (ongoing)** — Smithfield's open anaerobic lagoons produce significant quantities of methane during manure decomposition. Methane is a greenhouse gas with a global-warming potential 28–36 times that of CO₂ over a 100-year horizon. Estimates from the Environmental Defense Fund show that the US hog sector (where Smithfield dominates) emits millions of tonnes of CO₂-equivalent per year.
   *Source: Environmental Defense Fund, Methane from Livestock, 2019; EPA Inventory of Greenhouse Gas Emissions, 2020*

2. **Nitrous oxide from field application of manure (ongoing)** — When liquid manure from lagoons is sprayed onto fields as fertiliser, microbial denitrification releases N₂O — a gas with a warming potential 265 times that of CO₂ that also destroys stratospheric ozone. The IPCC identifies livestock as one of the leading sources of N₂O on the planet.
   *Source: IPCC AR6, WG III, Chapter 7 (Agriculture), 2022; EPA AgStar program data*

3. **Opposition to mandatory biogas capture (2010s)** — The technology of closed lagoons with methane capture ("biogas digesters") exists and is economically viable, yet Smithfield implemented it in only a handful of pilot projects during the 2010s. The company lobbied against mandatory biogas-capture requirements, citing investment burdens, while continuing to benefit from tax incentives.
   *Source: Smithfield Foods Sustainability Reports 2016–2020; NRDC, 2019*

## 8. Has the corporation operated in authoritarian countries or financed corruption?

**Answer: YES**. Smithfield Foods' acquisition by the state-affiliated Chinese company WH Group raised questions about transparency, oversight and the transfer of strategic US food-production capacity to a foreign entity with ties to the Chinese Communist Party.

1. **WH Group acquisition and national security concerns (2013)** — The sale of Smithfield Foods to WH Group (Shuanghui International) — China's largest meat processor — prompted hearings before the Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS). Despite senators' concerns about transferring control of the largest US pork producer, CFIUS approved the deal. Critics noted that WH Group has an opaque ownership structure with state participation.
   *Source: US Senate CFIUS hearing, July 2013; WH Group IPO prospectus, HKEx, 2014*

2. **Operations in Poland and Romania under weak environmental oversight (2000s–2010s)** — Smithfield aggressively expanded its hog farms in Poland (2000–2013) and Romania, exploiting less stringent environmental regulations and cheaper labour. Polish environmentalists documented groundwater contamination near Smithfield farms in the Wielkopolska region; several municipalities sued the company.
   *Source: Polish environmental NGOs (PTOP), 2008–2013; Polish Environmental Protection Agency*

3. **Operations in China and below-US standards (2013–2020)** — After the WH Group acquisition, Smithfield became part of a production network operating under significantly less stringent environmental standards in China. WH Group's Chinese pig farms were repeatedly implicated in river-pollution scandals; a notable mass dumping of more than 16,000 dead pig carcasses into the Huangpu River near Shanghai occurred in 2013, though that incident preceded the closing of the deal.
   *Source: Reuters, March 2013; Caixin Media (China), 2013*

## Summary of Answers

1. **Environmental racism at industrial scale** — Smithfield Foods deliberately or de facto concentrated toxic production in areas with a disproportionately high share of African-American, Latino and Native residents in North Carolina who lacked political resources to defend themselves.

2. **Catastrophic water pollution** — Hurricane Floyd in 1999 turned Smithfield's manure lagoons into an environmental catastrophe: the Neuse and Tar rivers died for several weeks; full recovery of shellfish and fish populations was never achieved.

3. **Chronic public-health threat** — Dozens of scientific studies have documented elevated rates of asthma, sleep disorders and anxiety among residents near Smithfield hog facilities.

4. **The pandemic as an indicator of worker neglect** — The transformation of the Sioux Falls plant into one of the largest COVID-19 clusters in the country exposed the systematic disregard for workplace safety in the name of uninterrupted production.

5. **Lobbying against environmental regulation** — The company spent significant resources blocking legislative standards that would have forced a transition to safer manure-management technologies.

6. **Climate footprint** — Open lagoons produce methane and nitrous oxide; Smithfield resisted mandatory biogas capture while publicly declaring "sustainable development" commitments.

7. **Opaque structure after the WH Group transition** — The Chinese acquisition created a corporate structure that complicates accountability and tax oversight in the USA.

8. **Court victories as a turning point** — Plaintiffs from neighbouring communities won multi-million-dollar awards in court, yet Smithfield's lobbying led to legislative caps on such payouts in North Carolina — a precedent for corporate suppression of legal protection.
