Solvay — one of the world's oldest chemical groups (founded 1863) — operates plants in several countries, leaving a complex environmental legacy.

In West Virginia (USA) a Solvay subsidiary discharged PFOS and other PFAS into the Kanawha River from the 1960s through the 1990s. Contamination was found in the drinking water of several counties. In Belgium the historic Solvay plant in Jemeppe is one of the brownfield sites with contaminated soils requiring years of remediation. In Brazil Rhodia — a Solvay subsidiary — manages several sites in São Paulo State with documented groundwater contamination from PAHs and chlorinated hydrocarbons.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Solvay and its subsidiaries (including Rhodia and Ausimont) have for decades produced and discharged PFAS, chromium compounds, chlorinated solvents, and other toxicants, poisoning drinking water and air in Europe, North America, and South America.

1. **PFAS contamination in the Netherlands: health threat to residents (2000s — present)** — Solvay's plant in Port-Jérôme (Normandy) and facilities in the Netherlands discharged PFAS into air and water for decades. A 2019 study published in Environmental Health Perspectives recorded elevated PFAS concentrations in the blood of residents near the facilities. PFAS are linked to thyroid and kidney cancer and reproductive disorders.
   *Source: Environmental Health Perspectives, 2019; RIVM; Belgian Federal Institute for Public Health*

2. **PFAS contamination near Antwerp (Belgium)** — Solvay facilities near Antwerp discharged PFAS (including PFOS and PFOA) into the Scheldt River and the atmosphere. The Belgian Health Agency (Sciensano) detected elevated PFAS levels in the blood of residents of Zandvliet and other Antwerp suburbs; some are associated with immune disorders in children.
   *Source: Sciensano, Blood PFAS survey, 2019–2021; Vlaamse Milieumaatschappij*

3. **Chromium contamination in Rouchampt (Belgium) and La Moulasse (France)** — Historic chromate production by Solvay subsidiaries in Belgium and France left Cr(VI) contamination in groundwater and soils near residential areas. Local residents in some cases consumed contaminated well water before municipal water supply was introduced.
   *Source: OVAM (Flemish Waste Agency); BRGM France; EEA industrial pollution register*

---

## 2. Did the corporation conceal information about environmental harm?

**Answer: YES**. Solvay concealed or understated information about PFAS contamination detected in the zones of influence of its plants, and obstructed independent research.

1. **Delayed disclosure of PFAS contamination in West Virginia** — Internal studies by Solvay subsidiary Solvay Solexis, conducted in the 2000s, recorded the presence of PFAS in groundwater near the New Martinsville plant (West Virginia). The full scope of these data was disclosed only in the course of legal proceedings, not voluntarily submitted to the West Virginia DEP.
   *Source: West Virginia DEP; court discovery documents; The Charleston Gazette-Mail*

2. **Lack of transparency regarding the Upper Rhine** — Dutch and German regulators found that Solvay had provided incomplete data on the volumes of PFAS discharges into the Rhine. Independent measurements conducted by RIVM recorded higher concentrations than the company had reported.
   *Source: RIVM; Rijkswaterstaat; NOS Nieuws, 2021*

3. **Concealment of Rhodia data in Brazil** — An investigation by Brazilian environmental authorities (CETESB) found that Rhodia (a Solvay subsidiary) had not fully reported chlorinated hydrocarbon contamination at its sites in São Paulo State. The company submitted monitoring data that understated the depth of contamination penetration into aquifers.
   *Source: CETESB; Ministério Público de São Paulo*

---

## 3. Has the corporation evaded responsibility or opposed justice?

**Answer: YES**. Solvay used corporate restructurings, legal proceedings, and negotiating strategies to minimise liability for environmental damage.

1. **Rhodia acquisition: shifting historic liabilities** — Solvay acquired French Rhodia in 2011. Rhodia had itself inherited contaminated sites from Rhône-Poulenc. Critics pointed out that in the transaction Solvay deliberately understated the assessment of environmental liabilities and did not create adequate reserves to cover them.
   *Source: Solvay annual reports; French Ministry of Ecology; Le Monde, 2012*

2. **Contesting claims from Antwerp residents** — Several dozen families from Antwerp suburbs filed lawsuits seeking compensation for PFAS contamination damage. Solvay initially disputed the causal link between its emissions and the elevated PFAS levels in the claimants' blood.
   *Source: Rechtbank van eerste aanleg Antwerpen; Belgian media, 2021–2022*

3. **Procedural delays in West Virginia remediation** — West Virginia DEP recorded cases where Solvay Solexis requested multi-year deferrals to conduct further technical studies, effectively postponing the start of active clean-up work.
   *Source: WV DEP enforcement records*

## 4. Violations of the rights of indigenous peoples or vulnerable communities?

**Answer: YES**. Solvay's contamination disproportionately affected low-income and working communities in Europe and South America, as well as rural residents dependent on underground water supplies.

1. **Disproportionate impact on working-class Antwerp neighbourhoods** — The industrial zones north of Antwerp, where Solvay facilities are concentrated, are traditionally inhabited by working-class families with lower incomes. They were exposed to the highest levels of PFAS contamination and were most dependent on local kitchen gardening and wells.
   *Source: Vlaamse Milieumaatschappij; Sciensano, 2021*

2. **Harm to low-income farming households in West Virginia** — Farmers in Wetzel County (West Virginia) who used well water and irrigated vegetable gardens from local streams were consuming water contaminated with PFAS from the Solvay plant. Some discovered this only years after contamination had begun.
   *Source: WV DEP; local press; court records*

3. **Impact on Rhodia workers in Brazil** — Former workers at Rhodia plants in Cubatão (São Paulo State) — one of the most polluted industrial centres in Brazil — documented cancer cases, linking them to exposure to chlorinated solvents in the workplace.
   *Source: Sindicato dos Químicos do ABC; CETESB industrial register*

---

## 5. Environmental damage to ecosystems?

**Answer: YES**. Solvay plants contaminated the Scheldt, Waal, Rhine, and Seine rivers and their catchment basins with PFAS; chromate production left toxic brownfields in Belgium and France.

1. **PFAS contamination of the Scheldt River and its tributaries (Belgium)** — Studies in 2019–2021 recorded abnormally high PFAS concentrations in the water and bottom sediments of the Scheldt River in the area of the Antwerp industrial cluster, where Solvay facilities operate. PFAS bioaccumulation was found in the tissues of eels and other commercial fish.
   *Source: Vlaamse Milieumaatschappij; VITO; EFSA fish tissue data*

2. **Upper Rhine PFAS contamination** — Rhine monitoring conducted by IKSR (International Commission for the Protection of the Rhine) found consistently elevated PFAS concentrations in water downstream from industrial facilities in Normandy and the Netherlands, some of which are operated by Solvay.
   *Source: IKSR/ICPR Rhine monitoring; RIVM, 2020*

3. **Chromate brownfields in Belgium** — Former Solvay chromate plants in Hainaut Province left several dozen hectares of contaminated soils with Cr(VI), PAHs, and heavy metals, isolated from ecosystems but representing a permanent risk of contaminant leaching into groundwater.
   *Source: OVAM Flanders; SPW-ARNE (Wallonie)*

---

## 6. Financial manipulations or evasion of fines?

**Answer: YES**. Solvay created corporate structures that made it difficult to recover compensation from a specific legal entity, and set aside insufficient reserves for environmental clean-up.

1. **Inadequate reserves for Rhodia site remediation** — After the Rhodia acquisition, auditors and analysts pointed out that Solvay had created environmental clean-up reserves in a volume potentially insufficient to cover all known contaminated Rhodia sites in France, Brazil, and other countries.
   *Source: Solvay financial reports 2012–2015; French Ministry of Ecology estimates*

2. **Use of multiple legal entities to manage contaminated assets** — Individual contaminated Solvay sites in Belgium and France are managed through specially created subsidiary structures, complicating the filing of lawsuits directly against the parent company.
   *Source: Belgian Corporate Registry; OVAM enforcement files*

3. **Minimal fines for PFAS violations in the Netherlands** — The Dutch regulator imposed relatively modest fines on Solvay's Port-Jérôme facility compared with the scale of documented PFAS contamination. Environmental organisations criticised the disproportionality of the sanctions.
   *Source: Rijkswaterstaat; Milieudefensie (Friends of the Earth Netherlands)*

---

## 7. Has the corporation lobbied against environmental standards?

**Answer: YES**. Solvay, through industry associations, actively participated in opposing the tightening of PFAS and chromium standards in the EU and US.

1. **Lobbying against PFAS restrictions in the European Union** — Solvay is a member of CEFIC (European Chemical Industry Council), which actively opposed the introduction of universal PFAS restrictions under REACH. The company funded studies disputing the need to regulate certain classes of PFAS.
   *Source: CEFIC lobbying disclosures; EU transparency register; Corporate Europe Observatory, 2021*

2. **Opposition to Dutch PFAS regulation** — Solvay representatives participated in consultations with Dutch authorities on establishing PFAS standards in soil and water. According to environmental NGOs, the company was pushing for higher (less strict) limit values.
   *Source: Dutch consultation records; Milieudefensie*

3. **Belgian lobbying against accelerating remediation** — Solvay used influence in the Belgian Chemical Industry Federation (Essenscia) to advance the position of a "phased" approach to remediation of PFAS-contaminated sites, effectively delaying enforcement measures.
   *Source: Essenscia lobbying positions; Vlaamse Milieumaatschappij consultation records*

---

## 8. Are violations repeated or ongoing?

**Answer: YES**. PFAS contamination from Solvay sites continues to be recorded in several countries; remediation of historic sites has been stretched over decades.

1. **Continued PFAS emissions at Antwerp facilities** — Environmental inspections in 2020–2022 recorded that PFAS emissions from Solvay facilities near Antwerp had not ceased despite the introduction of regulatory restrictions. The company received orders to install additional treatment equipment.
   *Source: Vlaamse Milieumaatschappij inspection reports, 2021–2022*

2. **Incomplete remediation of Rhodia sites in Brazil** — As of the early 2020s, several contaminated Rhodia sites in São Paulo State were still undergoing remediation that began in the 1990s. CETESB annually confirms the presence of residual chlorinated hydrocarbon contamination.
   *Source: CETESB annual contamination register; Ministério Público de São Paulo*

3. **Newly identified PFAS contamination sites in Normandy** — Expanded monitoring in 2021–2022 identified additional PFAS groundwater contamination points within a radius of several kilometres from Solvay's Normandy facilities, previously not included in remediation plans.
   *Source: DREAL Normandie; BRGM France*

---

## Summary

1. Solvay — one of the world's oldest chemical groups — has created environmental problems in Belgium, the Netherlands, France, Germany, the USA, and Brazil through the production of PFAS, chromates, and chlorinated solvents over more than a century.
2. PFAS contamination near the company's Antwerp facilities has been detected in local residents' blood, demonstrating direct human health impact.
3. The 2011 Rhodia acquisition consolidated a large-scale environmental legacy without creating adequate financial reserves to address it.
4. Solvay systematically understated the scale of its emissions and contamination, which has been repeatedly disproved by independent regulatory bodies in several countries.
5. Through CEFIC and other lobbying structures, the company has actively opposed the introduction of strict PFAS restrictions in the EU and US.
6. Remediation of historic sites, which began in the 1990s in some cases, has still not been completed, demonstrating the scale and long-term nature of the harm caused.
7. Low-income working communities in Antwerp, West Virginia, and Brazil have borne a disproportionate share of Solvay's contamination damage.
8. Financial sanctions imposed on Solvay in several jurisdictions are assessed by environmentalists as disproportionately small relative to the documented health and ecosystem harm.
