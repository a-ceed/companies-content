title: Starbucks

crime: Billions of disposable cups with plastic lids — largely unrecyclable
region: Entire planet, urban waste streams
---
Starbucks produces approximately 8 billion disposable cups per year. Despite pledges to introduce reusable cups, the vast majority of cups used are single-use, lined with plastic, and end up in landfills rather than recycling streams.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Starbucks has systematically violated labour rights — both in its own cafés and on the coffee plantations of its suppliers — and has employed aggressive tax schemes that deprive governments of resources.

1. **Working conditions on coffee plantations in Ethiopia and Guatemala (2003–2019)** — Investigative reporting by Global Exchange and a BBC documentary (2003, 2014) documented that workers on coffee plantations of Starbucks suppliers in Ethiopia and Guatemala receive less than $1 a day for long working hours. Children participate in seasonal harvesting. Starbucks declared purchases of "ethically sourced coffee" while working conditions remained unchanged.
   *Source: Global Exchange "Starbucks: Fair Trade or Foul", 2003; BBC Panorama "Starbucks: The Truth About Your Coffee", 2014*

2. **Aggressive tax schemes in the UK and EU (2009–2014)** — A Reuters investigation (2012) showed that Starbucks paid corporate income tax only twice during 15 years of presence in the UK, using a royalty scheme to shift profits to the Netherlands and Switzerland. The company reported losses while demonstrating the profitability of the British market to investors. Tax losses in government budgets directly reduced spending on healthcare and social protection.
   *Source: Reuters Special Report "Starbucks Pays Minimal UK Tax", 2012; UK Parliament Treasury Committee, 2012*

3. **Violations of union rights and unlawful dismissal of activists (2021–2023)** — The US National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) issued more than 100 complaints against Starbucks for unlawful obstruction of union activity — threats, unlawful dismissals, reductions in benefits for workers who joined unions. By 2023 the complaints covered more than 50 states.
   *Source: NLRB Case Filings 2021–2023; Workers United Union Complaint Database*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Starbucks is one of the world's largest producers of disposable cups, creates large-scale plastic pollution, and bears responsibility for environmental violations in its coffee supply chain.

1. **6–8 billion disposable cups per year (2000–2023)** — Starbucks cups have a polyethylene plastic lining making them unsuitable for most paper recycling lines. According to the Plastic Pollution Coalition, fewer than 1% of Starbucks cups are actually recycled. This is one of the largest streams of non-recyclable waste in the food industry.
   *Source: Plastic Pollution Coalition, "Starbucks Cup Problem", 2021; As You Sow shareholder report, 2022*

2. **Water pollution near supplier coffee plantations (2010–2020)** — Coffee cherry processing (wet method) generates highly concentrated organic effluent — "coffee pulp" — which enters rivers in Central America and Ethiopia. Several environmental organisations documented mass fish kills in Guatemalan rivers adjacent to coffee production zones for major exporters, including Starbucks supply chains.
   *Source: Rainforest Alliance Water Risk Assessment, 2019; Environmental Justice Foundation, 2020*

3. **Plastic lids and straws in the world ocean (2015–2022)** — Starbucks plastic lids and straws are recorded in hundreds of thousands of instances at beach clean-ups worldwide each year. The Ocean Conservancy report (2019) mentioned branded Starbucks lids as among the most commonly found pollution items on US beaches.
   *Source: Ocean Conservancy International Coastal Cleanup Report, 2019; Break Free From Plastic Audit*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Starbucks has systematically made public commitments to environmental transformation — reusable cups, compostable packaging, waste reduction — while real indicators remained virtually unchanged.

1. **"Reusable cup" promise by 2015, then 2025 (2008–2022)** — In 2008 Starbucks set a target to reach 25% reusable cups by 2015. By 2022 the share was approximately 1.5%. Deadlines were repeatedly pushed back; in 2020 the company again moved the target to 2025.
   *Source: As You Sow Shareholder Resolution, 2021; Starbucks Global Impact Reports*

2. **"Eco-friendly" paper straws as a "revolution" (2019–2020)** — Starbucks widely advertised the switch from plastic to paper straws as a key environmental step. Independent tests showed that laminated paper straws are also non-recyclable and become compostable waste only under industrial composting conditions — unavailable to most consumers.
   *Source: WRAP UK, 2019; BBC Science, "Are paper straws actually recyclable?", 2019*

3. **"Greener Cup" — shifting costs to a consortium (2012–2019)** — Starbucks proclaimed that it was funding the development of a "compostable cup" through a special consortium. The project, having consumed several million dollars, was not realised at industrial scale by the promised deadline (2015), while the volume of non-recyclable cups produced continued to grow.
   *Source: NextGen Cup Challenge history; Fortune, "Starbucks Cup Problem", 2021*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Starbucks actively uses ESG narratives in corporate reports, advertising campaigns, and partnerships with international organisations.

1. **C.A.F.E. Practices programme — proprietary "ethical" certification (2004–2023)** — Starbucks introduced its own C.A.F.E. Practices (Coffee and Farmer Equity) standard, labelling most of its purchases as "ethical". Critics point out that the standard was developed by Starbucks itself and is audited by an organisation paid by the company, which precludes independent assessment.
   *Source: Global Exchange, 2003; Ethical Consumer Magazine, 2021*

2. **Annual Global Environmental & Social Impact Report (2017–2023)** — Starbucks publishes extensive ESG reports with declarations on climate targets, waste reduction, and farmer support. The reports are accompanied by large-scale PR campaigns and distributed at business forums.
   *Source: Starbucks Corporation Global Impact Reports 2018–2023*

3. **"Greener Starbucks" campaign (2018–2022)** — A series of Starbucks marketing campaigns highlighted the transition to "greener" ingredients, packaging, and store formats. Advertising videos were actively promoted on social media and in press, while the volumes of single-use packaging remained unchanged.
   *Source: Starbucks Marketing Campaigns, 2018–2022*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Starbucks has repeatedly achieved high positions in ESG organisation rankings.

1. **Inclusion in "World's Most Ethical Companies" list (Ethisphere, 2019–2022)** — Starbucks was included for several consecutive years in the ranking of the world's most ethical companies by the Ethisphere Institute, which was actively used in corporate communications.
   *Source: Ethisphere Institute, Most Ethical Companies List 2019–2022*

2. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI) (2015–2021)** — Starbucks was included in the DJSI composition in the "Restaurants & Bars" category, using the index inclusion as an ESG marketing tool when engaging with institutional investors.
   *Source: S&P Global DJSI Yearbooks 2016–2021*

3. **CDP "A-" rating (2020–2022)** — CDP assigned Starbucks a high category for climate data disclosure. The company actively cited this rating in investor materials without disclosing the full scope of emissions from dairy products and single-use packaging.
   *Source: CDP Scores 2020–2022*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Starbucks cups and products carry labelling that creates a false environmental impression.

1. **Rainforest Alliance labelling on cups and menus (2015–2022)** — Starbucks places the Rainforest Alliance logo on its cups and in the menu of coffee beverages. The cup itself is non-recyclable, while the "sustainable coffee" labelling does not address the environmental consequences of the single-use packaging.
   *Source: Plastic Pollution Coalition, 2021; Ethical Consumer UK, 2021*

2. **"Recyclable" wording on new polypropylene lids (2019–2022)** — Starbucks developed a new lid for cold beverages, labelling it as "recyclable". However, PP-type plastic is accepted for recycling in only 10–15% of municipal recycling systems in the US, making the claim de facto misleading.
   *Source: The Recycling Partnership, 2020; Earth911 analysis, 2021*

3. **"Ethically sourced coffee" phrase on whole-bean packaging (2010–2023)** — Starbucks labels most C.A.F.E. Practices products as "ethically sourced", while working conditions on supplier plantations in Ethiopia and Guatemala have been documented as failing to meet basic ILO standards.
   *Source: BBC Panorama, 2014; Global Exchange Reports*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Starbucks regularly organises or sponsors environmental initiatives whose scale is negligible against the backdrop of the annual billions of disposable cups.

1. **"Bring Your Own Cup Day" — one day a year (2011–2022)** — Starbucks held an annual day when customers who brought their own cup received a 10-cent discount. Calculations show that even 100% participation on that day would not reduce the annual waste volume by more than 0.003%.
   *Source: Plastic Pollution Coalition analysis, 2019; As You Sow Report*

2. **"Earth Month" litter pick events (2015–2022)** — In April Starbucks organises clean-ups near its cafés in various countries. The volume of litter collected amounts to tonnes — while the company annually produces hundreds of thousands of tonnes of non-recyclable packaging.
   *Source: Starbucks CSR Reports 2016–2022*

3. **Reusable cup trial in 25 Seattle restaurants (2019)** — Starbucks announced a "revolutionary" reusable cup pilot in 25 Seattle cafés, widely covered by the press. At the same time the company was opening thousands of new outlets worldwide, producing billions of disposable cups.
   *Source: Starbucks Press Release, 2019; Fortune analysis*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Starbucks funded industry associations lobbying for voluntary approaches instead of mandatory standards, and promoted the concept of "conscious consumption" as an alternative to systemic regulation.

1. **Lobbying through NRA against plastic ban laws (2018–2021)** — Starbucks, through membership in the National Restaurant Association, participated in lobbying against legislative initiatives to restrict single-use plastic in several states, insisting on voluntary industry commitments.
   *Source: Center for Responsive Politics; NRA Lobbying Disclosures, 2018–2020*

2. **Opposition to mandatory "non-recyclable" labelling (2019–2022)** — Starbucks, together with other industry players, opposed the introduction of mandatory "not recyclable" labelling on its cups, preferring to continue placing potentially misleading environmental symbols.
   *Source: Earthjustice Petition, 2021; FTC Green Guides discussions*

3. **"My Starbucks Reward" loyalty programme with eco-emphasis — shifting responsibility to the consumer (2015–2022)** — Starbucks promoted a loyalty programme with an environmental positioning, offering "green" points for using a reusable cup, thereby emphasising the buyer's personal choice rather than the systemic responsibility of the producer.
   *Source: Starbucks Rewards Marketing materials, 2015–2022; Break Free From Plastic analysis*

## Summary

1. **Mass harm to people?** — **YES** — Working conditions on supplier plantations in Ethiopia and Guatemala (less than $1/day); tax schemes in the UK and EU (Reuters, 2012); more than 100 NLRB complaints for unlawful union suppression (2021–2023).
2. **Major environmental disasters?** — **YES** — 6–8 billion non-recyclable cups per year (less than 1% actually recycled); river pollution from coffee effluent at suppliers; plastic lids and straws top findings at beach clean-ups.
3. **Greenwashing?** — **YES** — Reusable cup commitments missed three times (2015, 2020, 2025); paper straw advertising without solving the real problem; failed "Greener Cup" consortium.
4. **ESG marketing?** — **YES** — Proprietary C.A.F.E. Practices certification; annual Global Impact Reports; "Greener Starbucks" campaign.
5. **ESG award wins?** — **YES** — World's Most Ethical Companies (Ethisphere, 2019–2022); DJSI inclusion (2015–2021); CDP "A-" rating (2020–2022).
6. **Eco-labels on harmful products?** — **YES** — Rainforest Alliance logo on non-recyclable cups; "Recyclable" on lids with limited actual recyclability; "Ethically sourced" despite labour standard violations on plantations.
7. **Negligible eco-events?** — **YES** — "Bring Your Own Cup Day" once a year against billions of disposable cups; April clean-ups; reusable cup pilot in 25 Seattle cafés.
8. **Lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?** — **YES** — Lobbying against plastic bans through NRA; opposition to "non-recyclable" labelling; eco-emphasis loyalty programme shifting responsibility to consumers.
