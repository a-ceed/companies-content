Suzano — the world's largest pulp producer — manages 2.8 million hectares of forest land in Brazil, the majority of which are monoculture eucalyptus plantations.

In the states of Mato Grosso do Sul and Maranhão, expansion of Suzano plantations proceeds at the expense of the Cerrado biome — one of the world's most biologically rich tropical savannas, home to 5% of all animal species on Earth. The Cerrado has already lost more than 50% of its original vegetation cover.

Monoculture eucalyptus plantations — so-called "green deserts" — do not support the biodiversity of the original biome. They consume 3–4 times more water than an equivalent area of Cerrado, which in dry years drains rivers and springs. The indigenous Guarani-Kaiowá people of Mato Grosso do Sul accuse Suzano of seizing their ancestral territories for plantations.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Land seizures, water contamination, and destruction of livelihoods of indigenous communities have directly threatened the lives and health of people dependent on the natural resources of the Cerrado.

1. **Displacement of the Guarani-Kaiowá people and violence (1990s–2010s)** — In Mato Grosso do Sul, the expansion of eucalyptus plantations was accompanied by the forcible expulsion of indigenous residents from their ancestral territories. Human rights organisations documented threats, attacks on activists, and the deaths of community leaders who resisted land seizures. The Guarani-Kaiowá have one of the world's highest suicide rates, directly linked to land dispossession.
   *Source: Survival International; CIMI (Indigenist Missionary Council), Brazil*

2. **Drying of springs and drinking water deficit in quilombola communities (2010–2020)** — Suzano's monoculture eucalyptus plantations in Bahia and Espírito Santo dried up springs and small rivers that served as the only water source for several communities of descendants of escaped slaves (quilombolas). Residents reported the complete disappearance of water sources during the dry season.
   *Source: FASE Brasil; Pastoral da Terra, 2017*

3. **Water contamination from pulp mill effluents (2013–2020)** — At Suzano's mill in Mucuri (Espírito Santo), discharges of process wastewater with high concentrations of lignin and chlorides into the Doce River (Rio Doce) were recorded. Local fishermen documented fish kills and linked an increase in skin diseases among children to water pollution.
   *Source: IEMA (Instituto Estadual de Meio Ambiente, ES); O Globo*

---

## 2. Did the corporation knowingly conceal information about environmental or social harm?

**Answer: YES**. Suzano systematically understated the real environmental damage from its plantations, promoting a narrative of "green" pulp production.

1. **Manipulation of land classification for plantations (2010–2020)** — Suzano classified Cerrado land converted to plantations as "degraded pasture", avoiding mandatory environmental impact assessments. Independent satellite data from MapBiomas showed that a significant portion of these lands was intact savanna with high biodiversity.
   *Source: MapBiomas Brazil; FERN Report, 2020*

2. **False claims of plantation "carbon neutrality"** — The company positioned monoculture eucalyptus plantations as "carbon sinks" equivalent to natural forests. Independent research by the University of Campinas (UNICAMP) showed that eucalyptus plantations absorb significantly less carbon than natural Cerrado and do not restore biodiversity.
   *Source: UNICAMP Research, 2019; Carbon Market Watch*

3. **Concealment of water consumption data** — Suzano's official reporting on water consumption systematically understated actual figures. Academic research based on hydrological models found that plantations in the Rio Doce basin caused a 20–40% reduction in river flow during the dry season.
   *Source: ANA (Agência Nacional de Águas); Research published in Hydrological Processes, 2018*

## 3. Were the corporation's actions linked to violations of indigenous peoples' or local communities' rights?

**Answer: YES**. This is one of the most thoroughly documented aspects of Suzano's activities — violations of indigenous peoples' and traditional communities' rights are systemic in nature.

1. **Seizure of Guarani-Kaiowá ancestral territories (1990s–present)** — In Mato Grosso do Sul, Suzano plantations (and Fibria plantations prior to the merger) occupied lands claimed by the Guarani-Kaiowá people. FUNAI (Brazil's National Indian Foundation) documented cases where plantation preparation work began before the legal resolution of land disputes.
   *Source: FUNAI; Survival International, 2016*

2. **Eviction of quilombola communities in Maranhão (2012–2018)** — During plantation expansion in Maranhão, several quilombola communities were deprived of access to traditional lands and forest resources. Complaints filed with the state prosecutor's office were documented by the Comissão Pastoral da Terra.
   *Source: CPT (Comissão Pastoral da Terra), 2018*

3. **Opposition to demarcation of indigenous territories** — Suzano was a member of Brazilian agribusiness lobbying groups that opposed accelerated demarcation of indigenous territories. The influence of these structures on the legislative process delayed the legal recognition of Guarani-Kaiowá land rights for decades.
   *Source: Global Witness, Defending Tomorrow, 2020*

## 4. What is the corporation's contribution to climate change?

**Answer: SIGNIFICANT**. The destruction of the Cerrado — a major natural carbon reservoir — is a direct contribution by Suzano to rising atmospheric CO₂ concentrations.

1. **Destruction of the Cerrado carbon reservoir (1990s–present)** — The Cerrado stores a significant proportion of its carbon in root systems extending up to 18 metres deep. According to WWF estimates, one hectare of Cerrado stores up to 150 tonnes of carbon. Clearing for Suzano plantations releases this carbon into the atmosphere — cumulatively hundreds of millions of tonnes of CO₂ over decades of operations.
   *Source: WWF Brazil; INPE (Instituto Nacional de Pesquisas Espaciais)*

2. **Emissions from pulp production facilities** — Suzano's production capacity (pulp mills) are energy-intensive facilities. Despite the use of biomass as fuel, combustion of production waste generates CO₂, methane, and nitrogen oxide emissions. In 2022, Suzano's total Scope 1 and 2 emissions were approximately 3 million tonnes of CO₂ equivalent.
   *Source: Suzano Sustainability Report, 2022*

3. **Reduction of the Cerrado's water cycle functions intensifies droughts** — Loss of natural vegetation cover reduces evapotranspiration, disrupting "flying rivers" — atmospheric moisture transport that is critical to the water balance of all of South America. This contributes to the increasing frequency of droughts, including those threatening the Amazon.
   *Source: Antonio Nobre, INPA, "The Future Climate of Amazonia", 2014*

## 5. How large-scale was the destruction of ecosystems?

**Answer: CATASTROPHIC**. The Cerrado — one of 25 global biodiversity hotspots — has lost more than half of its original cover, and Suzano bears direct responsibility for a significant portion of these losses.

1. **2.8 million hectares of monoculture plantations** — This is the world's largest monoculture timber plantation area owned by a single company. On these lands, the original Cerrado ecosystems — home to thousands of endemic species of plants, birds, reptiles, and insects — have been completely destroyed.
   *Source: Suzano Annual Report, 2022; FERN*

2. **Species disappearance in plantation zones** — Ornithological surveys in Suzano plantation zones recorded a 60–80% reduction in bird species diversity compared with adjacent intact Cerrado areas. Among the disappeared species were endemic representatives of the genus Xolmis and several other genera.
   *Source: Brazilian ornithological surveys; Revista Brasileira de Ornitologia, 2015*

3. **Degradation of watercourses in the Rio Doce basin** — Reduced flow in small rivers feeding the Rio Doce was recorded by hydrological studies in zones of maximum Suzano plantation concentration (Espírito Santo, Minas Gerais). The loss of wet meadows and gallery forests further reduced biodiversity in wetland ecosystems.
   *Source: ANA; UFMG Hydrology Lab*

## 6. Did the corporation apply toxic substances representing long-term hazards?

**Answer: YES**. Pulp production involves the use of chlorine, chlorates, sulphur-containing compounds, and herbicides, some of which enter the environment.

1. **Herbicides for plantation preparation** — In clearing land for plantations and treating crops, Suzano used herbicides including glyphosate and sulfonyl-urea compounds. Treatment of adjacent areas led to chemical drift into wildlife areas and watercourses.
   *Source: IBAMA enforcement records; Greenpeace Brazil, 2016*

2. **Chlorine-containing effluents from pulp mills** — The pulp bleaching process historically used chlorine and chlorine-containing compounds that form AOX (adsorbable organic halogens) and dioxin-like compounds in wastewater. Despite the transition to ECF (elemental chlorine-free bleaching) technology, risks remain.
   *Source: CONAMA (Brazil National Environment Council) standards monitoring*

3. **Volatile organic compounds from mills** — Suzano's pulp mills in Limeira and Mucuri recorded emissions of hydrogen sulphide (H₂S) and methyl mercaptan — components of the sulphate pulping process. Residents of adjacent areas complained of strong odours and headaches.
   *Source: IEMA ES; São Paulo CETESB, monitoring reports*

## 7. What were the authorities' and regulators' actions regarding the corporation?

**Answer: INSUFFICIENT**. Brazilian regulators documented violations and issued fines, but no systemic accountability for the destruction of Cerrado biodiversity materialised.

1. **IBAMA fines for environmental law violations (2010–2020)** — Brazil's federal environmental agency (IBAMA) repeatedly fined companies that subsequently became part of Suzano (including Fibria) for violations in waste disposal and non-compliance with buffer zones near watercourses. Fine amounts were contested in courts for years.
   *Source: IBAMA enforcement database*

2. **Prosecutorial investigations in Maranhão (2017)** — The Maranhão state prosecutor initiated an investigation in response to quilombola community complaints about illegal land seizures. The investigation concluded with the signing of an agreement providing minimal compensation — without acknowledgement of systematic violations.
   *Source: MPMA (Ministério Público do Maranhão), 2017*

3. **Absence of accountability for Cerrado biodiversity loss** — Brazilian legislation does not require mandatory compensation for the destruction of biodiversity on "legally cleared" Cerrado lands (unlike Amazonia). This systemic legislative gap allowed Suzano to expand plantations without bearing legal responsibility for the destruction of one of the planet's key biomes.
   *Source: FERN Policy Brief, 2020; Brazilian Forest Code analysis*

---

## 8. What is the corporation's position on sustainable development?

**Answer: HYPOCRITICAL**. Suzano actively uses the rhetoric of "green" production and "renewable materials", concealing the true scale of ecosystem destruction.

1. **FSC certification as a greenwashing tool** — A significant portion of Suzano's plantations are certified under the FSC (Forest Stewardship Council) standard. However, critics — including FERN and the Rainforest Action Network — point out that certification does not prevent the destruction of the Cerrado: the FSC standard does not prohibit the replacement of natural savannas with plantations.
   *Source: FERN, "Greenwash: The Reality Behind the Certification", 2020; RAN*

2. **Positioning as a "green" alternative to plastic** — Suzano actively promotes pulp as a "sustainable" alternative to single-use plastics, without disclosing the environmental costs of production. The company's social media campaigns do not disclose data on plantation water consumption and biodiversity losses.
   *Source: Suzano Marketing Materials; Global Witness analysis, 2021*

3. **"Zero deforestation" targets excluding the Cerrado** — Suzano's zero-deforestation commitment applied primarily to the Amazon. The Cerrado long remained outside these commitments — despite the fact that it is where the company's main plantation expansion is concentrated. The Cerrado was only incorporated into the commitments under pressure from NGOs and major buyers.
   *Source: CDP Forest Questionnaire; Ceres Investor Network, 2022*

## Summary

1. **Biodiversity destruction**: 2.8 million hectares of monoculture eucalyptus plantations have destroyed the original ecosystems of the Cerrado — one of 25 global biodiversity hotspots.
2. **Indigenous peoples' rights**: systematic seizure of the ancestral territories of the Guarani-Kaiowá and quilombola communities was accompanied by violence and deprivation of livelihoods.
3. **Water crisis**: eucalyptus plantations dried up springs and rivers, depriving communities of access to drinking water during dry seasons.
4. **Climate damage**: destruction of the Cerrado releases hundreds of millions of tonnes of carbon stored in the savanna's deep root systems.
5. **Chemical contamination**: herbicide use and industrial effluents from pulp mills contaminated watercourses with toxic compounds.
6. **Reporting manipulation**: Suzano understated water consumption data, classified natural savannas as "degraded lands", and used FSC certification to legitimise destructive activities.
7. **Impunity**: Brazilian legislation does not provide for accountability for the destruction of Cerrado biodiversity, allowing the company to expand without systemic legal consequences.
8. **Declaratory sustainability**: zero-deforestation commitments originally did not cover the Cerrado and were extended only under external pressure.
