Syngenta produces atrazine — a herbicide banned in the EU but widely used in the United States and exported to developing countries. Atrazine is one of the most common drinking water contaminants in the US: the Center for Biological Diversity has filed lawsuits against the EPA for inadequate regulation of the substance.

Paraquat — Syngenta's herbicide banned in 32 countries — continues to be sold in the US, Brazil, and other countries. Even small doses of paraquat irreversibly damage lung tissue; a few millilitres are fatal if ingested without an antidote. Hundreds of poisonings are recorded annually in Brazil.

Clothianidin and thiamethoxam — Syngenta's neonicotinoids — are recognised as the primary chemical causes of bee colony collapse. The EU banned these substances in 2018 for outdoor use; however, Syngenta continues to export them to countries without equivalent bans.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Syngenta's pesticides are linked to mass poisonings of agricultural workers and drinking water contamination in several countries. Paraquat causes irreversible organ damage, atrazine causes hormonal disruptions, and field workers are directly exposed without adequate protection.

1. **Paraquat poisonings in Brazil (2000–2023)** — Hundreds of acute paraquat poisoning cases are registered annually in Brazil, some of them fatal. According to Pesticide Action Network data, more than 5,000 cases of poisoning by this Syngenta herbicide were recorded in the country between 2007 and 2019. Paraquat has no antidote and causes irreversible pulmonary fibrosis.
   *Source: Pesticide Action Network International, PAN report "Paraquat: A Chronic Killer", 2019*

2. **Atrazine contamination of drinking water in the USA (2003–2020)** — Atrazine is found in the drinking water of more than 30 million Americans, predominantly in the corn belt of the Midwest. In 2012, Syngenta settled a class action lawsuit for $105 million from water utilities in 6 states, acknowledging contamination. The EPA established that atrazine disrupts the endocrine system and is associated with birth defects.
   *Source: EPA Atrazine Review, 2016; Reuters, "Syngenta agrees to $105 million settlement", 2012*

3. **Worker poisonings from neonicotinoids in India (2012–2018)** — Workers on tea and cotton plantations in India who applied Syngenta's thiamethoxam-based insecticides reported acute neurological symptoms. PAN Asia Pacific documented hospitalisation cases in the states of Maharashtra and Tamil Nadu due to inadequate labelling and the absence of instructions in local languages.
   *Source: PAN Asia Pacific, "Highly Hazardous Pesticides in India", 2018*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Syngenta's pesticides caused mass die-offs of bees and other pollinators, contamination of aquatic ecosystems with atrazine, and soil degradation in agricultural regions.

1. **Bee colony collapse in Europe (2008–2018)** — Syngenta's thiamethoxam (Cruiser) was directly linked to mass bee colony collapse in Western Europe. In France in 2008, a "bee massacre" was recorded following treatment of maize with Cruiser; the French government temporarily banned the product. In 2018 the EU fully banned outdoor application of thiamethoxam.
   *Source: EFSA, "Conclusion on the peer review of neonicotinoids", 2018; Greenpeace "Bees in Decline", 2013*

2. **Atrazine contamination of rivers in the USA (1990s–2010s)** — According to USGS data, atrazine was found in 75% of streams and 40% of groundwater aquifers in US agricultural areas. The substance causes feminisation of male frogs and disruption of reproductive function in fish even at concentrations of 0.1 μg/l. The ecosystems of rivers in Illinois, Iowa, and Kansas were subjected to long-term contamination.
   *Source: USGS "Pesticides in the Nation's Streams", 2006; Tyrone Hayes (UC Berkeley), Nature, 2002*

3. **Soil contamination by paraquat in Latin America (1980–2020)** — Decades of paraquat application in Brazil, Colombia, and Ecuador led to accumulation of the substance in soils. Paraquat binds to soil particles and remains an active toxin for years, suppressing microbial communities and reducing fertility. Indigenous communities in Amazonia documented fish kills in rivers adjacent to treated lands.
   *Source: FAO, "Paraquat Risk Assessment", 2004; Global Witness, "Poison Exports", 2020*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Syngenta systematically promoted the image of a "responsible agribusiness", while simultaneously lobbying against bans on its most toxic products and continuing to export them to countries with weaker regulation.

1. **"Good Growth Plan" programme (2013–2020)** — Syngenta launched a global "Good Growth Plan", promising to "improve biodiversity" on 5 million hectares. At the same time the company continued sales of atrazine, thiamethoxam, and paraquat — substances directly destroying biodiversity. An independent analysis by Pesticide Action Network (2018) showed that the declared targets were not accompanied by reduced sales of hazardous substances.
   *Source: PAN International, "Good Growth Plan: A Critical Review", 2018*

2. **Positioning atrazine as "safe" (2000–2012)** — After atrazine was banned in the EU in 2004, Syngenta funded studies in the US presenting atrazine as safe for aquatic ecosystems and human health. It subsequently emerged that the company had conducted a campaign to discredit scientist Tyrone Hayes (UC Berkeley), who had demonstrated atrazine's endocrine effects.
   *Source: The New Yorker, "A Valuable Reputation", 2014; Internal Syngenta documents, disclosed in litigation*

3. **Marketing pesticides as "pollinator protection tools" (2019–2022)** — After the European ban on neonicotinoids, Syngenta switched to marketing new insecticides as "safe for bees", while simultaneously lobbying for relaxation of the bans. Independent studies (EFSA, 2021) showed that the proposed "safe alternatives" are also toxic to pollinators under field application conditions.
   *Source: EFSA, "Updated risk assessment of cyantraniliprole", 2021; Unearthed (Greenpeace), 2020*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Syngenta actively uses ESG rhetoric in reporting and investor communications, presenting itself as a leader in sustainable agriculture, despite continued sales of substances banned in developed countries.

1. **ESG reporting with emphasis on "food security" (2018–2023)** — Syngenta's annual ESG reports emphasise the company's contribution to fighting hunger and agricultural sustainability. Excluded from the reports is information about the scale of exports of EU-banned pesticides to Global South countries, as well as poisoning statistics in developing countries.
   *Source: Syngenta Sustainability Report 2022; PAN International Counter-Report, 2023*

2. **Participation in the Science Based Targets initiative (2021)** — Syngenta declared CO₂ reduction commitments under Science Based Targets. Critics point out that synthetic pesticide production is energy-intensive, and their application disrupts the carbon balance of soils — a key natural CO₂ sink.
   *Source: Science Based Targets Initiative, Syngenta commitment page, 2021*

3. **Positioning in MSCI ESG Ratings (2019–2022)** — Syngenta received an "BBB" rating from MSCI ESG, despite numerous lawsuits and documented evidence of environmental damage. MSCI's methodology is criticised for underweighting the external costs of pesticide contamination.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings, Syngenta profile, 2022*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Syngenta has been included in various ESG rankings and received industry awards that are at odds with the real environmental and social consequences of the company's activities.

1. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (2010–2017)** — For several years Syngenta was included in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI) as a "sustainable company" in the chemicals and agribusiness sector. This status was actively used in marketing and negotiations with institutional investors.
   *Source: S&P Global / DJSI annual review reports, 2010–2017*

2. **Corporate Knights "green" business award (2016)** — Syngenta was included in Corporate Knights' "Global 100 Most Sustainable Corporations" list. Critics from PAN International and Greenpeace issued rebuttals pointing to the gap between declared principles and the volume of toxic pesticide sales.
   *Source: Corporate Knights Global 100, 2016; PAN response, 2016*

3. **WEF "innovation in agribusiness" recognition (2019)** — Syngenta was presented as a partner of the World Economic Forum's "New Vision for Agriculture" initiative and was recognised for "digital agro-innovations". The partnership was used in corporate PR communications, despite ongoing legal proceedings regarding toxic products.
   *Source: WEF New Vision for Agriculture, partnership announcement, 2019*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Several Syngenta products were labelled as "safe" or received certifications that do not reflect actual environmental harm.

1. **Labelling Cruiser (thiamethoxam) as "low-toxicity for bees" prior to the EU ban (2012–2018)** — Before the introduction of the European ban, Syngenta labelled Cruiser OSR as "suitable for use in areas with pollinator activity". Meanwhile, independent research was demonstrating lethal and sub-lethal harm to bee colonies under field conditions.
   *Source: EFSA Opinion on neonicotinoids, 2013; Buglife UK, "A review of the direct and indirect impacts of neonicotinoids on bees", 2012*

2. **Promoting atrazine with an "EPA-approved" mark as equivalent to safety (2005–2015)** — Syngenta used the fact of atrazine's EPA registration as a marketing safety argument in countries where buyers trust US regulatory standards. The EPA repeatedly expressed concern about health risks but did not introduce a ban due to lobbying pressure.
   *Source: EPA Atrazine Human Health Risk Assessment, 2016; Environmental Working Group, "Syngenta's Playbook", 2019*

3. **Positioning paraquat as "biodegradable" in marketing materials for Asia (1990s–2000s)** — Historical Syngenta marketing materials for Southeast Asian markets contained claims about the rapid decomposition of paraquat in soil. This contradicted scientific data on the substance's preserved toxicity in soil for years and decades.
   *Source: Pesticide Action Network Asia Pacific, "Paraquat Marketing Claims Analysis", 2003*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Syngenta conducted PR campaigns under environmental branding that are incommensurate in scale with the damage caused by its products.

1. **"Operation Pollinator" programme (from 2001)** — Syngenta launched a programme to plant wildflowers on field margins to support pollinators — "Operation Pollinator". The programme covered several thousand hectares in Europe. By comparison: Syngenta's neonicotinoid sales were applied to tens of millions of hectares annually, destroying the same bee populations.
   *Source: Syngenta Operation Pollinator official page; Friends of the Earth, "Bees and Pesticides", 2014*

2. **"Field day" demonstrating biodiversity (2018–2022)** — Syngenta annually held demonstration "field days" in Europe and Latin America, showcasing agroecological practices on experimental plots of a few hectares. The events were actively covered in corporate media, while sales volumes of toxic compounds remained unchanged.
   *Source: Syngenta press releases, 2018–2022*

3. **Soil restoration project in Brazil (2020–2022)** — Syngenta announced a "Regenerative Agriculture" project in Brazil covering 10,000 farms. Independent agroecologists pointed out that "regenerative farming" in Syngenta's interpretation still involves the application of synthetic herbicides and insecticides, which are fundamentally incompatible with the regenerative concept.
   *Source: Syngenta Brazil Regenerative Agriculture report, 2021; La Via Campesina statement, 2021*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Syngenta systematically funded research and communications campaigns aimed at creating scientific doubt around the proven harm of its products.

1. **Campaign against scientist Tyrone Hayes (2002–2014)** — After biologist Tyrone Hayes (UC Berkeley) published data on atrazine's endocrine effects on frogs, Syngenta launched a coordinated campaign to discredit him: it hired scientists to dispute his data, tracked his public appearances, and attempted to recruit EPA regulators. Internal company documents were disclosed during litigation.
   *Source: The New Yorker, "A Valuable Reputation" (Rachel Aviv), 2014; Court documents, Bell v. Syngenta, 2012*

2. **Funding "independent" scientific panels on pesticides (2010–2020)** — Syngenta funded a number of academic centres and "independent" expert panels that systematically published findings contradicting regulatory agency data on atrazine and neonicotinoid risks. Funding connections were not disclosed in publications.
   *Source: Unearthed (Greenpeace investigative journalism), "Syngenta's Influence Machine", 2020*

3. **Brussels lobbying against Farm to Fork (2020–2022)** — Syngenta was part of a consortium of agrochemical companies lobbying against the EU "Farm to Fork" strategy, which envisaged a 50% reduction in pesticide use by 2030. The company promoted a "food security" narrative, claiming that reducing pesticides would cause famine — despite FAO research showing the opposite.
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory, "Toxic Lobbying", 2021; Politico, "Pesticide lobby targets EU Green Deal", 2021*
