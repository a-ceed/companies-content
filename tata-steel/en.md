Tata Steel operates the largest integrated steelmaking complex in Europe in IJmuiden, Netherlands. The plant is the largest single source of CO₂ in the Netherlands, accounting for approximately 10% of the country's industrial emissions.

In 2022–2023 Dutch authorities recorded violations of dust and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon (PAH) standards near the plant. Residents of the villages of Wijk aan Zee and Beverwijk reported black dust settling on cars and kitchen gardens. The Dutch government required Tata Steel to invest in environmental upgrades under threat of enforcement measures.

In the Indian state of Odisha, planned expansion of iron ore extraction by Tata threatens forest lands of the Dongria Kondh people and water sources for millions of residents.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Tata Steel's plants in the UK, Netherlands, and India created direct threats to public health through air contamination with toxic particles, river poisoning, and disregard for occupational safety.

1. **Air pollution in Port Talbot, Wales (2010–2023)** — Tata Steel's Port Talbot plant repeatedly exceeded permitted PM2.5 and sulphur dioxide emission standards. Independent measurements in 2021 showed that PM2.5 concentrations near the plant exceeded WHO standards by 2–3 times. Local residents reported elevated rates of respiratory diseases and cancer. The Welsh Government launched a formal investigation following complaints from residents.
   *Source: Welsh Government Air Quality Report 2021; The Guardian, "Port Talbot steel pollution", 2022*

2. **Contamination of the Subarnarekha River, Jamshedpur, India (2015–2022)** — Industrial effluents from Tata Steel's Jamshedpur complex repeatedly entered the Subarnarekha River — a drinking water source for hundreds of thousands of people. In 2019, activists documented mass fish kills downstream. Water analysis showed exceedances of permissible levels of iron, zinc, and phenols.
   *Source: Centre for Science and Environment (CSE), India, 2019; Jharkhand Pollution Control Board reports*

3. **Industrial injuries and deaths at plants in India (2018–2023)** — Amnesty International documented systematic safety violations at Tata Steel plants in Odisha and Jharkhand. During 2018–2023, at least 12 workplace fatalities were recorded. Workers complained about the absence of protective equipment and pressure not to report accidents.
   *Source: Amnesty International, "Steel and Human Rights", 2022; Indian Ministry of Labour reports*

---

## 2. Has the corporation destroyed ecosystems and biodiversity?

**Answer: YES**. Ore extraction and industrial waste discharge destroyed river ecosystems, forests, and coastal areas.

1. **Destruction of the Subarnarekha River ecosystem, India (2010–2023)** — Long-term discharge of industrial effluents led to degradation of the river ecosystem. Biologists documented the disappearance of 15 fish species in the lower course of the river. Riparian forests over several thousand hectares are experiencing chronic stress from heavy metal soil contamination.
   *Source: Wildlife Institute of India, River Health Report 2020*

2. **Threat to Dongria Kondh forests in Odisha (2012–2023)** — Expansion of Tata Steel's mining operations in Odisha threatened forest territories that are home to the Dongria Kondh people. The planned quarries would affect several thousand hectares of tropical forest designated as an ecologically sensitive zone. Local communities with support from Amnesty International sought suspension of the projects.
   *Source: Amnesty International, 2021; Odisha Forest Department records*

3. **Marine zone contamination in the Netherlands (2020–2023)** — Industrial waste from the IJmuiden plant, including contaminated cooling water, affected the North Sea ecosystem. The Netherlands Institute for Sea Research (NIOZ) recorded elevated concentrations of heavy metals in bottom sediments near the plant. Coastal fishermen noted declining catches.
   *Source: NIOZ Research Report, 2022; Nederlandse Vissersbond complaints*

## 3. Has the corporation violated the rights of indigenous peoples and local communities?

**Answer: YES**. Tata Steel projects systematically violated indigenous peoples' right to free, prior, and informed consent.

1. **Eviction of tribal communities in Jharkhand (2006–2015)** — During the expansion of production capacity in Kalinganagar (Odisha), police opened fire on Adivasi tribal members protesting eviction in January 2006. Twelve people were killed. Amnesty International demanded an independent investigation. Tata Steel was forced to halt construction for several years.
   *Source: Amnesty International, "Kalinganagar killings", 2006; Human Rights Watch*

2. **Violation of FPIC rights in Odisha (2010–2022)** — Expansion of iron ore extraction in territories of the sacred mountains of the Dongria Kondh people was carried out without proper consultation with communities. Indigenous residents were not given the opportunity to participate in decision-making, despite the requirements of international law (ILO Convention No. 169).
   *Source: Survival International, 2021; UN Special Rapporteur on Indigenous Peoples, 2020*

3. **Displacement of fishing communities in Jharkhand (2014–2021)** — Industrial effluent contamination of rivers deprived thousands of fishing families dependent on the Subarnarekha River of their livelihoods. Local fishermen filed collective lawsuits, but courts delayed consideration of the cases for years. The company refused voluntary compensation.
   *Source: Centre for Science and Environment, 2019; Jharkhand High Court proceedings*

## 4. Has the corporation systematically violated environmental legislation?

**Answer: YES**. Violations were recorded in several jurisdictions over many years.

1. **Fines from the UK Environment Agency (2018–2022)** — The Port Talbot plant repeatedly violated permitted dust and SO₂ emission standards. Natural Resources Wales issued several warnings and imposed administrative fines. In 2021 the company received an official notice requiring immediate emissions reductions.
   *Source: Natural Resources Wales, Enforcement Notices 2021; UK Parliament inquiry*

2. **Orders from Dutch regulators (2022–2023)** — The Dutch Human Environment and Transport Inspectorate (ILT) recorded violations of PAH and heavy metal standards. The Dutch government publicly demanded that Tata Steel accelerate its "green" transformation under threat of forced closure of individual workshops.
   *Source: ILT Nederland, Annual Inspection Report 2023; NRC Handelsblad*

3. **Environmental standard violations in India (2015–2023)** — The Jharkhand State Pollution Control Board repeatedly issued notices to Tata Steel for violations of wastewater discharge standards. In 2020 the National Green Tribunal of India (NGT) ordered the company to pay compensation to communities affected by water resource contamination.
   *Source: National Green Tribunal Order, 2020; Jharkhand SPCB notices*

---

## 5. Has the corporation concealed information or misled the public?

**Answer: YES**. The company systematically understated the scale of pollution and delayed acknowledgement of problems.

1. **Underreporting of emissions data in the Netherlands (2019–2022)** — Investigative journalists from Follow the Money (Netherlands) found that Tata Steel's internal measurements showed higher pollution levels than the data submitted to regulators. The company denied deliberate underreporting but agreed to install additional monitoring systems.
   *Source: Follow the Money, "Tata Steel en de luchtkwaliteit", 2022*

2. **Denial of the connection to diseases in Port Talbot (2020–2023)** — Despite numerous epidemiological studies pointing to elevated lung cancer rates near the plant, Tata Steel for years denied a causal link between its activities and public health. The company funded alternative studies that disputed official data.
   *Source: Swansea University Health Research, 2021; The Guardian investigative report*

3. **Suppression of incidents in India (2006–2020)** — After the shooting of demonstrators in Kalinganagar in 2006, Tata Steel denied involvement in the decision to use force. Independent investigations showed that plant security had coordinated with the police. Information about industrial accidents was systematically excluded from official reports.
   *Source: Human Rights Watch, 2006; Amnesty International investigation*

---

## 6. Has the corporation opposed regulation and climate policy?

**Answer: YES**. Tata Steel actively lobbied against tightening environmental standards and delayed the transition to green technologies.

1. **Lobbying against tightening EU emissions standards (2018–2023)** — Tata Steel, together with other steel producers, lobbied the European Parliament against tightening CO₂ emission requirements for the steel industry. The company sought to expand free emission allowances under the EU ETS system, arguing the risk of "carbon leakage".
   *Source: Corporate Europe Observatory, "Steel lobbying in Brussels", 2021; Transparency Register EU*

2. **Delaying "green" transformation in the Netherlands (2019–2023)** — Despite public commitments to transition to hydrogen-based steelmaking by 2030, Tata Steel repeatedly postponed investment timelines. The Dutch government was forced to provide several billion euros in subsidies to accelerate the process.
   *Source: Dutch Ministry of Economic Affairs, 2023; Bankwatch analysis*

3. **Opposing closure of old furnaces in the UK (2020–2023)** — The company used the threat of job losses as leverage on the British government to obtain state subsidies and delay the closure of the most polluting blast furnaces in Port Talbot. The closure ultimately occurred only in 2023 after receiving £500 million in subsidies.
   *Source: UK Parliament hearings, 2023; The Financial Times*

---

## 7. Has the corporation violated workers' rights?

**Answer: YES**. Labour rights violations were recorded both in India and in European divisions.

1. **Forced labour of contractors in India (2015–2022)** — Human rights organisations documented Tata Steel's use of contract workers without adequate protection: without health insurance, without injury compensation, with wages below minimum levels. Migrants from poor states (Odisha, Chhattisgarh) worked in hazardous conditions without proper employment contracts.
   *Source: Labour Rights in Steel Report, 2021 (BSR); Amnesty International*

2. **Mass dismissals without adequate compensation in the UK (2015–2016)** — Following financial difficulties, Tata Steel announced the elimination of 1,200 jobs in the UK in 2015–2016. The Unite union accused the company of failing to comply with the consultation procedure and providing inadequate severance pay. The matter reached employment tribunals.
   *Source: Unite the Union press releases, 2016; BBC News*

3. **Anti-union actions in Jamshedpur (2017–2021)** — Several leaders of independent trade union organisations at the Jamshedpur plant reported pressure, surveillance, and dismissals following attempts to organise workers. The International Trade Union Confederation (ITUC) included Tata Steel in a list of companies with freedom of association violations.
   *Source: ITUC Global Rights Index, 2021; IndustriALL Global Union reports*

---

## 8. Is the corporation a significant greenhouse gas emitter?

**Answer: YES**. Tata Steel is one of the world's largest corporate CO₂ emitters.

1. **IJmuiden — the largest CO₂ source in the Netherlands** — The IJmuiden plant emits approximately 12–13 million tonnes of CO₂ annually, accounting for approximately 10% of the Netherlands' total industrial emissions. According to the Dutch PBL Environmental Assessment Agency, this makes Tata Steel the country's largest single industrial emitter.
   *Source: PBL Netherlands Environmental Assessment Agency, 2022; ETS registry EU*

2. **Total Tata Steel group emissions exceed 25 million tonnes of CO₂ per year** — According to the company's own annual reports, total Tata Steel group emissions (including plants in India, Netherlands, and other countries) exceed 25 million tonnes of CO₂ equivalent annually. This is comparable to the annual emissions of individual small countries.
   *Source: Tata Steel Sustainability Report 2022; Climate Accountability Institute*

3. **Slow progress in decarbonisation** — Despite declared targets for climate neutrality by 2045, the pace of emissions reduction remains insufficient. Independent analysts (SBTi) concluded that the company's current plans are incompatible with the goal of limiting warming to 1.5°C. The share of "green" steel in Tata Steel's production remains below 1%.
   *Source: Science Based Targets initiative analysis, 2023; Carbon Tracker*

## Summary

1. Tata Steel's Port Talbot plant (Wales) systematically exceeded PM2.5 and SO₂ standards for more than a decade, posing health threats to thousands of residents of adjacent areas.
2. Industrial effluents from facilities in Jamshedpur (India) contaminated the Subarnarekha River — a water source for hundreds of thousands of people — causing the destruction of river ecosystems.
3. The police shooting of 12 Adivasi protesters in Kalinganagar in 2006, linked to Tata Steel's expansion, went largely unpunished.
4. The company systematically violated the rights of indigenous peoples in Odisha, expanding extraction on their ancestral territories without obtaining proper consent.
5. The IJmuiden plant (Netherlands) is the country's largest single industrial CO₂ source — approximately 10% of the Netherlands' industrial emissions.
6. Tata Steel actively lobbied against tightening EU climate standards and delayed the transition to hydrogen technologies, seeking state subsidies.
7. Violations of the labour rights of contract workers in India and anti-union actions were documented by Amnesty International and ITUC.
8. Total group emissions exceed 25 million tonnes of CO₂ per year, and the pace of decarbonisation has been assessed by independent experts as incompatible with the Paris Agreement.
