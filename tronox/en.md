Tronox — the world's largest producer of titanium dioxide (a pigment for white paint, plastics, and sunscreens) — extracts ilmenite and rutile in unique coastal ecosystems of South Africa and Australia.

In KwaZulu-Natal (South Africa), ilmenite extraction in the Duvha region has destroyed large sections of coastal dunes with endemic vegetation. The International Union for Conservation of Nature has expressed concern about the impact on habitats of rare birds and amphibians.

At the Hamilton plant (Mississippi, USA), acid-process chemical processing of ilmenite produced toxic waste with high concentrations of sulphuric acid and heavy metals. The EPA required the company to conduct costly land reclamation.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Contamination from Tronox facilities has caused documented harm to the health of local communities.

1. **Site contamination in Hamilton, Mississippi** — Former chemical plant operations by Tronox in Hamilton left a legacy of chloride and heavy metal contamination in soil and groundwater. The EPA placed the site on the Superfund registry, citing health risks to local residents who obtain water from shallow wells.
   *Source: EPA Superfund Site — Kerr-McGee Chemical Hamilton Plant*

2. **Kerr-McGee contamination legacy (nuclear sites)** — Tronox is the successor to Kerr-McGee, which left a legacy of radioactive contamination at 52 sites across the United States. Lawsuits from descendants of the Navajo Nation documented health damage from radioactive waste generated by uranium production.
   *Source: U.S. Department of Justice — Kerr-McGee/Tronox bankruptcy settlement*

3. **$5.15 billion settlement (2014)** — During Tronox's bankruptcy (2009), the court ordered contamination damage compensation of $5.15 billion — one of the largest environmental settlements in US history. This sum was intended to cover cleanup at 2,000+ contaminated sites.
   *Source: U.S. DOJ — "United States v. Tronox" settlement (2014)*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Tronox's activities and those of its predecessor Kerr-McGee led to large-scale long-term contamination.

1. **Contamination of 2,000+ Kerr-McGee sites** — The Tronox bankruptcy proceedings revealed more than 2,000 contaminated sites across the US left by Kerr-McGee: oil refining, wood chemical, and nuclear production facilities. Total damage was assessed at $5.15 billion.
   *Source: U.S. DOJ Tronox settlement*

2. **Heavy mineral extraction in KwaZulu-Natal, South Africa** — Ilmenite extraction in the Duvha region destroyed sections of coastal dunes with unique flora. The IUCN expressed concern about the impact on habitats of endemic birds and reptiles.
   *Source: IUCN Red List assessments; EarthWorks mining impact reports*

3. **Acidic wastewater from processing plants** — The acid process for titanium dioxide production generates significant volumes of acidic wastewater containing iron, manganese, and other metals. Several Tronox facilities received regulatory orders for discharge standard violations.
   *Source: EPA enforcement actions against Tronox*

---

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Tronox positions titanium dioxide as a "safe" pigment, concealing the toxic production waste.

1. **Marketing TiO₂ as a "natural" and "safe" pigment** — The company promotes titanium dioxide as a non-toxic white pigment of natural origin, without disclosing that its production by the acid or chloride process generates toxic industrial waste requiring specialised storage.
   *Source: Tronox product marketing materials*

2. **Claims of "sustainable" production** — In corporate reports Tronox uses a narrative of "sustainable mining" and "minimal environmental footprint", while extraction in South Africa and Mozambique irreversibly destroys coastal dunes.
   *Source: Tronox Sustainability Report*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Tronox publishes ESG reports and declares emissions reduction targets.

1. **Annual ESG reports** — The company publishes ESG Reports to GRI and SASB standards, declaring reductions in CO₂ and water intensity per tonne of product.
   *Source: Tronox ESG Report 2022*

2. **Climate targets** — Tronox declared a target to reduce Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 30% by 2030 and achieve "net zero" by 2050.
   *Source: Tronox Corporate Website*

3. **"Responsible Mining" programme** — The company developed an internal responsible mining standard that is used in communications with paint and plastics buyers.
   *Source: Tronox Investor Relations materials*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: NO**. Tronox does not feature in lists of major ESG ratings or sustainability awards.

1. The company is not included in FTSE4Good, S&P 500 ESG, or similar indices. Its ESG ratings from MSCI and Sustainalytics remain average or below average for the chemicals industry.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings; Sustainalytics*

---

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Titanium dioxide in sunscreens is labelled as a "natural" and "safe" ingredient.

1. **"Natural" TiO₂ in sun protection cosmetics** — TiO₂ nanoparticles are widely used in "natural" and "mineral" sunscreens. Although the final product is safe, Tronox's production process generates toxic waste not reflected in the "natural mineral sunscreen" label.
   *Source: EWG Sunscreen Database; Environmental Science & Technology*

2. **TiO₂ from South African extraction sites in "eco-friendly" paints** — A number of "eco-friendly" paint manufacturers use Tronox TiO₂ without disclosing the environmental consequences of its extraction in South African coastal ecosystems.
   *Source: EarthWorks mining reports*

---

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Tronox's reclamation programmes are negligible compared with the scale of historical contamination.

1. **"Reclamation" after extraction in South Africa** — The company claims to be restoring coastal dunes after extraction, but ecological science assessments show that restored areas have significantly lower biodiversity than the original ecosystems.
   *Source: IUCN; South African National Biodiversity Institute*

2. **Local environmental education programmes near plants** — Tronox funds small educational programmes for schoolchildren in South Africa that are incommensurate with the scale of extractive operations' impact on coastal ecosystems.
   *Source: Tronox Community Reports*

---

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. The chemicals industry, including Tronox, lobbies against restrictions on the use of TiO₂ in food and cosmetics.

1. **Lobbying against the EU ban on TiO₂ in food** — The European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) in 2021 concluded that nano-TiO₂ as a food additive (E171) cannot be considered safe. Industry associations, including those representing Tronox, lobbied against this decision, promoting alternative scientific assessments.
   *Source: EFSA opinion on TiO₂ (2021); EU Commission regulation*

2. **Promoting TiO₂ as a "climate-neutral" pigment** — Industry associations (TDMA — Titanium Dioxide Manufacturers Association) promote a narrative that white TiO₂-based coatings reduce building energy consumption (cooling effect), positioning TiO₂ as a "climate solution" instead of disclosing its production footprint.
   *Source: TDMA Industry Communications*
