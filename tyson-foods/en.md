Tyson Foods processes more than 7 billion chickens, 25 million pigs, and 7 million head of cattle annually. Such production scales mean enormous volumes of manure and wastewater. According to environmental organisations, from 2010 to 2014 Tyson facilities discharged more hazardous pollutants into US water bodies than major oil companies.

The Illinois River in Oklahoma has become a virtual dead zone due to chicken manure discharge from Tyson supplier farms: excess nitrogen and phosphorus causes algal blooms that destroy fish. The state of Oklahoma filed a lawsuit against Tyson, accusing the company of hundreds of millions of dollars in damage to the river.

Tyson's animal feed production requires growing hundreds of millions of tonnes of soy and corn with intensive pesticide and fertiliser use, accelerating soil degradation in the Corn Belt and expanding the "Dead Zone" in the Gulf of Mexico.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Tyson Foods systematically created dangerous working conditions at its facilities, contaminated drinking water in rural areas, and contributed to outbreaks of infectious diseases among workers and local residents.

1. **COVID-19 outbreaks at meat processing plants (2020)** — Tyson Foods facilities in Waterloo (Iowa), Dakota City (Nebraska), and Logansport (Indiana) became epicentres of COVID-19 outbreaks in spring 2020. At the Waterloo plant, more than 1,000 of 2,800 workers became infected, and 6 people died. Company managers were placing bets on how many workers would become infected — this became known through court documents.
   *Source: The Guardian, "Tyson Foods managers bet on how many workers would get COVID", November 2020*

2. **Chronic nitrate drinking water contamination (2010–2017)** — The EWG (Environmental Working Group) in 2017 established that industrial livestock farming stimulated by Tyson Foods is the primary source of nitrate contamination of tap water in Iowa. Elevated nitrate levels in drinking water are associated with "blue baby syndrome" (methaemoglobinaemia) in infants and increased cancer risk.
   *Source: Environmental Working Group, "Nitrate in Drinking Water", 2017*

3. **Antibiotic resistance as a public health threat (2000s–present)** — Tyson Foods used antibiotics in industrial livestock farming at sub-therapeutic doses to accelerate animal growth. The FDA and WHO warned that this leads to the formation of antibiotic-resistant bacteria. Only in 2017 did Tyson announce a partial ban on antibiotics in chicken production, but continued their use in pig farming.
   *Source: Natural Resources Defense Council, "Chain Reaction", 2015–2022*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Tyson Foods has been recognised as the largest corporate water body polluter in the US in the studied period — the company discharged toxic effluents into hundreds of rivers and streams across the country.

1. **Largest corporate water polluter in the USA (2010–2014)** — According to an EWG study based on EPA data, Tyson Foods facilities discharged 104 million pounds of pollutants into US water bodies during this period — more than ExxonMobil, Dow Chemical, and other petrochemical giants. Contaminants included nitrates, phosphorus, chlorine, ammonia, and pathogens.
   *Source: Environmental Working Group, "Corporate Agribusiness and the Fouling of America's Waterways", 2020*

2. **Contamination of the Illinois River in Oklahoma and mass fish kills (1990s–2000s)** — The state of Oklahoma in 2005 filed a multi-million-dollar lawsuit against Tyson Foods and other poultry companies, accusing them of systematically destroying the Illinois River ecosystem. The riverbed became covered with phosphate sludge, and toxic algal blooms destroyed fish populations. Lake Tenkiller lost its tourism and recreational value.
   *Source: Oklahoma v. Tyson Foods et al., federal court case materials 2005–2010*

3. **Manure lagoon spill in Missouri (1997)** — In 1997, a manure lagoon spill occurred at the company's Missouri hog farm, killing fish over a river section several kilometres long. The EPA recorded a critical drop in dissolved oxygen levels. Tyson paid a fine but continued similar manure storage practices.
   *Source: EPA Enforcement Actions, Missouri, 1997*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Tyson Foods actively uses environmental rhetoric and PR initiatives, the scale of which is incomparably small relative to the actual environmental damage from its activities.

1. **"Carbon neutrality" claims amid growing emissions (2021)** — In 2021, Tyson announced a goal to achieve zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2050 and reduce emissions by 30% by 2030. However, independent analysts established that from 2016 to 2021 the company's emissions actually increased. The declared targets did not include supply chain emissions (Scope 3), which account for more than 90% of the company's carbon footprint.
   *Source: Institute for Agriculture and Trade Policy, "Tyson Foods Climate Claims", 2021*

2. **"Nature-Based Solutions" programme without measurable results (2022)** — Tyson announced a partnership with the Nature Conservancy for "watershed restoration", allocating $50 million over 10 years — with annual revenues exceeding $47 billion, this amounts to less than 0.01% of turnover. The programme did not address the sources of contamination — the company's livestock operations.
   *Source: Food and Water Watch, "Tyson's Greenwashing", 2022*

3. **Rebranding chicken products as "humane" and "sustainable" (2019–2023)** — Tyson actively promotes the "No Antibiotics Ever" (NAE) label for some products, while continuing large-scale antibiotic use in other production segments. Consumers were misled about the actual use of antibiotics in the production chain.
   *Source: Natural Resources Defense Council, "Chain Reaction VII", 2022*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Tyson Foods publishes annual ESG reports and positions itself as a responsible business leader, while continuing activities that destroy ecosystems and violate labour rights.

1. **ESG report declaring "sustainable livestock farming" (2020–2023)** — Tyson annually publishes a "Sustainability Report", declaring goals for emissions reductions, improved water use, and "humane animal treatment". Independent ratings, however, consistently place Tyson in the lower quartiles of ESG assessments in the meat processing industry.
   *Source: MSCI ESG Ratings, Sustainalytics, "Tyson Foods ESG Assessment", 2022–2023*

2. **Investment in alternative proteins as an ESG signal (2019–2021)** — Tyson acquired a stake in Beyond Meat and subsequently sold it, and invested in insect protein and plant-based protein startups. These investments were negligible compared with the scale of the livestock business and were used exclusively for PR positioning.
   *Source: Bloomberg, "Tyson's Alternative Protein Gamble", 2021*

3. **Inclusion in ESG indices despite systematic violations (2018–2022)** — Tyson was included in ESG indices such as FTSE4Good and the Dow Jones Sustainability Index, despite systematic environmental law violations recorded by the EPA. This became grounds for criticism of ESG rating agency methodologies.
   *Source: As You Sow, "ESG Accountability Project", 2022*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Tyson Foods received individual ESG distinctions that the company actively used in marketing, despite their divergence from its actual environmental practices.

1. **Inclusion in "World's Most Ethical Companies" list (2015)** — The Ethisphere Institute included Tyson Foods in the list of the world's most ethical companies in 2015. The inclusion occurred during a period when the company was under numerous EPA investigations for water body contamination, which drew criticism of Ethisphere's methodology.
   *Source: Ethisphere Institute, "World's Most Ethical Companies 2015"; criticism — Food Safety News, 2015*

2. **CDP award for water use transparency (2021)** — CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project) assigned Tyson a "B" rating for water use transparency, which is itself only an assessment of reporting quality, not actual environmental results. The company actively reproduced this fact in corporate communications.
   *Source: CDP Water Security Report, 2021*

3. **Inclusion in Fortune "World's Most Admired Companies" (2022)** — Tyson appeared in the Fortune ranking in the "food industry" category based on corporate responsibility criteria, despite multi-million dollar EPA fines and documented labour law violations in preceding years.
   *Source: Fortune, "World's Most Admired Companies", 2022*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Tyson Foods uses eco-labelling and "green" claims on product packaging whose production is associated with significant environmental damage.

1. **"Raised Without Antibiotics" labelling while continuing antibiotic use (2017–2022)** — Some Tyson products were labelled as raised "without antibiotics", but the USDA in 2017 found violations and required the label to be removed from certain product batches. In 2022 consumer organisations recorded similar violations.
   *Source: USDA Food Safety and Inspection Service, 2017; Consumer Reports, 2022*

2. **"All Natural" label on products with synthetic additives (2010s)** — Tyson used "All Natural" labelling on products containing synthetic ingredients and preservatives. This became the subject of several class action lawsuits in the US. In 2014 the company settled one such lawsuit by paying compensation to consumers.
   *Source: Class Action Lawsuit, Tyson Foods "All Natural" claims, 2014*

3. **"Sustainably Sourced" labelling without verifiable standards (2021–2023)** — A number of Tyson products, including the Jimmy Dean and Hillshire Farm brands, received "responsible production" labelling, but the company did not disclose certification criteria and did not undergo independent audits for compliance with the declared standards.
   *Source: Changing Markets Foundation, "Greenwashing in Animal Products", 2022*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Tyson Foods organises environmental actions and programmes whose scale is incommensurate with the company's damage and serves primarily PR purposes.

1. **"Trees for the Future" tree-planting programme (2021)** — Tyson announced a partnership with Trees for the Future to plant trees in Africa. The funding volume and programme scale were not publicly disclosed, while the company's activities annually destroy forests through soy supply chains in Brazil.
   *Source: Tyson Foods Sustainability Report, 2021*

2. **Riverbank cleanup action (2019)** — Tyson organised corporate volunteer events to clean riverbanks in Arkansas involving several hundred employees. These events were covered in corporate media, while company facilities continued to discharge contaminated effluents into the same rivers.
   *Source: Tyson Foods Corporate Newsroom, 2019*

3. **"Water strategy" with symbolic targets (2020)** — Tyson announced a "water strategy" providing for a 12% reduction in water consumption at plants by 2020. However, this target related exclusively to direct water consumption at facilities, ignoring the enormous indirect water use in livestock farming (up to 15,500 litres per kilogram of beef).
   *Source: Tyson Foods FY2020 Sustainability Report*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Tyson Foods funded industry associations and lobbying groups that opposed environmental regulation, and promoted the narrative of "progress in sustainable development" as an alternative to real reforms.

1. **Lobbying against tightening of effluent discharge standards (2010s)** — Tyson, together with the National Pork Producers Council and the National Chicken Council, funded lobbying efforts against updating the Clean Water Act as applied to agricultural operations (CAFOs). The company spent more than $10 million on lobbying in the period 2010–2020.
   *Source: OpenSecrets.org, Tyson Foods Lobbying Expenditures, 2010–2020*

2. **Promoting the "responsible livestock farming" narrative through industry media (2015–2022)** — Tyson funded educational programmes and publications positioning industrial meat production as "sustainable" and "feeding the world". These materials were distributed through farm associations and agricultural schools, cultivating loyalty among young farmers to practices beneficial to Tyson.
   *Source: Corporate Accountability, "Food Industry Influence in Agriculture Education", 2019*

3. **Opposition to methane emission regulation from manure (2021–2022)** — After the EPA announced plans to regulate livestock methane emissions, Tyson worked through industry associations to seek exemptions for large livestock operations. At the same time, the company publicly declared its commitment to the climate goals of the Paris Agreement.
   *Source: Environmental Defense Fund, "Methane Regulation and Agriculture Lobbying", 2022*
