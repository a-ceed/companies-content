Unilever — the world's largest consumer of palm oil after the oil industry — uses it in hundreds of products from soap to margarine. Palm oil production has become the primary cause of tropical deforestation in Indonesia and Malaysia — key habitats of orangutans, pygmy elephants, and Sumatran tigers.

In 2008, Greenpeace published a report directly linking Unilever suppliers to the destruction of peatland forests — ecosystems that store many times more carbon than regular forests. Burning peatlands during land clearing for plantations is one of the world's largest sources of CO₂ emissions.

Despite a commitment to "zero deforestation" by 2020, satellite data analysis by the Rainforest Action Network showed continued forest destruction in Unilever's supply chain. In 2021 the company moved the target date to 2023.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Unilever is implicated in mercury poisoning of thousands of residents of Kodaikanal, India, violations of the rights of plantation workers, and the use of toxic chemicals in its products.

1. **Mercury plant in Kodaikanal, India (1983–2001)** — Hindustan Unilever's thermometer factory in Tamil Nadu discharged mercury waste into the soil and surrounding forests. Workers and local residents suffered chronic mercury poisoning. The plant was closed in 2001 following exposés. Unilever required years to fully remediate the contamination, and compensation to victims was paid only after years of litigation.
   *Source: BBC News, "Unilever's mercury problem in India", 2015; Amnesty International*

2. **Working conditions on palm oil plantations in Indonesia (2016–2022)** — Amnesty International published a report on child labour and forced labour on plantations supplying Unilever. Workers reported production quotas impossible to meet without child labour, as well as pesticide exposure without adequate protection.
   *Source: Amnesty International, "The Great Palm Oil Scandal", 2016*

3. **Talc-containing products (2019–2023)** — In the USA and several European countries, lawsuits were filed against Unilever in connection with the discovery of asbestos traces in talc baby powder, which is linked to the development of mesothelioma. The company recalled products in individual cases, denying the systemic nature of the problem.
   *Source: Reuters, "Johnson's Baby Powder asbestos lawsuits", 2020; similar lawsuits against Unilever in several jurisdictions*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Unilever bears direct responsibility for mercury contamination in India, large-scale water body pollution in Nigeria, and stimulating peatland forest burning.

1. **Mercury contamination in Kodaikanal (1983–2001)** — The Hindustan Unilever plant released hundreds of tonnes of mercury-containing waste into the environment. Contamination was found in soil, water bodies, and forests of the Palani Hills protected zone. Full soil remediation was completed only in 2019 — 18 years after the plant's closure.
   *Source: The Hindu; Tamil Nadu Pollution Control Board reports*

2. **River contamination in Nigeria (2010–2020)** — Unilever Nigeria facilities in the Niger Delta were documented in NGO reports as sources of industrial effluents into rivers of the Bight of Benin. Local fishing communities lost access to clean water and fish resources.
   *Source: Friends of the Earth Nigeria; UNEP Delta Report 2011*

3. **Fires and peatland burning in the supply chain (2015–2020)** — Global Forest Watch satellite analysis showed that Unilever's palm oil suppliers continued to burn peatlands in Indonesia after the company's public "zero deforestation" commitments. The 2015 fires became a regional environmental catastrophe claiming thousands of lives from smoke inhalation.
   *Source: Global Forest Watch; Chain Reaction Research, 2019*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Unilever has repeatedly declared breakthrough environmental goals and postponed their deadlines, while simultaneously occupying leading positions in plastic pollution rankings.

1. **Top plastic pollution ranking (2018–2022)** — According to the Break Free From Plastic brand audit, Unilever occupied first or second place for five consecutive years among companies whose branded packaging is most frequently found at beach cleanups in Asia. This contradicts declarations of "100% recyclable packaging by 2025".
   *Source: Break Free From Plastic Brand Audit 2018–2022*

2. **Postponement of climate commitments (2020–2023)** — Unilever promised to halve Scope 3 emissions by 2030, but in 2023 quietly weakened several intermediate targets, citing "market constraints". Independent assessment showed that actual emissions reductions did not occur.
   *Source: Carbon Disclosure Project; Climate Action 100+ assessment 2023*

3. **"Clean Future" for dishwashing products (2021)** — Unilever labelled a line of cleaning products as "climate-positive", basing the claim on questionable offset credits. The UK Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) warned the company that the advertising did not meet honest description standards.
   *Source: UK Competition and Markets Authority, Green Claims Code investigation 2022*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Unilever positions itself as a pioneer of corporate sustainability and actively uses ESG narratives in investor communications.

1. **Unilever Sustainable Living Plan (2010–2020)** — Unilever's decade-long plan became a model in the corporate world, actively cited in ESG textbooks. At the conclusion of the plan, the company acknowledged that it had not achieved the goal of decoupling business growth from increased environmental footprint.
   *Source: Unilever Annual Report 2020; independent KPMG audit*

2. **"Sustainability Compass" (2021–2023)** — The new "Unilever Compass" strategy included commitments on climate, water, and human rights. Activist NGOs point out that the document contains primarily relative rather than absolute targets, allowing progress to be declared while absolute emissions grow.
   *Source: ShareAction, "Unilever's Climate Claims", 2022*

3. **WBCSD and GRI membership (2000–2023)** — Unilever is a permanent member of the World Business Council for Sustainable Development and one of the pioneers of GRI-standard reporting — despite the fact that actual Scope 3 emission indicators grew until 2019.
   *Source: WBCSD member directory; Unilever GRI Reports*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Unilever has headed prestigious ESG rankings for a number of years.

1. **GlobeScan/SustainAbility Leaders Survey (2011–2018)** — Unilever held first place for seven consecutive years among corporations leading in sustainability according to industry experts. This coincided with the period when the company topped the plastic pollution lists in Asia.
   *Source: GlobeScan/SustainAbility Survey of Sustainability Experts*

2. **Corporate Knights Global 100 (2015–2020)** — Unilever was regularly included in the list of 100 most sustainable corporations in the world according to Corporate Knights.
   *Source: Corporate Knights, "Global 100 Most Sustainable Corporations"*

3. **Dow Jones Sustainability Index — leader in the "household goods" category (2016–2021)** — Unilever was repeatedly recognised as the best in the household and personal care goods category under the DJSI.
   *Source: S&P Global, Dow Jones Sustainability Indices*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Unilever uses green labelling on products whose production is still associated with significant environmental damage.

1. **RSPO logo on products with uncertified palm oil (2010–2020)** — A number of Unilever products in Asia carried the RSPO (Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil) label, although in reality the company used a "credit balance" method — that is, it actually purchased conventional palm oil while merely buying green certificates on paper.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network, "RSPO Credits System", 2018*

2. **Dove "natural ingredients" labelling (2018–2022)** — The Dove line labelled "natural" contained silicones, synthetic fragrances, and polyethylene microbeads. The French regulator required the labelling to be corrected.
   *Source: Agence de la transition écologique (ADEME), France, 2021*

3. **Lipton "Rainforest Alliance" labelling (2008–2020)** — Lipton tea was positioned as Rainforest Alliance certified, but NGO Solidaridad and investigative journalists found that working conditions on a number of certified plantations in Kenya violated basic standards.
   *Source: Solidaridad, "Tea Sector Report", 2016; Reuters Investigates, 2018*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Unilever regularly organises symbolic environmental actions that are not commensurate with the volumes of pollution it produces.

1. **"Clean Beaches" beach cleanup campaign (2019–2022)** — Unilever annually organises plastic waste cleanups on beaches in Indonesia, India, and the Philippines, collecting tonnes of waste. At the same time, Break Free From Plastic audits annually found Unilever packaging to be among the most common finds at the same cleanups.
   *Source: Break Free From Plastic Brand Audit 2019–2022*

2. **Mangrove planting initiative (2021)** — Unilever announced the planting of 20 million mangrove trees in Indonesia — despite the fact that mangrove ecosystem degradation linked to clearing for palm plantations of the company's suppliers amounts to hundreds of thousands of hectares.
   *Source: Unilever press release, 2021; Global Mangrove Alliance data*

3. **"Plastic-Free Day" at headquarters (2022)** — Unilever held a symbolic "single-use plastic-free day" in its Rotterdam and London offices, covering several hundred employees — despite the fact that the company produces more than 700,000 tonnes of plastic packaging annually.
   *Source: Unilever sustainability blog, 2022*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Unilever actively promotes the narrative of "conscious consumption", shifting responsibility for pollution from the producer to the consumer.

1. **Promoting the "individual carbon footprint" concept (2019–2022)** — Unilever funded campaigns on "personal sustainable consumption", promoting the idea that environmental problems are solved by the buyer's personal choice. The concept of "individual footprint" was popularised by BP, and is now actively used by FMCG companies to divert attention from industrial pollution.
   *Source: InfluenceMap, "FMCG and climate lobbying", 2021*

2. **Lobbying for flexible plastic packaging as "recyclable" (2018–2023)** — Unilever, together with other FMCG giants, promoted the concept of "recyclability" of multi-layer plastic sachets, which are technically not accepted by any existing recycling system. This allowed green symbols to be placed on them without changing the packaging design.
   *Source: GAIA (Global Alliance for Incinerator Alternatives), "Sachets" report 2020*

3. **WRAP membership and funding of "educational" recycling programmes (2015–2023)** — Unilever funded educational programmes of the WRAP organisation (UK) on separate waste collection for consumers, while simultaneously lobbying against mandatory national deposit return schemes that actually increase recycling rates.
   *Source: WRAP Annual Report 2021; InfluenceMap UK policy tracker*

3. **Greenwashing?** — **YES** — Leader in plastic pollution rankings while declaring "100% recyclable packaging"; UK CMA warnings; postponement of climate goals.
4. **ESG marketing?** — **YES** — Unilever Sustainable Living Plan; Compass strategy; WBCSD and GRI membership.
5. **ESG award wins?** — **YES** — Seven-time GlobeScan/SustainAbility leader (2011–2018); Corporate Knights Global 100; DJSI leader in its category.
6. **Eco-labels on harmful products?** — **YES** — RSPO logo while using "credit balance"; Dove "natural" label with synthetic ingredients; Rainforest Alliance certificate for Lipton despite plantation violations.
