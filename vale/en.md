On 5 November 2015, the Fundão tailings dam at the Samarco mine (a joint venture of Vale and BHP) failed. 62 million cubic metres of toxic iron ore sludge poured into the Doce River. The mud wave travelled 663 km, reached the Atlantic Ocean, and destroyed the river's ecosystem. 19 people died; 700,000 were left without drinking water.

On 25 January 2019, the Brumadinho dam failed. The mud flow killed 270 people — the largest industrial disaster in Brazilian history. Vale was aware of the dam's critical defects: shortly before the collapse, the company had withdrawn the previous structural stability certificate.

In 2021, Vale reached a $7 billion compensation agreement with Brazilian authorities, but environmentalists consider the amount insufficient to restore the destroyed ecosystems. Both events radically changed international safety standards for mining tailings dams.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Vale bears direct responsibility for the deaths of nearly 300 people as a result of dam failures, as well as for chronic health damage to thousands of people deprived of drinking water and livelihoods.

1. **Brumadinho disaster (25 January 2019)** — The failure of the B-I dam at the Córrego do Feijão tailings facility claimed 270 lives, the majority of whom were Vale workers at a cafeteria in the impact zone. The investigation established that Vale had concealed data about the dam's instability from the regulator and the certifying organisation TÜV SÜD. Criminal charges were brought against 16 company managers.
   *Source: Brazilian Federal Police; Folha de S.Paulo (2019–2021)*

2. **Samarco/Fundão disaster, Mariana (5 November 2015)** — The dam failure at the Vale and BHP joint venture killed 19 people and contaminated the Doce River over 663 km. Hundreds of thousands of residents were left without drinking water for months. Local fishermen and farmers lost their source of income. Indigenous communities of the Krenak people, for whom the river was sacred, faced the destruction of their traditional way of life.
   *Source: IBAMA; Human Rights Watch (2016)*

3. **Mercury contamination in Pará State (chronic)** — Vale's iron ore and nickel mining operations in Pará State were accompanied by mercury contamination of small rivers and fishing grounds of indigenous peoples. FIOCRUZ (Brazilian Institute of Public Health) research recorded elevated mercury levels in the blood of community residents near Vale mines in the Amazon basin.
   *Source: FIOCRUZ; ISA — Instituto Socioambiental*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Vale caused the two largest environmental catastrophes in Brazilian history, and the total damage to the country's aquatic ecosystems is estimated in the tens of billions of dollars.

1. **Destruction of the Doce River ecosystem (2015)** — Following the Fundão dam failure, toxic sludge travelled 663 km and reached the Atlantic Ocean. The Doce River, previously the most important waterway in the states of Minas Gerais and Espírito Santo, was declared "dead". Monitoring in 2017–2022 recorded an absence of fish fauna recovery in a significant portion of the riverbed.
   *Source: IBAMA; Universidade Federal do Espírito Santo (2017)*

2. **Contamination of Parauapebas reservoir, Pará State (2018)** — A waste leak from Vale's Carajás iron ore complex contaminated a reservoir that supplied water to several municipalities. The authorities introduced a temporary ban on water use; local fishermen reported mass fish kills.
   *Source: Pará State Environmental Agency; O Liberal (2018)*

3. **Long-term silting of the Paraopeba River floodplain after Brumadinho (2019–2023)** — The toxic sludge from the Brumadinho dam failure spread across the Paraopeba River floodplain, destroying riparian ecosystems and farmland. Four years later, heavy metal levels in bottom sediments remained significantly above normal. No recovery of full-scale commercial fishing was recorded.
   *Source: UFMG / UFOP environmental monitoring (2020–2023)*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Vale actively positioned itself as a "responsible" iron ore and nickel producer in the context of the green transition, while simultaneously concealing risks and continuing to operate dams with critical defects.

1. **"Zero disasters" declarations before Brumadinho (2016–2018)** — After the 2015 Mariana disaster, Vale publicly committed to eliminating risks at all tailings dams and ensuring "zero disasters". In January 2019, the Brumadinho dam failed, and Vale's internal correspondence showed that managers were aware of the structure's critical condition.
   *Source: Brazilian Senate investigation (2019); O Globo*

2. **Positioning nickel as a "clean energy metal" (2020–2023)** — Vale actively promoted its nickel as a "green" component for electric vehicle batteries, publishing reports on the low carbon footprint of operations in Canada. Meanwhile, operations in Brazil and Indonesia were accompanied by continuing environmental violations.
   *Source: Vale Annual Report 2022; Global Witness*

3. **"Green steel" advertising without a real decarbonisation plan (2021)** — Vale announced partnerships with metallurgical companies to produce "green steel" with low CO₂ emissions. Critics noted that none of the declared hydrogen metallurgy technologies had been put into industrial operation at the time, and the narrative itself served to maintain investor interest.
   *Source: Global Steel Climate Council; Carbon Brief (2021)*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Vale uses ESG reporting and marketing to attract investment and manage reputational damage following the two disasters.

1. **ESG bonds and "green" loans (2020–2023)** — Following the Brumadinho disaster, Vale raised several billion dollars through ESG-labelled instruments, promising to direct funds towards remediation and dam safety upgrades. Some investors criticised insufficient transparency in the use of proceeds.
   *Source: Bloomberg; Institute for Energy Economics and Financial Analysis (2022)*

2. **Renaming and rebranding with a sustainability emphasis (2021)** — Vale refreshed its corporate image, replacing its logo and launching a campaign emphasising "transformation" and "responsible mining". Brazilian NGOs pointed out that the rebranding coincided with peak legal pressure and was aimed at reputation management.
   *Source: Agência Pública; Instituto Socioambiental (2021)*

3. **GRI and SASB-standard reports with unresolved lawsuits** — Vale annually publishes detailed non-financial reports to GRI standards, declaring progress in sustainable development. At the time of the most recent report publications, thousands of Brumadinho and Mariana victims had not received promised compensation in full.
   *Source: Vale Sustainability Report 2022; Conectas Direitos Humanos*

---

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Vale was included in sustainability indices and ratings even after the disasters, which critics viewed as a systemic failure of ESG assessment.

1. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI) before and after Mariana** — Vale was included in the DJSI, including during the period when the company was concealing tailings dam risks. Following the 2015 disaster, it was excluded, but several other sustainability indices retained it on their lists.
   *Source: S&P Dow Jones Indices; Responsible Investor (2016)*

2. **"Best Mining Company" awards from financial publications (2013–2014)** — Vale received industry sustainability awards from Euromoney and similar publications during the period when internal inspections were already recording critical dam risks.
   *Source: Euromoney Mining Awards; Reuters (2014)*

3. **CDP "A-List" for water risk disclosure (2017)** — In 2017, CDP assigned Vale a high score for water risk disclosure — two years after the Samarco disaster and two years before Brumadinho. The rating became an example of the gap between formal disclosure and real risk management.
   *Source: CDP Water Security (2017)*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Vale actively promoted selected products with "environmental" characteristics, selectively emphasising positive aspects while suppressing information about systemic damage.

1. **"Low carbon footprint" nickel marketing from Canada (2021–2023)** — Vale promoted Canadian division nickel as having one of the world's lowest carbon footprints per tonne of metal, actively using this argument in negotiations with battery manufacturers. Meanwhile, nickel operations in Indonesia with high environmental damage were not mentioned in marketing materials.
   *Source: Vale Vale+ Battery Materials marketing (2022)*

2. **"Vale Verde" programme and "iron ore for green steel" (2022)** — The company created a special "Vale Verde" brand to position its iron ore raw material as intended for "green" metallurgical production. The brand was used in client communications, although neither the product itself nor the methods of its extraction underwent significant changes.
   *Source: Vale corporate communications 2022*

3. **ISO 14001 certification of selected mines despite systemic violations** — Vale certified a number of mines under the ISO 14001 environmental management standard and used these certificates in marketing materials. The certificate did not prevent disasters, since the standard does not require independent technical inspection of hydraulic structures.
   *Source: ISO 14001 certification records; Brazilian Senate report (2019)*

---

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Vale systematically organised environmental actions that were incommensurate in scale with the environmental damage caused.

1. **"More Trees for Minas Gerais" afforestation (2019–2022)** — Following the Brumadinho disaster, Vale announced a programme to plant 100 million trees by 2030. In practice, several million trees were planted in the first three years — with disturbed and contaminated land in Minas Gerais alone numbering thousands of hectares.
   *Source: Vale Sustainability Report 2022; Folha de S.Paulo*

2. **"Doce River Day" with volunteers (2016–2018)** — Samarco/Vale annually organised public river bank cleanup events along the Doce River involving hundreds of volunteers. Ecologists noted that mechanical litter collection is incommensurate with the scale of toxic silting of the riverbed.
   *Source: Renova Foundation communications; Agência Pública critique (2018)*

3. **Environmental lessons in schools in the operations zone (2010–2019)** — Vale funded environmental education programmes in schools near its mines in Pará and Minas Gerais states. Programme content emphasised the "benefits" of mining for the region and did not address the real environmental risks of operations.
   *Source: Vale Foundation annual reports; ISA assessment*

---

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Vale actively influenced the regulatory environment in Brazil, seeking to weaken dam safety standards and opposing the tightening of environmental legislation.

1. **Lobbying against tightening tailings dam standards (2016–2018)** — Following the 2015 Samarco disaster, Brazil began developing new technical regulations for tailings dams. According to Brazilian investigative journalists, Vale actively participated in consultations with regulator ANM, seeking to preserve the "upstream" tailings dam construction method, recognised as the most dangerous. This was precisely the method used at the Brumadinho dam.
   *Source: Agência Pública "O lobby das barragens" (2019)*

2. **Funding "independent" dam safety studies (2017–2018)** — Vale commissioned technical assessments from organisations that had financial dependence on the mining industry. The conclusions of these assessments were used to obtain stability certificates, which were then provided to regulators. In the case of Brumadinho, the TÜV SÜD certificate was issued on the basis of knowingly unreliable data.
   *Source: German prosecutors; TÜV SÜD investigation (2020)*

3. **Participation in lobbying for weakening Brazil's Forest Code (2011–2012)** — Vale, as part of the agricultural and mining lobby, supported amendments to Brazil's Forest Code that weakened requirements for the protection of riparian forests. Environmentalists viewed this as systemic opposition to environmental legislation by resource corporations.
   *Source: Observatório do Clima; INPE forest monitoring data*

## Summary

1. **Harm to human health**: Vale bears responsibility for the deaths of 289 people in two disasters (2015 and 2019), as well as for chronic mercury and heavy metal poisoning of thousands of people in the Amazon basin.
2. **Environmental catastrophes**: The Fundão and Brumadinho dam failures are the largest mining disasters in Brazilian history, with long-term destruction of aquatic ecosystems.
3. **Greenwashing**: "Zero disasters" declarations after Mariana were accompanied by concealment of Brumadinho risks; the "green nickel" narrative ignores actual environmental damage.
4. **ESG marketing**: Post-disaster rebranding and ESG bonds were used for reputation management rather than systemic change of practices.
5. **ESG awards**: High CDP scores and inclusion in sustainability indices did not actually reflect real management of critical risks.
6. **Eco-labelling**: ISO 14001 certifications and the "Vale Verde" brand served as marketing tools unconnected to real impact reduction.
7. **Symbolic actions**: Tree-planting programmes and "river days" are incommensurate with the scale of destroyed ecosystems.
8. **Lobbying**: Vale opposed the tightening of tailings dam regulation, which directly contributed to the repetition of disaster in 2019.
