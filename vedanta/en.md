In May 2018, police opened fire on demonstrators protesting the expansion of Vedanta's (Sterlite) copper smelter in Thoothukudi (Tamil Nadu, India): 13 people died. The plant had systematically exceeded SO₂ emission standards and violated environmental legislation for 20 years, polluting air and groundwater.

In Odisha (India), Vedanta mined bauxite on Niyamgiri Hill — the sacred mountain of the indigenous Dongria Kondh people. After years of struggle, the Supreme Court of India ruled in 2013 that the decision rested with the indigenous people: in a referendum, all 12 villages voted against the project.

In Zambia, thousands of farmers filed a lawsuit in British courts, accusing Vedanta's subsidiary Konkola Copper Mines of contaminating the Kafue River with copper waste. In 2021 the UK Supreme Court confirmed the Zambians' right to legal proceedings in London.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. The shooting of demonstrators, systematic air and water pollution, and the deaths of miners are documented facts.

1. **Shooting of 13 demonstrators in Thoothukudi (2018)** — On 22–23 May 2018, Tamil Nadu State Police opened fire on peaceful demonstrators demanding the closure of the Sterlite copper smelter (a Vedanta subsidiary): 13 people were killed and dozens injured. The plant had systematically exceeded SO₂ standards and violated environmental norms for 20 years.
   *Source: Amnesty International — "India: Vedanta Sterlite killings" (2018)*

2. **Groundwater contamination in Tamil Nadu** — Tamil Nadu courts established that SO₂ and heavy metal emissions from the Sterlite plant in Thoothukudi had for decades contaminated atmospheric air and groundwater, which is linked to a rise in respiratory diseases among the population of adjacent areas.
   *Source: Madras High Court judgments on Sterlite plant*

3. **Deaths of miners in India** — Fatal accidents were recorded at Vedanta's mining facilities in Odisha and Rajasthan. Trade unions accused the company of inadequate occupational safety measures.
   *Source: India Ministry of Mines accident records*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Contamination of the Kafue River in Zambia and years of air pollution in Thoothukudi are major documented incidents.

1. **Kafue River contamination in Zambia** — Konkola Copper Mines (a Vedanta subsidiary) discharged copper-containing wastewater into the Kafue River — Zambia's primary water supply source. More than 2,000 farmers and residents filed lawsuits in English courts, documenting fish kills, livestock poisoning, and agricultural land destruction.
   *Source: Lungowe & Others v Vedanta (UK Supreme Court, 2021)*

2. **SO₂ emissions from the Sterlite plant in Thoothukudi** — The plant annually emitted thousands of tonnes of sulphur dioxide — several times above permissible standards. Tamil Nadu courts established that the plant systematically violated environmental licence conditions. The plant was permanently closed in 2018 following the shooting.
   *Source: Madras High Court; National Green Tribunal India*

3. **Niyamgiri: threat to the sacred mountain and biodiversity** — The bauxite mining project on Niyamgiri Hill threatened unique mountain forests, water bodies, and elephant habitat. The Supreme Court of India halted the project following the referendum of the indigenous Dongria Kondh people in 2013.
   *Source: India Supreme Court — Vedanta vs. MoEF (2013)*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Vedanta declares ESG goals against the backdrop of some of the most high-profile environmental scandals in India.

1. **"Responsible Stewardship" after plant closure following the shooting** — After the Sterlite closure in 2018, Vedanta continued using a "responsible business" narrative in corporate communications, without acknowledging systemic culpability for decades of violations.
   *Source: Amnesty International analysis; Vedanta Annual Reports*

2. **ESG declarations while losing the Dongria Kondh lawsuit** — Vedanta claimed "compliance with indigenous peoples' rights" in corporate reports, while the Indian court found violations of those rights at Niyamgiri.
   *Source: Survival International; India Supreme Court ruling*

3. **"Zero Harm" policy amid worker deaths** — Vedanta declares a corporate "Zero Harm" policy, but official accident statistics at its mining facilities contradict this claim.
   *Source: Vedanta Sustainability Report; India Ministry of Mines*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Vedanta actively publishes ESG reports and uses ESG narratives on the London and Mumbai stock exchanges.

1. **Annual sustainability reports** — Vedanta annually publishes Sustainability Reports to GRI standards, declaring progress in reducing CO₂ emissions, water use, and waste generation.
   *Source: Vedanta Sustainability Report 2023*

2. **ESG-oriented investor relations** — Vedanta actively promoted the ESG narrative among institutional investors on the London Stock Exchange (LSE), where the company's shares were traded before delisting.
   *Source: Vedanta Resources investor presentations*

3. **"Transforming for Good" — corporate ESG brand** — The "Transforming for Good" campaign is used by Vedanta as an umbrella ESG brand covering everything from emissions reductions to social programmes.
   *Source: Vedanta Corporate Website*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: NO**. Vedanta has not received significant recognition in international ESG ratings.

1. Vedanta Resources was excluded from FTSE4Good due to human rights violations and environmental scandals. Sustainalytics and MSCI ratings classified the company as "high ESG risk".
   *Source: FTSE Russell; Sustainalytics*

---

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Vedanta's aluminium and zinc are marketed as "sustainable metals" for "green" industry.

1. **"Sustainable Aluminium" for "green" packaging** — Vedanta sells aluminium as a "sustainable" metal to beverage and automotive manufacturers, positioning it as a contribution to the "green" economy. Meanwhile, bauxite extraction and aluminium production generate a significant environmental footprint.
   *Source: Vedanta Aluminium marketing materials*

2. **"Responsible Zinc" for galvanic coatings** — Vedanta zinc is sold with a "responsible mining" narrative, despite documented violations in Rajasthan.
   *Source: Vedanta Zinc International communications*

---

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Against the backdrop of serious environmental violations, Vedanta's CSR programmes appear disproportionately small.

1. **Tree-planting programmes near mining facilities** — Vedanta annually reports on planting millions of trees in India and Zambia, but the greening area is incommensurate with the area of land disturbed by mining operations.
   *Source: Vedanta Community Reports*

2. **Anil Agarwal Foundation philanthropy** — Vedanta and its founder fund educational and medical programmes in India through the Anil Agarwal Foundation. Critics call this an attempt to divert attention from human rights violations.
   *Source: Amnesty International*

---

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Vedanta sought the reversal of plant closure decisions through judicial and administrative mechanisms, promoting a "economic necessity" narrative.

1. **Lobbying for the reopening of Sterlite (2018–2023)** — Following the closure of the Thoothukudi plant, Vedanta filed dozens of appeals in Indian courts and executive bodies, arguing that the plant was necessary for "national security" (copper production). This narrative was used to pressure authorities against the backdrop of documented violations.
   *Source: The Hindu; Wire.in*

2. **Lobbying against environmental bond requirements** — Vedanta lobbied against the tightening of financial guarantee requirements for mining licences in India, arguing that this "harms the investment climate".
   *Source: India Ministry of Mines consultation records*
