Wilmar International — the world's largest palm oil trader — controls approximately 45% of global palm oil trade and supplies raw materials to Nestlé, Procter & Gamble, Unilever, and Mondelēz. The company has long been linked to large-scale deforestation in Indonesia and Malaysia, land conflicts with indigenous communities, and forced labour on supplier plantations.

Despite adopting a "No Deforestation, No Peat, No Exploitation" (NDPE) policy in 2013, Wilmar continued to purchase palm oil from suppliers documented by Greenpeace and Rainforest Action Network as having cleared primary forests and peatlands. The 2015 peatland fires, linked to the agricultural expansion of Indonesian palm oil companies including Wilmar's suppliers, caused one of the worst air pollution crises in recent decades.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Forced labour on supplier plantations, deaths from smoke caused by peatland fires, and violent conflicts with indigenous peoples are documented.

1. **Forced labour and abuse on supplier plantations (2016)** — An Amnesty International investigation found that workers on plantations supplying Wilmar in Indonesia were subjected to forced labour, child labour, withheld wages, and physical abuse. The company's suppliers systematically violated ILO conventions on workers' rights, yet Wilmar continued purchasing from them after the violations were documented.
   *Source: Amnesty International — "The Great Palm Oil Scandal" (2016)*

2. **Peatland fires and mass smoke pollution (2015)** — Indonesian peatland fires in 2015 — partly caused by the drainage and burning of peatlands for palm oil plantations, including those of Wilmar's suppliers — released toxic smoke over six countries. A Harvard and Columbia University study estimated that approximately 100,000 premature deaths were caused by the haze in Indonesia, Malaysia, and Singapore.
   *Source: Marlier et al., Harvard / Columbia University, GeoHealth (2015)*

3. **Violent displacement of indigenous communities in West Kalimantan** — Land conflicts between Wilmar's subsidiary plantations and indigenous Dayak communities in West Kalimantan have been documented by Global Witness and Chain Reaction Research. Residents report the destruction of community gardens, blocking of access to ancestral lands, and intimidation of community leaders opposing plantation expansion.
   *Source: Global Witness — "Wilmar's Dirty Dozen" (2013); Chain Reaction Research (2018)*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Destruction of Kalimantan peat forests and contribution to the 2015 fire catastrophe are documented major environmental incidents.

1. **2015 peatland fires: CO₂ emissions exceeding Germany's annual output** — The 2015 fires in Indonesia burned millions of hectares of peatlands, including areas linked to the plantation supply chains of Wilmar and its suppliers. According to Global Fire Emissions Database (GFED) estimates, CO₂ equivalent emissions from Indonesian peatland fires in that season exceeded Germany's total annual greenhouse gas output. The fires also destroyed unique ecosystems — peat swamp forests that took thousands of years to form.
   *Source: GFED; World Resources Institute (2015)*

2. **Destruction of orangutan habitat in Kalimantan** — Palm oil plantation expansion by Wilmar's subsidiary and supplier companies in Borneo (Kalimantan) has led to large-scale destruction of primary forests — the key habitat of Bornean orangutans (Pongo pygmaeus), listed as Critically Endangered on the IUCN Red List. Greenpeace investigations documented the felling of forests in orangutan habitat in concessions directly linked to Wilmar.
   *Source: Greenpeace — "Wilmar: Certifying Destruction" (2013); IUCN Red List*

3. **Peatland drainage and irreversible loss of carbon stocks** — Wilmar's supplier concessions in Sumatra and Kalimantan are located on deep peatlands. Drainage and burning of peat destroys carbon stocks accumulated over thousands of years and is effectively irreversible on a human timescale. Chain Reaction Research identified specific Wilmar supplier concessions with ongoing peatland clearance after the NDPE commitment was adopted.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research, "Wilmar's Exposure to Deforestation" (2019)*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Wilmar's NDPE policy was adopted without effective enforcement mechanisms, and the company continued purchasing from suppliers documented as violating the declared standards.

1. **NDPE commitment (2013) without real enforcement** — In December 2013, Wilmar adopted one of the first NDPE policies among palm oil traders. However, subsequent investigations by Greenpeace, RAN, and Chain Reaction Research repeatedly documented that the company's suppliers continued to clear forests and drain peatlands. Wilmar's response to violations was to issue warnings and temporary suspensions rather than permanent exclusions.
   *Source: Rainforest Action Network — "Wilmar's NDPE Implementation Gap" (2018)*

2. **Continuing purchases from violating suppliers** — In 2018, Greenpeace published satellite data showing that palm oil companies with active deforestation were still supplying Wilmar. The company acknowledged "non-compliance" on the part of some suppliers but maintained commercial relationships, citing "engagement" as preferable to exclusion. Critics characterised this as a systematic gap between public commitments and business practice.
   *Source: Greenpeace — "massif destruction" report (2018)*

3. **"Sustainable palm oil" narrative amid documented violations** — Wilmar actively used the narrative of "industry transformation" and "responsible sourcing" in investor and client communications while Amnesty International documented forced child labour in its supply chain. After the Amnesty report was published, the company's response was limited to a review of "grievance procedures" rather than systemic supply chain changes.
   *Source: Amnesty International (2016); Wilmar response statements*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Wilmar actively publishes sustainability reports, maintains a public supplier monitoring dashboard, and participates in international initiatives.

1. **Annual sustainability reports to GRI standards** — Wilmar annually publishes Sustainability Reports in accordance with GRI standards, declaring progress in reducing deforestation, implementing NDPE policy, and improving working conditions. The reports are used in investor relations and tender documentation for clients.
   *Source: Wilmar Sustainability Report 2022*

2. **Wilmar Sustainability Dashboard (from 2020)** — The company launched a public online dashboard for monitoring the sustainability status of its supplier base, positioning this as evidence of supply chain transparency. Critics noted that the dashboard relies on supplier self-declarations and does not include independent verification by satellite or ground inspection.
   *Source: Wilmar Sustainability Dashboard; Chain Reaction Research critique (2021)*

3. **Membership in RSPO and Tropical Forest Alliance** — Wilmar is a member of the Roundtable on Sustainable Palm Oil (RSPO) and the Tropical Forest Alliance, positioning this as evidence of commitment to industry transformation. NGOs point out that RSPO membership does not prevent documented violations in the supply chain.
   *Source: RSPO membership records; Tropical Forest Alliance*

---

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Wilmar has been included in sustainability indices and received industry recognition despite documented violations.

1. **Inclusion in the FTSE4Good ASEAN 5 Index** — Wilmar is included in the FTSE4Good ASEAN 5 Index, which is used in ESG investment products focused on Southeast Asian markets. Index inclusion criteria are based primarily on corporate disclosure quality rather than on independent verification of actual impact.
   *Source: FTSE Russell, FTSE4Good ASEAN Index*

2. **RSPO Impact Award** — Wilmar has received recognition within the RSPO framework as a company contributing to the transformation of the palm oil market. Critics note that RSPO awards are distributed among the organisation's members, creating a structural conflict of interest.
   *Source: RSPO Awards; NGO critique of RSPO governance*

---

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Wilmar sells RSPO-certified palm oil as "sustainable" despite documented supply chain violations.

1. **RSPO-certified oil from plantations with documented violations** — Wilmar sells palm oil volumes under RSPO "Mass Balance" and "Segregated" certification to consumer goods manufacturers. Chain Reaction Research and Greenpeace documented cases where RSPO-certified volumes were physically mixed with palm oil from suppliers that had active deforestation, making the certification label misleading.
   *Source: Chain Reaction Research (2019); Greenpeace analysis of RSPO Mass Balance*

2. **"NDPE-compliant" labelling in B2B marketing** — In communications with corporate clients (Nestlé, Unilever, P&G), Wilmar positions its palm oil as "NDPE-compliant", citing a company-level policy rather than verification of each supply chain link. Amnesty International demonstrated that this labelling did not reflect actual conditions on supplier plantations.
   *Source: Amnesty International (2016); Wilmar supplier communications*

3. **"Sustainable sourcing" narrative in retailer supply chains** — Palm oil from Wilmar's supply chain enters consumer products labelled "sustainably sourced" on retail shelves, although the end products cannot be traced to specific plantations due to the mass balance system used.
   *Source: RAN — "Conflict Palm Oil in the Candy Bar" (2020)*

---

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Wilmar runs peatland and biodiversity protection programmes that are incommensurate in scale with the environmental damage caused by its supply chain.

1. **Peat Care programme** — Wilmar declared the Peat Care programme, targeting the protection and restoration of peatlands within its own concessions. The declared coverage of several thousand hectares is negligible compared with the millions of hectares of peatlands destroyed in the company's supply chain over decades.
   *Source: Wilmar Sustainability Reports; Chain Reaction Research assessment*

2. **Biodiversity protection zones near plantations** — Wilmar reports on maintaining High Conservation Value (HCV) zones near its plantation concessions. Environmental assessments note that HCV zones are often located in degraded or low-biodiversity areas, while primary forests were cleared at the plantation expansion stage.
   *Source: HCV Network assessment; Greenpeace Indonesia*

3. **Supplier training programmes** — Wilmar periodically organises training for suppliers on good agricultural practice and NDPE requirements. NGOs document that training does not translate into measurable changes in supply chain deforestation metrics.
   *Source: RAN supplier engagement critique (2021)*

---

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Wilmar lobbied against stricter biofuel regulations in the EU and promotes RSPO as an adequate substitute for mandatory government standards.

1. **Lobbying against EU RED II biofuels restrictions (2017–2019)** — As the EU developed the Renewable Energy Directive (RED II), which introduced restrictions on palm oil biofuels due to deforestation risk, Wilmar and its trade associations actively lobbied against these measures. The company funded analytical publications arguing that "sustainable" palm oil should not be excluded, and that the rules discriminated against developing country producers.
   *Source: InfluenceMap, EU biofuels lobbying database (2018–2019)*

2. **Promoting RSPO as a sufficient alternative to government regulation** — Wilmar consistently presents RSPO as a sufficient mechanism for governing sustainability in the palm oil sector, opposing mandatory legislation (such as the EU Deforestation Regulation) that would require independent supply chain verification. The company argues that "voluntary market mechanisms" are more effective than regulatory approaches.
   *Source: Wilmar position papers on EU Deforestation Regulation (2021–2022)*

3. **Countering scientific data on deforestation through industry associations** — Through the Indonesian Palm Oil Association (GAPKI) and Malaysian Palm Oil Council, which Wilmar supplies, the industry promoted claims about the low deforestation risk of certified palm oil, contradicting satellite monitoring data from Global Forest Watch.
   *Source: Global Forest Watch; GAPKI industry communications*

## Summary

1. **Mass harm to people?** — **YES** — Forced labour on supplier plantations (Amnesty 2016); ~100,000 deaths from 2015 peatland fire smoke (Harvard/Columbia); violent displacement of Dayak communities in West Kalimantan.
2. **Major environmental disasters?** — **YES** — 2015 peatland fires with CO₂ emissions exceeding Germany's annual output; destruction of Bornean orangutan habitat; irreversible peatland drainage.
3. **Greenwashing?** — **YES** — NDPE policy without enforcement; continuing purchases from violating suppliers; "sustainable" narrative amid documented forced labour.
4. **ESG marketing?** — **YES** — GRI reports; Wilmar Sustainability Dashboard; RSPO and Tropical Forest Alliance membership.
5. **ESG award wins?** — **YES** — FTSE4Good ASEAN 5 Index inclusion; RSPO Impact Award.
6. **Eco-labels on harmful products?** — **YES** — RSPO "sustainable" certification mixed with deforestation-linked volumes; "NDPE-compliant" B2B labelling without verification.
7. **Negligible eco-events?** — **YES** — Peat Care programme covering thousands of hectares against millions destroyed; HCV zones in degraded areas; supplier training without measurable impact.
8. **Lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?** — **YES** — Lobbying against EU RED II palm oil restrictions; promoting RSPO as substitute for mandatory regulation; countering satellite deforestation data through industry associations.
