Yara International — the world's largest producer of mineral nitrogen fertilisers — is responsible both for direct N₂O emissions from production and for secondary emissions when fertilisers are applied in agriculture.

Ammonia production (the basis of nitrogen fertilisers) by the Haber-Bosch process requires enormous volumes of natural gas: global ammonia production consumes approximately 2% of the world's primary energy. Yara is attempting to transition to "green" ammonia, but the scale of this effort remains negligible. The application of Yara fertilisers on corn and wheat fields across Europe and North America generates nitrate runoff. EU monitoring records exceedances of nitrate standards in groundwater near intensively fertilised agricultural land. Excess nitrates in rivers feed algal blooms and "dead zones" in the Baltic Sea and the Gulf of Mexico.

## 1. Has the corporation's activity led to mass harm to human lives?

**Answer: YES**. Nitrate contamination of drinking water and "dead zones" destroying fishing grounds have a direct impact on public health and livelihoods.

1. **Nitrate contamination of drinking water in Europe** — The EU Nitrates Directive records exceedances of nitrate standards (50 mg/l) in groundwater near intensively fertilised agricultural regions in Germany, the Netherlands, and Poland. "Blue baby syndrome" (methaemoglobinaemia) — a life-threatening condition in infants — is a direct consequence of nitrate water contamination linked to agricultural fertiliser use.
   *Source: EEA — Nitrates in European Waters; EU Nitrates Directive reports*

2. **"Dead zone" in the Baltic Sea** — The Baltic Sea contains one of the world's largest oxygen-depleted zones. HELCOM (Helsinki Commission) has established that the primary source of nitrate runoff is agriculture, which makes extensive use of nitrogen fertilisers from Yara and other producers. Fishermen in the Baltic region are losing commercial fishing grounds as a result of the oxygen depletion.
   *Source: HELCOM — "State of the Baltic Sea" reports*

3. **Explosion at the West Fertilizer plant in Texas (2013)** — At the West Fertilizer Co. facility in West, Texas, which stored ammonium nitrate supplied by Yara (among other providers), an explosion killed 15 people (including 12 firefighters) and injured more than 160. This was the largest industrial disaster in Texas in recent decades and raised urgent questions about the safety of ammonium nitrate storage and regulation.
   *Source: U.S. Chemical Safety and Hazard Investigation Board — West Fertilizer Co. report (2016)*

## 2. Has the corporation been the cause of major environmental disasters?

**Answer: YES**. Systemic contribution to the "dead zones" of the Baltic Sea and Gulf of Mexico represents one of the largest chronic environmental catastrophes.

1. **"Dead zone" in the Gulf of Mexico** — Nitrate runoff from fertilised Midwest fields flowing down the Mississippi River annually creates a "dead zone" in the Gulf of Mexico of up to 22,000 km² — a zone with critically low oxygen levels in which all marine life perishes. NOAA directly links the dead zone to overloading with nitrogen fertilisers from agricultural runoff.
   *Source: NOAA — Gulf of Mexico Hypoxic Zone annual monitoring*

2. **Baltic Sea — the world's largest "dead zone"** — The area of the anoxic zone in the Baltic reaches 70,000 km² — the largest in the world. HELCOM documents a direct connection to nitrogen pollutants from agriculture across Scandinavia and the Baltic states, where Yara is the dominant fertiliser supplier.
   *Source: HELCOM State of the Baltic Sea (2023)*

3. **Emergency ammonia release in the Netherlands** — At Yara's plant in Sluiskil (Netherlands), an ammonia release occurred in 2020 that required personnel evacuation. Similar incidents have been recorded at other Yara facilities.
   *Source: RIVM Netherlands; Yara incident reports*

## 3. Has the company engaged in greenwashing?

**Answer: YES**. Yara promotes "green ammonia" as a climate solution while more than 99% of its production remains based on natural gas.

1. **"Green ammonia" as the main marketing narrative at ~0% of production** — Yara actively promotes "green" and "blue" ammonia projects, yet by 2024 their combined capacity represents a negligible fraction of the company's total production (~9 million tonnes/year). The marketing emphasis on "green ammonia" creates a false impression of climate transformation while fossil-based production continues at scale.
   *Source: IEA Ammonia Technology Roadmap; Yara Annual Report 2023*

2. **"Precision Farming" as the response to the nitrate crisis** — Yara promotes "precision agriculture" (Atfarm, YaraIrix digital tools) as a solution to nitrate contamination, while the volumes of fertilisers sold by the company continue to grow. Precision agriculture optimises fertiliser application but does not eliminate the systemic nitrate runoff problem at existing production volumes.
   *Source: Yara Digital Farming; EEA Nitrates Directive*

3. **Positioning as a "climate company"** — Yara describes itself as a "climate company" introducing sustainable plant nutrition technologies, despite ammonia production from natural gas being one of the world's largest industrial sources of CO₂ emissions.
   *Source: Yara Corporate Website; IEA Ammonia analysis*

## 4. Does the company engage in ESG marketing?

**Answer: YES**. Yara actively promotes the ESG narrative, positioning itself as a leader in the transition to sustainable fertilisers.

1. **Annual sustainability reports** — Yara publishes detailed Sustainability Reports in accordance with GRI and SASB standards, including data on emission intensity per tonne of ammonia and progress on "green" hydrogen projects.
   *Source: Yara Sustainability Report 2023*

2. **"Climate Roadmap" to 2030 and 2050** — The company declared targets to reduce Scope 1 and 2 emissions by 30% by 2030 and achieve "net zero" by 2050, which is actively used in ESG communications with investors and clients.
   *Source: Yara Climate Roadmap*

3. **"Farming for the Future" campaign** — Yara launched a public campaign "Farming for the Future", promoting sustainable agriculture as the company's mission and positioning fertiliser use as essential to feeding a growing world population sustainably.
   *Source: Yara Farming for the Future campaign*

## 5. Has the corporation won any global ESG awards?

**Answer: YES**. Yara has received recognition in ESG ratings as a leader in "sustainable fertilisers".

1. **Inclusion in the Dow Jones Sustainability Index (DJSI)** — Yara is included in DJSI World as one of the sustainability leaders in the chemicals sector, which is used in investor relations to attract ESG-oriented capital.
   *Source: S&P Global DJSI*

2. **CDP A-List Climate** — Yara has repeatedly received an "A" rating from CDP in the Climate Change category, positioning itself as a climate leader in agrochemicals. The rating is based primarily on disclosure quality and declared targets rather than independently verified emission reductions.
   *Source: CDP Climate Scores*

## 6. Has the company applied eco-labels to products with negative environmental impact?

**Answer: YES**. Yara fertilisers are labelled as "precise", "efficient", and "sustainable", although their application inevitably contributes to nitrate runoff.

1. **"Environmentally Responsible Fertiliser" — ENTEC labelling** — Yara promotes fertilisers with nitrification inhibitors (ENTEC) as "environmentally responsible", claiming reduced N₂O emissions. However, nitrate runoff into water bodies from the use of these fertilisers persists, and the environmental claims are made without reference to downstream water quality impacts.
   *Source: Yara ENTEC product marketing*

2. **"Efficient" and "Sustainable" labelling on the YaraMila range** — The YaraMila compound fertiliser range is labelled as "more efficient" and "more sustainable" without disclosing data on nitrate footprint under specific application conditions or environmental context.
   *Source: Yara product catalog*

## 7. Has the company organised eco-events negligible in scale?

**Answer: YES**. Yara's "precision agriculture" and "green ammonia" programmes are negligible compared with the scale of nitrate contamination driven by its products.

1. **Pilot "green ammonia" projects** — Yara launched pilot green ammonia installations in Norway (Porsgrunn) and Australia with combined capacity of less than 1% of total production. These projects receive extensive PR coverage, creating an impression of large-scale climate transformation while the vast majority of production remains fossil-based.
   *Source: Yara Green Ammonia projects; IEA Ammonia*

2. **Atfarm precision agriculture digital tool** — The Atfarm application helps farmers optimise fertiliser doses, but covers a negligible fraction of the land area fertilised with Yara products. The systemic contribution to nitrate runoff from Yara's production volumes is not addressed by this tool.
   *Source: Yara Digital Farming*

## 8. Has the company engaged in lobbying for pseudo-ecological theories?

**Answer: YES**. Yara actively lobbies policy, promoting the narrative that mineral fertilisers are an indispensable tool for "sustainably" feeding the world.

1. **Lobbying against EU nitrate restrictions** — Yara and industry associations (Fertilizers Europe) lobbied against the tightening of EU nitrate directives and requirements to reduce synthetic fertiliser use under the EU "Farm to Fork" strategy, arguing that this threatens food security. The lobbying helped delay binding reduction targets for synthetic fertiliser application.
   *Source: Fertilizers Europe lobbying disclosures; EU Farm to Fork opposition*

2. **Promoting "green ammonia" as a substitute for regulation** — Yara actively promotes "green ammonia" as the future of the sector, using this to postpone stricter regulation of existing fossil-based production. This narrative has been adopted by EU regulators, despite the negligible scale of actual green production.
   *Source: IEA Ammonia Technology Roadmap*

3. **"Without fertilisers, hunger" narrative** — Yara systematically promotes the thesis that without synthetic nitrogen fertilisers it is impossible to feed humanity, crowding out discussion of reduced meat consumption, agroecology, and organic farming as alternatives to industrial fertiliser dependency.
   *Source: Yara Food Chain Initiative; IPES Food analysis*
